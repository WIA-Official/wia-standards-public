## Theory

### The Misconception of "Building from Scratch"

When GPT-3 was released in 2022, many people thought:

> "To build that, you'd need a GPU cluster worth hundreds of millions of won and hundreds of engineers."

That's correct. If you want to build GPT-3 **from scratch**. But we're talking about a different path.

The AI development ecosystem after 2024 has transformed completely. Stability AI released Stable Diffusion as open-source, Meta released Llama 3.1, and Mistral released Mixtral. The **weights** of models trained with hundreds of millions of dollars in investment are freely available.

**Fine-tuning** these weights to match your specific purpose—adjusting them just a little—is what it's about. And that can be done by one person in 2-3 weeks with a single A100 GPU.

### Hongik Ingan and Open Source

The philosophical foundation for writing this book is Hongik Ingan (弘益人間—benefit all humanity). **Benefit people widely.**

Open-source is its modern practice. Thousands of researchers freely share knowledge accumulated over years, allowing the next person to stand on those shoulders and reach even higher places. Newton's saying about "standing on the shoulders of giants" becomes a reality in the AI era through open-source.

This book teaches you how to stand on those shoulders.

### A Solo Developer's Practical Strategy

The strategy we recommend for solo developers starting AI development consists of three layers.

**Layer 1: Leveraging Open-Source Foundation Models**
Build on already-validated models like Stable Diffusion XL, Flux.1, Llama 3.1, Qwen2, etc. These are products of months of training and hundreds of millions of won in computational costs. They're free.

**Layer 2: API Combination**
Paid APIs like Claude, GPT-4V, and Gemini Vision are powerful auxiliary engines. Without GPU infrastructure, simple API calls handle image description, text correction, and quality assessment. You're essentially renting functionality worth hundreds of millions of won for tens of dollars per month.

**Layer 3: One Core Fine-Tuning**
Concentrate domain-specific fine-tuning in exactly one place. For image restoration, use SDXL Inpainting. For Korean OCR, use Tesseract + LLM post-processing. If you try to train everything, your research budget runs out in 6 months.

### The Arrival of Claude Code

There's one more reason this strategy became feasible. Claude Code.

Until 2025, the biggest bottleneck for solo developers was **code writing speed**. Building a single pipeline took a week. Now, just explain your intent in natural language and Claude Code immediately generates executable code. Debugging, architecture design, documentation—all included.

We've entered an era where a solo developer's productivity approaches a 10-person team.

## Pitfall Cards — Five Common Mistakes

1. **"You can't start without a GPU"** → False. Theory learning, code writing, and data preparation work fine on CPU. GPU is only needed for actual training, which you can rent from the cloud on an hourly basis.

2. **"You need to master Python perfectly"** → Basics are enough. Function definitions, lists, dictionaries, for loops. Claude Code fills in the rest.

3. **"You should use the latest models"** → For fine-tuning purposes, validated older versions are actually better. Latest models often lack fine-tuning recipes.

4. **"Building from scratch is the real deal"** → Leveraging open-source is now the standard in the AI industry. OpenAI itself uses open-source models internally.

5. **"There are limits to working alone"** → The author of this book didn't know Rust at all but wrote 6 volumes herself. With Claude Code as your partner, a solo developer's limits are much further than you'd think.

## Practice Problems

**Problem 1.** Explain the three-layer structure of the open-source foundation model utilization strategy in your own words. Clearly distinguish the role of each layer.

> **Answer:** Layer 1 is the foundation (open-source weights), Layer 2 is auxiliary intelligence (APIs), and Layer 3 is domain specialization (one fine-tuning). The core principle is "don't build from scratch." You build only what you need on top of existing assets.

**Problem 2.** If you had to choose the most cost-effective option among Lambda Labs, AWS g5, Paperspace, and Vast.ai, which would you choose? Why? (No fixed answer—describe your reasoning)

> **Answer:** Generally Vast.ai is cheapest but may have lower reliability. The goal is understanding the tradeoff between stability and cost. For important training, Lambda Labs or AWS is reasonable; for exploratory experiments, Vast.ai is a practical choice.

**Problem 3.** Why is the principle "concentrate fine-tuning in exactly one place" important? Describe 2 or more practical problems that arise when trying to fine-tune multiple domains simultaneously.

> **Answer:** ① Research budget (GPU costs) gets spread thin, preventing completion of any single project ② Each domain requires separate time for data collection and preprocessing ③ Quality comparison validation between fine-tuned models requires additional time. Selective focus is a solo developer's survival strategy.
