Chapter 5

Indoor Air Quality

Ensuring healthy, productive indoor environments through advanced ventilation, filtration, and air quality management strategies.

The Health Impact of Indoor Air Quality

People spend approximately 90% of their time indoors, where air pollutant concentrations can be 2-5 times higher than outdoor levels. Poor indoor air quality contributes to respiratory diseases, allergies, cognitive impairment, and reduced productivity. Research shows that improved IAQ can increase cognitive function by 61% and reduce sick building syndrome complaints by 80-90%. Smart HVAC systems prioritize air quality as equally important as temperature control.

The COVID-19 pandemic dramatically elevated awareness of indoor air quality's role in disease transmission. ASHRAE updated ventilation standards to emphasize airborne infection control, and building operators worldwide upgraded filtration and increased outdoor air ventilation rates. These changes persist as occupants demand healthier indoor environments and employers recognize the productivity benefits of superior air quality.

Ventilation Strategies

Ventilation dilutes indoor pollutants with outdoor air, serving as the primary IAQ management strategy. ASHRAE Standard 62.1 defines minimum ventilation rates based on occupancy and building type, but many applications benefit from higher rates. Smart systems balance ventilation with energy efficiency, increasing outdoor air when needed while minimizing conditioning costs.

Demand-Controlled Ventilation (DCV)

DCV modulates outdoor air ventilation based on measured CO2 levels, which serve as a proxy for occupancy and metabolic activity. When CO2 concentrations rise, indicating higher occupancy or metabolic rates, systems automatically increase outdoor air flow. This maintains air quality while reducing over-ventilation during low-occupancy periods, typically saving 20-40% of ventilation energy compared to fixed-rate approaches.

Natural Ventilation Integration

When outdoor conditions permit, natural ventilation through operable windows and mechanical window controls can provide fresh air without fan energy. Smart systems monitor outdoor temperature, humidity, and air quality to determine when natural ventilation is beneficial, automatically opening windows or louvers while reducing mechanical ventilation. This hybrid approach delivers energy savings of 30-60% in appropriate climates.

ASHRAE Ventilation Rate Guidelines (Standard 62.1-2022)

  • Office Spaces: 5 CFM per person + 0.06 CFM per square foot
  • Conference Rooms: 5 CFM per person + 0.06 CFM per square foot
  • Classrooms: 10 CFM per person + 0.12 CFM per square foot
  • Retail Spaces: 7.5 CFM per person + 0.06 CFM per square foot
  • Gymnasiums: 20 CFM per person + 0.06 CFM per square foot
  • Healthcare (patient rooms): 25 CFM per person minimum

Filtration and Air Cleaning

Mechanical filtration removes particulate matter from recirculated air, with efficiency rated by Minimum Efficiency Reporting Value (MERV) scales. Higher MERV ratings capture smaller particles but increase air resistance and fan energy. Smart systems monitor filter differential pressure to optimize replacement schedules and alert operators when filters become loaded.

Filter Type MERV Rating Captured Particles Applications
Basic Pleated MERV 6-8 Dust, pollen, mold spores (>3 µm) Residential, light commercial
High-Efficiency Pleated MERV 11-13 Fine dust, bacteria, virus carriers (1-3 µm) Commercial buildings, improved IAQ
HEPA (High-Efficiency Particulate Air) MERV 17-20 (H13-H14) 99.97% of 0.3 µm particles Hospitals, cleanrooms, high-risk environments
Activated Carbon N/A (gas-phase) VOCs, odors, gaseous pollutants Chemical labs, industrial, urban environments
Electrostatic MERV 8-16 Charged particle attraction Retrofit applications, washable filters

UV-C Disinfection

Ultraviolet-C light (254 nm wavelength) inactivates airborne pathogens by damaging their DNA/RNA, preventing replication. UV-C systems installed in HVAC ducts or upper-room applications provide continuous disinfection without chemical treatments. Research shows properly designed UV-C systems can reduce airborne virus concentrations by 90-99%, complementing filtration for comprehensive pathogen control.

UV-C Safety Considerations

Direct UV-C exposure causes skin burns and eye damage. Systems must be designed to prevent human exposure through shielding, interlocking with fan operation, and proper installation in ducts or above-head-height applications. Ozone-producing UV lamps (185 nm) should be avoided in occupied spaces due to respiratory irritation risks.

Source Control and Material Selection

The most effective IAQ strategy eliminates pollutants at their source rather than diluting or filtering them after release. Building material selection significantly impacts VOC emissions, with low-emission paints, adhesives, flooring, and furniture reducing indoor pollution by 60-80%. Green building standards like LEED and WELL mandate low-VOC materials and require IAQ testing before occupancy.

Low-Emission Material Standards

  • Greenguard Gold: Stringent chemical emissions limits for sensitive populations
  • FloorScore: Certified low-emission flooring products
  • California Section 01350: VOC emission testing for commercial interiors
  • BIFMA e3: Furniture emissions and sustainability standard
  • CDPH Standard Method V1.2: Indoor emissions testing for materials

Humidity Control for Health

Maintaining relative humidity between 40-60% optimizes human comfort while minimizing biological contaminant growth and virus survival. Humidity below 30% causes respiratory irritation and increases influenza transmission, while levels above 70% promote mold growth and dust mite proliferation. Smart HVAC systems actively manage humidity through dedicated dehumidification equipment or coordinated cooling and ventilation strategies.

Seasonal Humidity Challenges

Winter heating reduces indoor humidity to uncomfortable and unhealthy levels (often 15-25% RH), requiring humidification to reach target ranges. Summer conditions in humid climates challenge cooling systems to dehumidify while maintaining temperature setpoints. Advanced systems employ desiccant dehumidifiers or subcooling with reheat to achieve humidity control independent of sensible cooling loads.

IAQ Monitoring and Reporting

Real-time IAQ monitoring with occupant-facing displays builds trust and demonstrates building operator commitment to health. Studies show that visible IAQ metrics improve occupant satisfaction even without actual air quality improvements—the transparency itself communicates care and attention. Many smart buildings now feature lobby displays showing current CO2, PM2.5, VOC levels, and outdoor air ventilation rates.

Key Takeaways

  • Poor indoor air quality significantly impacts health, cognitive function, and productivity, with indoor pollutant concentrations often 2-5 times higher than outdoors.
  • Comprehensive IAQ management monitors CO2, particulate matter, VOCs, formaldehyde, humidity, and pathogens, employing tailored strategies for each parameter.
  • Demand-controlled ventilation based on CO2 sensing reduces ventilation energy by 20-40% while maintaining superior air quality compared to fixed ventilation rates.
  • High-efficiency filtration (MERV 13-16) and UV-C disinfection provide complementary approaches to particulate and pathogen removal, with UV-C achieving 90-99% virus inactivation.
  • Source control through low-emission material selection eliminates pollutants before release, reducing indoor VOC levels by 60-80% compared to conventional materials.
  • The 弘益人間 philosophy guides IAQ priorities toward creating healthy environments that enhance human flourishing and demonstrate technology serving humanity's highest interests.
