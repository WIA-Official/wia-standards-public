3.1 System Architecture Overview

The WIA-ENE-007 technical architecture defines a comprehensive, modular framework for hydrogen energy systems that enables interoperability, scalability, and safety across all deployment scenarios. This chapter details the architectural components, interfaces, and design principles that form the foundation of compliant hydrogen energy systems.

Three-Layer Architecture Model

Layer 1: Physical Infrastructure

  • Production facilities (electrolyzers, reformers, etc.)
  • Storage systems (tanks, caverns, carriers)
  • Distribution networks (pipelines, transport vehicles)
  • Utilization equipment (fuel cells, combustion systems)
  • Safety systems and sensors

Layer 2: Control & Monitoring

  • SCADA (Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition) systems
  • Process control systems and PLCs
  • Safety instrumented systems (SIS)
  • Quality monitoring and analytics
  • Predictive maintenance algorithms

Layer 3: Information & Integration

  • Enterprise resource planning (ERP) integration
  • Energy management systems (EMS)
  • Market platforms and trading interfaces
  • Regulatory reporting and compliance tracking
  • Customer interfaces and billing systems

3.2 Production Architecture

Electrolyzer System Design

A complete electrolyzer system comprises multiple subsystems that must be carefully integrated:

Core Components

Component Function Key Specifications Criticality
Electrolyzer Stack H₂O → H₂ + O₂ conversion Power: 0.5-10+ MW, Efficiency: 65-80% Critical
Power Supply / Rectifier AC to DC conversion, power conditioning Efficiency: >96%, THD: <5% Critical

Gas Processing H₂ drying, purification, compression Output purity: 99.9-99.999% Critical
Thermal Management Cooling, heat recovery ±2°C temperature stability High
Control System Process automation, safety interlocks SIL 2 or higher rating Critical
O₂ Management Oxygen handling, sale, or venting Purity: 99.5%+ for sale Medium

Water Treatment Architecture

Electrolyzer water quality is critical for performance and longevity. The treatment train must achieve ultra-pure water specifications: 

Multi-Stage Water Treatment Process:

Stage 1: Pre-filtration
- Sediment removal (5-10 μm)
- Chlorine removal (activated carbon)
- Input: Potable water or better

Stage 2: Reverse Osmosis (RO)
- TDS removal: 95-98%
- Output: <10 ppm TDS
- Pressure: 10-20 bar

Stage 3: Electrodeionization (EDI) or Mixed Bed Deionization
- Ion removal to trace levels
- Output conductivity: <0.1 μS/cm
- Output resistivity: >10 MΩ·cm

Stage 4: Final Polishing
- UV sterilization (optional)
- 0.2 μm absolute filtration
- Storage in non-leaching tanks

Water Quality Targets for PEM Electrolyzers:
- Conductivity: < 0.1 μS/cm (at 25°C)
- Total Organic Carbon (TOC): < 100 ppb
- Silica (SiO₂): < 5 ppb
- Chloride (Cl⁻): < 5 ppb
- Sodium (Na⁺): < 5 ppb
- Calcium (Ca²⁺): < 5 ppb
- Bacteria: < 1 CFU/mL

Gas Processing and Purification

Raw hydrogen from the electrolyzer must be processed to meet end-use specifications:

Processing Train

  1. Phase Separation: Remove entrained water droplets (knockout drum, coalescing filters)
  2. Compression: Increase pressure for storage/transport (reciprocating, diaphragm, or ionic liquid compressors)
  3. Drying: Remove water vapor to prevent freezing and corrosion
    • Pressure swing adsorption (PSA) with molecular sieves
    • Target: < -70°C pressure dew point
  4. Final Purification (if required): Remove trace impurities
    • PSA for bulk gas purification
    • Catalytic purification for CO/CO₂ removal
    • Membrane separation for final polishing
  5. Quality Verification: Online and laboratory analysis to verify ISO 14687 compliance

3.3 Storage System Architecture

Compressed Gas Storage Design

Large-scale compressed hydrogen storage requires careful engineering to ensure safety, efficiency, and longevity:

Multi-Vessel Storage Configuration

Typical Industrial Storage Layout:

Configuration: Cascaded pressure levels for efficient filling
- Low pressure bank: 200-300 bar (40% of vessels)
- Medium pressure bank: 400-500 bar (30% of vessels)
- High pressure bank: 600-700 bar (30% of vessels)

Vessel Specifications:
- Material: ASME SA-372 Grade J or equivalent
- Design pressure: 1.25 × operating pressure
- Test pressure: 1.5 × design pressure
- Minimum wall thickness per ASME BPVC Section VIII
- Periodic inspection: 3-5 years depending on jurisdiction

Capacity Calculation Example:
Storage target: 1,000 kg H₂
Pressure: 450 bar average
Temperature: 15°C

Using ideal gas law (with compressibility factor Z ≈ 1.05):
Volume required ≈ 28 m³ (water capacity)

Typical implementation:
- 8 vessels × 3.5 m³ each
- Redundancy: n+1 configuration
- Total installed capacity: 31.5 m³

Liquid Hydrogen Storage Architecture

Cryogenic liquid hydrogen storage presents unique engineering challenges requiring specialized design:

Vacuum-Jacketed Storage Vessels

Component Design Requirements Typical Values
Inner Vessel Cryogenic-grade stainless steel (304L, 316L) Temperature: -253°C, Pressure: 1-5 bar
Insulation Multi-layer insulation (MLI) + vacuum Vacuum: <10⁻⁵ mbar, 30-80 layers MLI
Outer Vessel Carbon steel or stainless steel Ambient temperature, structural support
Support Structure Low thermal conductivity materials (G10, titanium) Minimize heat leak to <1% of capacity/day
Safety Relief Multiple pressure relief devices Set pressure: 110-120% design pressure

Boil-off Management

Boil-off Minimization Strategies:

1. Passive Measures:
   - High-performance vacuum insulation
   - Minimized penetrations and thermal bridges
   - Optimized tank geometry (spherical preferred)

   Target: <0.3%/day for large tanks (>50 m³)

2. Active Measures:
   - Cryocoolers for zero boil-off operation
   - Sub-cooling to increase margin
   - Pressure building for demand response

3. Boil-off Utilization:
   - Fuel cell power generation
   - Blending into natural gas network
   - Re-liquefaction (for very large facilities)

Economic Optimization:
- Boil-off <0.2%/day: Generally acceptable
- Boil-off >1%/day: Justifies investment in mitigation
- Zero boil-off: Required for long-term storage (>30 days)

Pipeline Sizing Methodology

Hydrogen Pipeline Flow Calculations:

Given:
- Mass flow rate: ṁ (kg/s)
- Inlet pressure: P₁ (bar)
- Outlet pressure: P₂ (bar)
- Pipeline length: L (m)
- Operating temperature: T (K)

Simplified sizing (isothermal, horizontal):

Diameter calculation using Weymouth equation (modified for H₂):

D = 0.0212 × (ṁ² × L × T × Z / (P₁² - P₂²))^0.2

Where:
- D = internal diameter (m)
- Z = compressibility factor (~1.0 for H₂ at typical conditions)

Example:
Transport 1,000 kg/h H₂ over 50 km
P₁ = 60 bar, P₂ = 50 bar, T = 288 K

D ≈ 0.30 m (12 inches NPS)

Velocity check:
v = ṁ / (ρ × A)
Target: 15-40 m/s for hydrogen (higher than NG due to lower density)

Compression requirements:
ΔP per 100 km ≈ 10-20 bar (depending on flow rate and diameter)
Recompression stations: Every 100-200 km

Industrial Process Integration

Hydrogen integration into industrial processes requires careful interface design:

Steel Production - Direct Reduced Iron (DRI)

H₂-DRI Process Architecture:

Iron Ore Reduction Reaction:
Fe₂O₃ + 3H₂ → 2Fe + 3H₂O
Operating conditions: 800-900°C, 1-5 bar

Process Flow:
1. Ore Preparation
   - Pelletizing or briquetting
   - Size: 10-20 mm typical

2. Hydrogen Supply System
   - Flow rate: ~60 kg H₂ per ton Fe produced
   - Purity requirement: >95% (less stringent than fuel cells)
   - Pressure: 2-4 bar
   - Temperature: 800-900°C (preheating required)

3. Reduction Shaft/Reactor
   - Counter-current flow (ore downward, gas upward)
   - Residence time: 4-8 hours
   - Metallization: >92% target

4. Gas Recycling
   - Exhaust gas composition: ~30% H₂, ~60% H₂O, ~10% CO/CO₂
   - Water removal via cooling and condensation
   - H₂ enrichment and reinjection
   - Makeup H₂: 30-40% of total input

5. Product Handling
   - Hot DRI briquetting (for handling/transport)
   - Electric arc furnace (EAF) steelmaking

CO₂ Emissions Reduction:
- Conventional blast furnace: ~2.0 tons CO₂/ton steel
- H₂-DRI with green H₂: <0.1 tons CO₂/ton steel
- Reduction: >95%

3.6 Safety System Architecture

Multi-Layer Safety Design

Safety Integrity Levels (SIL) Approach

The WIA-ENE-007 standard mandates risk-based safety design following IEC 61508/61511:

  1. Basic Process Control System (BPCS): Normal operation and optimization
  2. Safety Instrumented System (SIS): Protection against identified hazards
    • SIL 1: Risk reduction 10-100×
    • SIL 2: Risk reduction 100-1000× (typical for H₂ systems)
    • SIL 3: Risk reduction 1000-10000× (critical applications)
  3. Physical Protection: Pressure relief, rupture disks, blast walls
  4. Emergency Response: Fire suppression, evacuation systems
