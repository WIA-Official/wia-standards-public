Hydrogen in Steel Production: The Green Steel Revolution

Steel production accounts for approximately 7-9% of global CO₂ emissions, making it one of the most carbon-intensive industrial processes. Traditional steelmaking relies on coal-based blast furnaces that use coke (processed coal) as both fuel and reducing agent to convert iron ore into metallic iron. This process is inherently carbon-intensive, emitting approximately 1.8-2.0 tonnes of CO₂ per tonne of crude steel produced.

Hydrogen-based direct reduced iron (H₂-DRI) technology offers a revolutionary alternative. Instead of carbon, hydrogen serves as the reducing agent, extracting oxygen from iron ore at temperatures of 800-1000°C. The chemical reaction is elegantly simple: Fe₂O₃ + 3H₂ → 2Fe + 3H₂O. The only byproduct is water vapor, eliminating essentially all carbon emissions from the ironmaking process.

Technology Pathways

Multiple approaches to hydrogen-based steelmaking are under development and demonstration:

  • HYBRIT Process (Sweden): Using 100% hydrogen in shaft furnaces to produce sponge iron, then electric arc furnace steelmaking. Pilot plant operational since 2020, with commercial production beginning 2026. Demonstrates 95% emission reduction compared to blast furnaces.
  • SALCOS (Germany): Thyssenkrupp's direct reduction process integrating hydrogen production, DRI, and electric arc furnaces. Flexible operation allowing hydrogen/natural gas blending during transition period.
  • H2 Green Steel: Purpose-built green steel facility in Sweden designed for 5 million tonnes annual capacity by 2030, using only renewable electricity and green hydrogen.
  • Hydrogen Plasma Smelting Reduction: Advanced process using hydrogen plasma at extremely high temperatures, potentially enabling even greater efficiency and flexibility with lower-grade ores.

The economics of green steel depend heavily on hydrogen costs and carbon pricing. With green hydrogen at $2/kg and carbon prices above $50/tonne CO₂, hydrogen-based steel becomes competitive. Several companies have already secured premium pricing commitments from customers seeking zero-carbon steel for automotive, construction, and consumer products.

Ammonia Production: Hydrogen's Largest Current Market

Ammonia (NH₃) production consumes approximately 40 million tonnes of hydrogen annually—nearly half of all hydrogen produced globally. The Haber-Bosch process, developed in the early 20th century, combines nitrogen from air with hydrogen at high pressure and temperature over iron catalysts. This process enabled modern agriculture by providing nitrogen fertilizer at scale, but currently relies almost entirely on hydrogen from natural gas steam reforming.

Green Ammonia

Replacing gray hydrogen with green hydrogen in ammonia production offers multiple benefits:

  • Decarbonized Fertilizer: Eliminates approximately 400 million tonnes of CO₂ emissions annually from ammonia production
  • Hydrogen Carrier: Ammonia serves as a hydrogen carrier, with higher volumetric energy density than compressed hydrogen gas
  • Direct Fuel: Ammonia can be used directly in internal combustion engines, turbines, and fuel cells
  • Chemical Feedstock: Beyond fertilizer, ammonia is used in plastics, explosives, and pharmaceutical production

Green ammonia production facilities are rapidly scaling globally. Projects in Australia, Chile, Norway, and the Middle East plan multi-million-tonne annual capacity, taking advantage of excellent renewable energy resources. The levelized cost of green ammonia has fallen below $400/tonne in optimal locations, approaching parity with conventional ammonia.

Power-to-Ammonia-to-Power

An emerging application is using ammonia for long-duration energy storage and transport. Excess renewable electricity produces hydrogen via electrolysis, hydrogen is converted to ammonia for storage and transport, then ammonia is either cracked back to hydrogen or used directly in fuel cells or turbines. While round-trip efficiency (35-50%) is lower than hydrogen, ammonia's easier handling and existing infrastructure offer practical advantages for intercontinental renewable energy trade.

Petroleum Refining: Transitioning Essential Hydrogen Use

Oil refineries are currently the second-largest consumers of hydrogen, using approximately 35 million tonnes annually for:

  • Hydrocracking: Breaking down heavy crude oil fractions into lighter, more valuable products (gasoline, diesel, jet fuel)
  • Hydrotreating: Removing sulfur, nitrogen, and other impurities from petroleum products to meet environmental regulations
  • Hydroisomerization: Rearranging molecular structures to improve fuel properties

Nearly all refinery hydrogen today comes from steam methane reforming of natural gas—gray hydrogen with significant carbon emissions. Refineries represent an immediate opportunity for deploying blue hydrogen (SMR with carbon capture) or green hydrogen, especially as carbon pricing increases.

The long-term future of refining is complex. As transportation electrifies, gasoline and diesel demand will decline. However, demand for jet fuel, petrochemicals, and lubricants will persist. Refineries may evolve into integrated energy complexes producing both traditional petroleum products and green hydrogen for external uses, leveraging existing infrastructure and expertise.

Chemical Industry: Methanol and Beyond

Methanol Production

Methanol (CH₃OH) is a crucial chemical building block, with annual production exceeding 100 million tonnes. Traditional methanol synthesis uses syngas (mixture of H₂ and CO) produced from natural gas or coal. Green methanol can be produced by combining green hydrogen with captured CO₂:

CO₂ + 3H₂ → CH₃OH + H₂O

This process achieves multiple objectives: produces methanol without fossil fuel consumption, utilizes captured CO₂, and creates a liquid fuel that can use existing infrastructure. Green methanol is particularly promising for maritime fuel, with several major shipping companies committing to methanol-powered vessels.

Other Chemical Applications

Hydrogen enables production of numerous essential chemicals:

  • Synthetic Fuels (E-fuels): Combining hydrogen with captured CO₂ to produce synthetic gasoline, diesel, or jet fuel with near-zero net carbon emissions
  • Polymers and Plastics: Hydrogen used in producing polyethylene, polypropylene, and other plastics
  • Pharmaceuticals: Hydrogen in catalytic hydrogenation reactions for drug synthesis
  • Food Industry: Hydrogenation of vegetable oils to produce margarine and shortenings
  • Electronics: Ultra-high-purity hydrogen for semiconductor manufacturing

Glass Manufacturing: High-Temperature Heat

Glass production requires extremely high temperatures (1400-1600°C) traditionally provided by natural gas combustion. Hydrogen combustion can reach these temperatures while producing only water vapor, enabling zero-emission glass manufacturing. Several glass manufacturers are conducting trials with hydrogen burners, demonstrating technical feasibility.

Challenges include:

  • Hydrogen flames have different radiative properties than natural gas, requiring burner redesign
  • Higher hydrogen cost compared to natural gas
  • Need for hydrogen supply infrastructure at manufacturing sites

Despite challenges, the combination of carbon pricing, customer demand for low-carbon products, and improving hydrogen economics is driving adoption. Several plants plan conversion to 100% hydrogen firing by 2030.

Cement Production: Tackling Process Emissions

Cement production is unique among industrial processes: approximately 60% of emissions come from the chemical reaction converting limestone (CaCO₃) to lime (CaO), releasing CO₂ regardless of fuel source. The remaining 40% of emissions from fuel combustion can be addressed with hydrogen, but process emissions require carbon capture or alternative chemistries.

Integrated approaches under development include:

  • Hydrogen firing for all thermal energy requirements
  • Carbon capture from process emissions
  • Alternative cement formulations reducing limestone requirements
  • Carbonation of concrete during curing, reabsorbing some released CO₂

Food Processing and Industrial Heat

Numerous industrial processes require heat at temperatures ranging from 150°C to over 1000°C. Currently supplied predominantly by natural gas, coal, or oil, this industrial heat represents an enormous decarbonization opportunity. Hydrogen can provide clean heat across the entire temperature range through direct combustion or high-temperature fuel cells.

Applications include:

  • Food Processing: Baking, drying, pasteurization, and sterilization
  • Textile Industry: Dyeing, drying, and finishing processes
  • Paper and Pulp: Drying and chemical processing
  • Ceramics: Firing kilns for brick, tile, and pottery
  • Metal Processing: Heat treatment, forging, and annealing

Many processes can use hydrogen with minimal equipment modification, particularly where natural gas is currently used. The key barrier is cost and supply availability rather than technical compatibility.

First-Mover Advantages

Several factors drive early adoption despite current cost premiums:

  • Brand Value: "Green steel," "green ammonia," and other low-carbon products command premium pricing from environmentally-conscious customers
  • Regulatory Compliance: Anticipating carbon pricing and emissions regulations
  • Long-Term Cost Hedging: Locking in green hydrogen supply agreements at projected future costs
  • Technology Development: Gaining experience with new processes before they become mandatory
  • Market Positioning: Establishing competitive advantage in emerging low-carbon economy

Implementation Strategies

Phased Approach

Most industrial facilities adopt hydrogen through staged implementation:

  1. Assessment Phase: Detailed analysis of hydrogen requirements, facility modifications needed, and economic modeling
  2. Pilot Testing: Small-scale trials validating technical feasibility and optimizing processes
  3. Hydrogen Blending: Gradual increase in hydrogen fraction mixed with existing fuel (10%, 30%, 50%)
  4. Dedicated Hydrogen Systems: New equipment designed for 100% hydrogen operation
  5. Integration: Connecting with hydrogen production, storage, and distribution infrastructure

Infrastructure Development

Industrial hydrogen adoption requires coordinated infrastructure:

  • On-Site Production: Electrolyzers at industrial facilities, particularly those with renewable energy access
  • Industrial Hydrogen Hubs: Clusters of hydrogen-consuming industries sharing production and distribution infrastructure
  • Pipeline Networks: Dedicated hydrogen pipelines connecting production with consumption
  • Import Terminals: For regions lacking domestic hydrogen production capacity

The Industrial Hydrogen Transition

The industrial sector represents hydrogen's most immediate and largest-scale application. With 75+ million tonnes of current hydrogen consumption in refining and ammonia production alone, replacing gray hydrogen with green hydrogen would eliminate over 750 million tonnes of annual CO₂ emissions—equivalent to the total emissions of Germany.

Beyond existing uses, new hydrogen applications in steel, chemicals, and industrial heat could expand hydrogen demand to 500+ million tonnes annually by 2050. This transformation represents one of the largest infrastructure and industrial projects in human history, requiring trillions in investment but offering a pathway to sustainable industry.

The WIA-ENE-007 standard provides essential frameworks for this transition, from production standards ensuring hydrogen quality to safety protocols protecting workers and facilities to integration guidelines enabling efficient adoption. By following these standards, industries can confidently transition to hydrogen while maintaining operational excellence and safety.
