Fundamentals of Fuel Cell Operation

A fuel cell is an electrochemical device that converts the chemical energy of a fuel directly into electrical energy without combustion. Unlike batteries, which store a fixed amount of energy, fuel cells produce electricity continuously as long as fuel and oxidant are supplied. This fundamental characteristic makes them ideal for applications requiring sustained power delivery.

The basic operating principle involves splitting hydrogen into protons and electrons at the anode, conducting protons through an electrolyte while electrons flow through an external circuit (generating electricity), then recombining protons, electrons, and oxygen at the cathode to form water. This process occurs at temperatures ranging from ambient (some PEM systems) to 1000°C (solid oxide fuel cells), depending on technology.

Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) Fuel Cells

Technology Overview

PEM fuel cells operate at relatively low temperatures (60-80°C) using a solid polymer electrolyte membrane. This membrane conducts protons while blocking electron flow and gas crossover. The combination of low operating temperature, high power density, and rapid startup makes PEM the dominant technology for transportation applications.

Modern PEM fuel cells achieve power densities exceeding 1 W/cm² and electrical efficiencies of 50-60% (LHV basis). System-level efficiencies including balance of plant are typically 40-50%, still significantly higher than internal combustion engines. When heat is recovered for useful purposes (combined heat and power), total efficiency can exceed 85%.

Components and Materials

  • Membrane: Perfluorosulfonic acid polymers (Nafion® and similar materials) providing excellent proton conductivity when hydrated. Thickness typically 10-50 micrometers. Newer membranes operate at higher temperatures (100-120°C) enabling better heat rejection.
  • Catalyst Layers: Platinum nanoparticles on carbon support at both anode and cathode. Total platinum loading has decreased from >4 mg/cm² in early systems to <0.3 mg/cm² in modern automotive fuel cells through improved dispersion and utilization.
  • Gas Diffusion Layers: Carbon paper or cloth enabling reactant gas delivery to catalyst while removing product water. Requires careful balance of hydrophobicity and permeability.
  • Bipolar Plates: Graphite or metal plates with flow channels distributing reactants and collecting current. Account for significant stack weight and cost; stamped metal plates increasingly replace machined graphite.

Applications

PEM fuel cells dominate multiple application sectors:

  • Automotive: Over 50,000 fuel cell vehicles on roads globally; major manufacturers offering commercial models
  • Buses and Trucks: Thousands of fuel cell buses operating in cities worldwide; heavy-duty trucks under development
  • Forklifts: Over 40,000 fuel cell forklifts in warehouse operations; proven economics versus batteries
  • Backup Power: Telecommunications and data center backup replacing diesel generators
  • Marine: Small vessels and auxiliary power for larger ships; emissions-free harbor operation

Solid Oxide Fuel Cells (SOFC)

High-Temperature Electrochemistry

SOFCs operate at 600-1000°C using a ceramic electrolyte that conducts oxide ions (O²⁻) from cathode to anode. This high temperature enables several advantages: use of non-precious metal catalysts, fuel flexibility (can reform natural gas internally), and high-quality waste heat suitable for cogeneration or bottoming cycles.

The oxygen ion conductivity of the ceramic electrolyte increases dramatically with temperature, but so do materials degradation rates. Modern SOFCs balance these factors, with 700-800°C systems offering good performance with improved durability. Electrical efficiency reaches 60% for large systems, with combined heat and power efficiencies exceeding 90%.

Materials and Challenges

SOFC materials must withstand extreme conditions:

  • Electrolyte: Yttria-stabilized zirconia (YSZ) is most common, though ceria and lanthanum gallate enable lower operating temperatures. Thickness 5-20 micrometers in modern designs.
  • Anode: Nickel-YSZ cermet (ceramic-metal composite) provides electronic and ionic conductivity plus catalytic activity. Susceptible to coking with hydrocarbon fuels.
  • Cathode: Lanthanum strontium manganite or cobaltite providing oxygen reduction catalysis and electronic conductivity.
  • Interconnects: Ferritic stainless steels for planar designs; ceramic for tubular configurations.

Key challenges include thermal cycling durability (materials expansion mismatch causes cracking), seal integrity at high temperatures, and long-term degradation (0.2-1% per thousand hours depending on operating conditions). Research focuses on reducing operating temperature to 600-700°C while maintaining performance.

Applications

  • Stationary Power: 1 kW to multi-MW combined heat and power systems for buildings and industrial facilities
  • Distributed Generation: Natural gas-fueled systems providing electricity and heat for commercial buildings
  • Heavy-Duty Transport: Under development for trucks and ships where high temperature and fuel flexibility are advantageous
  • Industrial Applications: Integration with industrial processes providing both power and high-temperature heat

Alkaline Fuel Cells (AFC)

Pioneer Technology

Alkaline fuel cells were the first practical fuel cells, powering the Apollo spacecraft. They use liquid potassium hydroxide as electrolyte and can achieve remarkable efficiencies (60-70%) with simple, low-cost catalyst materials. However, sensitivity to carbon dioxide has limited terrestrial applications.

CO₂ from air reacts with KOH electrolyte forming carbonate precipitates that degrade performance. This requires pure oxygen rather than air, or elaborate CO₂ scrubbing. Despite this limitation, renewed interest stems from use in niche applications and development of anion exchange membrane variants addressing the CO₂ issue.

Phosphoric Acid Fuel Cells (PAFC)

Operating at 150-200°C with phosphoric acid electrolyte, PAFC systems represent the first commercially deployed fuel cell technology for stationary power. Over 400 MW of PAFC capacity has been installed globally, primarily in hospitals, hotels, and industrial facilities requiring reliable combined heat and power.

While largely superseded by PEM and SOFC for new installations, PAFC demonstrated fuel cell commercial viability and established service networks, operating procedures, and customer acceptance. Electrical efficiency of 40% and total CHP efficiency of 85% remain competitive for appropriate applications.

Molten Carbonate Fuel Cells (MCFC)

MCFCs operate at 600-650°C using molten carbonate salt as electrolyte. Like SOFCs, the high temperature enables internal fuel reforming and produces high-quality waste heat. Unique among fuel cells, MCFCs can electrochemically concentrate CO₂ from low-concentration sources (flue gas), enabling carbon capture integration.

Several MW-scale MCFC installations operate globally, providing combined heat and power for industrial facilities. The technology particularly excels in applications with biogas or where carbon capture is valuable. However, the corrosive molten electrolyte creates materials challenges limiting lifetime.

Fuel Cell Systems Engineering

Balance of Plant

The fuel cell stack produces DC electricity, but practical systems require extensive supporting equipment:

  • Fuel Processing: For systems using fuels other than pure hydrogen, reformers convert methanol, natural gas, or other fuels to hydrogen-rich gas. Includes desulfurization, steam reforming or partial oxidation, and CO cleanup (for PEM systems).
  • Air Management: Compressor or blower providing oxygen for cathode. Pressure and flow rate significantly impact performance and efficiency. Humidity control critical for PEM systems.
  • Thermal Management: Cooling system maintaining optimal operating temperature. May include radiator, heat exchanger for CHP applications, or integration with building HVAC.
  • Water Management: PEM systems must maintain membrane hydration while avoiding flooding. Includes water recovery from cathode exhaust for anode humidification.
  • Power Conditioning: DC-DC converters and DC-AC inverters converting fuel cell output to usable electricity. Maximum power point tracking optimizes operation.
  • Controls: Monitoring sensors, control algorithms, and safety systems ensuring reliable, efficient, safe operation across varying loads and conditions.

Degradation and Lifetime

All fuel cells experience gradual performance degradation through various mechanisms:

  • Catalyst Degradation: Platinum dissolution and agglomeration reduces active surface area. Poisoning by trace contaminants (sulfur, CO) can be reversible or permanent.
  • Membrane Degradation: Chemical attack by peroxide radicals, mechanical stress from humidity cycling, thinning from dissolution.
  • Electrode Flooding or Drying: Changes in hydrophobicity alter water management, degrading performance.
  • Corrosion: Carbon support corrosion, particularly during startup/shutdown cycling or fuel starvation events.

Automotive fuel cells target 5,000-8,000 hour lifetimes (equivalent to 150,000-200,000+ miles), with degradation rates below 2% per 1,000 hours. Stationary systems target 40,000-80,000 hours with degradation under 0.5% per 1,000 hours. Proper operating conditions, contaminant control, and improved materials continue extending lifetimes.

Emerging Fuel Cell Technologies

Anion Exchange Membrane (AEM) Fuel Cells

AEM fuel cells use solid polymer membranes conducting hydroxide ions (OH⁻) instead of protons. This alkaline environment enables use of non-precious metal catalysts (silver, nickel, manganese oxides) while offering PEM-like compact design and low-temperature operation. If technical challenges (membrane stability, carbonate formation) are overcome, AEM could provide PEM performance at significantly reduced cost.

Direct Methanol Fuel Cells (DMFC)

DMFCs feed liquid methanol directly to the anode where it is electrochemically oxidized. This eliminates fuel reforming and enables simple liquid fuel storage. However, methanol crossover through the membrane reduces efficiency, and power density remains lower than hydrogen PEM systems. Applications include portable power and small-scale stationary systems where methanol's higher energy density justifies lower efficiency.

Reversible Fuel Cells

Some fuel cell types can operate in reverse as electrolyzers, enabling bidirectional energy storage. SOFCs demonstrate excellent reversibility, with systems capable of electrolyzing steam to hydrogen when excess electricity is available, then generating power from stored hydrogen when needed. This "round-trip" efficiency can exceed 70%, competitive with battery storage for long-duration applications.

Future Outlook

Fuel cell deployment is accelerating across all major sectors. Automotive applications lead in volume production, with major manufacturers committed to fuel cell vehicle expansion. Heavy-duty transport increasingly recognizes fuel cells as the optimal zero-emission solution for long-haul trucks, buses, and rail. Maritime and aviation explore fuel cells for decarbonization, with demonstration projects underway.

Stationary applications grow as distributed generation and microgrid markets expand. Fuel cells provide grid services, peak shaving, and resilient backup power while supporting renewable integration through load balancing. Combined heat and power applications achieve remarkable total efficiency, displacing fossil fuel combustion.

The WIA-ENE-007 standard provides comprehensive frameworks for fuel cell system design, integration, and operation. From test protocols to safety requirements to performance metrics, these standards accelerate fuel cell adoption while ensuring reliability and interoperability. As fuel cells transition from emerging technology to mainstream power generation, standardized approaches enable rapid scaling and cost reduction.
