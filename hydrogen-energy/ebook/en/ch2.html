2.1 Fundamental Terminology

Understanding the precise terminology used in hydrogen energy systems is essential for effective communication, safety, and standardization. This chapter provides comprehensive definitions and explanations of key concepts that underpin the WIA-ENE-007 standard.

Hydrogen Purity Classifications

Grade Purity (%) Applications Key Impurities
Commercial 98.0-99.0 Industrial processes, hydrogenation N₂, O₂, CO, CO₂
High Purity 99.95-99.995 Electronics, fuel cells (automotive) H₂O, O₂ < 5 ppm each
Ultra High Purity 99.999+ Semiconductor manufacturing Total impurities < 10 ppm
Research Grade 99.9999 Laboratory research, calibration Total impurities < 1 ppm

Energy Content and Conversion Factors

Hydrogen's energy content can be expressed in multiple ways:

Lower Heating Value (LHV): 120 MJ/kg = 33.3 kWh/kg
Higher Heating Value (HHV): 142 MJ/kg = 39.4 kWh/kg

Note: LHV excludes heat of vaporization of water vapor
      HHV includes total heat released including condensation

Volume-based (at NTP):
- Energy density (LHV): 10.8 MJ/m³ = 3.0 kWh/m³
- Comparison: Natural gas ~38 MJ/m³

Mass-based energy density:
- Hydrogen: 120 MJ/kg
- Gasoline: 44 MJ/kg
- Diesel: 43 MJ/kg
- Natural gas: 50 MJ/kg

2.2 Electrolysis Technologies

Alkaline Electrolysis (AEL)

The most mature and widely deployed electrolysis technology, operating for over a century in industrial applications.

Operating Principles

  • Electrolyte: Potassium hydroxide (KOH) or sodium hydroxide (NaOH), typically 25-30% concentration
  • Operating Temperature: 60-80°C
  • Pressure: 1-30 bar
  • Efficiency: 63-70% (HHV basis)
  • Current Density: 0.2-0.4 A/cm²

Electrode Reactions

Cathode (reduction): 4H₂O + 4e⁻ → 2H₂ + 4OH⁻
Anode (oxidation):   4OH⁻ → O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻
Overall:             2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂

Advantages and Limitations

Advantages Limitations
Proven technology with long lifetime (60,000-90,000 hours) Lower current density limits power density
Non-precious metal catalysts (Ni-based) Slower response to load changes
Lower capital cost per kW Corrosive electrolyte requires careful handling
Scalable to multi-MW systems Lower efficiency than PEM

Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) Electrolysis

Advanced technology offering superior performance characteristics, particularly suited for renewable energy integration.

Operating Principles

  • Electrolyte: Solid polymer membrane (Nafion or similar)
  • Operating Temperature: 50-80°C
  • Pressure: 30-80 bar (high-pressure variant)
  • Efficiency: 67-82% (HHV basis)
  • Current Density: 1.0-2.0 A/cm²

Electrode Reactions

Anode (oxidation):   2H₂O → O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻
Cathode (reduction): 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ → 2H₂
Overall:             2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂

Key Characteristics

  • Fast Response: Can ramp from 0-100% in seconds, ideal for renewable integration
  • Compact Design: High power density enables smaller footprint
  • High Purity: Produces 99.999% pure hydrogen directly
  • Differential Pressure: Can produce hydrogen at 30+ bar without mechanical compression

💡 Technical Insight

PEM electrolyzers excel in applications with variable renewable energy input. Their rapid response time (milliseconds to seconds) allows seamless integration with solar and wind power, unlike alkaline systems which require 10-15 minutes for safe ramping.

Solid Oxide Electrolysis Cells (SOEC)

High-temperature electrolysis offering the highest theoretical efficiency by utilizing waste heat or renewable thermal energy.

Operating Principles

  • Electrolyte: Yttria-stabilized zirconia (YSZ) ceramic
  • Operating Temperature: 700-900°C
  • Pressure: Near-atmospheric to 30 bar
  • Efficiency: 80-90% (HHV basis, with heat integration)
  • Current Density: 0.3-1.0 A/cm²

Advantages for Industrial Integration

  • Can utilize waste heat from industrial processes
  • Reversible operation (can function as fuel cell or electrolyzer)
  • Highest electrical efficiency potential
  • Can process steam directly

Current Status

SOEC technology is still emerging, with demonstration projects worldwide. Key challenges include materials durability, thermal cycling resistance, and cost reduction through manufacturing scale-up. Expected commercial readiness: 2027-2030.

2.3 Hydrogen Storage Concepts

Compressed Gas Storage

The most common and commercially mature storage method:

Pressure Levels and Applications

Pressure (bar) Storage Density (kg H₂/m³) Applications Tank Type
200-250 15-19 Industrial, stationary storage Type I (steel)
350 23-25 Buses, light commercial vehicles Type III/IV (composite)
700 39-42 Passenger cars, forklifts Type IV (composite)
900-1000 45-50 Future long-range vehicles Advanced Type IV

Tank Construction Types

Type I:   All-metal (typically steel)
          - Lowest cost
          - Heaviest weight
          - Limited to ≤200 bar

Type II:  Metal liner with hoop-wrapped composite
          - Intermediate weight and cost
          - Up to 300 bar

Type III: Metal liner (aluminum) fully wrapped with composite
          - Good strength-to-weight ratio
          - Up to 700 bar
          - Moderate cost

Type IV:  Polymer liner fully wrapped with carbon fiber composite
          - Lightest weight
          - Up to 700+ bar
          - Highest cost
          - Best for mobile applications

Type V:   Linerless all-composite (emerging)
          - Ultimate weight reduction
          - Highest cost
          - Under development for 1000+ bar

Liquid Hydrogen (LH₂) Storage

Cryogenic storage offering high volumetric density for large-scale applications:

Key Parameters

  • Temperature: -253°C (-423°F) at atmospheric pressure
  • Density: 71 kg/m³ (almost 800x denser than gaseous H₂ at STP)
  • Energy for Liquefaction: 10-13 kWh/kg H₂ (30-40% of LHV)
  • Boil-off Rate: 0.2-1.0% per day depending on tank size and insulation

Applications

  • Aerospace (rocket fuel)
  • Long-distance maritime transport
  • Large-scale storage terminals
  • Future aviation fuel

Material-Based Storage

Metal Hydrides

Certain metals and alloys can absorb hydrogen, forming metal hydrides through reversible exothermic reactions: 

Absorption:  M + x/2 H₂ → MHₓ + Heat
Desorption:  MHₓ + Heat → M + x/2 H₂

Common materials:
- LaNi₅: 1.4 wt% H₂, operates near room temperature
- TiFe: 1.9 wt% H₂, low cost, requires activation
- Mg₂Ni: 3.6 wt% H₂, requires 250-300°C
- NaAlH₄: 5.6 wt% H₂, catalyzed reversibility

Storage density: 2-7 wt% H₂ (material basis)
Volumetric density: 100-150 kg H₂/m³ (higher than liquid!)

Advantages and Challenges

Advantages Challenges
Low pressure operation (<10 bar) Heavy weight of storage medium
Inherently safe (no high pressure) Slow kinetics for some materials
High volumetric density Heat management during charge/discharge
Can provide hydrogen purity Cost of materials

2.4 Fuel Cell Fundamentals

Proton Exchange Membrane Fuel Cells (PEMFC)

The dominant fuel cell technology for transportation and portable applications:

Operating Principles

Anode:    H₂ → 2H⁺ + 2e⁻
Cathode:  ½O₂ + 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂O
Overall:  H₂ + ½O₂ → H₂O + Electricity + Heat

Operating temperature: 60-80°C
Operating pressure: 1-3 bar
Voltage per cell: ~0.6-0.7V (under load)
Electrical efficiency: 50-60% (HHV)
Combined heat and power: up to 90%

Stack Configuration

Individual cells produce ~0.6-0.7V, so hundreds of cells are stacked in series to achieve desired voltage: 

Example automotive fuel cell stack:
- 370 cells in series
- Voltage: ~260V (0.7V × 370)
- Power output: 100-150 kW
- Weight: 80-120 kg
- Volume: 30-50 liters
- Power density: 2-3 kW/kg, 3-5 kW/L

Solid Oxide Fuel Cells (SOFC)

High-temperature fuel cells ideal for stationary power generation:

Characteristics

  • Operating Temperature: 600-1000°C
  • Electrical Efficiency: 50-65%
  • CHP Efficiency: 80-90%
  • Fuel Flexibility: Can reform natural gas, biogas internally
  • Applications: Residential/commercial CHP, grid support, APU

Advantages

  • High-grade waste heat suitable for additional power generation (bottoming cycle)
  • Excellent fuel flexibility including direct use of hydrocarbons
  • No precious metal catalysts required
  • Can operate in reverse as SOEC for hydrogen production

2.5 Distribution Infrastructure Concepts

Pipeline Transmission

Dedicated hydrogen pipelines offer the most cost-effective transport for large volumes over long distances:

Pipeline Materials and Specifications

Parameter Natural Gas Hydrogen (Pure) H₂ Blend (20%)
Operating Pressure 50-100 bar 10-100 bar 50-100 bar
Flow Velocity 15-25 m/s 15-40 m/s 15-25 m/s
Materials Carbon steel Special alloys, coated steel Existing with assessment
Energy Density 39 MJ/m³ 11 MJ/m³ 34 MJ/m³

Tube Trailer Transport

Compressed gas tube trailers are the primary method for medium-volume hydrogen distribution: 

Typical tube trailer specifications:
- Pressure: 200-500 bar
- Total volume: 3,000-6,000 liters (water capacity)
- H₂ capacity: 300-1,200 kg
- Trailer weight (empty): 20,000-30,000 kg
- Delivery radius: 200-500 km economically

Cost structure:
- Capital: $500,000-800,000 per trailer
- Operating: $2-5 per kg H₂ per 100 km
- Loading/unloading: 2-4 hours per trailer

2.6 Safety Classification and Hazard Assessment

Hazard Classification

Hydrogen is classified under multiple safety frameworks:

System Classification Key Criteria
GHS (Globally Harmonized System) Flammable Gas Category 1 Extremely flammable gas
NFPA 704 Health: 0, Fire: 4, Reactivity: 0 Severe fire hazard, minimal health risk
DOT (US Department of Transportation) Class 2.1 - Flammable Gas UN1049 (compressed), UN1966 (liquid)
ATEX (European) Zone 0, 1, 2 (gas) Explosive atmosphere classification

Risk Assessment Framework

Five-Layer Safety Approach

  1. Prevention: Design to prevent leaks and ignition sources
  2. Detection: Multiple sensor types and monitoring systems
  3. Mitigation: Ventilation, spacing, protective barriers
  4. Control: Automatic shutdown systems and isolation valves
  5. Emergency Response: Fire suppression, evacuation procedures, training

2.7 Quality Standards and Specifications

ISO 14687: Hydrogen Fuel Quality

International standard specifying hydrogen quality for PEM fuel cell vehicles:

Component/Characteristic Maximum Limit Reason for Limit
Total non-H₂ gases 2% (mol/mol) Fuel cell performance
Water (H₂O) 5 μmol/mol Freezing in fuel cell
Total hydrocarbons (as C₁) 2 μmol/mol Catalyst poisoning
Oxygen (O₂) 5 μmol/mol Catalyst degradation
Carbon monoxide (CO) 0.2 μmol/mol Severe catalyst poisoning
Carbon dioxide (CO₂) 2 μmol/mol Catalyst performance
Total sulfur compounds (as S) 0.004 μmol/mol Permanent catalyst damage
Ammonia (NH₃) 0.1 μmol/mol Membrane contamination

Testing and Verification Methods

Multiple analytical techniques ensure hydrogen purity compliance:

  • Gas Chromatography (GC): Quantifies non-hydrogen gases, hydrocarbons
  • Mass Spectrometry (MS): Detects trace impurities with high sensitivity
  • Electrochemical Sensors: Real-time monitoring of O₂, CO
  • FTIR Spectroscopy: Identifies specific molecular species
  • Dew Point Analysis: Measures water content

2.8 Sustainability Metrics and Certification

Carbon Intensity of Hydrogen

The WIA-ENE-007 standard classifies hydrogen based on lifecycle carbon intensity:

Category CO₂ Intensity (kg CO₂/kg H₂) Certification Level
Green (Tier 1) < 0.5 WIA Gold Certified
Green (Tier 2) 0.5 - 1.0 WIA Green Certified
Low-Carbon 1.0 - 3.0 WIA Low-Carbon
Transitional 3.0 - 6.0 Conditional approval
Conventional > 6.0 Not certified

Renewable Energy Credits and Guarantees of Origin

Green hydrogen certification requires demonstrable connection to renewable energy:

  • Direct Connection: Electrolyzer powered by dedicated renewable plant
  • Grid-Connected with RECs: Renewable energy certificates matching consumption
  • Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs): Long-term contracts for renewable power
  • Temporal Matching: Hourly or sub-hourly matching of production and consumption

2.9 Economic Metrics and Performance Indicators

Levelized Cost of Hydrogen (LCOH)

Comprehensive cost metric analogous to LCOE (Levelized Cost of Energy): 

LCOH = (CAPEX + Σ OPEX) / Total H₂ Production

Components:
- CAPEX: Capital expenditure (electrolyzer, balance of plant, installation)
- OPEX: Operating expenditure (electricity, water, maintenance, labor)
- Discount rate: Weighted average cost of capital (WACC)
- Project lifetime: Typically 20-30 years
- Capacity factor: % of time system operates at full capacity

Example calculation (simplified):
- Electrolyzer: 1 MW, $1000/kW = $1,000,000
- Electricity: $30/MWh, 50 kWh/kg H₂, 60% capacity factor
- Water, O&M: $0.50/kg H₂
- Production: 9,500 kg/year
- Project life: 20 years, 5% discount rate

LCOH = ~$4.50/kg H₂

System Efficiency Metrics

Metric Definition Target Value
Faradaic Efficiency Actual H₂ produced / Theoretical H₂ from charge > 99%
System Efficiency (HHV) H₂ energy output / Electrical energy input 65-75%
Capacity Factor Actual production / Maximum possible production 40-90%
Availability Operational time / Total time > 95%
