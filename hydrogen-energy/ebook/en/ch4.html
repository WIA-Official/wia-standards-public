4.1 Project Planning and Feasibility Assessment

Successful hydrogen energy projects begin with thorough planning and feasibility analysis. This section provides a structured approach to project development following the WIA-ENE-007 standard, ensuring technical viability, economic feasibility, and regulatory compliance.

Feasibility Study Framework

Assessment Area Key Questions Required Data Deliverable
Market Analysis Who will buy the hydrogen? What price? Demand forecasts, competitive analysis, pricing trends Market sizing report
Technical Feasibility Can we produce the required volume/purity? Production technology, site conditions, utilities Technical design basis
Economic Viability What are the costs and returns? CAPEX, OPEX, revenue projections, financing Financial model (IRR, NPV, payback)
Regulatory Compliance What permits/approvals are needed? Zoning, environmental, safety regulations Permitting strategy and timeline
Risk Assessment What could go wrong? Technical, market, regulatory, safety risks Risk register with mitigation plans

Site Selection Criteria

Optimal site selection is critical for project success. Key considerations include:

Resource Availability

  • Renewable Energy Access: Co-location with wind/solar or grid connection with high renewable penetration
  • Water Supply: 9-10 liters per kg H₂ (electrolyzer feed + cooling), quality and reliability assessment
  • Land Area: Minimum 0.5-2 hectares per MW electrolyzer capacity (including setbacks, storage, utilities)
  • Grid Connection: Electrical capacity, voltage level, grid stability, and connection costs

Logistics and Infrastructure

  • Proximity to Demand: Minimize distribution costs (pipeline, trucking distance)
  • Transportation Access: Road access for equipment delivery (heavy/oversized loads), trailer operations
  • Existing Infrastructure: Potential to repurpose existing facilities, utility connections
  • Future Expansion: Land availability for capacity growth

Regulatory and Social Factors

  • Zoning and Land Use: Industrial zoning, setback requirements, land use restrictions
  • Environmental Permitting: Air quality permits (even for zero-emission facilities), water discharge permits, NEPA/environmental reviews
  • Community Acceptance: Public perception, stakeholder engagement, local benefits
  • Safety and Emergency Services: Fire department capabilities, emergency response plans, mutual aid agreements

Hazard and Operability Study (HAZOP)

HAZOP is a systematic examination of process design to identify potential hazards and operability problems. For hydrogen systems, this is a critical safety requirement:

HAZOP Methodology for Hydrogen Systems

HAZOP Process Overview:

1. System Breakdown
   - Divide system into nodes (e.g., electrolyzer, compression, storage)
   - Define design intent for each node
   
2. Guide Word Application
   Common guide words: NO, MORE, LESS, AS WELL AS, PART OF, REVERSE, OTHER THAN
   
Example Node: Hydrogen Compression

Parameter: Flow Rate
Design Intent: 100 kg/h at 30 bar inlet → 700 bar outlet

Guide Word Analysis:
┌─────────────┬──────────────────┬────────────────┬───────────────────┬──────────────────┐
│ Guide Word  │ Deviation        │ Causes         │ Consequences      │ Safeguards       │
├─────────────┼──────────────────┼────────────────┼───────────────────┼──────────────────┤
│ NO          │ No flow          │ Inlet valve    │ Compressor dry    │ Flow switch,     │
│             │                  │ closed, supply │ running, damage   │ interlock        │
│             │                  │ disruption     │                   │                  │
├─────────────┼──────────────────┼────────────────┼───────────────────┼──────────────────┤
│ MORE        │ Higher flow      │ Demand spike,  │ Compressor        │ Flow control,    │
│             │ >120 kg/h        │ control fault  │ overload          │ capacity limit   │
├─────────────┼──────────────────┼────────────────┼───────────────────┼──────────────────┤
│ LESS        │ Lower flow       │ Upstream       │ Inefficient       │ Performance      │
│             │ <80 kg/h         │ issue, leak    │ operation         │ monitoring       │
├─────────────┼──────────────────┼────────────────┼───────────────────┼──────────────────┤
│ REVERSE     │ Backflow         │ Pressure       │ Compressor        │ Check valves,    │
│             │                  │ reversal       │ damage            │ isolation valves │
├─────────────┼──────────────────┼────────────────┼───────────────────┼──────────────────┤
│ AS WELL AS  │ Flow + impurity  │ Contamination  │ Purity failure,   │ Online analysis, │
│             │ (O₂, moisture)   │ from upstream  │ customer impact   │ purification     │
└─────────────┴──────────────────┴────────────────┴───────────────────┴──────────────────┘

3. Risk Ranking
   - Assign severity (1-5) and likelihood (1-5)
   - Risk matrix determines priority: High (>15), Medium (8-15), Low (<8)

4. Recommendations
   - Design modifications
   - Additional safeguards
   - Procedure enhancements
   - Operator training

5. Follow-up
   - Track implementation of recommendations
   - Re-assess residual risk
   - Update process safety information

4.3 Procurement and Equipment Selection

Electrolyzer Selection Process

Choosing the right electrolyzer technology is one of the most critical decisions in project implementation. The selection should balance performance, cost, and compatibility with the specific application:

Technology Evaluation Matrix

Criteria Weight Alkaline PEM SOEC
CAPEX ($/kW) 25% 800-1000 (9/10) 1200-1800 (6/10) 2500-4000 (3/10)
Efficiency (% HHV) 20% 63-70 (6/10) 67-82 (8/10) 80-90 (10/10)
Load Flexibility 15% 20-100%, slow ramp (5/10) 0-100%, fast ramp (10/10) Poor thermal cycling (3/10)
Maturity 15% 100+ years, proven (10/10) Commercialized (8/10) Demonstration phase (4/10)
Footprint 10% Large (6/10) Compact (9/10) Moderate (7/10)
Lifetime 10% 60-90k hours (9/10) 30-80k hours (7/10) 20-40k hours (5/10)
Purity 5% 99.8-99.9% (7/10) 99.999% (10/10) 99.9% (8/10)

Application-Specific Recommendations

  • Grid Balancing / Renewable Integration: PEM (fast response, part-load efficiency)
  • Steady Industrial Production: Alkaline (lowest CAPEX, proven reliability)
  • High-Temperature Process Integration: SOEC (highest efficiency with waste heat)
  • Fuel Cell Vehicle Fuel: PEM (ultra-high purity output)
  • Remote/Islanded Systems: Alkaline (simplicity, lower maintenance requirements)

Vendor Qualification and RFP Process

Request for Proposal (RFP) Structure

Electrolyzer RFP Key Sections:

1. Project Overview
   - Hydrogen production target: [X] kg/day or [Y] MW electrolyzer capacity
   - Purity requirement: ISO 14687 or specify
   - Operating profile: baseload, variable, load-following
   - Project location and site conditions
   - Commissioning target date

2. Technical Requirements
   [ ] Rated capacity: ___ Nm³/h or ___ kg/h
   [ ] Efficiency: Minimum ___ % (HHV basis) at rated load
   [ ] Part-load efficiency: ___ % at 50% load, ___ % at 25% load
   [ ] Minimum turndown ratio: ___:1 (e.g., 10:1 = 10% minimum load)
   [ ] Ramp rate: ___ %/second or %/minute
   [ ] Operating pressure: ___ bar (if differential pressure compression)
   [ ] Ambient operating range: ___ to ___ °C
   [ ] Stack lifetime: Minimum ___ hours to 10% degradation
   [ ] Footprint: Maximum ___ m² for electrolyzer skid

3. Balance of Plant Requirements
   [ ] Water treatment system (specify feed water quality available)
   [ ] Gas drying and compression (to ___ bar)
   [ ] Oxygen handling (vent, compress, or liquefy)
   [ ] Cooling system (air-cooled, water-cooled, or integrated)
   [ ] Power supply / rectifier
   [ ] Control system and SCADA integration
   [ ] Safety systems (H₂ detection, fire suppression, etc.)

4. Commercial Terms
   [ ] CAPEX: Itemized cost breakdown
   [ ] OPEX: Maintenance costs, consumables, major overhauls
   [ ] Payment terms and milestones
   [ ] Warranty: ___ years full warranty, extended warranty options
   [ ] Performance guarantees (capacity, efficiency, availability)
   [ ] Liquidated damages for underperformance
   [ ] Spare parts availability and pricing
   [ ] Service and support (response time, technician availability)

5. Vendor Qualifications
   [ ] Company background and experience in hydrogen
   [ ] References: Minimum 3 installations of similar size
   [ ] Financial stability (credit rating, balance sheet)
   [ ] Manufacturing capacity and lead times
   [ ] Safety record (TRIR, incidents)
   [ ] Quality certifications (ISO 9001, ASME, PED, etc.)

6. Deliverables and Schedule
   [ ] Proposal due date
   [ ] Design drawings and calculations
   [ ] FAT (Factory Acceptance Test) requirements
   [ ] Delivery timeline
   [ ] Installation and commissioning support
   [ ] Training (operations, maintenance)
   [ ] Documentation (O&M manuals, as-builts, safety data)

7. Evaluation Criteria (weightings)
   [ ] Technical compliance: 30%
   [ ] Price (CAPEX + 10-year OPEX NPV): 35%
   [ ] Vendor experience and references: 15%
   [ ] Delivery schedule: 10%
   [ ] Warranty and support: 10%

4.5 Commissioning and Startup

Commissioning Phases

⚠️ Safety First in Commissioning

Hydrogen commissioning must follow a structured approach with multiple hold points for safety verification. Never rush commissioning - taking shortcuts can lead to equipment damage, safety incidents, or compromised long-term performance.

Phase 1: Nitrogen Commissioning

All initial system testing uses inert nitrogen gas to eliminate ignition risk:

Nitrogen Commissioning Checklist:

□ Purge all process lines with nitrogen (O₂ <1% by portable analyzer)
□ Pressure test storage vessels and piping (hydrostatic or pneumatic)
□ Verify pressure relief devices (set pressure, capacity)
□ Test compressor operation (flow, pressure rise, vibration)
□ Calibrate pressure and temperature instrumentation
□ Verify control valve operation (stroking, response time)
□ Test safety instrumented systems (SIS):
  □ High pressure shutdown
  □ Low flow shutdown  
  □ Emergency stop (E-stop) circuits
  □ Gas detection interlocks
□ SCADA integration and alarming verification
□ Nitrogen flow testing to verify design flow rates and pressures

Phase 2: First Hydrogen Introduction

Safe Hydrogen Introduction Procedure:

1. Atmospheric Purge
   - Slowly displace nitrogen with hydrogen
   - Monitor oxygen content: maintain <1% at all times
   - Vent to safe location (above buildings, away from ignition sources)
   - Minimum purge: 5× system volume

2. Leak Detection Survey
   - Portable hydrogen detector survey of all flanges, valves, connections
   - Soap solution testing of suspected leak points
   - Fixed gas detector functionality verification
   - Accept criteria: <500 ppm H₂ at any point

3. Low-Pressure Operation
   - Initial operation at 25% of design pressure
   - 24-hour hold for observation
   - Monitor for:
     * Pressure decay (<1% in 24 hours acceptable)
     * Temperature anomalies
     * Unusual sounds or vibrations
     * Gas detector alarms

4. Pressure Ramp-Up
   - Increase to 50% design pressure (24-hour hold)
   - Increase to 75% design pressure (24-hour hold)
   - Increase to 100% design pressure
   - Leak survey at each pressure step

5. Authorization to Proceed
   - Commissioning manager approval
   - Safety review completion
   - Client acceptance of system readiness

Phase 3: Electrolyzer First Start

Electrolyzer Initial Operation:

Pre-start Checks:
□ DI water system operational (resistivity >10 MΩ·cm)
□ Cooling system operational (flow, temperature control)
□ Gas drying system ready (molecular sieve regenerated)
□ All safety interlocks verified functional
□ Stack resistance measurement (compare to baseline)
□ Electrolyte condition verified (alkaline only)

First Energization:
1. Apply 10% of rated current for 1 hour
   - Monitor cell voltages (uniformity ±50 mV)
   - Verify hydrogen production (compare to theoretical)
   - Check for leaks (internal and external)
   - Temperature rise within specification

2. Ramp to 25% current (2-hour hold)
   - Efficiency verification
   - Purity sampling and analysis
   - Thermal balance check

3. Ramp to 50% current (4-hour hold)
   - Performance testing
   - Control system tuning
   - Load response characterization

4. Ramp to 75% current (8-hour hold)
   - Extended operation observation
   - Fine-tune control parameters

5. Rated capacity test (24-hour continuous)
   - Performance guarantee verification
   - Efficiency measurement (kWh/kg H₂)
   - Purity confirmation (ISO 14687)
   - Availability demonstration

Acceptance Criteria:
□ Hydrogen production rate: ≥98% of nameplate
□ Efficiency: ≥95% of guaranteed efficiency
□ Purity: Meets ISO 14687 all parameters
□ Availability: >95% during test period
□ Safety systems: 100% functional
□ No unresolved punchlist items (Category A)
