1.1 What is Haptic Feedback?

Haptic technology creates tactile experiences through forces, vibrations, or motions applied to the user. The word "haptic" comes from the Greek word "haptikos," meaning "able to touch." In assistive technology, haptic feedback provides an essential channel of communication that operates independently of vision and hearing.

1.1.1 The Science of Touch

Human skin contains specialized mechanoreceptors that detect different types of tactile stimuli:

Receptor Type Sensitivity Detection Location
Meissner Corpuscles 10-50 Hz Light touch, flutter Fingertips, palms
Pacinian Corpuscles 40-400 Hz Vibration, pressure Deep tissue
Merkel Discs 0.4-3 Hz Sustained pressure Superficial skin
Ruffini Endings 0.4-3 Hz Stretch, continuous pressure Deep skin

1.1.2 Haptic Perception Range

The human tactile system is most sensitive to vibrations between 100-300 Hz, with peak sensitivity around 200-250 Hz. This sensitivity range forms the foundation of the WIA Haptic Standard's frequency bands.

1.2 Haptic Technology in Assistive Devices

1.2.1 Current Applications

  • Screen Readers: Haptic cues for navigation and confirmation
  • Braille Displays: Refreshable tactile output for text
  • White Canes: Electronic canes with vibration feedback for obstacles
  • Wearables: Smartwatches providing tactile notifications
  • Navigation Systems: Wayfinding through directional vibrations

1.2.2 The Potential of Standardization

Currently, each device and platform implements haptic feedback differently. A vibration pattern on one device has no guaranteed meaning on another. This fragmentation creates significant barriers:

  • Users must relearn patterns for each device
  • Developers create incompatible implementations
  • No shared vocabulary for tactile communication
  • Reduced effectiveness of haptic cues

1.3 The WIA Haptic Standard Vision

"Creating a universal language of touch for assistive technology."

1.3.1 Mission Statement

The WIA Haptic Standard aims to establish an open, interoperable framework for haptic feedback that enables:

  1. Consistency: Same patterns produce same perceptions across devices
  2. Learnability: Users learn patterns once, use everywhere
  3. Accessibility: Optimized for users with visual impairments
  4. Interoperability: Works across platforms and devices
  5. Extensibility: Supports custom patterns while maintaining core vocabulary

1.3.2 Core Principles

Accessibility First: All patterns designed to be distinguishable without visual reference, optimized for human tactile perception.

Hardware Agnostic: Primitives abstract over device capabilities with graceful degradation for limited hardware.

Energy Efficient: Optimized for mobile and wearable battery constraints with short, effective patterns.
