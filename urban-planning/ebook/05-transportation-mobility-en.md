# Chapter 4: Transportation Systems and Mobility

## The Transportation Challenge

Transportation is fundamental to cities—enabling access to jobs, education, services, and social connections. Yet it's also the source of major urban problems:

### Transportation Impacts

**Environmental**
- **33% of urban greenhouse gas emissions** (50%+ in some U.S. cities)
- **Major air pollution source**: NOx, particulate matter affecting public health
- **Noise pollution**: Traffic noise impacting quality of life
- **Habitat fragmentation**: Roads dividing ecosystems
- **Stormwater runoff**: Paved surfaces overwhelming drainage systems

**Social**
- **Traffic fatalities**: 1.3 million deaths globally per year
- **Congestion**: Average U.S. commuter loses 54 hours/year in traffic
- **Inequity**: Car dependency disadvantages poor, elderly, disabled
- **Physical inactivity**: Driving-oriented lifestyles contributing to obesity, chronic disease
- **Social isolation**: Sprawling, car-dependent communities

**Economic**
- **Infrastructure costs**: Roads, parking consuming 30-60% of urban land
- **Household costs**: Transportation is 2nd largest expense (15-20% of income)
- **Congestion costs**: $166 billion annually in U.S. alone
- **Health costs**: Air pollution, inactivity, crashes
- **Lost productivity**: Time wasted in traffic

**The Vision**: Sustainable mobility that is accessible, affordable, safe, efficient, and environmentally friendly.

## The Mobility Hierarchy

Sustainable transportation prioritizes modes in order of sustainability:

```
PRIORITY
  ↑
  │  1. WALKING
  │     - Zero emissions, health benefits, most accessible
  │
  │  2. CYCLING
  │     - Low emissions, health benefits, efficient
  │
  │  3. PUBLIC TRANSIT
  │     - Efficient use of space and energy per passenger
  │
  │  4. SHARED MOBILITY
  │     - Car-sharing, ride-sharing reducing vehicle ownership
  │
  │  5. PRIVATE VEHICLES
  │     - Electric vehicles preferred
  │     - Last priority, but necessary for some trips
  ↓
```

Sustainable cities invest in and prioritize the top of this hierarchy.

## Walking: The Foundation of Urban Mobility

Every trip involves walking, even if just to/from a parked car or transit stop. Walkable cities are healthier, more equitable, and more sustainable.

### Principles of Walkable Urban Design

**1. Density and Mix**
- Sufficient density to support services (15-25 dwellings per acre minimum)
- Mix of uses within walking distance (housing, shops, services, jobs)
- 15-minute neighborhood concept: daily needs within 15-minute walk

**2. Street Network**
- Connected grid or network (not cul-de-sacs and disconnected subdivisions)
- Short blocks (80-120 meters) providing route choices
- Frequent intersections for accessibility

**3. Pedestrian Infrastructure**
- Sidewalks on all streets (minimum 1.5-2m width, 2.5-3m in commercial areas)
- Pedestrian crossings every 80-120m
- Curb ramps at all corners (ADA compliant)
- Street trees and shade
- Pedestrian-scale lighting
- Benches and resting places

**4. Safety**
- Traffic calming slowing vehicles
- Highly visible crosswalks
- Refuge islands on wide streets
- Reduced turning radii at intersections
- Separation from vehicles (buffers, bollards, curbs)

**5. Comfort and Appeal**
- Weather protection (awnings, arcades)
- Active ground-floor uses (shops, cafes, not blank walls)
- Public art and wayfinding
- Clean and well-maintained
- Green elements

### Pedestrian Streets and Zones

**Types**
- **Full-time pedestrian streets**: No vehicles except service/emergency
- **Part-time pedestrianization**: Closed to traffic during certain hours
- **Shared space**: No curbs, vehicles and pedestrians share, vehicles slow
- **Woonerfs** (Dutch): Residential streets designed for play, walking, very slow driving

**Benefits**
- 40-50% increase in retail sales typical
- Reduced crashes and injuries
- Social interaction and community
- Cultural events and programming
- Tourism attraction

**Examples**
- **Copenhagen Strøget**: World's longest pedestrian street (1.1 km)
- **Barcelona Superblocks**: 9-block areas with restricted vehicle access
- **Melbourne**: Extensive pedestrian zones in CBD
- **New York Times Square**: Pedestrianization of iconic intersection

### Case Study: Copenhagen's Pedestrian Evolution

Copenhagen has systematically pedestrianized its center since 1962:

**Timeline**
- **1962**: Strøget pedestrianized (controversial at the time)
- **1968**: First adjacent streets closed
- **1980s-90s**: Progressive expansion of car-free zones
- **2000s**: Conversion of parking to public spaces
- **Present**: 100,000 m² of pedestrian areas

**Results**
- 4x increase in pedestrian activity
- Vibrant public life year-round (despite cold climate)
- Thriving retail and hospitality
- 90% approval rating from citizens
- Model for cities worldwide

**Lessons**
- Start small and expand progressively
- Monitor and demonstrate benefits
- Make spaces attractive (not just banning cars)
- Year-round programming and activation
- Courage to overcome initial resistance

## Cycling: Efficient and Healthy Mobility

Bicycles are remarkably efficient: transporting a person 20-25 km/h using just human power. In cities, cycling is often faster than driving for trips under 5 km (3 miles).

### Types of Cycling Infrastructure

**Protected Bike Lanes (Cycle Tracks)**
- Physical separation from motor vehicles (curb, planters, bollards)
- Same direction as traffic (one-way or two-way)
- Gold standard for safety and comfort
- Dramatic increases in ridership when installed

**Buffered Bike Lanes**
- Painted lane with striped buffer from traffic
- Less protection than cycle tracks but better than paint alone
- Lower cost, faster implementation

**Conventional Bike Lanes**
- Paint-only separation
- Minimal protection but better than nothing
- Insufficient for "interested but concerned" potential cyclists

**Shared-Use Paths (Multi-Use Trails)**
- Off-street paths for bikes and pedestrians
- Good for recreation, less suitable for transportation
- Conflicts between cyclists and pedestrians

**Bike Boulevards (Neighborhood Greenways)**
- Low-traffic residential streets optimized for cycling
- Traffic calming and traffic diverters
- Wayfinding and branding
- Network connecting destinations

**Intersection Treatments**
- Protected intersections with physical protection through crossing
- Bike boxes at traffic signals
- Two-stage turn queues for left turns
- Colored/marked bike lanes through intersections

### Cycling Success Factors

**Safety**
- Physical protection from motor vehicles
- Intersection safety measures
- Speed management (slower vehicles = safer mixing)
- Enforcement of traffic laws protecting cyclists

**Connectivity**
- Network reaching all major destinations
- Few gaps or barriers
- Directness and route options
- Integration with transit (bike parking at stations)

**Parking and Storage**
- Abundant, convenient bike parking
- Secure long-term parking at home, work
- Weather protection
- E-bike charging

**Culture and Norms**
- Social acceptance of cycling
- Education for all road users
- Marketing and encouragement programs
- Visible political commitment

**Complementary Policies**
- Traffic calming and speed reduction
- Parking restrictions for cars
- Land use density and mix
- Transit integration

### Case Study: Copenhagen's Cycling Success

Copenhagen has achieved 62% cycling mode share for commuting—the highest of any major city.

**Infrastructure**
- 380 km of protected cycle tracks
- Protected intersections throughout city
- 45 bicycle bridges and tunnels
- Parking for 75,000 bikes

**Innovation**
- Green Wave: Traffic signals timed for 20 km/h cycling speed
- RFID chips in bikes for priority at signals
- Real-time bike counters showing daily totals
- Bike highways connecting suburbs to center (up to 20 km long)

**Support Systems**
- Bike-share system (Bycyklen) with e-bikes
- Parking at all transit stations
- Bike repair shops every few blocks
- Winter maintenance: bike lanes cleared before roads

**Results**
- 1.4 million km cycled daily
- 13,000 fewer tons CO2 annually
- $230 million health benefits per year
- Faster commute than car for most trips

**Success Factors**
- 50+ years of consistent investment
- Protected infrastructure throughout network
- Integration with land use and transit
- Cultural shift: cycling is normal for everyone
- Political commitment across parties

### E-Bikes: Expanding Cycling's Reach

Electric-assist bicycles are transforming urban mobility:

**Advantages**
- Extends range: 10-15 km comfortable vs. 5 km for conventional bikes
- Overcomes hills and headwinds
- Reduces sweat (important for commuting)
- Enables cargo hauling and child transport
- Accessible to older adults and less fit riders

**Challenges**
- Higher cost ($1,000-5,000 vs. $300-1,000)
- Battery charging infrastructure
- Weight (20-30 kg) complicating storage and transit integration
- Regulatory questions (speed limits, where allowed)
- Theft risk

**Impact**
- 40-50% of bike sales in some European cities
- Enabling longer commutes by bike
- Replacing car trips more than conventional bikes
- Cargo e-bikes replacing delivery vans

## Public Transit: Moving Masses Efficiently

Public transit is 5-10x more space-efficient than private cars and produces far lower emissions per passenger.

### Transit Modes

**Heavy Rail (Metro/Subway)**
- High capacity: 30,000-80,000 passengers/hour/direction
- Fully grade-separated (underground or elevated)
- High speed and reliability
- Very expensive: $200-500 million per km
- Justified in high-density corridors
- Examples: London Underground, New York Subway, Tokyo Metro

**Light Rail Transit (LRT) / Tram**
- Medium capacity: 5,000-20,000 passengers/hour/direction
- Mostly at-grade with some grade separation
- Moderate cost: $50-150 million per km
- City-building potential: attracts development
- Examples: Portland MAX, Amsterdam trams, Melbourne trams

**Bus Rapid Transit (BRT)**
- Bus capacity: 5,000-30,000 passengers/hour/direction
- Dedicated lanes, signal priority, off-board payment
- Low to moderate cost: $5-50 million per km
- Flexible and expandable
- Examples: Curitiba, Bogotá TransMilenio, Guangzhou BRT

**Conventional Bus**
- Most flexible and lowest cost
- Can operate in mixed traffic or dedicated lanes
- Good for lower-demand corridors
- Foundation of most transit networks

**Commuter Rail**
- Long-distance service to suburbs and satellite cities
- Uses existing freight rail corridors
- Peak-hour oriented
- Examples: Paris RER, Tokyo JR lines, San Francisco Caltrain

**Ferry**
- Water-based transit for coastal cities
- Avoiding congested roads
- Examples: Sydney, Hong Kong, Istanbul, New York

### Transit-Oriented Development (TOD)

Concentrating development around transit stations:

**TOD Principles**
- High density within 400-800m of station
- Mixed-use: housing, offices, retail, services
- Pedestrian-oriented design
- Reduced parking
- Bike infrastructure and bike-share
- Quality public space

**Benefits**
- High transit ridership (50-70% of residents)
- Reduced vehicle ownership and driving
- Economic vitality and rising property values
- Efficient use of infrastructure
- Community and walkability

**Examples**
- **Hong Kong**: 75% of population within 800m of rail
- **Tokyo**: Private rail companies develop real estate at stations
- **Copenhagen**: Finger Plan—growth along rail spines for 75 years
- **Curitiba**: BRT as backbone for urban development

### Transit Best Practices

**Speed and Reliability**
- Dedicated lanes preventing traffic delay
- Signal priority at intersections
- All-door boarding reducing dwell time
- Real-time information and apps

**Frequency**
- High frequency eliminates need for schedules
- "Turn up and go" service every 10-15 minutes minimum
- More important than coverage for ridership

**Coverage vs. Ridership**
- Trade-off between serving everywhere and serving well
- Frequent service on key corridors vs. infrequent everywhere
- Different strategies for different contexts

**Fare Policy**
- Simple, easy-to-understand fares
- Free or low fares for equitable access
- Integration across modes and operators
- Contactless payment and mobile apps

**Network Design**
- Frequent grid networks outperform hub-and-spoke
- Direct connections between destinations
- Legible, easy-to-understand routes
- Integration between modes

**Customer Experience**
- Clean, safe, well-maintained
- Real-time information at stops and online
- Comfortable shelters and seating
- Accessible to all abilities

### Case Study: Curitiba's BRT Revolution

Curitiba, Brazil pioneered bus rapid transit in the 1970s:

**System Design**
- Dedicated bus lanes on major corridors
- Tube stations with off-board fare payment
- High-capacity articulated buses
- Feeder routes connecting to trunk lines
- Integrated land use and transit planning

**Innovation**
- Raised tube stations for level boarding
- Pay before boarding for faster service
- Express routes with fewer stops
- Integration with land use regulations

**Results**
- 75% of commuters using transit (extraordinary for car-oriented Brazil)
- 25% less fuel consumption than comparable cities
- 28% reduction in traffic
- Economic development along corridors
- Replicated in 180+ cities worldwide

**Lessons**
- BRT can deliver metro-like performance at fraction of cost
- Integration of transit and land use essential
- Political leadership and long-term vision
- Continuous innovation and improvement
- Appropriate technology for context

## Shared Mobility

New models of vehicle sharing changing urban transportation:

### Car-Sharing

**Station-Based**
- Vehicles at designated parking spots
- Reservation in advance
- Hourly or daily rental
- Examples: Zipcar, Communauto

**Free-Floating**
- Park anywhere in service area
- Pick up and drop off on demand
- Examples: Car2Go (discontinued), Share Now

**Peer-to-Peer**
- Owners rent personal vehicles
- Examples: Turo, Getaround

**Impact**
- Each car-share vehicle replaces 9-13 private cars
- Members reduce driving 20-40%
- Reduced parking demand
- Enables car-free or car-lite lifestyles

### Bike-Share

**Station-Based**
- Bikes docked at fixed stations
- Examples: Vélib' (Paris), Citi Bike (NYC), Santander Cycles (London)

**Dockless**
- Park anywhere within service area
- GPS-enabled locks
- Early versions had problems (clutter, vandalism)
- Examples: Mobike, Lime

**E-Bike Share**
- Growing portion of fleets
- Extending range and overcoming barriers

**Impact**
- Complements transit for first/last mile
- Enables spontaneous trips
- 30-40% of trips replace car or taxi

### Ride-Hailing and TNCs

Transportation Network Companies (Uber, Lyft, Didi, Grab):

**Benefits**
- Convenient, on-demand mobility
- Complements transit in low-density areas
- Employment opportunities

**Concerns**
- Increased vehicle traffic and congestion (evidence shows 50-60% net increase)
- Competing with transit rather than complementing
- Low pay and job insecurity for drivers
- Equity: unserved or upcharged in low-income areas
- Lack of accessibility (wheelchair vehicles)

**Regulation Approaches**
- Congestion pricing and fees
- Vehicle standards (electric, wheelchair-accessible)
- Data sharing requirements
- Labor protections for drivers

### Mobility-as-a-Service (MaaS)

Integration of all mobility options in single app:

**Concept**
- Single app for trip planning across all modes
- Real-time information and booking
- Integrated payment
- Subscription packages (like phone plans)

**Examples**
- **Whim** (Helsinki): Transit, taxi, bike-share, car-share in one app
- **Smile** (Vienna): Integration of city and regional transit
- **Jelbi** (Berlin): Transit, bike, scooter, car-share integration

**Potential**
- Seamless multimodal trips
- Reduced private car ownership
- Data for planning and optimization

**Challenges**
- Competing private interests
- Data privacy and ownership
- Ensuring equity and accessibility
- Role of public vs. private operators

## Sustainable Freight and Logistics

Goods delivery is essential but creates major impacts:

**Challenges**
- Trucks producing disproportionate emissions and pollution
- Delivery vehicles congesting streets and bike lanes
- E-commerce increasing delivery vehicle trips
- First/last mile most expensive and impactful

**Solutions**

**Urban Consolidation Centers**
- Freight transferred from large trucks to smaller, cleaner vehicles for final delivery
- Reduces truck traffic and emissions
- Examples: Lucca (Italy), Paris, London

**Cargo Bikes and E-Cargo Bikes**
- Replacing vans for small deliveries
- Up to 50% of urban deliveries could be by cargo bike
- Lower cost, zero emissions, faster in congestion
- Examples: DHL, UPS, Amazon pilots

**Delivery Windows and Zones**
- Time restrictions on truck deliveries
- Low-emission zones excluding polluting vehicles
- Loading zones managed dynamically

**Underground Freight**
- Pneumatic tube systems for small items
- Automated underground logistics
- Experimental: cargo trams, underground autonomous vehicles

## Managing Private Vehicle Use

Even as alternatives improve, private vehicles will remain part of the urban transportation mix. The key is managing their impact.

### Parking Management

Parking policy is powerful tool for managing vehicle use:

**Parking Minimums → Maximums**
- Traditional: require developers to build excessive parking
- Reform: set maximums or eliminate requirements entirely
- Unbundle parking from housing (pay separately)
- Result: less parking, more housing, less driving

**Parking Pricing**
- Price to achieve 85% occupancy (1-2 spaces available per block)
- Variable pricing by location and time
- Curb management for delivery, ride-hail, bike lanes
- Examples: SFpark (San Francisco), LA Express Park

**Parking Cash-Out**
- Employers offer cash instead of parking
- Employees who don't drive keep the money
- 17% reduction in solo driving typical

**Residential Parking Permits**
- Prevent spillover from commercial areas
- Limited supply maintaining scarcity
- Revenue for transit and bike infrastructure

### Road Pricing and Congestion Charging

Charging for road use to manage congestion:

**Cordon Pricing**
- Fee to enter central area
- Examples: Singapore, London, Stockholm, Milan

**Congestion Pricing**
- Variable pricing by time of day and congestion level
- Examples: Singapore ERP, planned New York congestion pricing

**Vehicle Miles Traveled (VMT) Fee**
- Charge per kilometer driven
- Eventually replacing fuel taxes
- GPS-based tracking raises privacy concerns
- Pilots: Oregon, Utah

**Results**
- 15-25% reduction in traffic typical
- Improved travel speeds and reliability
- Air quality improvements
- Revenue for transit investment
- Political challenges but growing acceptance

### Speed Management

Speed kills. Reducing speeds saves lives and improves livability:

**20 mph (30 km/h) Zones**
- Residential areas and city centers
- Pedestrian fatality risk: 90% at 30 mph, 10% at 20 mph
- Increasing adoption: Paris, Brussels, Barcelona (80 km/h → 30 km/h)

**Traffic Calming**
- Speed humps and tables
- Curb extensions (bulb-outs)
- Chicanes and narrowing
- Raised intersections and crosswalks

**Design Speed**
- Engineer streets for desired speed
- Narrow lanes, tight turning radii, trees
- Visual friction slowing drivers

**Enforcement**
- Automated speed cameras (where legal)
- Vision Zero commitment to zero traffic deaths
- Police enforcement

## Electric Vehicles

Even as we shift to walking, cycling, and transit, remaining vehicle trips should be electric:

**Benefits**
- Zero tailpipe emissions
- 50-80% lower lifecycle emissions (depending on electricity source)
- Lower operating costs
- Quieter

**Challenges**
- Higher upfront cost (though falling rapidly)
- Charging infrastructure needed
- Range anxiety (though 300+ km now typical)
- Supply chain and materials (lithium, cobalt)
- Electricity grid capacity

**Policies**
- Purchase incentives and rebates
- Emissions standards phasing out ICE vehicles
- Charging infrastructure deployment
- Fleet electrification (buses, taxis, delivery)
- Targets: UK, Norway, California banning ICE sales by 2030-2035

**Note**: EVs reduce emissions but don't solve congestion, parking, or safety issues. Vehicle efficiency must complement mode shift.

## Future Mobility

### Autonomous Vehicles

Self-driving vehicles promise to transform urban transportation:

**Potential Benefits**
- Increased safety (94% of crashes involve human error)
- Shared autonomous vehicles reducing vehicle ownership
- More productive travel time
- Mobility for those unable to drive
- Curb space reclaimed from parking

**Potential Harms**
- Induced demand: sprawl and more vehicle travel
- Job losses: truck drivers, taxi drivers, bus drivers
- Privacy: tracking of movements
- Cybersecurity vulnerabilities
- Equity: benefits to those who can afford

**Uncertainty**
- Timeline remains unclear (harder than anticipated)
- How people will use AVs unclear (shared or private?)
- Regulatory frameworks still developing
- Urban planning implications depend on adoption patterns

**Planning Approach**
- Anticipate multiple scenarios
- Ensure AVs are electric and shared
- Manage curb space and right-of-way
- Protect transit and active transportation investments

### Urban Air Mobility

Electric vertical take-off and landing (eVTOL) aircraft:

**Potential**
- Short-distance air taxis
- Emergency services and medical transport
- Reduced travel time for some trips

**Challenges**
- Noise and visual impact
- Safety and regulation
- Energy consumption
- Equity: likely to serve mainly wealthy
- Competes for investment with ground transportation

**Verdict**: Niche applications likely, mass transportation unlikely

## Conclusion

Sustainable urban mobility prioritizes walking, cycling, and public transit—efficient modes that create livable, equitable, environmentally responsible cities. Cities like Copenhagen, Curitiba, and Tokyo demonstrate what's possible through integrated planning, sustained investment, and political commitment.

The key principles:
- **Hierarchy**: Prioritize efficient, sustainable modes
- **Integration**: Seamless connections between modes
- **Accessibility**: Mobility for all regardless of age, ability, income
- **Safety**: Vision Zero commitment to eliminating traffic deaths
- **Land Use**: Density and mix supporting sustainable modes
- **Pricing**: Make sustainable modes affordable, charge for car use
- **Investment**: Fund what we want to see more of

The future of urban mobility is multimodal, electric, shared, and centered on people rather than vehicles.

### Key Takeaways

1. **Transportation is responsible for major environmental, social, and economic impacts**
2. **Sustainable mobility prioritizes walking, cycling, and transit over private vehicles**
3. **Copenhagen demonstrates world-leading cycling** through protected infrastructure and cultural support
4. **Curitiba pioneered BRT** as affordable, high-capacity transit
5. **Shared mobility** (car-share, bike-share, MaaS) enables car-free lifestyles
6. **Parking management and road pricing** are powerful tools to manage vehicle use
7. **Electric vehicles should complement, not replace, mode shift to sustainable transportation**
