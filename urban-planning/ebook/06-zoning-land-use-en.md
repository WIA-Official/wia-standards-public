# Chapter 5: Zoning, Land Use, and Mixed-Use Development

## The Power of Zoning

Zoning is perhaps the most powerful tool in urban planning—determining what can be built where, how tall, how dense, and for what purpose. These regulations shape urban form, economic opportunity, environmental outcomes, and social equity for generations.

### What is Zoning?

Zoning divides a city into districts with different regulations controlling:

**Use**: What activities are allowed
- Residential, commercial, industrial, agricultural, institutional
- Subcat

egories: single-family, multifamily, office, retail, manufacturing

**Intensity/Density**: How much development
- Floor area ratio (FAR): total floor area ÷ lot area
- Dwelling units per acre/hectare
- Lot coverage percentage

**Form**: Physical characteristics
- Height limits (feet/meters or stories)
- Setbacks from property lines
- Parking requirements
- Design standards

**Procedure**: How projects are approved
- By-right development meeting all standards
- Conditional uses requiring special permits
- Variances for exceptions to standards

### History of Zoning

**Pre-Zoning Era (Before 1900s)**
- Common law nuisance standards
- Private covenants and deed restrictions
- Little systematic regulation
- Result: incompatible uses (slaughterhouse next to housing)

**Birth of Modern Zoning (1900s-1920s)**
- New York City 1916 Zoning Resolution (first comprehensive zoning in U.S.)
- Response to massive buildings blocking light and air
- Separation of uses (especially noxious industry from housing)
- Euclid vs. Ambler (1926): Supreme Court upholds zoning power

**Euclidean Zoning**
- Hierarchical zones: residential → commercial → industrial
- Strict separation of uses
- Single-family residential as "highest" use
- Became dominant model in North America

**Unintended Consequences**
- Sprawl: low-density zoning consuming land
- Automobile dependence: separation of uses requiring driving
- Social segregation: exclusionary zoning keeping out affordable housing
- Economic inefficiency: rigid separation preventing organic development
- Environmental harm: land consumption, longer trips

**Contemporary Reforms (1980s-Present)**
- Mixed-use zoning allowing combinations
- Form-based codes regulating built form instead of use
- Inclusionary zoning requiring affordable housing
- Elimination of parking minimums
- Upzoning to allow greater density

## Land Use Planning

While zoning implements land use plans, the plans themselves reflect community vision and strategy:

### Components of Land Use Plans

**Future Land Use Map**
- Shows desired development patterns 10-30 years out
- Broader categories than zoning (residential, mixed-use, employment, open space)
- Guides rezoning decisions
- Not legally binding like zoning

**Land Use Policies**
- Criteria for land use decisions
- Protection of sensitive areas
- Infill and redevelopment priorities
- Growth boundaries and urban service areas
- Rural and agricultural preservation

**Implementation Program**
- Zoning updates to align with plan
- Public investments (transit, parks, utilities)
- Regulatory reforms
- Partnership strategies

### Land Use Patterns and Urban Form

**Sprawl Pattern**
- Low-density, single-use zones
- Leapfrog development beyond urbanized area
- Segregation of housing, jobs, services
- Car-dependent

**Compact Pattern**
- Higher densities supporting transit and walking
- Mixed-use neighborhoods
- Clear urban edge with growth boundaries
- Multiple centers rather than single CBD

**Transit-Oriented Pattern**
- Development concentrated along transit corridors
- High density at station areas
- Mixed-use centers every few kilometers
- Lower density between corridors

**Polycentric Pattern**
- Multiple urban centers with employment and services
- Regional coordination
- Examples: Randstad (Netherlands), Rhine-Ruhr (Germany)

## Zoning Reform and Innovation

### Form-Based Codes

Form-based codes regulate the physical form of buildings rather than use:

**Key Features**
- **Regulating plan**: Map showing street types and building types
- **Building form standards**: Height, setback, facade composition, parking location
- **Street standards**: Cross-sections defining right-of-way
- **Use**: Flexible, focusing on intensity and character rather than specific uses

**Advantages**
- Predictability: Clear vision of resulting built environment
- Flexibility: Wide range of uses allowed if form is appropriate
- Context-sensitive: Different standards for different contexts
- Pedestrian-oriented: Emphasizes street frontage and walkability

**Examples**
- **Miami, Florida**: Downtown zoning replaced with form-based code
- **Denver, Colorado**: Main streets form-based code
- **SmartCode**: Model form-based code used nationally

**Challenges**
- Complexity: More detailed than conventional zoning
- Cultural shift: Different way of thinking about regulation
- Requires design expertise

### Mixed-Use Zoning

Allowing and encouraging combinations of uses:

**Benefits**
- Vitality: Activity throughout the day
- Walkability: Services within walking distance of housing
- Transit support: Higher density and diverse trip purposes
- Economic resilience: Multiple sectors in area
- Social interaction: Diverse people sharing spaces

**Design Considerations**
- **Horizontal mixed-use**: Different buildings on same block
- **Vertical mixed-use**: Different floors in same building (e.g., retail below, offices middle, housing above)
- **Compatibility**: Managing conflicts (noise, hours of operation, parking)
- **Affordable housing**: Prevent pure commercial conversion in hot markets

**Examples**
- **Vancouver**: Mixed-use zoning throughout city
- **Paris**: Traditionally mixed-use with housing above shops
- **Singapore**: Mixed-use developments integrating transit, housing, retail

### Inclusionary Zoning

Requiring or incentivizing affordable housing in new development:

**Mandatory Inclusionary Zoning**
- All developments over threshold (e.g., 10 units) must include affordable units
- Typical: 10-20% of units affordable
- May offer density bonus in exchange

**Voluntary Inclusionary Zoning**
- Developers opt-in for density bonus or other incentives
- Less common: Weaker at producing affordable housing

**Design**
- **Set-aside percentage**: How many units must be affordable (10-25% typical)
- **Affordability level**: Income levels served (e.g., 60-80% AMI)
- **Duration**: Affordability period (30 years to perpetuity)
- **Alternatives**: Payment in-lieu, off-site construction
- **Incentives**: Density bonus, parking reduction, fee waivers

**Examples**
- **Washington, D.C.**: 8-10% affordable housing required, density bonus offered
- **San Francisco**: 20% affordable required or 30% off-site or pay fee
- **New York**: 25-30% affordable in exchange for FAR bonus

**Effectiveness**
- Produces modest numbers of affordable units
- Creates economically mixed communities
- Controversial: Does it reduce overall housing production?
- Most effective in strong markets with development pressure

### Eliminating Parking Minimums

Minimum parking requirements force oversupply of parking:

**Problems with Parking Minimums**
- Increases housing costs ($50,000-100,000 per space)
- Induces driving ("free" parking encourages car ownership)
- Consumes land (parking covers 30% of some cities)
- Prioritizes cars over people
- Conflicts with transit, cycling, pedestrian goals
- One-size-fits-all ignores local variation

**Reform Approaches**
- **Eliminate minimums**: Let market determine parking supply
- **Parking maximums**: Cap parking to discourage driving
- **Unbundle parking**: Charge separately so non-drivers don't subsidize
- **District parking**: Shared facilities instead of site-by-site
- **Reduced minimums**: Lower requirements near transit

**Results**
- More housing produced (especially affordable)
- Lower housing costs
- More sustainable transportation
- Reclaimed land for better uses

**Examples**
- **San Francisco**: Eliminated minimums in much of city
- **Minneapolis**: Eliminated minimums citywide
- **London**: Parking maximums instead of minimums
- **Tokyo**: No citywide minimums (micro-neighborhood level only)

### Accessory Dwelling Units (ADUs)

Additional small dwellings on single-family lots:

**Types**
- **Detached ADU**: Separate structure in backyard
- **Attached ADU**: Addition to main house
- **Internal ADU**: Conversion of garage, basement, attic

**Benefits**
- Increases housing supply affordably
- Multigenerational living and elderly aging in place
- Rental income for homeowners
- Gently increases density
- Uses existing infrastructure

**Barriers**
- Zoning prohibitions or overly restrictive regulations
- Parking requirements making ADUs infeasible
- Utility connection fees
- Neighbor opposition
- Financing challenges

**Reforms**
- Allowing ADUs by-right (no discretionary review)
- Eliminating/reducing parking requirements
- Reducing fees
- Pre-approved designs
- Education and technical assistance

**Examples**
- **Vancouver**: 10,000+ laneway houses built since 2009
- **California**: State law overriding local restrictions
- **Portland**: 1,800+ ADUs built after reform
- **Auckland, New Zealand**: Allowing up to two ADUs per lot

## Mixed-Use Development

Mixed-use developments combine residential, commercial, and sometimes institutional uses in integrated projects.

### Types of Mixed-Use

**Vertical Mixed-Use**
- Different uses stacked in same building
- Typical: Retail ground floor, office middle floors, residential above
- Activates street while providing services to residents

**Horizontal Mixed-Use**
- Different buildings on same site or block
- More separation but still walkable integration

**Master-Planned Communities**
- Large-scale mixed-use with housing, jobs, services, parks
- Often new development on greenfield or large redevelopment sites

### Benefits of Mixed-Use

**For Residents**
- Convenience: Services within walking distance
- Vitality and safety: Activity throughout day and evening
- Community: Encounters with neighbors and local businesses
- Reduced transportation costs

**For Businesses**
- Customer base: Built-in customers from residents
- Extended hours: Lunch, evening, weekend traffic
- Synergies: Cross-visitation between complementary businesses

**For Cities**
- Efficient land use: Multiple purposes on same land
- Transit support: Higher density and diverse trip purposes
- Tax base: Commercial and residential
- Sustainability: Shorter trips, less driving

### Design Principles

**Ground Floor Activation**
- Retail, restaurants, services on street level
- Transparent facades (glass, not blank walls)
- Frequent entrances (every 15-25 meters)
- Weather protection (awnings, arcades)

**Transitions**
- Gradual change from commercial to residential
- Buffer zones and landscaping
- Hours of operation management
- Parking and loading separated from residential entrances

**Parking Solutions**
- Underground or structured parking
- Shared parking for different uses (office vs. residential peak times)
- Reduced requirements in walkable areas

**Public Space**
- Plazas, courtyards, parks as amenities
- Programming and activation
- Privately owned public spaces (POPS)

### Case Study: Vancouver's Mixed-Use Success

Vancouver has built one of North America's most livable cities through mixed-use development:

**Strategies**

**1. Neighborhood Centres**
- Mixed-use zoning along arterial streets
- 4-6 story buildings with retail below, housing above
- Services within walking distance of single-family neighborhoods

**2. High-Density Neighborhoods**
- Downtown: Towers with retail podiums
- Broadway corridor: Mid-rise mixed-use
- Integration with transit stations

**3. False Creek and Olympic Village**
- Comprehensive waterfront redevelopment
- Mix of housing types, offices, retail, parks
- LEED sustainability standards
- Public amenities and community facilities

**4. Protected View Corridors**
- Height limits preserving mountain and water views
- Creates distinctive skyline and character

**Results**
- 85,000+ people living downtown
- 50% commute by walking, cycling, transit
- Vibrant street life and neighborhood vitality
- High livability rankings
- Model for cities worldwide

**Challenges**
- Housing affordability crisis
- Gentrification and displacement
- "Vancouverism" criticized as monotonous
- Debate over balance of growth and preservation

## Special Purpose Districts and Overlays

Beyond base zoning, special districts address specific areas or goals:

### Historic Preservation Districts

**Regulations**
- Review of changes to building exteriors
- Standards for new construction (compatible but distinguishable)
- Prohibition of demolition
- Incentives: tax credits, density bonuses

**Benefits**
- Preserves cultural heritage and character
- Economic value: Heritage tourism, distinctive places
- Sustainability: Reuse of existing buildings
- Community identity and sense of place

**Challenges**
- Balancing preservation and change
- Gentrification: Historic areas become expensive
- Maintenance costs
- Debate over what to preserve

**Examples**
- **Paris**: Strict preservation of central districts
- **Rome**: Comprehensive historic center protection
- **Charleston, South Carolina**: Early preservation pioneer

### Environmental Overlay Districts

**Purpose**
- Protect sensitive environmental features
- Wetlands, steep slopes, flood plains, habitat

**Regulations**
- Development restrictions or prohibitions
- Buffer requirements
- Special design standards
- Environmental impact assessment

### Transit Overlay Districts

**Purpose**
- Concentrate development at transit stations
- Maximize transit ridership

**Regulations**
- Increased density allowances
- Reduced parking requirements
- Mixed-use encouraged or required
- Pedestrian-oriented design standards

**Examples**
- **Portland**: Transit-oriented development zones
- **Minneapolis**: Transit overlay district along light rail

### Affordable Housing Overlay Districts

**Purpose**
- Encourage affordable housing production
- Enable density bonuses for affordable housing

**Regulations**
- Additional density allowed for projects with affordable units
- Streamlined approval
- Fee reductions

## Challenges and Controversies

### Exclusionary Zoning

Zoning has been used to exclude people by race, ethnicity, and class:

**Techniques**
- **Large-lot zoning**: Requiring large minimum lot sizes (1-5 acres)
- **Single-family-only zoning**: Prohibiting apartments and multifamily
- **Excessive standards**: Parking, setbacks, design review increasing costs
- **Growth controls**: Limits on housing production

**Impacts**
- Housing shortages and unaffordability
- Racial and economic segregation
- Sprawl: Development pushed to outer areas
- Climate impacts: More driving, land consumption
- Inequality: Concentration of poverty and opportunity

**Reforms**
- **Ending single-family-only zoning**: Minneapolis, Oregon, California
- **Affordable housing requirements**: Inclusionary zoning
- **State-level intervention**: Overriding local restrictions
- **Fair housing enforcement**: Federal civil rights laws

**Case Study: Minneapolis Ends Single-Family Zoning**
In 2018, Minneapolis became first major U.S. city to eliminate single-family-only zoning:

**Changes**
- Allowed triplexes on all residential lots
- Eliminated parking minimums
- Increased density near transit

**Goals**
- Increase housing supply and affordability
- Address racial segregation and exclusion
- Climate action (reduced driving)

**Results (as of 2024)**
- Significant increase in multifamily permits
- Broader geographic distribution of new housing
- Reduced opposition to density (normalized)
- Model for other cities (Oregon, California followed)

### NIMBYism and Community Opposition

Not In My Back Yard (NIMBY) opposition to development:

**Common Objections**
- Traffic and parking impacts
- Character change and aesthetics
- Property value concerns
- Construction disruption
- School overcrowding
- "Too much, too fast"

**Tactics**
- Attendance at public hearings
- Appeals and lawsuits
- Political pressure on elected officials
- Organizing neighborhood opposition

**Issues**
- Vocal minority blocking projects with broad benefits
- Environmental review weaponized
- Legitimate concerns mixed with exclusion
- Democratic participation vs. housing crisis

**Reforms**
- By-right development reducing discretionary review
- State preemption of local obstruction
- Requiring evidence-based objections
- Balancing local and regional interests

### Gentrification and Displacement

When neighborhoods improve, who benefits?

**Gentrification Dynamics**
- Disinvestment → Low property values
- Artists, pioneers move in → Neighborhood "discovered"
- Amenities improve → Rising property values
- Original residents priced out → Demographic change

**Causes**
- Market dynamics and investment flows
- Transportation improvements
- Zoning changes increasing development
- Public investments (parks, infrastructure)

**Impacts**
- Loss of affordable housing
- Cultural change and loss of community
- Small business displacement
- Social networks disrupted
- But also: Improved services, safety, investment

**Anti-Displacement Strategies**
- **Affordable housing preservation**: Buy buildings, regulate rent increases
- **Community land trusts**: Permanently affordable housing
- **Rent control/stabilization**: Limit increases (controversial)
- **Tenant protections**: Just-cause eviction, relocation assistance
- **Community benefits agreements**: Local hiring, affordability requirements
- **Commercial affordability**: Protect local businesses
- **Equitable development**: Community involvement and benefit

**Case Study: Portland's North/Northeast Gentrification**
Portland's historically Black neighborhoods gentrified rapidly:

**Context**
- Redlining and urban renewal had concentrated Black community
- 2000s-2010s: Light rail, development pressure
- Black population declined 70% in some neighborhoods

**Responses**
- Preference policy for displaced residents in new affordable housing
- N/NE Community Development Initiative
- Inclusionary housing zoning
- Community land trust
- Acknowledgment of historical injustice

**Lessons**
- Displacement prevention must precede reinvestment
- Needs resources at scale
- Community voice and leadership essential
- Addressing past harms is ongoing work

## Future of Land Use and Zoning

### Trends

**Liberalization and Upzoning**
- Eliminating single-family-only zoning
- Allowing missing middle housing (duplexes, fourplexes, townhomes)
- Increasing density near transit
- Driven by housing affordability crisis

**Climate-Responsive Zoning**
- Retreat from flood-prone and wildfire-risk areas
- Density to reduce driving
- Green infrastructure requirements
- Embodied carbon in construction materials

**Flexibility and Adaptive Reuse**
- Easier conversion between uses (office to housing)
- Responding to remote work and retail changes
- Temporary and pop-up uses

**Digital Tools**
- 3D zoning visualization
- Automated compliance checking
- Data-driven zoning reform
- Public engagement platforms

**Equity-Centered**
- Mandatory affordable housing
- Anti-displacement protections
- Community benefits requirements
- Reparative zoning addressing historical harms

### Questions for the Future

- How do we balance local control with regional needs?
- Can zoning reform meaningfully improve affordability?
- How do we prevent zoning changes from causing displacement?
- What role should community input play in development decisions?
- How do we zone for resilience to climate change?

## Conclusion

Zoning and land use regulations powerfully shape cities. The 20th-century model—strict separation of uses, low density, abundant parking—has produced sprawl, segregation, and unsustainability. Reform is essential and underway.

Key directions:
- **Mixed-use and compact development** creating walkable, vibrant neighborhoods
- **Eliminating exclusionary zoning** to enable housing production and integration
- **Form-based codes** focusing on built form rather than use
- **Transit-oriented development** concentrating growth at stations
- **Anti-displacement strategies** ensuring existing residents benefit from improvement
- **Climate adaptation** zoning for resilience and reduced emissions

Done well, land use planning creates opportunity, sustainability, and equity. Done poorly, it reinforces inequality and environmental harm. The stakes are high, and the tools are in our hands.

### Key Takeaways

1. **Zoning determines what can be built where**, shaping urban form, sustainability, and equity
2. **Euclidean zoning's strict separation of uses** has produced sprawl and automobile dependence
3. **Mixed-use zoning and form-based codes** enable walkable, vibrant neighborhoods
4. **Exclusionary zoning has created segregation and housing shortages**; reform is essential
5. **Inclusionary zoning and ADUs** can increase affordable housing supply
6. **Eliminating parking minimums** reduces costs and supports sustainable transportation
7. **Anti-displacement strategies** must accompany neighborhood improvement
