Phase 4: Integration

Connecting enzymatic recycling to the circular economy ecosystem

7.1 The Integration Challenge

Enzymatic plastic degradation does not operate in isolation. To realize its full potential, it must integrate seamlessly with the existing waste management infrastructure, chemical supply chains, and product manufacturing systems. Phase 4 of the WIA-PLASTIC-ENZYME standard addresses these integration requirements.

7.2 Waste Collection Integration

Enzymatic recycling facilities need consistent feedstock. Integration with waste collection systems is essential.

Municipal Waste Systems

Partnerships with municipal recycling programs to receive sorted PET waste.

  • Data exchange: Volume forecasts, contamination levels
  • Quality requirements: Minimum sorting accuracy specifications
  • Logistics: Scheduled pickup or delivery arrangements
  • Payments: Gate fees or material purchase pricing

Industrial Waste Streams

Direct sourcing from manufacturers with clean, consistent plastic waste.

  • Off-spec production: Products that don't meet QC standards
  • Trim and scrap: Manufacturing byproducts
  • End-of-life returns: Take-back program materials

Consumer Take-back Programs

Brand-operated collection points for post-consumer packaging.

  • Deposit systems: Incentivized returns
  • Retail collection: In-store drop-off points
  • Mail-back: Prepaid shipping labels for small items

Enzymatic TPA must compete with virgin TPA from petroleum or bio-based sources. Key success factors:

  • Quality: Meet or exceed virgin TPA specifications
  • Consistency: Batch-to-batch reliability
  • Traceability: Chain of custody documentation
  • Price: Competitive with virgin alternatives
  • Sustainability premium: Value for recycled content claims

EG Supply Chain

Ethylene glycol has diverse applications beyond PET manufacturing:

  • Antifreeze: Automotive coolants
  • Polyesters: Fiber and film production
  • Plasticizers: Flexible PVC applications
  • Deicing: Aircraft and runway treatment

Carbon Credit Integration

Enzymatic recycling can generate carbon credits through verified emission reductions:

  • Avoided virgin production: ~2 tons CO2e per ton of plastic recycled
  • Avoided incineration: ~1.5 tons CO2e per ton
  • Certification: Verra, Gold Standard, or equivalent protocols

7.5 Regulatory Integration

Compliance with environmental and product safety regulations across jurisdictions.

Food Contact Certification

For bottle-to-bottle recycling, monomers must meet food contact standards:

  • EU: EC 282/2008 recycled plastic regulations
  • US: FDA no-objection letters for recycling processes
  • Japan: JHOSPA positive list compliance

Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR)

Integration with EPR schemes for compliance reporting:

  • Material tracking: Link processed waste to producer obligations
  • Recycling credits: Generate tradeable recycling certificates
  • Reporting: Automated submission to regulatory portals

7.7 Partner Ecosystem

The WIA-PLASTIC-ENZYME standard facilitates partnerships across the value chain:

Technology Providers

  • Enzyme manufacturers and suppliers
  • Equipment vendors for reactors, separators, purification
  • Software providers for process control and optimization
  • Testing laboratories for quality analysis

Brand Partners

  • Consumer goods companies seeking recycled content
  • Textile manufacturers for fiber recycling
  • Packaging converters for food-grade applications

Financial Partners

  • Green bonds and sustainability-linked financing
  • Carbon credit buyers
  • Impact investors focused on circular economy

7.9 Chapter Summary

Phase 4 integration connects enzymatic plastic recycling to the broader economic ecosystem:

  • Waste collection: Municipal, industrial, and consumer take-back integration
  • Chemical supply chain: Meeting specifications for polymer manufacturers
  • Carbon accounting: Tracking and reporting environmental benefits
  • Regulatory compliance: Food contact, EPR, and environmental permits
  • Digital integration: Real-time monitoring and optimization
  • Partner ecosystem: Technology, brand, and financial partnerships

In our final chapter, we explore the ultimate goal: a true circular economy for plastics.
