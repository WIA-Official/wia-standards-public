Chapter 3

PETase & Beyond

Breakthrough enzymes driving the future of plastic degradation

3.1 The Cutting Edge of Enzyme Research

The years 2024-2025 have witnessed remarkable advances in plastic-degrading enzyme technology. Building on nearly a decade of research since the original PETase discovery, scientists have developed variants that operate under industrial conditions, achieve near-complete degradation, and can process real-world plastic waste rather than just laboratory samples.

This chapter explores the most significant enzyme discoveries and engineering achievements that are shaping the WIA-PLASTIC-ENZYME standard.

3.2 TurboPETase: Computational Design Triumph

TurboPETase

Published in Nature Communications in February 2024, TurboPETase represents a major breakthrough in computationally designed enzyme engineering. Developed through iterative rounds of molecular dynamics simulations and experimental validation, this enzyme overcomes critical limitations of earlier PETase variants.

Nature 2024 Computational Design Industrial Grade

Key Innovations

TurboPETase addresses the historical challenge of the "10% barrier"—the observation that early PETase variants would stall at around 90% degradation due to product inhibition and substrate accessibility issues. Through careful engineering of the enzyme's surface properties and active site geometry, researchers achieved:

  • Near-complete degradation: 98-99% PET conversion at 10% substrate loading
  • Product tolerance: Maintained activity at high concentrations of TPA and EG
  • Robust expression: High yields in standard E. coli expression systems
  • Thermal stability: Active at 50-60°C for extended periods
Parameter Wild-type PETase TurboPETase
Optimal Temperature 30°C 55°C
Half-life at 50°C ~2 hours >72 hours
Maximum Degradation ~75% ~99%
Substrate Tolerance 2% 15%

3.3 GuaPA: The Archaeal Discovery

GuaPA (Guaymas PETase)

Published in PMC in 2025, GuaPA represents the first PETase discovered in archaea—a domain of life previously unexplored for plastic-degrading enzymes. Found in Bathyarchaeia from deep-sea sediments of the Guaymas Basin, this enzyme demonstrates that plastic degradation capability has evolved independently in multiple lineages of life.

PMC 2025 First Archaeal PETase Deep-Sea Origin

Unique Characteristics

GuaPA's archaeal origin gives it distinct properties from bacterial PETases:

  • Salt tolerance: Maintains activity in high-salinity conditions, useful for marine plastic cleanup
  • Pressure stability: Evolved at deep-sea pressures, suggesting potential for high-pressure industrial processes
  • Novel active site: Structural differences open new engineering possibilities
  • Rapid degradation: PET film breakdown within 48 hours

Synergy with BHETase

When combined with BHETase (BHET hydrolyzing enzyme), GuaPA shows remarkable synergistic effects, achieving 68% improvement in overall degradation efficiency. This finding has important implications for enzyme cocktail formulations in the WIA-PLASTIC-ENZYME standard.

WIA Standard Implementation

The WIA-PLASTIC-ENZYME standard includes specific protocols for enzyme cocktail optimization, enabling facilities to combine enzymes like GuaPA and BHETase for maximum efficiency.

3.4 DIEGO: Geothermal Thermostability

DIEGO (Geothermal PETase)

Published in ScienceDirect in October 2025, DIEGO was isolated from metagenomic samples at geothermal sites. Its natural thermostability addresses one of the key challenges in enzymatic PET recycling—the need for elevated temperatures to increase substrate accessibility.

ScienceDirect 2025 Geothermal Origin 60°C Operation

Performance Highlights

  • Temperature optimum: 60°C—closer to PET's glass transition temperature
  • Complete degradation: 100% PET conversion within 96 hours
  • Real-world substrates: Successfully degraded actual consumer PET trays
  • Native thermostability: No engineering required for heat resistance

3.5 Engineered Wastewater Bacteria

A parallel approach to enzyme discovery focuses on engineering common environmental bacteria to degrade plastics in situ. Research published in R&D World in January 2025 demonstrated:

Wastewater PETase System

Scientists transferred PETase genes into bacteria commonly found in wastewater treatment plants, creating organisms that can degrade microplastics during water treatment.

R&D World 2025 In-Situ Treatment Microplastics

Results

  • 30% degradation of PET microplastics in laboratory conditions
  • 40% degradation of 0.25mm PET film in 4 days at 50°C
  • Compatible with existing wastewater treatment infrastructure
  • Potential for distributed microplastic remediation

3.6 Enzyme Cocktails and Synergy

No single enzyme is optimal for all aspects of plastic degradation. The WIA-PLASTIC-ENZYME standard recognizes the importance of enzyme cocktails—carefully formulated combinations that achieve results beyond what individual enzymes can accomplish.

Common Cocktail Components

Enzyme Function Optimal Ratio
PETase Cleaves PET into MHET and TPA 1.0
MHETase Converts MHET to TPA + EG 0.5-1.0
BHETase Cleaves BHET intermediate 0.3
Cutinase Additional ester hydrolysis 0.2

Design Principles

Effective enzyme cocktails follow these principles:

  1. Sequential pathway coverage: Enzymes for each step of degradation
  2. Product removal: Preventing intermediate accumulation that inhibits upstream enzymes
  3. Compatible conditions: All enzymes active at the same temperature and pH
  4. Ratio optimization: Balancing enzyme concentrations for bottleneck elimination

3.7 Engineering Challenges and Solutions

As summarized in Nature Communications Materials (2025), several engineering challenges must be addressed for industrial enzyme deployment:

Challenge: Activity

Enzymes must degrade plastic faster than economically feasible.

Solution: Directed evolution and computational design have increased activity 30-fold over wild-type.

Challenge: Thermostability

Higher temperatures increase substrate accessibility but denature enzymes.

Solution: Thermophilic enzyme discovery and protein engineering.

Challenge: Product Inhibition

Accumulated monomers slow enzyme activity.

Solution: Continuous product removal and inhibition-resistant variants.

Challenge: Production Cost

Enzyme production must be economical at industrial scale.

Solution: Solid-state fermentation reduces costs 10-fold.

3.8 Looking Ahead: Next-Generation Enzymes

Research continues on several fronts to push the boundaries of enzymatic plastic degradation:

  • AI-designed enzymes: Machine learning models predicting optimal sequences
  • Multi-plastic enzymes: Single enzymes that can degrade multiple plastic types
  • Self-assembling systems: Enzyme scaffolds that concentrate activity at plastic surfaces
  • Cell-surface display: Whole-cell biocatalysts for simplified processing

3.9 Chapter Summary

The latest generation of plastic-degrading enzymes—TurboPETase, GuaPA, DIEGO, and others—represents major advances toward industrial viability. Key achievements include:

  • Near-complete PET degradation (>98%) under industrial conditions
  • Discovery of enzymes from previously unexplored microbial lineages
  • Synergistic enzyme cocktails that outperform individual enzymes
  • Approaches to all major engineering challenges

The WIA-PLASTIC-ENZYME standard provides the framework for characterizing, comparing, and deploying these enzymes globally. In the next chapter, we will explore Phase 1 of the standard: the data formats that enable interoperability across the enzyme recycling ecosystem.
