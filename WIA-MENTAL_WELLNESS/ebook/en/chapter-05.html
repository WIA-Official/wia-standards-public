5.1 Understanding Suicide and Crisis

Suicide is a global public health crisis, claiming over 700,000 lives annually worldwide. For every death by suicide, there are an estimated 20 suicide attempts. The impact extends far beyond these numbers, affecting families, communities, and society as a whole.

Global Suicide Statistics

  • 703,000 people die by suicide each year globally (WHO, 2019)
  • One death every 40 seconds
  • 4th leading cause of death among 15-29 year olds
  • 77% of suicides occur in low- and middle-income countries
  • More people die by suicide than from malaria, breast cancer, war, and homicide combined
  • Firearms, pesticide poisoning, and hanging are the most common methods

5.1.1 Risk Factors for Suicide

Suicide rarely results from a single cause. Multiple risk factors often interact to increase vulnerability:

Major Risk Factors

Mental Health Conditions:

  • Depression and mood disorders (present in 90% of suicide deaths)
  • Substance use disorders
  • Schizophrenia and other psychotic disorders
  • Borderline personality disorder
  • PTSD and trauma history
  • Eating disorders

Life Circumstances:

  • Recent loss (death, divorce, job loss)
  • Financial problems
  • Legal difficulties
  • Chronic pain or serious illness
  • Social isolation and loneliness
  • Homelessness or housing instability

Personal History:

  • Previous suicide attempts (strongest predictor)
  • Family history of suicide
  • Childhood abuse or trauma
  • Impulsivity and aggression

Access to Lethal Means:

  • Firearms in the home
  • Stockpiled medications
  • Knowledge of lethal methods

5.1.2 Warning Signs

Warning signs suggest immediate risk and require urgent action:

  • Talking about wanting to die, feeling hopeless, or being a burden
  • Looking for ways to kill oneself (searching online, obtaining means)
  • Talking about feeling trapped or in unbearable pain
  • Increased use of alcohol or drugs
  • Acting anxious or agitated; behaving recklessly
  • Sleeping too little or too much
  • Withdrawing or isolating from others
  • Showing rage or talking about seeking revenge
  • Extreme mood swings
  • Giving away possessions, saying goodbye, making a will

5.2 Suicide Risk Assessment

Comprehensive risk assessment is the foundation of suicide prevention. Digital mental health platforms must implement validated assessment tools and protocols.

5.2.1 Columbia-Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS)

The C-SSRS is the gold standard for suicide risk assessment. It systematically evaluates:

C-SSRS Assessment Components

1. Suicidal Ideation:

  • Wish to be dead
  • Non-specific active suicidal thoughts
  • Active suicidal ideation with any methods (not plan) without intent to act
  • Active suicidal ideation with some intent to act, without specific plan
  • Active suicidal ideation with specific plan and intent

2. Intensity of Ideation:

  • Frequency (how often do thoughts occur?)
  • Duration (how long do they last?)
  • Controllability (can you stop thinking about it?)
  • Deterrents (what would stop you?)
  • Reasons for ideation (why do you have these thoughts?)

3. Suicidal Behavior:

  • Actual attempt (potentially self-injurious act with intent to die)
  • Interrupted attempt (prevented by external circumstances)
  • Aborted attempt (stopped oneself from attempting)
  • Preparatory acts (gathering means, preparing for attempt)

5.2.2 Risk Stratification

After assessment, categorize risk level to determine appropriate intervention:

Risk Level Indicators Response
Low Risk No current ideation, no history of attempts, good protective factors Continue routine care, schedule follow-up, provide resources
Moderate Risk Passive ideation without plan, history of attempts (not recent), some protective factors Increase contact frequency, create safety plan, consider medication review
High Risk Active ideation with plan, recent attempt or preparation, limited protective factors Daily contact, activate crisis team, remove means, consider hospitalization
Imminent Risk Intent to act imminently, means available, severe hopelessness Emergency intervention, contact emergency services, hospitalization

5.2.3 Digital Assessment Implementation

When implementing suicide risk screening in digital platforms:

  • Frequency: Screen at intake, periodically during treatment (at least monthly), and when symptoms worsen
  • Immediate Alerts: Automatic flagging of high-risk responses to clinical team
  • Crisis Resources: Provide hotline numbers and resources immediately upon any concerning response
  • Human Escalation: Never rely solely on automated responses - involve human clinicians for high-risk cases
  • Location Services: Obtain consent for location tracking for emergency situations
  • 24/7 Support: Ensure crisis support is available around the clock
  • Documentation: Thorough documentation of all risk assessments and interventions

5.3 Crisis Response Systems

Effective crisis response requires coordinated systems and clear protocols.

5.3.1 Crisis Hotlines and Text Lines

988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (US):

  • 24/7 crisis support by phone or chat
  • Trained crisis counselors
  • Can dispatch mobile crisis teams or emergency services
  • Confidential and free
  • Available in English and Spanish

Crisis Text Line (US/Canada):

  • Text HOME to 741741
  • 24/7 support via text message
  • Preferred by many younger individuals
  • Average response time under 5 minutes

International Resources:

  • International Association for Suicide Prevention (IASP) maintains global directory
  • Befrienders Worldwide (befrienders.org)
  • Local emergency numbers (112 in Europe, etc.)

5.3.2 Mobile Crisis Teams

Mobile crisis teams provide in-person crisis intervention in community settings:

  • Respond to psychiatric emergencies
  • Conduct on-site risk assessment
  • Provide crisis counseling and de-escalation
  • Connect individuals to treatment
  • Can divert from emergency department or arrest
  • Available 24/7 in many communities

5.3.3 Emergency Department Care

Emergency departments are often the entry point for individuals in suicidal crisis:

  • Medical stabilization if needed (e.g., after overdose)
  • Psychiatric evaluation
  • Safety assessment
  • Determination of disposition (discharge vs. admission)
  • Safety planning before discharge
  • Linkage to outpatient care

5.4 Safety Planning

A safety plan is a prioritized list of coping strategies and sources of support that individuals can use during or before a suicidal crisis.

5.4.1 Stanley-Brown Safety Plan

The Stanley-Brown Safety Planning Intervention is an evidence-based approach consisting of six steps:

Six Steps of Safety Planning

Step 1: Warning Signs

Identify personal warning signs that a crisis may be developing:

  • Thoughts: "I'm worthless," "Nobody cares," "Things will never get better"
  • Images: Visualizing suicide method
  • Mood: Feeling hopeless, desperate, trapped
  • Behaviors: Withdrawing from others, not eating, drinking more

Step 2: Internal Coping Strategies

Things the person can do on their own to distract from suicidal thoughts:

  • Go for a walk
  • Listen to music
  • Exercise
  • Watch a favorite show
  • Take a shower
  • Use relaxation techniques

Step 3: Social Settings and People

Places to go and people to be around (without discussing suicidal feelings):

  • Coffee shop
  • Library
  • Community center
  • Religious community
  • Friend's house

Step 4: People to Ask for Help

Friends or family members who may help resolve a crisis:

  • Name, phone number, relationship
  • Multiple contacts listed in order

Step 5: Professional Contacts

Mental health professionals or agencies to contact:

  • Therapist/counselor name and number
  • Psychiatrist name and number
  • Local emergency room address
  • Crisis hotline: 988

Step 6: Means Restriction

Making the environment safer by reducing access to lethal means:

  • Secure firearms (gun locks, safe storage, temporary storage by friend/family)
  • Secure medications (give to family member, get only small supplies)
  • Remove other potential means

5.4.2 Digital Safety Planning

Digital platforms can enhance safety planning through:

  • Guided Creation: Step-by-step wizard to create comprehensive plan
  • Easy Access: Lock screen widget, home screen shortcut, always available
  • Quick-Dial Contacts: One-touch calling of support people or crisis lines
  • Coping Strategy Library: Pre-populated ideas that users can customize
  • Reminders: Periodic prompts to review and update plan
  • Sharing: Option to share plan with therapist or family
  • Emergency Button: Prominent crisis help access

5.5 Means Restriction

Reducing access to lethal means is one of the most effective suicide prevention strategies. Many suicide attempts are impulsive, and delaying access to means can save lives.

5.5.1 Firearms

Firearms are used in about half of suicide deaths in the US, with a case fatality rate over 85%.

Means Restriction Strategies:

  • Temporary storage by friend, family, or gun shop
  • Gun locks or lock boxes
  • Store ammunition separately
  • Extreme risk protection orders (where available)

Counseling Approach:

  • Normalize the conversation: "I ask all my patients about firearms"
  • Emphasize temporary nature: "Just while you're going through this difficult period"
  • Frame as suicide prevention, not gun control
  • Involve supportive family members

5.5.2 Medications

Medication overdoses are a common suicide attempt method, though less often lethal than firearms.

Reduction Strategies:

  • Provide smaller quantities more frequently
  • Have family member dispense medications
  • Use blister packs instead of bottles
  • Dispose of unused medications
  • Prescribe less lethal medications when possible

5.6 Post-Crisis Follow-Up

The period immediately following a suicidal crisis is extremely high-risk. Careful follow-up can prevent subsequent attempts.

5.6.1 Caring Contacts

Caring contacts are brief, non-demanding messages expressing care and concern, sent periodically after a crisis. Research shows they reduce suicide attempts and deaths.

Caring Contact Principles:

  • Brief (1-2 sentences)
  • Express care without demanding response
  • No therapeutic content
  • Remind of resources
  • Sent regardless of whether patient responds

Timing:

  • 1 week after crisis or discharge
  • 1 month
  • 3 months
  • 6 months
  • 12 months

Example Messages:

  • "We're thinking of you and hope you're doing well."
  • "Just wanted to check in and let you know we care."
  • "Remember, you're not alone. Crisis support is always available at 988."

5.6.2 Rapid Access to Follow-Up Care

Timely connection to outpatient care after a crisis is crucial:

  • Appointment within 48 hours of hospital discharge or crisis
  • Reduce barriers (offer teletherapy, sliding scale fees)
  • Active outreach if appointment missed
  • Care coordination between emergency services and outpatient providers

5.7 Responding to Suicide Loss

When a suicide death occurs, survivors (friends, family, community members) need support and are themselves at increased risk.

5.7.1 Postvention

Postvention refers to activities following a suicide to support survivors and prevent suicide contagion.

  • Psychological first aid for survivors
  • Support groups for suicide loss survivors
  • Risk screening for other patients (in clinical settings)
  • Responsible media coverage guidance
  • Memorialization that doesn't glorify suicide

5.7.2 Supporting Survivors

  • Provide information about grief after suicide
  • Connect to survivor support groups
  • Normalize complex emotions (guilt, anger, confusion)
  • Screen for depression and PTSD
  • Assess suicide risk in survivors

Chapter Summary

Suicide prevention requires comprehensive assessment, immediate crisis response, collaborative safety planning, means restriction, and careful follow-up. Digital platforms must implement validated assessment tools like the C-SSRS, provide 24/7 crisis support, enable easy safety plan creation and access, and ensure seamless connection to human support when needed. Post-crisis follow-up through caring contacts and rapid access to care can prevent subsequent attempts. Every interaction is an opportunity to save a life.

"Suicide is not chosen; it happens when pain exceeds resources for coping with pain." - David Shneidman
