Chapter 03

Water Management and Irrigation

3.1 Water Crisis in Agriculture

Agriculture consumes approximately 70% of global freshwater withdrawals, making water management one of the most critical challenges in sustainable agriculture. With climate change intensifying droughts, depleting aquifers, and creating more variable precipitation patterns, efficient water management is essential for both productivity and environmental sustainability.

Global Water Statistics

● 2 billion people live in water-scarce regions
● 4 billion projected to face water scarcity by 2050
● 40% of global irrigated areas face groundwater depletion
● 1kg of wheat requires 1,500-2,000 liters of water
● 20-40% of irrigation water is lost to inefficiency

3.2.2 Drip System Design Principles

  • Emitter Selection: Choose based on soil type (sandy: 1-2 L/hr closely spaced; clay: 4-8 L/hr wider spacing)
  • Lateral Spacing: Match crop row spacing; multiple laterals per row for wide crops
  • Operating Pressure: Typically 0.5-2.0 bar (7-30 psi) for optimal performance
  • Filtration: Critical to prevent clogging; filter rating depends on emitter size
  • Flushing: Regular flushing of lines to remove sediment buildup
  • Uniformity: Target >90% distribution uniformity across the field

3.3 Irrigation Scheduling

Applying the right amount of water at the right time is as important as the irrigation system itself. Over-irrigation wastes water and can leach nutrients; under-irrigation stresses crops and reduces yields.

3.3.2 Evapotranspiration (ET) Based Scheduling

ET-based scheduling calculates water needs based on crop water use, which varies with weather conditions, crop type, and growth stage. 

    EVAPOTRANSPIRATION CALCULATION

    ETc (Crop ET) = ETo × Kc

    Where:
    ETc = Crop evapotranspiration (mm/day)
    ETo = Reference evapotranspiration (mm/day)
          Calculated from weather data (temp, humidity, wind, radiation)
    Kc  = Crop coefficient (varies by crop and growth stage)

    Example: Tomatoes in Mid-Season
    ────────────────────────────────────────────────
    ETo = 6.5 mm/day (from weather station)
    Kc  = 1.15 (tomato mid-season coefficient)
    ETc = 6.5 × 1.15 = 7.48 mm/day

    Irrigation Requirement = ETc - Effective Rainfall

    If rainfall = 2 mm/day:
    Irrigation needed = 7.48 - 2.0 = 5.48 mm/day

    For 1 hectare (10,000 m²):
    Daily water need = 10,000 m² × 0.00548 m = 54.8 m³/day

    Accounting for 85% irrigation efficiency:
    Applied water = 54.8 ÷ 0.85 = 64.5 m³/day

3.4 Water Conservation Strategies

3.4.1 Soil Management for Water Retention

Healthy soil is the foundation of water conservation. Each 1% increase in soil organic matter can increase water holding capacity by approximately 25,000 gallons per acre (234,000 liters per hectare).

  • Organic Matter Addition: Compost, cover crops, and reduced tillage increase water holding capacity
  • Mulching: 5-10cm of organic mulch reduces evaporation by 20-40%
  • Reduced Tillage: Preserves soil structure and pore spaces for water storage
  • Subsoiling: Breaks compaction layers that restrict root growth and water infiltration
  • Contour Farming: Slows runoff and increases infiltration time

3.4.2 Crop Selection and Breeding

Strategy Description Water Savings Examples
Drought-Tolerant Varieties Crops bred for low water requirements 20-40% Drought-tolerant maize, sorghum, millet
Deficit Irrigation Adapted Varieties that maintain yield under limited water 15-30% Certain wheat, cotton cultivars
Deep Rooting Crops Access moisture from deeper soil layers 10-25% Alfalfa, sunflower, safflower
Early Maturing Varieties Complete lifecycle before peak drought period 20-35% Short-season beans, early corn hybrids

3.4.3 Deficit Irrigation Strategies

Deficit irrigation intentionally applies less water than full crop requirements, either throughout the season or during specific growth stages less sensitive to water stress.

Regulated Deficit Irrigation (RDI)

RDI applies full irrigation during critical growth stages (flowering, fruit set) and reduced irrigation during less sensitive periods. In orchards, RDI can reduce water use by 25-40% while maintaining or even improving fruit quality (higher sugar content, better color) with minimal yield impact.

3.5.2 Pond Design Calculations

    FARM POND SIZING CALCULATION

    Step 1: Determine Water Demand
    ────────────────────────────────────────────────
    Irrigated area: 2 hectares
    Crop: Vegetables
    Peak ET rate: 6 mm/day
    Irrigation efficiency: 80%
    Growing season: 120 days

    Daily demand = 20,000 m² × 0.006 m ÷ 0.80
                 = 150 m³/day

    Seasonal demand = 150 m³/day × 120 days
                    = 18,000 m³

    Step 2: Estimate Rainfall Capture
    ────────────────────────────────────────────────
    Catchment area: 5 hectares (includes farm roofs, roads)
    Annual rainfall: 800 mm
    Runoff coefficient: 0.6 (accounts for infiltration, evaporation)

    Annual capture = 50,000 m² × 0.8 m × 0.6
                   = 24,000 m³

    Step 3: Size Storage
    ────────────────────────────────────────────────
    Consider:
    - Dry period duration: 60 days
    - Safety factor: 1.2
    - Evaporation from pond: 15%

    Storage needed = (150 m³/day × 60 days × 1.2) ÷ 0.85
                   = 12,700 m³

    Step 4: Pond Dimensions
    ────────────────────────────────────────────────
    Target storage: 13,000 m³
    Depth: 4 meters (allows for siltation, freeboard)
    Side slope: 2:1 (horizontal:vertical)

    Using trapezoidal prism formula:
    Surface area needed ≈ 3,500 m²
    Approximate dimensions: 70m × 50m × 4m deep

    Cost estimate: $40,000-60,000 (excavation, lining optional)

3.6 Water Quality Management

Water quality affects both irrigation system performance and crop health. Poor quality water can clog emitters, damage crops, and degrade soils.

3.6.1 Water Quality Parameters

Parameter Concern Acceptable Range Problematic Level Management
Salinity (EC) Crop stress, soil sodification <0.7 dS/m >3.0 dS/m Leaching fraction, salt-tolerant crops
Sodium (SAR) Soil structure degradation <3 >9 Gypsum application, drainage
pH Nutrient availability, emitter clogging 6.5-8.0 <5.5 or >8.5 Acidification or alkalization
Suspended Solids Emitter clogging <50 mg/L >100 mg/L Filtration, settling basins
Bacterial Growth Biofilm formation, clogging <10,000 CFU/mL >50,000 CFU/mL Chlorination, UV treatment

3.6.2 Filtration Requirements

  • Screen Filters: Remove debris; 20-200 mesh; clean manually or automatically; $100-2,000
  • Disc Filters: Effective for organic matter; 50-200 mesh; easy maintenance; $200-3,000
  • Media Filters: Sand or gravel; best for high sediment loads; backflush required; $1,000-10,000
  • Combination Systems: Screen + media for comprehensive filtration; $2,000-15,000

3.7 Fertigation

Fertigation—applying fertilizers through irrigation systems—improves nutrient use efficiency, reduces labor, and allows precise timing and placement.

3.7.1 Fertigation Benefits and Considerations

Fertigation Advantages

  • Nutrient use efficiency increased by 20-50% vs. broadcast application
  • Labor savings of 40-70% compared to manual application
  • Precise timing matches crop nutrient demand
  • Reduced soil compaction (no heavy equipment passes)
  • Uniform distribution across the field
  • Ability to apply frequent small doses (spoon-feeding)

3.8 Smart Irrigation Technologies

Digital technologies and precision agriculture tools are revolutionizing irrigation management, enabling farmers to optimize water use with unprecedented accuracy.

3.8.1 Sensor-Based Automation

  • Soil Moisture Sensors: Trigger irrigation when soil moisture drops below threshold; $50-800 per sensor
  • Weather Stations: Calculate real-time ET for precise scheduling; $500-5,000
  • Flow Meters: Monitor water usage and detect leaks; $100-2,000
  • Pressure Sensors: Ensure optimal system performance; $50-500
  • Rain Sensors: Shut off irrigation during rainfall; $20-200

3.9 Practical Implementation Roadmap

Phase 1: Assessment (Months 1-2)

  • Conduct water audit: measure current usage, efficiency, costs
  • Test water quality: salinity, pH, suspended solids
  • Evaluate existing irrigation infrastructure
  • Assess soil water holding capacity across fields
  • Document crop water requirements and schedules

Phase 2: Low-Cost Improvements (Months 3-6)

  • Repair leaks and replace worn components
  • Install flow meters to track water use
  • Implement basic irrigation scheduling (weather-based or soil feel method)
  • Begin soil health improvements (mulching, organic matter addition)
  • Install rain sensors to prevent unnecessary irrigation

Phase 3: System Upgrades (Months 6-18)

  • Upgrade to more efficient irrigation system where ROI justifies
  • Install soil moisture sensors in representative field locations
  • Implement fertigation system for nutrient efficiency
  • Construct rainwater harvesting infrastructure
  • Install filtration system appropriate for water quality

Phase 4: Precision Management (Months 18+)

  • Implement automated irrigation control based on sensors
  • Adopt variable rate irrigation for within-field variation
  • Integrate remote monitoring and control capabilities
  • Use satellite or drone imagery for irrigation management
  • Continuously refine practices based on data analysis

Success Story: Vineyard Water Efficiency

A 50-hectare vineyard in California transitioned from flood irrigation to subsurface drip with soil moisture sensors and weather-based scheduling. Results over 3 years: water use reduced by 45% (from 8,000 m³/ha to 4,400 m³/ha), wine grape quality improved (higher Brix levels), labor costs decreased by 60%, and fertilizer use efficiency increased by 40%. The system paid for itself in 4 years through water and labor savings alone, not counting quality improvements.

3.10 Key Takeaways

Efficient water management is not just an environmental imperative—it's an economic opportunity. By investing in appropriate irrigation technologies, monitoring tools, and management practices, farmers can reduce water use, lower costs, improve crop quality, and build resilience to climate variability. The key principles include:

  • Match irrigation system to crop, soil, and economic conditions
  • Monitor soil moisture and weather to schedule irrigation precisely
  • Maintain and optimize existing systems before major investments
  • Improve soil health to maximize natural water storage
  • Capture and utilize rainwater where feasible
  • Embrace technology for precision and automation
  • Regularly assess and adapt practices based on results

Water is the lifeblood of agriculture. Managing it sustainably ensures productive farms today and fertile land for generations to come.
