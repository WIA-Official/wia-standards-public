WIA-ENE-003 Carbon Capture & Storage Standard

1.1 Welcome to Carbon Capture Technology

Carbon capture and storage (CCS) technology represents one of humanity's most critical tools in the fight against climate change. As we continue to rely on fossil fuels for energy production and industrial processes, the ability to capture carbon dioxide (CO₂) emissions before they enter the atmosphere has become increasingly vital.

The WIA-ENE-003 standard provides a comprehensive framework for implementing, operating, and certifying carbon capture systems worldwide. This standard embodies the philosophy of 弘益人間 (홍익인간) - "Benefit All Humanity" - by ensuring that carbon capture technology is accessible, effective, and economically viable for organizations of all sizes.

What is Carbon Capture?

Carbon capture is the process of capturing carbon dioxide (CO₂) from large point sources, such as power plants and industrial facilities, before it is released into the atmosphere. The captured CO₂ is then transported and stored in geological formations or utilized in various industrial applications.

1.2 The Climate Challenge

Global carbon dioxide concentrations have risen from approximately 280 parts per million (ppm) in pre-industrial times to over 420 ppm today. This increase is directly linked to rising global temperatures, changing weather patterns, and increasing frequency of extreme weather events.

1.3 How Carbon Capture Works

Carbon capture technology operates through three fundamental stages: capture, transport, and storage. Each stage requires careful engineering, monitoring, and compliance with safety and environmental standards.

Stage 1: Capture

The capture stage involves separating CO₂ from other gases produced in industrial processes or power generation. There are three primary capture methods:

Capture Method Description Efficiency Best Applications
Post-Combustion Captures CO₂ from flue gases after fuel combustion 85-90% Existing power plants, cement factories
Pre-Combustion Removes CO₂ before combustion process 90-95% Integrated gasification combined cycle (IGCC)
Oxy-fuel Combustion Burns fuel in pure oxygen to produce concentrated CO₂ 95-98% New power plants, industrial furnaces
Direct Air Capture Captures CO₂ directly from ambient air 75-80% Atmospheric CO₂ removal, negative emissions

Stage 2: Transport

Once captured, CO₂ must be transported to storage sites. The captured CO₂ is typically compressed to a supercritical state (typically above 31°C and 7.4 MPa) to reduce volume and facilitate transport through pipelines, similar to natural gas distribution.

Stage 3: Storage

The final stage involves injecting CO₂ into deep geological formations for permanent storage. Suitable storage sites include depleted oil and gas reservoirs, deep saline aquifers, and basalt formations. These sites must be carefully selected and monitored to ensure long-term containment and prevent leakage.

1.4 Types of Carbon Capture Technologies

Chemical Absorption

Chemical absorption is the most mature and widely deployed carbon capture technology. It uses liquid solvents (typically amines) to chemically bind with CO₂, separating it from other gases. The CO₂-rich solvent is then heated to release pure CO₂ for compression and storage.

Chemical Absorption Process: 1. Flue Gas + Amine Solvent → CO₂-Rich Solvent 2. Heat Application → Pure CO₂ + Regenerated Solvent 3. CO₂ Compression → Supercritical CO₂ (31°C, 7.4+ MPa) 4. Transport → Geological Storage Site Common Solvents: - Monoethanolamine (MEA): 30% solution, 85-90% capture efficiency - Piperazine (PZ): Promoter, increases capture rate by 30% - Advanced amines: Reduced energy consumption, higher efficiency

Physical Adsorption

Physical adsorption uses solid materials with high surface areas (such as activated carbon, zeolites, or metal-organic frameworks) to physically adsorb CO₂ molecules. This method is particularly effective for high-pressure, high-CO₂ concentration streams.

Membrane Separation

Membrane technology uses semi-permeable materials that allow CO₂ to pass through while blocking other gases. This method offers lower energy consumption compared to chemical absorption but is currently limited by membrane durability and selectivity.

Cryogenic Separation

Cryogenic separation cools flue gases to very low temperatures, causing CO₂ to condense and separate from other gases. This method is energy-intensive but produces very pure CO₂ streams, making it suitable for applications requiring high-purity CO₂.

1.6 Benefits of Carbon Capture

Environmental Benefits

  • Reduces atmospheric CO₂ concentrations by up to 90% from point sources
  • Enables continued use of existing industrial infrastructure while reducing emissions
  • Supports achievement of net-zero emissions targets
  • Reduces ocean acidification by preventing CO₂ from entering the atmosphere
  • Complements renewable energy deployment for hard-to-decarbonize sectors

Economic Benefits

  • Creates new jobs in engineering, construction, and operations (estimated 100,000+ jobs globally)
  • Generates revenue through carbon credits and enhanced oil recovery (EOR)
  • Extends the operational life of existing power plants and industrial facilities
  • Develops new supply chains for CO₂ transport and storage services
  • Reduces costs associated with carbon taxes and emissions penalties

Social Benefits

  • Improves air quality by reducing overall emissions from industrial facilities
  • Demonstrates corporate commitment to climate action and sustainability
  • Provides energy security by enabling cleaner use of domestic fossil fuel resources
  • Supports energy-intensive industries critical for economic development
  • Contributes to global climate goals and international cooperation

1.7 Challenges and Limitations

While carbon capture technology offers tremendous potential, several challenges must be addressed for widespread deployment:

Challenge Impact Mitigation Strategy
High Capital Costs $50-100 per tonne CO₂ captured Government incentives, technological innovation, economies of scale
Energy Penalty 15-30% reduction in plant efficiency Advanced solvents, waste heat recovery, process optimization
Storage Site Availability Limited geological formations Comprehensive geological surveys, international cooperation
Public Perception Concerns about safety and effectiveness Transparent monitoring, community engagement, education
Regulatory Uncertainty Varying standards across jurisdictions International standards (like WIA-ENE-003), policy harmonization

1.8 The Role of WIA-ENE-003 Standard

The WIA-ENE-003 standard addresses many of these challenges by providing:

  1. Unified Technical Specifications: Standardized methods for measuring, reporting, and verifying carbon capture performance across different technologies and regions.
  2. Safety and Environmental Protocols: Comprehensive guidelines for ensuring the safe operation of capture, transport, and storage systems.
  3. Certification Framework: Clear criteria for certifying carbon capture facilities and validating captured CO₂ for carbon credit markets.
  4. Interoperability Standards: APIs and data formats that enable seamless integration with existing industrial control systems and carbon accounting platforms.
  5. Best Practice Guidelines: Lessons learned from existing facilities to optimize performance and reduce costs.
  6. Monitoring and Verification: Standardized protocols for long-term monitoring of storage sites and verification of permanent CO₂ sequestration.

Standard Development Process

WIA-ENE-003 was developed through collaboration with leading research institutions, industrial operators, environmental organizations, and government agencies across 25 countries. The standard underwent rigorous peer review and field testing at 15 pilot facilities before final publication.

1.9 Future Outlook

The future of carbon capture technology is promising, with several exciting developments on the horizon:

Technological Advances

  • Next-generation solvents with 50% lower energy requirements
  • Advanced materials for membrane separation with 10x higher CO₂ permeability
  • Direct air capture systems with costs below $100 per tonne
  • Integration with renewable energy for zero-carbon capture operations
  • CO₂ utilization technologies converting captured CO₂ into valuable products

Market Growth

The global carbon capture market is projected to grow from $3 billion in 2025 to over $50 billion by 2035, driven by:

  • Strengthening carbon pricing mechanisms (currently averaging $40-60 per tonne)
  • Government mandates for industrial emission reductions
  • Corporate net-zero commitments from Fortune 500 companies
  • Growing demand for carbon removal credits in voluntary markets
  • International climate agreements with specific CCS deployment targets

Policy Support

Governments worldwide are implementing supportive policies, including:

  • Tax credits for carbon capture (e.g., US 45Q tax credit: $85 per tonne for geological storage)
  • Low-carbon fuel standards requiring CCS in refineries
  • Public funding for CCS infrastructure development ($20+ billion committed globally)
  • Streamlined permitting processes for storage site development
  • International cooperation on cross-border CO₂ transport and storage
