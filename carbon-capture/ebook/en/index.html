WIA-CARBON_CAPTURE: Carbon Capture Technology

Standard Overview: WIA-CARBON_CAPTURE defines comprehensive protocols and specifications for carbon capture, utilization, and storage (CCUS) technologies. This standard addresses the critical need for carbon dioxide removal to mitigate climate change and achieve global net-zero emissions targets.

The Climate Change Imperative

Climate change represents one of the most significant challenges facing humanity in the 21st century. The concentration of carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere has risen from approximately 280 parts per million (ppm) during pre-industrial times to over 420 ppm today. This unprecedented increase in greenhouse gas concentrations is driving global temperature rise, extreme weather events, sea-level rise, and ecosystem disruption.

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) has made it clear that limiting global warming to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels requires not only drastic reductions in emissions but also the active removal of carbon dioxide from the atmosphere. Carbon capture technology plays a vital role in this global effort, providing solutions for both point-source emissions and atmospheric carbon removal.

Understanding Carbon Capture

Carbon capture technology encompasses a range of processes designed to capture carbon dioxide from various sources before it enters the atmosphere, or to remove CO2 directly from ambient air. The captured carbon can then be stored permanently underground, utilized in industrial processes, or converted into valuable products.

Economic Importance of Carbon Capture

Carbon capture technology is not only an environmental necessity but also presents significant economic opportunities. The emerging carbon capture industry is expected to grow into a multi-trillion dollar market by 2050. Early investment in CCUS technology positions companies and nations to benefit from carbon credits, enhanced oil recovery revenue, and the production of carbon-derived products.

Economic Impact: The International Energy Agency (IEA) estimates that carbon capture could become a $150 billion annual industry by 2035, creating millions of jobs worldwide while addressing climate change.
