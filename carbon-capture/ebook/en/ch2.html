WIA-ENE-003 Carbon Capture & Storage Standard

2.1 Fundamental Terminology

Understanding the terminology used in carbon capture and storage is essential for effective communication and implementation. This section defines the core concepts that form the foundation of the WIA-ENE-003 standard.

Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS)

Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS)
A process consisting of the separation of CO₂ from industrial and energy-related sources, transport to a storage location, and long-term isolation from the atmosphere. CCS encompasses the entire chain from capture through storage and includes monitoring, verification, and accounting.

Key Performance Indicators

Term Definition Unit Typical Range
Capture Rate Percentage of CO₂ captured from source emissions % 85-98%
Capture Efficiency Actual capture vs. theoretical maximum % 90-99%
Energy Penalty Additional energy required for capture process % 15-30%
Specific Energy Consumption Energy per tonne of CO₂ captured GJ/tonne 2.5-4.5
CO₂ Purity Concentration of CO₂ in captured stream % 95-99.9%
Storage Efficiency Percentage of injected CO₂ retained in formation % 99-99.9%

2.2 Capture Technologies and Mechanisms

Post-Combustion Capture

Post-combustion capture separates CO₂ from flue gases produced by combustion of fossil fuels or biomass. This is the most mature technology and can be retrofitted to existing power plants and industrial facilities.

Process Flow - Post-Combustion Capture: 1. Flue Gas Input (10-15% CO₂, N₂, O₂, H₂O) ↓ 2. Gas Conditioning (Cooling, Particulate Removal) ↓ 3. Absorption Column (Contact with Amine Solvent) ↓ 4. CO₂-Rich Solvent → Stripper Column ↓ 5. Heat Application (100-120°C) ↓ 6. Pure CO₂ Stream (>95% purity) + Regenerated Solvent ↓ 7. CO₂ Compression (7.4+ MPa) ↓ 8. Transport & Storage Key Parameters: - Solvent: 30% MEA or advanced amine blend - Absorption Temperature: 40-60°C - Regeneration Temperature: 100-120°C - Capture Rate: 85-90% - Energy Requirement: 3.5-4.5 GJ/tonne CO₂

Pre-Combustion Capture

Pre-combustion capture involves processing fuel before combustion to produce a synthesis gas (syngas) composed of hydrogen and CO₂. The CO₂ is separated before combustion of the hydrogen-rich fuel.

Integrated Gasification Combined Cycle (IGCC)
A power generation technology that gasifies solid or liquid fuels to produce syngas, which is then used to generate electricity. CO₂ can be separated from the syngas before combustion, achieving 90-95% capture efficiency with lower energy penalties compared to post-combustion capture.

Oxy-fuel Combustion

Oxy-fuel combustion burns fuel in nearly pure oxygen instead of air, producing a flue gas that is primarily CO₂ and water vapor. After water condensation, a high-purity CO₂ stream is obtained with minimal additional processing.

Parameter Air Combustion Oxy-fuel Combustion Improvement
CO₂ Concentration in Flue Gas 10-15% 70-90% 6x increase
Separation Energy 3.5-4.5 GJ/tonne 2.0-2.5 GJ/tonne 40-50% reduction
CO₂ Purity 95-97% 98-99.9% Higher purity
Capture Rate 85-90% 95-98% 8-10% improvement

2.3 Transport and Storage Concepts

CO₂ Transport Methods

Once captured, CO₂ must be transported to storage or utilization sites. The choice of transport method depends on distance, volume, terrain, and infrastructure availability.

Transport Method State Pressure Best For Cost Range
Pipeline Supercritical 8-15 MPa Large volumes, short-medium distances $1-5 per tonne per 100km
Ship Liquid 0.7-2 MPa Long distances, offshore storage $10-30 per tonne
Truck/Rail Liquid 1.5-2.5 MPa Small volumes, flexible routing $20-50 per tonne

Geological Storage Types

Geological storage involves injecting CO₂ into deep underground rock formations where it is trapped and mineralized over time. Four primary storage types are recognized:

Depleted Oil and Gas Reservoirs
Previously productive hydrocarbon reservoirs with proven containment capability. Advantages: Well-characterized geology, existing infrastructure, potential for enhanced oil recovery (EOR). Typical depth: 800-3,000 meters. Storage capacity: 675-900 Gt CO₂ globally.
Deep Saline Aquifers
Porous rock formations saturated with brine (salt water) unsuitable for drinking or agriculture. Advantages: Largest storage potential, widely distributed geographically. Typical depth: 800-2,500 meters. Storage capacity: 10,000+ Gt CO₂ globally.
Unmineable Coal Seams
Deep coal deposits that cannot be economically mined. CO₂ is adsorbed onto the coal surface, potentially displacing methane (coalbed methane recovery). Typical depth: 800-1,500 meters. Storage capacity: 3-15 Gt CO₂ globally.
Basalt Formations
Volcanic rock formations where CO₂ reacts with minerals to form stable carbonates through mineralization. Advantages: Permanent storage through chemical reaction, rapid mineralization (2-5 years). Typical depth: 500-2,000 meters. Storage capacity: 25,000+ Gt CO₂ globally.

2.4 CO₂ Trapping Mechanisms

Once injected into geological formations, CO₂ is retained through multiple trapping mechanisms that provide increasing security over time:

Trapping Mechanism Time Scale Effectiveness Description
Physical Trapping Immediate 99%+ CO₂ trapped beneath impermeable cap rock layers
Residual Trapping Years 99.5%+ CO₂ immobilized as disconnected bubbles in pore spaces
Solubility Trapping Decades 99.9%+ CO₂ dissolves in formation water, increasing density
Mineral Trapping Centuries 100% CO₂ reacts with minerals to form stable carbonates
CO₂ Trapping Timeline: Year 0: Injection ├─ Physical Trapping: 100% (Cap rock containment) │ Year 1-10: Migration Period ├─ Physical Trapping: 95% ├─ Residual Trapping: 5% │ Year 10-100: Stabilization ├─ Physical Trapping: 60% ├─ Residual Trapping: 25% ├─ Solubility Trapping: 15% │ Year 100-1000: Long-term Storage ├─ Residual Trapping: 30% ├─ Solubility Trapping: 40% ├─ Mineral Trapping: 30% │ Year 1000+: Permanent Storage └─ Mineral Trapping: 100%

2.5 Monitoring and Verification Concepts

Monitoring Technologies

Continuous monitoring of CO₂ storage sites is essential to ensure containment and detect any potential leakage. The WIA-ENE-003 standard requires multiple monitoring technologies deployed in a comprehensive monitoring plan.

Technology What It Measures Detection Limit Frequency
Seismic Monitoring CO₂ plume migration, pressure changes ±5 meters Annual surveys
Pressure/Temperature Sensors Reservoir conditions, injection performance ±0.1 MPa, ±0.5°C Continuous (real-time)

Surface Monitoring Atmospheric CO₂, soil gas 10 ppm above background Continuous

Well Integrity Monitoring Cement integrity, casing condition 0.1 mm defects Annual inspections

2.6 Economic and Financial Terminology

Cost Components

Understanding the economics of carbon capture requires familiarity with specific cost terminology and metrics:

Cost Component Description Typical Range % of Total
Capital Expenditure (CAPEX) Initial investment for equipment and construction $500-1,500M for 1 Mt/year facility 60-70%
Operating Expenditure (OPEX) Annual costs for operations and maintenance $20-50M per year 30-40%
Levelized Cost of Capture Total cost per tonne CO₂ over project lifetime $40-120 per tonne -
Transport Cost Pipeline, ship, or truck transport $5-30 per tonne 5-15%
Storage Cost Injection and long-term monitoring $10-25 per tonne 10-20%

Revenue Streams

Carbon Credits
Tradable certificates representing the capture and storage of one tonne of CO₂. Can be sold in compliance markets (regulatory requirement) or voluntary markets (corporate sustainability). Current prices: $20-80 per tonne (compliance), $10-50 per tonne (voluntary).
Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR)
Injection of CO₂ into oil reservoirs to increase oil production. Revenue from additional oil production can offset 30-50% of carbon capture costs. Typical payment: $15-30 per tonne CO₂ delivered.
Tax Credits and Incentives
Government incentives to encourage carbon capture deployment. Examples: US 45Q tax credit ($85 per tonne for geological storage, $60 per tonne for utilization), EU Innovation Fund grants (up to 60% CAPEX coverage).

Safety Classifications

CO₂ safety is categorized according to concentration and exposure duration:

CO₂ Safety Levels (Atmospheric Concentration): Normal Atmosphere: 400-420 ppm (0.04%) ├─ Status: Safe, typical outdoor air Indoor Air Quality Concern: 1,000-2,000 ppm (0.1-0.2%) ├─ Effects: Drowsiness, reduced concentration ├─ Action: Improve ventilation Occupational Exposure Limit (8-hour TWA): 5,000 ppm (0.5%) ├─ Effects: Breathing rate increases ├─ Action: Continuous monitoring required Short-term Exposure Limit (15-min): 30,000 ppm (3%) ├─ Effects: Headache, dizziness, increased heart rate ├─ Action: Evacuation procedures Dangerous: 40,000-50,000 ppm (4-5%) ├─ Effects: Confusion, vision impairment ├─ Action: Immediate evacuation, emergency response Immediately Dangerous to Life: >50,000 ppm (>5%) ├─ Effects: Loss of consciousness, asphyxiation ├─ Action: Rescue operations with breathing apparatus Safety Measures: - Continuous atmospheric monitoring - Ventilation systems - Emergency alarms - Personal CO₂ detectors - Emergency response training
