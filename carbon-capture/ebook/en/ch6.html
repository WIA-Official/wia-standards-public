WIA-ENE-003 Carbon Capture & Storage Standard

6.1 Regulatory Compliance Framework

Carbon capture and storage facilities must comply with comprehensive regulatory requirements spanning environmental protection, occupational safety, geological storage, and carbon accounting. The WIA-ENE-003 standard provides a framework for achieving and maintaining compliance across all jurisdictions.

6.2 Environmental Compliance

Air Emissions Management

Carbon capture facilities must manage various air emissions beyond CO₂, including solvent emissions, combustion byproducts, and fugitive releases.

Pollutant Source Typical Limit Control Technology
Amine Slip Absorber vent <1 ppm Water wash column
Ammonia (NH₃) Solvent degradation <10 ppm Acid scrubber
VOCs Fugitive leaks LDAR program, <500 ppm at components Leak detection and repair
Particulate Matter Auxiliary equipment <20 mg/Nm³ Filters, separators
NOx/SOx Auxiliary boilers (if any) Jurisdiction-specific Low-NOx burners, SCR

Water Management and Discharge

Wastewater Treatment Requirements

Sources of Wastewater:

  • Condensate from CO₂ compression (50-100 m³/day for 1 Mt/year facility)
  • Water wash blowdown (20-40 m³/day)
  • Cooling tower blowdown (if cooling water system used)
  • Equipment washdown and maintenance water
  • Stormwater runoff from process areas

Treatment Requirements (typical):

  • pH adjustment to 6-9
  • Amine removal (biological treatment or stripping) to <1 mg/L
  • COD reduction to <100 mg/L
  • Oil and grease removal to <10 mg/L
  • Heavy metals removal if present

Discharge Options:

  • Municipal wastewater treatment plant (most common)
  • Direct discharge to surface water (requires NPDES permit in US)
  • Deep well injection (if geological conditions suitable)
  • Zero liquid discharge with evaporation/crystallization

6.4 Geological Storage Compliance

Underground Injection Control (UIC) Class VI Requirements

In the United States, CO₂ injection for geological storage is regulated under EPA's Underground Injection Control Program, specifically Class VI wells designed for long-term storage.

Class VI Permit Application Requirements

Site Characterization:

  • 3D seismic survey of injection zone and confining layers
  • Characterization well drilling and testing (minimum 1, typically 2-4 wells)
  • Core analysis: porosity, permeability, mineralogy, geochemistry
  • Formation fluid sampling and analysis
  • Geomechanical testing and fracture pressure determination
  • Historical oil/gas production data review (if depleted reservoir)

Area of Review (AoR) Delineation:

  • Computational modeling to predict CO₂ plume migration
  • Identify all penetrations (wells, faults) within AoR
  • Corrective action plan for wells with inadequate integrity
  • Reevaluation of AoR every 5 years or when significant changes occur

Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification Plan:

  • Continuous monitoring: injection pressure, rate, volume, annulus pressure
  • Periodic monitoring: seismic surveys, groundwater sampling, surface deformation
  • Quarterly reporting to EPA
  • Annual MRV report submitted to carbon registry (if seeking credits)

Financial Assurance:

  • Demonstrate financial resources for closure and post-closure care (50 years)
  • Typical instruments: surety bond, trust fund, insurance, letter of credit
  • Amount: $5-50 million depending on site complexity and scale

CO₂ Stored Injection flow totalizer Custody transfer meter (±0.3%) ±0.5%

Third-Party Verification Process

VERIFICATION PROCESS - ISO 14064-3: STEP 1: VERIFICATION PLANNING (Month 1) ├─ Select accredited verification body ├─ Define verification scope and criteria ├─ Risk assessment and materiality threshold (typically 5%) └─ Develop verification plan STEP 2: DOCUMENT REVIEW (Month 2-3) ├─ Review GHG assertion and supporting documentation ├─ Assess data management systems and controls ├─ Identify areas requiring detailed testing └─ Preliminary findings communicated STEP 3: SITE VISIT AND DATA VALIDATION (Month 4) ├─ Interview facility personnel ├─ Inspect monitoring equipment and calibration records ├─ Review operational logs and calculations ├─ Sample test calculations (typically 10% of data) └─ Assess quality assurance/quality control procedures STEP 4: EVALUATION AND CONCLUSION (Month 5) ├─ Evaluate findings against materiality threshold ├─ Request corrective actions if needed ├─ Determine verification opinion: │ ├─ Reasonable assurance (most stringent, <5% error) │ ├─ Limited assurance (less stringent, <10% error) │ └─ Qualified or adverse opinion (if material misstatements) └─ Issue verification statement STEP 5: CERTIFICATION AND REGISTRATION (Month 6) ├─ Submit verified GHG statement to carbon registry ├─ Carbon credits issued (typically 1 credit = 1 tonne CO₂) ├─ Credits listed in registry account └─ Available for sale or retirement ANNUAL COST: $50,000-200,000 depending on facility complexity

6.6 Data Security and Cybersecurity

Industrial Control System (ICS) Security

Carbon capture facilities rely on extensive SCADA and control systems, making cybersecurity critical to operational safety and reliability.

ICS Cybersecurity Framework (IEC 62443)

Network Segmentation:

  • Level 0 (Field Devices): Sensors, actuators - physically secured
  • Level 1 (Control Layer): PLCs, DCS - isolated network
  • Level 2 (Supervisory): HMI, SCADA - DMZ with firewall
  • Level 3 (Enterprise): Business systems - separate network
  • No direct internet connection to control systems

Access Control:

  • Role-based access control (RBAC) for all systems
  • Multi-factor authentication for remote access
  • Individual user accounts (no shared passwords)
  • Access logs maintained and reviewed monthly
  • Quarterly access rights review and update

Security Monitoring:

  • Intrusion detection systems on all network segments
  • Security Information and Event Management (SIEM)
  • 24/7 security monitoring (or managed security service)
  • Incident response plan with defined escalation procedures

Patch Management:

  • Risk-based prioritization of security patches
  • Testing in non-production environment before deployment
  • Critical patches applied within 30 days
  • Quarterly patch review and planning

6.8 Insurance and Liability Management

Risk Transfer Strategies

Recommended Insurance Coverage

Construction Phase:

  • Builder's risk insurance (replacement value coverage)
  • Professional liability (design errors and omissions)
  • Contractor's general liability ($10-20M minimum)
  • Environmental impairment liability

Operations Phase:

  • General liability ($50-100M for major facility)
  • Property insurance (replacement value, business interruption)
  • Pollution liability ($25-50M per occurrence)
  • Underground injection liability (if required)
  • Directors and officers liability
  • Cyber liability ($5-10M)

Long-term Storage:

  • Stored CO₂ liability insurance or financial assurance
  • Typically structured as trust fund or surety bond
  • Covers monitoring and remediation for 50+ years post-closure
  • Amount determined by regulatory requirements ($10-100M typical)

6.9 Audit and Inspection Preparedness

Regulatory Audit Readiness Checklist

AUDIT PREPARATION - 30 DAYS BEFORE INSPECTION: WEEK 1: DOCUMENTATION REVIEW □ Compile all required permits and licenses □ Review monitoring data for completeness and accuracy □ Verify all required reports submitted on time □ Check calibration records for monitoring equipment □ Review training records for all employees □ Prepare summary of any deviations or exceedances WEEK 2: PHYSICAL INSPECTION PREPARATION □ Conduct internal walkthrough with checklist □ Verify all required signage and labeling in place □ Check safety equipment (eyewash, showers, fire extinguishers) □ Ensure housekeeping meets standards □ Test emergency alarms and communication systems □ Verify lockout/tagout compliance WEEK 3: PERSONNEL PREPARATION □ Brief all employees on audit process and expectations □ Designate audit coordinator and subject matter experts □ Prepare site map and facility orientation materials □ Schedule availability of key personnel □ Arrange for conference room and facilities for inspectors □ Prepare questions and answer document WEEK 4: FINAL READINESS □ Management review of audit preparation □ Mock audit with internal team □ Address any identified gaps □ Confirm audit logistics (date, time, attendees) □ Prepare opening presentation for inspectors DURING AUDIT: ✓ Designate escort for inspectors at all times ✓ Take detailed notes of all inspector observations ✓ Answer questions honestly and directly ✓ If answer unknown, commit to follow-up timeline ✓ Do not volunteer information beyond questions asked ✓ Request clarification if question unclear ✓ Coordinate all communications through audit coordinator POST-AUDIT: ✓ Debrief with team on findings ✓ Develop corrective action plan for any deficiencies ✓ Implement corrections promptly (typically 30-90 days) ✓ Document all actions taken ✓ Respond formally to inspection report ✓ Update procedures to prevent recurrence
