7.1 Quality Assurance Framework and Philosophy

Robust quality assurance (QA) and quality control (QC) systems ensure data reliability, repeatability, and comparability across laboratories and time periods. The WIA-MICROPLASTIC_DETECTION standard specifies comprehensive QA/QC procedures covering method validation, blank analysis, reference materials, proficiency testing, instrument maintenance, and documentation. The QA philosophy emphasizes prevention over detection—establishing rigorous systems to prevent quality problems occurring, rather than identifying problems after they occur.

Core QA Principles:

  • Standardization: All procedures must follow documented standard operating procedures (SOPs) with minimal deviation
  • Documentation: Complete record-keeping enables reproducibility and troubleshooting
  • Verification: Independent verification of results through blanks, replicates, and standards
  • Traceability: All measurements traceable to established standards and references
  • Continuous Improvement: Regular performance assessment and systematic corrective action
  • Training: All personnel must receive comprehensive training and maintain competency

7.2 Method Validation and Verification

Before routine analysis, each laboratory must validate all methods for accuracy, precision, sensitivity, and selectivity. Validation studies involve analysis of spiked samples, certified reference materials, and inter-laboratory comparison materials. Documentation of validation parameters forms the basis for QA/QC criteria during routine analysis.

7.3 Blank Analysis and Contamination Control

The WIA standard specifies three types of blanks that must be analyzed with every sample batch to document contamination at different process stages: Type 1: Procedural Blanks - Processed identically to field samples but without environmental material. These quantify contamination introduced during extraction, filtration, and spectroscopic analysis in the laboratory. Required: minimum 3 per sample batch. Acceptance criterion: < 5% of sample particle count (or < 10% for challenging samples).

Type 2: Field Blanks - Unexposed sampling equipment taken to field sites and handled identically to actual samples, then processed through full analytical pipeline. These detect contamination from handling, transport, storage, and field environment. Required: minimum 1 per sampling event per site. Field blanks typically show higher contamination than procedural blanks (2-10x higher) due to atmospheric deposition and exposure during fieldwork.

Type 3: Equipment Blanks - Analysis of clean filters, glassware, and reagents without actual sample analysis. These isolate laboratory contamination sources from sampling contamination. Required: analyze at end of each working day and every 10 samples. Equipment blanks detect contamination from lab air, water, chemicals, and equipment.

Blank results are tracked on control charts (temporal plots) to identify trends in contamination levels. Acceptable variation in blanks: < 2x average level. If blanks exceed 2x average, investigate source and implement corrective action before resuming routine analysis.

7.4 Reference Materials and Standards

Certified reference materials (CRMs) from suppliers like NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology), LGC Proficiency Testing, or specialized microplastic manufacturers enable method validation and inter-laboratory comparisons. CRMs provide known quantities of specific polymer types with certified size distributions and purity levels.

The WIA standard recommends using at least three different polymer types as CRMs, with different particle sizes to span the analytically relevant range (1-10 μm, 10-100 μm, 100-1000 μm). A typical CRM validation set includes: PE (polyethylene) in three size fractions, PP (polypropylene) in three fractions, and PS (polystyrene) in three fractions. This covers ~60% of environmental microplastic composition while providing reasonable cost for routine validation.

CRM usage: add known quantity (typically 10-100 particles) to procedural blank, process identically to sample, and verify recovery. Acceptable recovery: 70-120% for routine QC, 80-120% for method validation. Results tracked on control charts to monitor performance over time. Lot-to-lot variation in CRM properties requires regular re-validation when using new batches.

7.5 Proficiency Testing and Inter-laboratory Comparisons

Participation in external proficiency testing (PT) programs enables independent assessment of laboratory performance and identification of systematic biases. The WIA protocol recommends participation in formal PT schemes such as: - FAPAS (Food Analysis Performance Assessment Scheme) microplastic tests - LGC Proficiency Testing microplastic programs - Specialized microplastic PT schemes (various research networks)

Typically, PT programs distribute unknown samples to participating laboratories quarterly or annually. Laboratories analyze samples using their routine methods, report results, and compare to consensus values. Results showing laboratory bias or excessive variability trigger corrective action (staff retraining, method review, equipment service).

PT participation documents ongoing competency and provides evidence of QA/QC compliance for regulatory or accreditation purposes. Results typically show 2-3% of laboratories with significantly biased results (indicating systematic problems), 10-15% with higher-than-expected variability (indicating precision issues), and 82-87% with acceptable performance. Regular participation identifies and corrects problems before they compromise routine analysis.

7.6 Instrument Calibration and Maintenance

All analytical instruments require regular calibration and maintenance to ensure reliable performance. The WIA standard specifies calibration schedules and maintenance requirements for different equipment categories:

  • Microscopes (visual counting): Daily calibration check (magnification verification using stage micrometer, focus adjustment); weekly lighting adjustment and optical cleaning; quarterly professional maintenance
  • FTIR Spectrometers: Weekly spectral calibration using CRM reference materials; monthly wavelength verification using polystyrene or other calibrant; quarterly replacement of desiccant and optical surfaces cleaning
  • Raman Spectrometers: Daily laser power verification; weekly wavelength calibration; monthly detector sensitivity check; quarterly professional alignment
  • Balances: Daily zero-point check; weekly verification with calibration weights; monthly professional recalibration
  • Filtration Equipment: Daily cleaning and air-drying; weekly sanitization; monthly replacement of seals and gaskets
  • Automated Image Analysis Software: Monthly verification on standard reference images; annual update and re-training on new versions

7.7 Documentation, Training and Accreditation

Comprehensive record-keeping is mandatory for demonstrating QA/QC compliance and enabling root cause analysis of deviations. Required documentation includes:

Procedure Documentation: - Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) for all analytical methods with version control and approval signatures - Method specifications including acceptable ranges, detection limits, and performance criteria - Reference standards and reagent lists with lot numbers and expiration dates - Deviations from SOP procedures documented with justification and supervisor approval

Equipment Records: - Maintenance logs with dates, technician identification, and work performed - Calibration certificates with dates, standards used, and results - Equipment serial numbers and acquisition dates - Service records and spare parts inventory

Quality Records: - Control charts tracking QC metric performance over time (blanks, CRM recovery, replicate agreement) - Validation reports for each method and parameter - Proficiency testing results and PT provider feedback - Non-conformance reports documenting problems and corrective actions taken - Corrective action effectiveness verification

Personnel Records: - Training completion certificates for each analyst - Competency assessments and periodic re-training records - Performance reviews and continuing education documentation

Accreditation: The WIA standard enables achievement of ISO 17025 accreditation (International Standard for competence of testing and calibration laboratories). Accreditation requires documented QA/QC systems, trained personnel, validated methods, and proficiency testing participation. Accredited laboratories provide third-party verification of data quality and enhanced credibility for regulatory and policy applications.

Key Takeaways

1. Standardization is Critical: Following established WIA and ISO protocols ensures reliability, reproducibility, and enables inter-study comparisons essential for addressing the global microplastic challenge.
2. Quality Control is Mandatory: Rigorous QA/QC protocols integrated throughout all procedures are non-negotiable for generating defensible data that supports policy and management decisions.
3. Contamination Prevention Essential: Proactive contamination control through proper equipment selection, careful handling, and comprehensive blanks is far more effective than attempting to correct problems after they occur.
4. Documentation Enables Excellence: Complete, meticulous documentation enables troubleshooting, verification, regulatory compliance, and continuous improvement of analytical procedures.
5. Multiple Methods Provide Confidence: Combining complementary analytical approaches provides more defensible, comprehensive results than single-method approaches and reveals aspects not detectable through any single method.
6. Training and Competency Essential: Comprehensive personnel training and ongoing professional development maintain analytical performance and enable innovation in emerging technologies and applications.
7. Continuous Improvement Drives Progress: Regular assessment of performance metrics, systematic implementation of corrective actions, and participation in proficiency testing programs enable progressive improvement in data quality.
8. Philosophy of弘益人間: Adherence to the WIA philosophy of "benefit all humanity" means providing open, accessible, scientifically rigorous standards that enable researchers and policymakers worldwide to collaboratively address microplastic pollution.
