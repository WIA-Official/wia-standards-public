6.1 Data Processing and Statistical Foundations

Raw microplastic data from laboratory analysis requires careful processing to generate defensible, comparable results. The WIA standard specifies standardized approaches for particle counting, concentration calculation, size distribution analysis, and uncertainty quantification. Proper data processing is essential for generating reproducible results and enabling inter-laboratory data synthesis.

6.1.1 Particle Counting Protocols

Manual counting under stereomicroscopy remains the standard approach for microplastic enumeration, though increasingly complemented by automated image analysis for improved speed and consistency. Manual counting requires systematic counting procedures to ensure accuracy and reproducibility. The WIA protocol specifies counting all particles >10 μm in size from the entire filter (if <500 particles) or a defined subsample (if >500 particles total).

For microscopic counting, standardized procedures include: (1) Mount filter on glass slide with optical medium ensuring transparent mounting; (2) Divide entire filter or subsample into systematic grid regions; (3) Count all particles in each grid region using consistent magnification (typically 40-100x); (4) Record particle dimensions (length, width) and morphology (fiber, fragment, film, bead, foam); (5) Calculate total particle count accounting for subsample fraction; (6) Prepare replicate counts (minimum 2) from separate regions of filter to assess counting variance.

Statistical power analysis determines optimal subsample size. For filters containing N total particles, counting subsample containing n particles results in counting uncertainty of sqrt(n). Counting 50-100 particles per subsample yields 7-10% counting uncertainty (relative standard deviation); counting 200+ particles yields 2-5% uncertainty. For samples with <50 particles total, entire filter must be counted; for >500 particles, subsampling at 10-50% level is appropriate to balance labor efficiency with statistical reliability.

6.2 Concentration Calculations and Uncertainty

Microplastic concentrations are expressed in standardized units: particles/liter (water), particles/kg (sediment), particles/m² (deposition), or particles per organism unit. The WIA protocol specifies calculation methods accounting for subsampling, blanks, recovery efficiency, and analytical variability.

Standard Concentration Calculation:
C = [(P_sample - P_blank) × (F_filter/F_counted) × (1/V)] / R

Where:
- P_sample = particles counted in sample
- P_blank = average particles in method blanks
- F_filter/F_counted = subsample correction factor (e.g., 2 if counted 50% of filter)
- V = sample volume (liters)
- R = recovery efficiency as decimal (0-1)

Example: 150 particles counted in 50% of filter, 2 particles in blanks, 10 L sample volume, 90% recovery efficiency results in: C = [(150 - 2) × 2 × (1/10)] / 0.90 = 32.9 particles/L

6.2.1 Uncertainty Estimation

All reported concentrations must include uncertainty estimates (95% confidence intervals or ± standard deviation). The WIA standard specifies bottom-up uncertainty approaches combining uncertainty components from: - Sampling variance (differences among replicate samples) - Analytical variance (counting variance, subsampling uncertainty) - Blank subtraction (uncertainty in contamination level) - Recovery efficiency (uncertainty in extraction efficiency) Combined uncertainty typically ranges 20-50% for routine analysis, 10-20% for high-quality studies with large sample numbers.

6.3 Particle Characterization and Classification

Beyond simple particle counts, characterization data (polymer type, morphology, size) provides critical information for understanding contamination sources and predicting environmental impacts. The WIA standard recommends comprehensive characterization of at least 10-30% of particles per sample, with 100% characterization preferred when sample sizes permit.

Morphological Classification: Particles are classified as: (1) Fibers: length/width ratio >3, typically from textiles and ropes; (2) Fragments: irregular shapes with sharp edges, from degraded macroscopic plastics; (3) Films: thin flexible sheets from bags and packaging; (4) Foams: porous structures from expanded polystyrene; (5) Beads/Pellets: spherical or cylindrical particles from microbeads or pre-production pellets; (6) Flakes: thin, flat particles from coatings and weathered surfaces.

Polymer Identification: FTIR or Raman spectroscopy confirms polymer identity, essential for accurate source attribution since morphology alone cannot reliably distinguish polymer types. Common polymers encountered include: PE (polyethylene), PP (polypropylene), PET (polyethylene terephthalate), PVC (polyvinyl chloride), PS (polystyrene), PA (nylon/polyamide), and polyester (common in textiles). Identification of polymer allows inference of likely source (e.g., polyester fibers from laundry, PE/PP fragments from degraded packaging).

6.5 Data Management and Archiving

The WIA standard promotes adoption of open data management systems enabling long-term archiving, version control, and inter-laboratory sharing. Recommended platforms include PANGAEA (oceanographic data), Zenodo (general scientific data), FIGSHARE (research outputs), and institutional repositories, all providing persistent Digital Object Identifiers (DOIs) ensuring data permanence and citeability.

Metadata requirements include: sample collection date/time, location, environmental conditions (temperature, salinity, pH, turbidity), personnel involved, instruments used, analysis date, analyst identity, and all QA/QC data. Machine-readable formats (CSV, NetCDF, HDF5) are preferred over PDFs to enable automated data processing and synthesis. The WIA standard provides templates for standardized reporting compatible with major data repositories.

6.6 Comparative Data Analysis and Trends

Comparative studies require appropriate statistical tests accounting for non-normal distributions and unequal variances typical of microplastic data. The WIA protocol recommends non-parametric approaches (Kruskal-Wallis, Mann-Whitney U tests) as primary methods, reserving parametric tests (ANOVA, t-tests) only for log-transformed data verified to meet normality assumptions.

Trend Analysis: Time-series analysis identifies meaningful changes despite background variability. Linear regression or non-parametric trend tests (Mann-Kendall) applied to monthly or annual average concentrations can detect trends requiring minimum 3-5 years of regular sampling. Adequate statistical power requires 20-30 data points minimum to detect 25-30% changes in concentration over time.

Source Apportionment: Polymer composition and morphology data enable source attribution. Chemical signature matching (e.g., specific polymer:morphology combinations) enables identification of major pollution sources. For example, 70%+ polyester fibers indicates textile source, while PE/PP fragments suggest food packaging source.

6.7 Documentation and Case Studies

6.7.1 Multi-Year Temporal Trend Analysis

A 5-year coastal monitoring program (monthly sampling, 12 sites, 720 total samples) tracked microplastic concentrations and composition using standardized WIA protocols. Proper uncertainty estimation revealed statistically significant (p<0.05) increasing trends only after 3 years of data accumulation, highlighting importance of adequate monitoring duration. Key findings: (1) average concentrations increased 40-80% over 5 years, consistent with global plastic production trends; (2) polymer composition shifted toward higher proportion of microbeads as regulations banned them from personal care products (reflecting lag between regulation and market response); (3) seasonal patterns identified with highest concentrations during summer months, consistent with increased recreational use and atmospheric transport. Consistent reporting formats enabled direct comparison with 8 other international programs, revealing similar trends globally.

6.7.2 Regional Comparative Study

A 3-country study compared microplastic levels in 15 major rivers using identical WIA protocols. Large variation among countries (1-15 fold differences) correlated with plastic production per capita, waste management infrastructure, and wastewater treatment capabilities. Standardized reporting enabled robust conclusions about relative pollution levels and identification of most problematic rivers. Follow-up policy discussions used this data to justify investment in microplastic treatment technology in most-polluted regions.

Key Takeaways

1. Standardization is Critical: Following established WIA and ISO protocols ensures reliability, reproducibility, and enables inter-study comparisons essential for addressing the global microplastic challenge.
2. Quality Control is Mandatory: Rigorous QA/QC protocols integrated throughout all procedures are non-negotiable for generating defensible data that supports policy and management decisions.
3. Contamination Prevention Essential: Proactive contamination control through proper equipment selection, careful handling, and comprehensive blanks is far more effective than attempting to correct problems after they occur.
4. Documentation Enables Excellence: Complete, meticulous documentation enables troubleshooting, verification, regulatory compliance, and continuous improvement of analytical procedures.
5. Multiple Methods Provide Confidence: Combining complementary analytical approaches provides more defensible, comprehensive results than single-method approaches and reveals aspects not detectable through any single method.
6. Training and Competency Essential: Comprehensive personnel training and ongoing professional development maintain analytical performance and enable innovation in emerging technologies and applications.
7. Continuous Improvement Drives Progress: Regular assessment of performance metrics, systematic implementation of corrective actions, and participation in proficiency testing programs enable progressive improvement in data quality.
8. Philosophy of弘益人間: Adherence to the WIA philosophy of "benefit all humanity" means providing open, accessible, scientifically rigorous standards that enable researchers and policymakers worldwide to collaboratively address microplastic pollution.
