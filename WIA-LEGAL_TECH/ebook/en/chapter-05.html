Chapter 5: Electronic Discovery (eDiscovery)

EDRM Framework, Data Processing, Technology-Assisted Review, and Quality Control

Table of Contents > Chapter 5

Overview: Electronic discovery (eDiscovery) is the process of identifying, preserving, collecting, processing, reviewing, and producing electronically stored information in litigation. eDiscovery can involve hundreds of thousands or millions of documents. This chapter explores the EDRM framework, data processing standards, Technology-Assisted Review (TAR/predictive coding), and quality control mechanisms that ensure eDiscovery processes are efficient, accurate, and compliant with legal obligations.

5.1 The eDiscovery Challenge

Modern litigation involves massive volumes of electronically stored information (ESI). A single enterprise email system might contain millions of messages. Chat systems (Slack, Teams, WhatsApp) create new discovery sources. Cloud storage services scatter documents across multiple platforms. Traditional document review—having lawyers manually read millions of documents—is economically infeasible.

eDiscovery must balance multiple objectives:

  • Completeness: Find all relevant documents without missing critical evidence
  • Efficiency: Manage costs by avoiding unnecessary review of non-responsive documents
  • Compliance: Meet legal obligations for timeliness, accuracy, and proper production
  • Privilege Protection: Avoid accidentally producing privileged communications
  • Confidentiality: Protect sensitive business information and personal data

5.2 EDRM Framework Compliance

The Electronic Discovery Reference Model (EDRM) provides a standard framework for eDiscovery processes. The WIA standard requires compliance with EDRM phases:

1. Information Governance

Establish policies for information management, retention, and destruction. Define what information is subject to discovery.

2. Identification

Identify sources of potentially relevant electronically stored information (ESI). Determine scope of discovery.

3. Preservation

Implement litigation hold to preserve potentially relevant information from destruction or alteration.

4. Collection

Collect ESI from identified sources in proper format with chain of custody documentation.

5. Processing

Process collected data: deduplication, near-duplicate detection, OCR, metadata extraction, filtering.

6. Review

Review documents for responsiveness and privilege. Prepare for production.

7. Analysis

Analyze produced documents to support case theories and develop evidence.

8. Production

Produce responsive documents to opposing counsel in agreed format with proper metadata.

9. Presentation

Present evidence to judge/jury during litigation. Link documents to case themes.

5.4 Data Processing Standards

Processing converts raw data into searchable, reviewable documents. The WIA standard specifies required processing steps:

Deduplication

Eliminate duplicate documents using cryptographic hash values (MD5 or SHA-256). This dramatically reduces review volume by eliminating multiple copies of identical documents. Systems must:

  • Calculate hash values for each document
  • Identify and flag duplicates
  • Preserve one copy as representative; mark others as duplicates
  • Allow search across duplicates while showing consolidated results

Email Threading

Group email messages into conversation threads showing the complete exchange context. This helps reviewers understand communications in proper context.

Near-Duplicate Detection

Identify documents that are substantially similar (e.g., emails with minor variations, or form documents with slightly different data). This helps reviewers avoid redundant review.

OCR for Images and PDFs

Apply optical character recognition to scanned documents and image-based PDFs, making text searchable. The standard requires:

  • OCR accuracy of 95%+ for readable documents
  • Language support for common languages in multilingual matters
  • Preservation of original image alongside OCR text
  • Handling of handwriting with appropriate confidence scoring

Metadata Extraction

Extract structured metadata from documents including:

  • For email: To, From, CC, BCC, Date, Subject, attachments
  • For files: Creation date, modification date, author, last modified by, file size
  • For databases: Record ID, timestamps, user who created/modified

Data Culling and Filtering

Reduce review volume by filtering out clearly non-responsive documents:

  • Filter by date range (outside relevant period)
  • Filter by custodian (document creator or recipient not relevant to dispute)
  • Filter by keyword exclusions (clearly non-responsive terms)
  • Filter by file type (system files, temporary files, application metadata)

5.5 Technology-Assisted Review (TAR)

Technology-Assisted Review (TAR), also called predictive coding, uses machine learning to identify responsive documents, dramatically reducing manual review volume. The WIA standard specifies TAR requirements:

TAR Methodology

TAR 1.0: Traditional approach where lawyers manually review a sample of documents, marking them responsive/non-responsive. AI model is trained on this sample and predicts responsiveness for remaining documents. Lawyers review the highest-uncertainty predictions to refine the model.

TAR 2.0: Continuous active learning where the AI model continuously asks for human feedback on documents it's most uncertain about, iteratively improving the model. This can be more efficient than TAR 1.0.

Accuracy Requirements

  • Recall Target: 75%+ The system must find at least 75% of truly responsive documents. Missing responsive documents can harm a case.
  • Precision Target: 60%+ Of documents the system identifies as responsive, at least 60% should actually be responsive. This minimizes wasted review on false positives.
  • Quality Control: Statistically validate model accuracy by manually reviewing samples of documents the system identified as non-responsive.
  • Human Review Minimum: Minimum 5% of documents the system identifies as responsive must be manually reviewed by humans to verify accuracy.

5.6 Quality Control and Sampling

eDiscovery processes must include rigorous quality control:

  • Blind QA samples: Randomly select documents the system marked non-responsive and have humans manually review to verify accuracy
  • Privilege verification: Statistically sample documents for privilege designations to ensure accuracy
  • Metadata verification: Validate that extracted metadata matches source documents
  • Completeness checks: Verify that all promised custodians were collected and all date ranges covered
  • Format validation: Spot-check produced documents for proper format and completeness

5.7 Privilege and Confidentiality Protection

eDiscovery must protect privileged and confidential information:

Privilege Designation

  • Attorney-client privilege: Communications with attorneys seeking or providing legal advice
  • Work product doctrine: Materials prepared by lawyers in anticipation of litigation
  • Litigation hold procedure: Establish procedures for attorneys to mark privileged documents
  • Privilege logs: Maintain detailed logs of withheld privileged documents

Confidentiality Protocols

  • Designate documents as confidential (attorney eyes only, outside counsel only, etc.)
  • Limit access to documents marked confidential
  • Track who accessed confidential documents
  • Implement protective orders restricting how opposing counsel can use confidential documents

5.8 Cost and Time Considerations

eDiscovery cost and timeline depend on volume and complexity:

  • Small matters (100K-500K documents): Can be processed in weeks, cost $50K-150K
  • Medium matters (500K-5M documents): Typically 2-3 months, cost $150K-500K
  • Large matters (5M-100M+ documents): Can take many months, cost $500K-multimillion

TAR/predictive coding can reduce review costs by 50-70% compared to linear review, but requires investment in setup and model development.

5.9 eDiscovery Implementation Best Practices

  • Early Planning: Plan eDiscovery strategy early in litigation before key documents are lost or destroyed
  • Preservation: Implement litigation holds immediately when litigation is reasonably anticipated
  • Vendor Selection: Evaluate eDiscovery vendors carefully based on capacity, expertise, and security
  • Protocols: Establish clear protocols for what is discoverable, who makes responsiveness decisions, how privilege is protected
  • Transparency: Be transparent with opposing counsel about methodology and quality control procedures
  • Cost Control: Negotiate fixed prices and caps with eDiscovery vendors where possible
  • Quality Focus: Invest in quality control sampling to ensure accuracy and defensibility
