Chapter 1: Introduction & Overview

1.1 What is Eye Tracking?

Eye tracking, also known as gaze tracking, is a technology that measures where a person is looking and how their eyes move. Modern eye trackers use infrared cameras and sophisticated algorithms to detect the position and movement of the eyes with millisecond precision.

How Eye Tracking Works

Eye tracking devices typically use one of two approaches:

  • Corneal Reflection - Infrared light reflects off the cornea while a camera tracks the pupil position
  • Pupil Center Tracking - The camera detects the dark pupil against the lighter iris
  • Combined Method - Most professional systems use both methods for maximum accuracy

Key Eye Movement Types

Understanding eye movements is essential for working with eye tracking data:

  • Fixations - When the eyes remain relatively still, focusing on a specific point (typically 100-500ms)
  • Saccades - Rapid eye movements between fixations (typically 20-200ms)
  • Smooth Pursuits - Slow tracking movements following a moving object
  • Blinks - Brief closures of the eyelids (typically 100-400ms)

1.2 Why Eye Tracking Matters for Accessibility

For individuals with motor disabilities affecting their ability to use traditional input devices (keyboard, mouse, touchscreen), eye tracking provides a powerful alternative communication and control method.

1.3 The Need for Standardization

Before the WIA Eye Gaze Standard, the eye tracking ecosystem faced significant challenges:

Problems Without Standardization

  • Vendor Lock-In - AAC software tied to specific eye tracker brands
  • Incompatible Data Formats - Each manufacturer uses different coordinate systems and data structures
  • Limited Interoperability - Cannot mix devices and software from different vendors
  • Development Complexity - Developers must support multiple proprietary APIs
  • User Lock-In - Users cannot switch devices without losing their software setup

Benefits of the WIA Standard

  • Users can choose any WIA-compliant eye tracker with their preferred AAC software
  • Developers write to one API instead of supporting multiple proprietary systems
  • Device manufacturers gain access to a larger ecosystem of compatible applications
  • Research data becomes more comparable across different studies and devices
  • Lower costs through increased competition and reduced integration effort

1.4 Core Design Principles

1. Normalization

All screen coordinates are normalized to the range 0.0 to 1.0, making the protocol resolution-independent and adaptable to any screen size or multi-monitor setup.

// Screen-independent coordinates
{
  "x": 0.5,  // Center horizontally (50%)
  "y": 0.5,  // Center vertically (50%)
  "timestamp": 1704067200000
}

2. Binocular Support

The standard supports both individual eye data and combined binocular gaze points, allowing applications to use the most appropriate data for their needs.

3. Extensibility

The data schema uses required and optional fields, allowing devices to expose advanced features while maintaining compatibility with basic implementations.

4. Precision

Microsecond-precision timestamps and floating-point coordinates ensure accurate data for both accessibility and research applications.

5. Quality Indicators

Every data point includes confidence and validity information, allowing applications to respond appropriately to tracking quality issues.

1.5 Primary Use Cases

Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC)

The most critical use case for eye tracking. Users with speech impairments or no ability to speak can communicate by looking at letters, words, or symbols on a screen.

AAC Interaction Methods

  • Dwell Selection - Look at a target for a set duration (e.g., 800ms) to select it
  • Blink Selection - Intentional blinks trigger selections while looking at targets
  • Gaze Gestures - Specific eye movement patterns trigger actions
  • Switch + Gaze - Eye tracking for targeting combined with external switch for selection

Computer Control

Full computer access using eye movements as a mouse replacement. Users can:

  • Navigate operating system interfaces
  • Browse the web
  • Compose emails and documents
  • Control smart home devices
  • Access entertainment (music, videos, games)

Gaming & Entertainment

Eye tracking enables new gameplay mechanics and makes games accessible to players with motor disabilities:

  • Gaze Aiming - Look where you want to shoot or move
  • Menu Navigation - Select options by looking at them
  • Camera Control - Look around the game world naturally
  • Attention-Based Mechanics - Game elements respond to where you're looking

Research & Analysis

Eye tracking provides insights into attention, cognition, and user experience:

  • Reading Research - Analyze reading patterns and comprehension
  • Usability Testing - Understand where users look on interfaces
  • Cognitive Load - Measure mental effort through pupil dilation and fixation patterns
  • Marketing Research - Test advertisement effectiveness and product placement

1.6 Device Types & Form Factors

Screen-Based Trackers

Mounted below or above a monitor. Most common for AAC and desktop use.

  • Examples: Tobii Pro Fusion, Gazepoint GP3
  • Typical Accuracy: 0.3° - 1.0°
  • Sampling Rate: 60Hz - 250Hz
  • Operating Distance: 50-80cm

Wearable Trackers

Glasses or headsets with integrated eye tracking. Ideal for mobile use and VR.

  • Examples: Tobii Glasses, Pupil Labs Core
  • Typical Accuracy: 0.5° - 2.0°
  • Sampling Rate: 30Hz - 200Hz
  • Advantage: Freedom of movement

Integrated Trackers

Built into laptops, tablets, or VR headsets.

  • Examples: Some gaming laptops, Meta Quest Pro
  • Typical Accuracy: 1.0° - 3.0°
  • Sampling Rate: 30Hz - 120Hz
  • Advantage: No additional hardware needed

Webcam-Based Solutions

Software-only tracking using standard webcams.

  • Examples: WebGazer, GazeCloudAPI
  • Typical Accuracy: 2.0° - 5.0°
  • Sampling Rate: 10Hz - 30Hz
  • Advantage: Low cost, no special hardware
  • Limitation: Lower accuracy, not suitable for critical AAC use

Understanding Accuracy vs. Precision

Accuracy measures how close the tracked gaze point is to where you're actually looking (systematic error).

Precision measures consistency when looking at the same point repeatedly (random error).

For AAC, both matter: accuracy ensures you can reach all screen targets, while precision ensures reliable dwell selection.

1.8 Getting Started

For AAC Users

  1. Choose a WIA-compliant eye tracker appropriate for your needs and environment
  2. Complete calibration (typically 5-9 point calibration)
  3. Select AAC software supporting the WIA standard
  4. Customize dwell times and selection methods to your preference
  5. Practice with training exercises to build proficiency

1.9 Standard Structure

The WIA Eye Gaze Tracking Standard consists of four main components:

Phase 1: Data Format

Defines the core data structures: GazePoint, EyeData, Events, and coordinate systems. This ensures all devices speak the same language.

Phase 2: API Interface

Specifies the API endpoints for device control, calibration, data streaming, and event handling. Enables software to control any compliant device.

Phase 3: Protocol Specification

Details the communication protocols (WebSocket, IPC, binary format) for efficient, low-latency data transmission.

Phase 4: Integration & Certification

Provides integration guidance and certification procedures to ensure quality and interoperability.
