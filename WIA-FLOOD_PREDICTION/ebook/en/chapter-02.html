Chapter 2: Satellite Remote Sensing for Flood Detection

Eyes in the Sky: How Satellites Detect and Monitor Floods

2.1 Why Satellites for Flood Prediction?

Traditional flood monitoring relies on ground-based river gauges - but there are only about 9,000 USGS gauges in the entire United States, and far fewer globally. Satellites provide:

  • Global Coverage: Monitor remote, inaccessible areas
  • Frequent Updates: Daily to weekly revisit times
  • Synoptic View: See entire watersheds simultaneously
  • Historical Archive: 40+ years of satellite data (since Landsat-1 in 1972)
  • All-Weather Capability: Radar satellites penetrate clouds
Key Advantage: Sentinel-1 radar can detect floods even at night and through heavy clouds - conditions when optical satellites are blind.

2.2 Types of Earth Observation Satellites

2.2.1 Optical Satellites (Visible & Infrared)

How They Work: Measure sunlight reflected from Earth's surface, like a camera in space.

Best For: Clear-weather flood mapping, vegetation monitoring, land cover classification

Limitation: Cannot see through clouds or operate at night

Flood Detection Method: NDWI (Normalized Difference Water Index)

NDWI = (Green - NIR) / (Green + NIR)

Green: Band 3 (560 nm) - Water reflects green light
NIR: Band 8 (842 nm) - Water absorbs near-infrared

Interpretation:
NDWI > 0.3 = Water
NDWI 0.0 to 0.3 = Wet soil/vegetation
NDWI < 0.0 = Dry land

2.2.2 Radar Satellites (SAR - Synthetic Aperture Radar)

How They Work: Transmit microwave pulses, measure backscattered energy. Works day/night, through clouds.

Best For: All-weather flood detection, change detection, emergency response

Sentinel-1 (European Space Agency)

ParameterSpecification

Flood Detection Method: Backscatter Thresholding

Principle: Water appears DARK in SAR images

Why? Smooth water surfaces act like mirrors, reflecting radar energy away from the satellite (specular reflection).

Threshold: VH backscatter < -20 dB = Water
Accuracy: 85-95% for open water, 70-85% for flooded vegetation

2.3 Satellite Data Processing Workflow

Step 1: Data Acquisition

Copernicus Open Access Hub: https://scihub.copernicus.eu/

Alternative: Google Earth Engine (preprocessed, cloud-hosted)

Step 2: Preprocessing

For Sentinel-1 SAR:

  1. Apply Orbit File: Refine satellite position (±10m → ±5cm accuracy)
  2. Radiometric Calibration: Convert digital numbers to backscatter coefficient (σ°)
  3. Speckle Filtering: Reduce salt-and-pepper noise (Lee Sigma filter, 7x7 window)
  4. Terrain Correction: Remove geometric distortions using SRTM DEM

For Sentinel-2 Optical:

  1. Atmospheric Correction: Remove haze, convert to surface reflectance (Sen2Cor algorithm)
  2. Cloud Masking: Exclude cloud pixels using Scene Classification Layer
  3. Geometric Correction: Already done by ESA (orthorectified)

Step 3: Water Detection

Sentinel-1: Apply -20 dB threshold on VH polarization

Sentinel-2: Calculate NDWI, threshold at 0.3

Step 4: Change Detection

Compare current image with pre-flood baseline:

Flood Extent = Current Water - Permanent Water

This isolates the new floodwater, excluding lakes and rivers that are always present.

2.4 Real-World Example: 2022 Pakistan Floods

Event: Monsoon rains (June-August 2022) caused catastrophic flooding affecting 33 million people, submerging 1/3 of Pakistan.

Satellite Response:

  • Sentinel-1: Detected 20,000 km² of floodwater (size of New Jersey)
  • MODIS: Daily monitoring tracked flood progression
  • Planet Labs: High-resolution (3m) imagery for damage assessment

2.5 Advanced Techniques

2.5.1 Multi-Temporal Analysis

Stack 10-20 Sentinel-1 images over 1 year to create:

  • Permanent Water Mask: Areas always wet (rivers, lakes)
  • Seasonal Flood Zones: Areas that flood annually (floodplains)
  • Anomaly Detection: Flag unusual flooding

2.5.2 Flood Depth Estimation

Combine satellite flood extent with Digital Elevation Model (DEM):

Method:
1. Identify flood boundary in Sentinel-1 image
2. Extract elevation at boundary (e.g., 25m above sea level)
3. Assume water level is flat at 25m
4. Calculate depth = Water level - Ground elevation

Accuracy: ±0.5m (limited by DEM accuracy)

2.5.3 Flooded Vegetation Detection

Challenge: Vegetation poking through floodwater scatters radar back to satellite (high backscatter), masking flood.

Solution: Use cross-polarization (VH) instead of co-polarization (VV). VH is more sensitive to double-bounce scattering from water-vegetation interface.

2.7 Limitations of Satellite Monitoring

  1. Temporal Resolution: 5-6 day revisit misses rapid flash floods
  2. Cloud Cover: Optical satellites useless during storms (when floods happen!)
  3. Urban Areas: Tall buildings block radar, create shadows
  4. Dense Vegetation: Canopy hides floodwater in forests
  5. Data Latency: 1-24 hour delay from image acquisition to availability

2.8 Key Takeaways

  1. Satellites provide global, frequent, and objective flood monitoring
  2. Sentinel-1 radar works through clouds, crucial for flood emergencies
  3. Sentinel-2 optical provides detailed flood mapping in clear weather
  4. NDWI and backscatter thresholding are the primary water detection methods
  5. Change detection isolates new floodwater from permanent water bodies
  6. Free satellite data from Copernicus/NASA democratizes flood monitoring
