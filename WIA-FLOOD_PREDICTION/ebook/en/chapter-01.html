Chapter 1: Flood Science & Hydrology

Understanding the Natural Processes Behind Flooding

1.1 What is a Flood?

A flood is an overflow of water that submerges land that is usually dry. Floods are the most common and widespread natural disaster, affecting more people globally than any other hazard. According to the UN Office for Disaster Risk Reduction (UNDRR), floods account for 43% of all documented natural disasters between 1995 and 2015, affecting 2.3 billion people worldwide.

Key Definition: A flood occurs when water accumulates faster than the land can absorb it or rivers can carry it away, resulting in inundation of normally dry areas.

1.2 Types of Floods

1.2.1 Flash Floods

Characteristics: Rapid onset (within 6 hours), high velocity, short duration

Causes: Intense rainfall, dam failures, ice jams

Danger Level: Extreme - Most deadly flood type

Example: The 2021 European floods (Germany, Belgium) killed 222 people, caused by 154mm rainfall in 24 hours.

1.2.2 River (Fluvial) Floods

Characteristics: Slower onset (days to weeks), longer duration, predictable

Causes: Prolonged rainfall, snowmelt, upstream releases

Affected Areas: Floodplains, low-lying valleys

Example: The 2011 Mississippi River floods lasted 2 months, inundated 30,000 km² of land.

1.2.3 Coastal Floods

Characteristics: Triggered by storm surges, high tides, tsunamis

Causes: Hurricanes, cyclones, sea level rise

Risk Factor: 40% of global population lives within 100km of coastlines

Example: Hurricane Katrina (2005) caused 6-meter storm surge, flooding 80% of New Orleans.

1.2.4 Urban Floods

Characteristics: Rapid accumulation due to impervious surfaces (concrete, asphalt)

Causes: Overwhelmed drainage systems, inadequate infrastructure

Challenge: Urban areas drain 2-6x faster than natural land, increasing flood risk

1.3 The Hydrological Cycle

Understanding floods requires understanding the water cycle - the continuous movement of water on, above, and below Earth's surface.

Hydrological Cycle: ☁️ EVAPORATION → CLOUDS → PRECIPITATION 🌧️ ↑ ↓ [Ocean/Lake] [Land Surface] ↑ ↓ ← RUNOFF ← [Rivers] ← INFILTRATION → [Groundwater]

Key Processes:

  • Precipitation: Rain, snow, hail - Source of all flooding
  • Infiltration: Water soaking into soil - Reduces flood risk
  • Runoff: Water flowing over land surface - Direct cause of floods
  • Evapotranspiration: Water returning to atmosphere via evaporation and plant transpiration

1.4 Watershed and Catchment Basics

A watershed (or catchment) is an area of land where all water drains to a common point - like a river, lake, or ocean. Understanding watersheds is critical for flood prediction.

Watershed Characteristics Affecting Floods:

  1. Size: Larger watersheds have longer response times (hours to days vs. minutes)
  2. Shape: Fan-shaped watersheds concentrate runoff faster than elongated ones
  3. Slope: Steep slopes → Faster runoff → Flash floods
  4. Land Cover:
    • Forest: 10-30% runoff (high infiltration)
    • Grassland: 30-50% runoff
    • Agricultural: 40-70% runoff
    • Urban (pavement): 80-95% runoff
  5. Soil Type:
    • Sandy soil: High infiltration (low flood risk)
    • Clay soil: Low infiltration (high flood risk)
    • Saturated soil: Near-zero infiltration (extreme flood risk)

1.5 Factors That Cause Floods

1.5.1 Meteorological Factors

  • Rainfall Intensity: >25mm/hour = Flash flood risk
  • Rainfall Duration: Multi-day storms saturate soil
  • Spatial Extent: Widespread rain affects entire watersheds
  • Antecedent Moisture: Wet soil from previous rain reduces capacity

1.5.2 Human Factors

  • Urbanization: Replacing vegetation with concrete increases runoff by 400-600%
  • Deforestation: Removes natural water storage, accelerates runoff
  • Channel Modification: Straightening rivers increases flow velocity
  • Dam Operations: Sudden releases can cause downstream floods
  • Climate Change: Increases extreme precipitation events by 7% per °C warming (Clausius-Clapeyron relation)

1.6 Flood Physics: Discharge and Velocity

The fundamental equation of flood hydrology is the continuity equation:

Q = A × V
Q = Discharge (m³/s or cubic feet per second)
A = Cross-sectional area (m²)
V = Flow velocity (m/s)

Why This Matters:

  • Doubling river depth often triples flow velocity (increases flood force 9x)
  • Just 6 inches (15cm) of fast-flowing water can knock down an adult
  • 2 feet (60cm) of water can float most vehicles

Manning's Equation (calculates flow velocity):

V = (1/n) × R^(2/3) × S^(1/2)

  • n = Manning's roughness coefficient (0.015 for smooth concrete, 0.15 for dense brush)
  • R = Hydraulic radius (area/wetted perimeter)
  • S = Channel slope (m/m)

1.7 Flood Frequency Analysis

Floods are described by their return period - the average time between events of a given magnitude.

Flood Event Return Period Annual Probability Example Impact
2-year flood Every 2 years 50% Nuisance flooding
10-year flood Every 10 years 10% Minor property damage
100-year flood Every 100 years 1% Serious damage (FEMA base flood)
500-year flood Every 500 years 0.2% Catastrophic
Common Misconception: A "100-year flood" does NOT mean it only happens once per century. It means there's a 1% chance EVERY year. Over a 30-year mortgage, there's a 26% chance of experiencing at least one 100-year flood.

1.8 Climate Change Impact on Flooding

Global warming is intensifying the water cycle, leading to more extreme precipitation events:

  • +7% precipitation per °C: For every 1°C of warming, the atmosphere holds 7% more moisture (Clausius-Clapeyron)
  • Increasing frequency: 100-year floods now occur every 20-30 years in many regions
  • Sea level rise: +3.4mm/year globally, exacerbating coastal floods
  • Shifting patterns: Wetter regions getting wetter, dry regions getting drier

Future Projections (IPCC AR6, 2021):

  • By 2050: 1.6 billion people at risk of flooding (up from 1.2 billion today)
  • By 2100: Economic damages from floods could reach $52 trillion annually under high-emission scenarios

1.9 Key Takeaways

  1. Floods are the world's most common natural disaster, affecting billions
  2. Flash floods are most deadly due to rapid onset and high velocity
  3. Watershed characteristics (size, slope, land cover) determine flood behavior
  4. Urbanization dramatically increases flood risk by preventing infiltration
  5. Climate change is making extreme floods more frequent and severe
  6. Understanding flood physics (discharge, velocity) is crucial for prediction and safety
