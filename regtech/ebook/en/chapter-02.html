Chapter 2: Regulatory Framework

Global Regulatory Landscape

The global regulatory environment for financial services is complex and multi-layered, with regulations spanning international standards, regional requirements, and jurisdiction-specific rules. Understanding this landscape is essential for implementing effective RegTech solutions.

International Standards

Basel III/IV - Banking Supervision

The Basel Accords, developed by the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision, establish international standards for bank capital adequacy, stress testing, and market liquidity risk. Basel III (2010) and Basel IV (ongoing implementation) significantly strengthened capital requirements and introduced new leverage and liquidity standards.

  • Capital Requirements: Minimum capital ratios banks must maintain
  • Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR): Ensuring short-term resilience
  • Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR): Long-term liquidity management
  • Leverage Ratio: Non-risk-based backstop to risk-weighted requirements

FATF Recommendations - AML/CFT

The Financial Action Task Force (FATF) sets global standards for combating money laundering and terrorist financing. The 40 Recommendations provide a comprehensive framework that countries implement through national legislation.

  • Customer due diligence (CDD) and enhanced due diligence (EDD)
  • Beneficial ownership identification and verification
  • Transaction monitoring and suspicious activity reporting
  • Cross-border information sharing and cooperation

Regional Regulations

European Union

MiFID II (Markets in Financial Instruments Directive)

Comprehensive regulation governing investment services, increasing transparency, protecting investors, and strengthening oversight of financial markets.

  • Pre-trade and post-trade transparency requirements
  • Best execution obligations and reporting
  • Product governance and suitability assessments
  • Transaction reporting to regulatory authorities

GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)

Landmark privacy regulation protecting personal data of EU residents, with significant implications for financial services data processing.

  • Lawful basis for data processing (consent, legitimate interest, etc.)
  • Data subject rights (access, rectification, erasure, portability)
  • Privacy by design and by default requirements
  • Mandatory data breach notification within 72 hours
  • Significant penalties up to 4% of global annual turnover

PSD2 (Payment Services Directive 2)

Enabling open banking through third-party access to payment accounts, strong customer authentication, and enhanced consumer protection.

5AMLD/6AMLD (Anti-Money Laundering Directives)

Strengthening AML/CFT frameworks with expanded scope including virtual currencies, beneficial ownership registers, and enhanced customer due diligence.

United States

Dodd-Frank Act

Comprehensive financial reform legislation following the 2008 crisis, establishing new regulatory agencies, stress testing requirements, and consumer protection measures.

  • Volcker Rule restricting proprietary trading
  • Comprehensive Capital Analysis and Review (CCAR)
  • Orderly Liquidation Authority for systemic institutions
  • Enhanced risk management and governance requirements

Bank Secrecy Act (BSA)

Foundation of AML regulations in the US, requiring financial institutions to assist government agencies in detecting and preventing money laundering.

  • Currency Transaction Reports (CTR) for transactions ≥$10,000
  • Suspicious Activity Reports (SAR) for suspicious transactions
  • Customer Identification Program (CIP) requirements
  • Enhanced Due Diligence for high-risk customers

Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX)

Corporate governance and financial disclosure requirements, mandating internal controls over financial reporting and executive certification of financial statements.

CCPA/CPRA (California Consumer Privacy Act)

State-level privacy regulation providing California residents with rights over their personal data, influencing privacy practices nationwide.

Asia-Pacific Region

Singapore - MAS Regulations

The Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) implements progressive regulations supporting innovation while maintaining robust oversight.

  • Payment Services Act governing digital payments and cryptocurrencies
  • Technology Risk Management guidelines
  • Regulatory sandboxes for fintech innovation
  • AML/CFT Notice and guidelines

Hong Kong - HKMA/SFC Frameworks

Dual regulatory structure with HKMA (banking) and SFC (securities) implementing Basel standards and local requirements.

Japan - JFSA Regulations

Japan Financial Services Agency oversight of banking, securities, and emerging digital asset sectors.

Australia - APRA/ASIC Standards

Prudential regulation by APRA and conduct regulation by ASIC, including AML/CTF Act requirements.

Key Regulatory Requirements

Know Your Customer (KYC)

Fundamental requirement to verify customer identity and assess risk before establishing business relationships.

Component Requirements
Customer Identification Full name, date of birth, address, government-issued ID
Verification Documentary evidence, biometric authentication, database checks
Risk Assessment Customer risk profiling based on multiple factors
Ongoing Monitoring Periodic review and update of customer information

Anti-Money Laundering (AML)

Comprehensive systems to detect and prevent money laundering activities through transaction monitoring, screening, and reporting.

  • Transaction Monitoring: Real-time analysis of customer transactions
  • Sanctions Screening: Checking against global sanctions lists (OFAC, UN, EU)
  • PEP Screening: Identifying politically exposed persons
  • Adverse Media Screening: Monitoring negative news and reputational risks
  • Suspicious Activity Reporting: Filing SARs when required

Data Privacy and Protection

Protecting customer personal data through technical and organizational measures, ensuring lawful processing and respecting data subject rights.

Conduct and Market Abuse

Preventing insider trading, market manipulation, and ensuring fair treatment of customers through surveillance and controls.

Cybersecurity

Protecting financial systems and data from cyber threats through robust security controls, incident response, and resilience planning.

Regulatory Reporting Requirements

Types of Regulatory Reports

  1. Prudential Reports: Capital adequacy, liquidity, risk exposures
  2. Transaction Reports: Trade reporting under MiFID II, Dodd-Frank
  3. AML Reports: CTR, SAR, large cash transactions
  4. Conduct Reports: Complaints, product sales, suitability
  5. Operational Reports: Incidents, outages, cyber events

Reporting Challenges

  • Multiple jurisdictions with different requirements and formats
  • Tight deadlines (often T+1 for transaction reporting)
  • Data quality and completeness requirements
  • Validation rules and reconciliation with regulatory systems
  • Version control and amendments to previously submitted reports

Penalties and Enforcement

Regulatory violations can result in severe consequences including fines, license revocation, and criminal prosecution.

Notable Enforcement Actions

  • HSBC (2012): $1.9 billion for AML violations
  • Standard Chartered (2019): $1.1 billion for sanctions violations
  • Goldman Sachs (2020): $2.9 billion for 1MDB scandal
  • Google (2019): €50 million GDPR fine
  • Capital One (2020): $80 million for data breach
RegTech Solution: Automated compliance systems can prevent violations through real-time monitoring, automated controls, and comprehensive audit trails, significantly reducing regulatory risk and potential penalties.

Emerging Regulatory Trends

  • Digital Assets: Regulations for cryptocurrencies and digital securities
  • ESG Reporting: Environmental, social, and governance disclosures
  • AI Governance: Regulations for AI/ML use in financial services
  • Operational Resilience: Requirements for business continuity and disaster recovery
  • Open Banking/Finance: API standards and third-party access requirements
