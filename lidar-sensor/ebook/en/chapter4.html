Chapter 4: Detector Technologies

SPAD, APD, and SiPM - The Eyes of LiDAR Systems

4.1 The Critical Role of Photodetectors

While lasers emit the pulses that probe the environment, photodetectors are the sensors that capture returning light and convert it to measurable electrical signals. The detector's sensitivity, speed, noise characteristics, and spectral response fundamentally determine a LiDAR system's maximum range, resolution, and reliability. Understanding detector technologies is essential for evaluating LiDAR performance and predicting future capabilities.

Automotive LiDAR photodetectors must simultaneously achieve:

  • High Quantum Efficiency: Convert 70-90% of incident photons to electrical signal
  • Fast Response Time: Sub-nanosecond rise/fall times for precise ToF measurement
  • Low Noise: Minimal dark current and thermal noise for weak signal detection
  • High Gain: Internal amplification (10^3 to 10^6×) to boost weak returns
  • Wide Dynamic Range: Detect both nearby high-reflectivity and distant low-reflectivity targets
  • Automotive Qualification: -40°C to +85°C operation, 15-year lifetime, AEC-Q100 compliance

4.2 Avalanche Photodiodes (APD)

Avalanche Photodiodes represent the most mature and widely deployed detector technology in automotive LiDAR. APDs leverage the avalanche multiplication effect - a single photon-generated electron can trigger a cascade of additional electron-hole pairs, providing internal gain that amplifies weak optical signals.

4.2.1 Operating Principle

An APD consists of a p-n junction semiconductor operated under high reverse bias voltage (typically 50-400V). When a photon is absorbed in the depletion region, it creates an electron-hole pair. These charge carriers accelerate in the strong electric field, gaining sufficient energy to ionize additional atoms through impact ionization. Each impact creates new electron-hole pairs, which in turn ionize more atoms - an avalanche multiplication cascade.

The avalanche gain M depends on applied voltage and is described by:

M = 1 / (1 - (V/V_br)^n)
where V = applied voltage, V_br = breakdown voltage, n = material constant

Typical APD gains range from 10 to 1000, providing significant signal amplification while maintaining relatively low noise compared to external amplifiers.

4.2.2 Silicon APDs (Si-APD) for 905nm LiDAR

Silicon APDs are the detector of choice for 905nm automotive LiDAR, offering excellent quantum efficiency (70-90% at 905nm), low cost through mature fabrication processes, and compatibility with CMOS electronics for integrated receiver circuits.

Key Characteristics:

  • Spectral response: 400-1100nm (peak efficiency 800-900nm)
  • Quantum efficiency: 70-90% at 905nm
  • Gain: 10-100 (linear mode) or 100-1000 (Geiger mode approaching SPAD)
  • Response time: 0.5-5 nanoseconds (depending on active area)
  • Dark current: 1-100 nA (temperature dependent)
  • Operating voltage: 100-400V
  • Cost: $5-50 per detector (volume pricing)

Temperature Sensitivity: APD gain and breakdown voltage vary significantly with temperature (approximately 0.1%/°C). Automotive LiDAR systems must implement temperature compensation through voltage adjustment or algorithmic calibration to maintain consistent performance across -40°C to +85°C operating range.

4.2.3 InGaAs APDs for 1550nm LiDAR

Indium Gallium Arsenide (InGaAs) APDs enable detection at 1550nm where silicon is completely insensitive. InGaAs is a III-V compound semiconductor with a bandgap engineered for near-infrared sensitivity, widely used in fiber-optic telecommunications and now adapted for automotive LiDAR.

Key Characteristics:

  • Spectral response: 900-1700nm (optimized for 1550nm telecom band)
  • Quantum efficiency: 70-85% at 1550nm
  • Gain: 10-40 typical (limited by excess noise factor)
  • Response time: 1-10 nanoseconds
  • Dark current: 10-500 nA (higher than Si-APD, temperature sensitive)
  • Operating voltage: 50-90V
  • Cost: $50-500 per detector (5-10× more than Si-APD)

Excess Noise Challenges: InGaAs APDs exhibit higher excess noise factor (F) compared to silicon, limiting practical gain to 10-40×. The noise arises from statistical variations in the avalanche process, particularly the difference in ionization coefficients between electrons and holes. Higher noise reduces detection sensitivity, partially offsetting the benefit of internal gain.

4.3 Single Photon Avalanche Diodes (SPAD)

Single Photon Avalanche Diodes represent the ultimate in optical sensitivity - devices capable of detecting individual photons. SPADs operate in Geiger mode, biased above the breakdown voltage, where a single photon can trigger a self-sustaining avalanche that produces a measurable current pulse.

4.3.1 Geiger Mode Operation

Unlike linear-mode APDs that operate just below breakdown voltage, SPADs are biased several volts above breakdown. In this regime, a single photon-generated carrier initiates an avalanche that rapidly spreads across the entire junction, producing a large current pulse (milliamps). This binary detection (photon detected or not) provides exceptional sensitivity but loses intensity information.

After a detection event, the SPAD must be "quenched" (bias voltage reduced) to stop the avalanche, then recharged for the next detection. This quenching process requires dedicated circuitry:

  • Passive Quenching: Simple resistor limits current, slower recovery (100-500ns)
  • Active Quenching: Electronic circuit rapidly reduces voltage, fast recovery (10-50ns)

4.3.2 SPAD Arrays for LiDAR

Modern automotive LiDAR leverages SPAD arrays - thousands to millions of individual SPADs integrated on a single silicon chip, each with dedicated quenching, timing, and readout circuits. This enables:

  • Photon Counting: Multiple SPADs detect photons from same laser pulse, improving SNR
  • Timing Resolution: Time-to-Digital Converters (TDC) measure photon arrival time with 10-100 picosecond precision
  • Histogram Building: Accumulate photon detections over multiple laser pulses to build timing histograms, extracting weak signals from noise
  • Flash LiDAR: Each SPAD becomes a pixel in a 3D imaging array

Leading SPAD Technologies:

  • STMicroelectronics FlightSense: 64×64 to 512×512 SPAD arrays for automotive and consumer applications
  • Sony DepthSense: Back-illuminated SPAD arrays with improved fill factor
  • ON Semiconductor (ams OSRAM): Automotive-qualified SPAD arrays for solid-state LiDAR

4.3.3 Advantages and Challenges

Advantages:

  • Ultimate sensitivity: single-photon detection capability
  • Picosecond timing resolution: sub-millimeter range accuracy
  • Digital output: direct compatibility with CMOS logic
  • CMOS fabrication: integrates with signal processing electronics
  • Scalability: arrays with millions of pixels possible

Challenges:

  • Dark Count Rate (DCR): Thermal noise generates false detections (1-1000 counts/sec per SPAD)
  • Afterpulsing: Trapped carriers cause false triggers after genuine detection
  • Dead Time: 10-500ns recovery time after detection reduces dynamic range
  • Fill Factor: Quenching/readout circuits occupy space, reducing light-sensitive area to 1-50%
  • Saturation: Cannot distinguish 1 photon from 10 photons in single event

4.4 Silicon Photomultipliers (SiPM)

Silicon Photomultipliers are arrays of SPADs connected in parallel with common output. Each SPAD operates in Geiger mode with passive quenching, and the combined output current is proportional to the number of SPADs that fire. This provides photon-counting capability with analog output - bridging SPAD and APD technologies.

4.4.1 Operating Concept

A SiPM consists of hundreds to thousands of individual SPAD cells (typically 10-100μm each) on a common silicon substrate. When photons arrive, multiple SPADs trigger, and their currents sum at the output. If 1000 SPADs each produce 1mA when triggered, detecting 100 photons produces ~100mA output - enabling photon counting while maintaining analog response.

Key Features:

  • Cell density: 100-10,000 cells/mm²
  • Photon Detection Efficiency (PDE): 30-70% (lower than single APD due to dead space)
  • Gain: 10^5 to 10^6 (product of SPAD gain and number of cells)
  • Dynamic range: Limited by number of cells (saturation when all cells fire)
  • Timing resolution: 50-500 picoseconds FWHM

4.4.2 SiPM Applications in LiDAR

SiPMs find applications in automotive LiDAR where high gain and photon-counting sensitivity are needed:

  • Long-Range Detection: Extremely weak returns from distant targets benefit from high SiPM gain
  • Flash LiDAR Receivers: Large-area SiPMs collect light from wide FOV illumination
  • Low-Cost Systems: SiPMs provide SPAD-like sensitivity at lower cost than custom SPAD arrays

4.6 Future Detector Technologies

Emerging detector technologies promise further improvements in sensitivity, integration, and cost:

  • 3D-Stacked SPAD Arrays: Vertical integration of SPAD sensors with CMOS processing for increased fill factor and functionality
  • Superconducting Nanowire Single-Photon Detectors (SNSPD): Ultimate sensitivity but requires cryogenic cooling - unlikely for automotive
  • Quantum Dot Photodetectors: Tunable spectral response through nanostructure engineering
  • Graphene Photodetectors: Broadband response and ultra-fast operation, but low quantum efficiency

4.7 Chapter Summary

Photodetector technology fundamentally shapes LiDAR performance. Silicon APDs provide cost-effective, high-efficiency detection at 905nm with moderate gain (10-100×), enabling the majority of automotive LiDAR systems. InGaAs APDs extend sensitivity to 1550nm at higher cost and noise, supporting long-range eye-safe LiDAR. SPADs offer ultimate single-photon sensitivity with picosecond timing but face challenges in dark count rate, fill factor, and dynamic range. SPAD arrays power flash LiDAR and advanced photon-counting systems. SiPMs bridge APD and SPAD technologies with high gain and analog output. The choice of detector technology - alongside wavelength and architecture - defines a LiDAR system's range, sensitivity, cost, and application suitability. Future advances in detector integration, noise reduction, and manufacturing efficiency will continue to push the boundaries of automotive LiDAR performance.
