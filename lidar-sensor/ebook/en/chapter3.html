Chapter 3: Wavelength Selection

905nm vs 1550nm - Critical Trade-offs in Automotive LiDAR

3.1 The Wavelength Decision

One of the most fundamental design choices in automotive LiDAR is the laser wavelength. Two wavelengths dominate the automotive market: 905nm (near-infrared) and 1550nm (short-wave infrared). This seemingly technical decision has profound implications for eye safety, detection range, component cost, atmospheric performance, and detector technology. Understanding these trade-offs is essential for LiDAR system design and evaluation.

3.1.1 The Electromagnetic Spectrum Context

Both 905nm and 1550nm fall within the infrared spectrum, invisible to human vision (which spans 380-750nm). This invisibility is intentional - visible lasers would create distracting light pollution and raise additional safety concerns. The infrared region offers several advantages: minimal interference with human vision, well-developed laser and detector technologies, and atmospheric transmission windows with low absorption.

3.2 905nm Near-Infrared Systems

The 905nm wavelength has been the workhorse of automotive LiDAR since Velodyne's pioneering systems. This choice was driven by mature technology, cost-effective components, and a balance of performance characteristics suitable for autonomous vehicle development.

3.2.1 Advantages of 905nm

Silicon Detector Compatibility: The overwhelming advantage of 905nm is detector technology. Silicon-based photodetectors (APDs and SPADs) exhibit peak quantum efficiency around 800-900nm, achieving 70-90% efficiency at 905nm. This means most incident photons are converted to electrical signal, maximizing sensitivity and range.

Low Component Cost: The telecommunications industry's decades of development in the 850nm band (for fiber optic data links) created a mature supply chain for 905nm laser diodes, APDs, and optical components. Automotive LiDAR leverages this ecosystem, benefiting from high-volume production and competitive pricing.

High Peak Power Availability: Edge-emitting laser diodes and VCSEL (Vertical-Cavity Surface-Emitting Laser) arrays at 905nm can achieve very high peak powers (tens to hundreds of watts) in nanosecond pulses. This high peak power, combined with efficient silicon detectors, enables long detection ranges (200m+) despite eye safety constraints.

Atmospheric Transmission: The 905nm wavelength sits in an atmospheric transmission window with minimal water vapor absorption. Light transmission through clear air approaches 99% per 100 meters, enabling long-range detection with minimal signal loss.

3.2.2 Limitations and Challenges

Eye Safety Constraints (Class 1M): The critical limitation of 905nm is eye safety. The human retina contains melanin pigments that strongly absorb 900nm light, and the cornea and lens are transparent at this wavelength. This combination means 905nm laser light reaches and can damage the light-sensitive retina. IEC 60825-1 safety standards therefore impose strict limits on permissible exposure.

Most 905nm automotive LiDAR achieves Class 1M classification - safe for naked eye viewing but potentially hazardous when viewed through optical instruments (binoculars, telescopes, camera zoom lenses). The "M" designation acknowledges this magnified beam hazard. While Class 1M is generally acceptable for automotive applications, it does impose constraints on laser power and thus maximum range.

Solar Interference: The sun emits significant energy around 905nm. Automotive LiDAR systems operating in direct sunlight must distinguish weak LiDAR returns from intense solar background. This requires temporal filtering (narrow pulse detection), spectral filtering (narrow optical bandpass filters), and sophisticated signal processing. Performance can degrade in bright sunlight, particularly for long-range detection of low-reflectivity objects.

  • Velodyne Alpha Prime: 128-channel mechanical LiDAR, 300m range @ 10% reflectivity, Class 1M safety
  • Ouster OS2-128: Digital 905nm mechanical LiDAR, 240m range, uniform angular resolution
  • Innoviz InnovizTwo: MEMS solid-state, 250m range, automotive-grade qualification
  • Livox Horizon: Non-repetitive scanning pattern, cost-optimized 905nm system

3.3 1550nm Short-Wave Infrared Systems

The 1550nm wavelength represents the premium choice for automotive LiDAR where maximum range, true eye safety, and premium performance justify higher costs. Originally developed for fiber-optic telecommunications, 1550nm technology is mature but more expensive than silicon-based 905nm systems.

3.3.1 Advantages of 1550nm

True Class 1 Eye Safety: The revolutionary advantage of 1550nm is eye safety. At this wavelength, the human cornea and lens (the eye's front optical elements) absorb virtually all incident light. Less than 1% of 1550nm light reaches the retina, and what does arrive is further attenuated by water in the vitreous humor. The result: 1550nm lasers can operate at 10-40× higher power than 905nm while achieving true Class 1 safety classification - safe under all conditions including direct viewing with optical instruments.

This safety margin enables aggressive laser power scaling to achieve exceptional range. Luminar's 1550nm LiDAR demonstrates 500m detection of vehicles and 250m+ detection of pedestrians - far exceeding 905nm capabilities.

Reduced Solar Background: Solar radiation is substantially lower at 1550nm compared to 905nm (approximately 50% reduction in solar spectral irradiance). This improves signal-to-noise ratio in bright sunlight, enhancing detection reliability in harsh lighting conditions. The atmospheric transmission window at 1550nm is also excellent, with minimal water vapor absorption.

Superior Range Performance: The combination of higher permissible laser power, good atmospheric transmission, and reduced solar background enables 1550nm systems to achieve 30-50% greater range than comparable 905nm systems. For highway autonomous driving requiring 300m+ detection distances, this advantage can be decisive.

3.3.2 Limitations and Challenges

Detector Technology and Cost: The primary disadvantage of 1550nm is detector technology. Silicon photodetectors are blind at 1550nm - quantum efficiency drops to near zero beyond 1100nm. Instead, 1550nm LiDAR requires InGaAs (Indium Gallium Arsenide) APDs or alternative III-V semiconductor detectors. InGaAs APDs are significantly more expensive than silicon APDs, with per-unit costs 5-10× higher. This cost differential impacts overall system economics.

Laser Source Cost: While 1550nm lasers benefit from telecommunications industry development, high-peak-power pulsed lasers for automotive LiDAR remain more expensive than 905nm VCSELs and edge-emitting diodes. Fiber lasers and semiconductor lasers capable of the required pulse energy (microjoules to millijoules) are specialized components with higher costs.

Quantum Efficiency: Even the best InGaAs APDs achieve 70-80% quantum efficiency at 1550nm, comparable to silicon at 905nm. However, InGaAs detectors exhibit higher noise (dark current) and require more sophisticated thermal management, potentially offsetting some sensitivity advantages.

Component Ecosystem: The 1550nm supply chain, while mature for telecommunications, is smaller and more expensive than the massive 905nm ecosystem. Optical components (lenses, filters, beam splitters) optimized for 1550nm are less commodity-priced than their 905nm equivalents.

3.3.3 Leading 1550nm LiDAR Systems

  • Aeva 4D LiDAR: 1550nm FMCW system with integrated velocity measurement, targeting 300m+ range
  • AEye iDAR: Agile 1550nm system with intelligent scanning and perception-driven beam control

3.5 Application-Specific Recommendations

Choose 905nm when:

  • Cost is a primary constraint (ADAS, mass-market autonomy)
  • Range requirements are 200m or less (urban autonomous driving)
  • Proven, mature technology with extensive field data is required
  • Multiple LiDAR sensors are needed (cost-constrained redundancy)
  • Supply chain maturity and component availability are critical

Choose 1550nm when:

  • Maximum range is critical (highway autonomy, 300m+ detection)
  • True Class 1 eye safety is required (regulatory or marketing)
  • Premium performance justifies higher costs (luxury vehicles, robotaxis)
  • Operation in bright sunlight with low-reflectivity targets is common
  • Differentiation through superior sensor performance is valued

3.6 Future Wavelength Technologies

Emerging research explores alternative wavelengths and multi-wavelength approaches. Some concepts include:

  • 940nm: Slightly better eye safety than 905nm while maintaining silicon detector compatibility, but still Class 1M
  • 1060nm: Potential compromise wavelength, but falls in water absorption band with poor atmospheric transmission
  • Dual-Wavelength Systems: Combining 905nm for cost-effective coverage and 1550nm for long-range forward detection
  • SWIR Imaging (1000-2500nm): Potential for FMCW systems to leverage different wavelengths for multi-modal sensing

3.7 Chapter Summary

Wavelength selection - 905nm versus 1550nm - fundamentally shapes automotive LiDAR performance, cost, and safety characteristics. The 905nm near-infrared wavelength leverages mature silicon detector technology and cost-effective laser sources to deliver 200-300m range at accessible price points, making it the choice for mass-market autonomous driving. However, eye safety constraints limit laser power to Class 1M levels.

The 1550nm short-wave infrared wavelength enables true Class 1 eye safety through corneal absorption, permitting 10-40× higher laser power. This translates to exceptional range (300-500m), superior solar background rejection, and premium performance. The cost penalty from InGaAs detectors and specialized components is justified in applications demanding maximum capability.

The market is bifurcating: 905nm dominates cost-sensitive applications and urban autonomy, while 1550nm captures premium segments requiring long-range highway autonomy. As autonomous vehicles proliferate and costs decline, both wavelengths will likely coexist, each optimized for specific performance and cost requirements.
