Lending and Borrowing

弘益人間 - Democratizing Access to Capital

3.1 Introduction to DeFi Lending

Decentralized lending protocols represent one of DeFi's most valuable use cases, enabling anyone to access credit without credit checks, paperwork, or intermediaries. Unlike traditional lending where banks assess your creditworthiness and maintain custody of collateral, DeFi lending is permissionless, over-collateralized, and trustlessly enforced by smart contracts.

By 2026, DeFi lending has matured into a $80+ billion market, with protocols like Aave V4, Compound v3, and Morpho Blue offering sophisticated features including cross-chain borrowing, credit delegation, and AI-powered risk management. These protocols have facilitated over $500 billion in loans since inception.

Why DeFi Lending Matters
  • For Lenders: Earn yield on idle crypto assets (typically 2-15% APY depending on market conditions)
  • For Borrowers: Access liquidity without selling your crypto holdings, enable leveraged trading, or obtain working capital
  • For Humanity: Democratizes access to capital markets, especially valuable in countries with restrictive banking or high inflation

3.2 How DeFi Lending Works

3.2.1 The Basic Model

DeFi lending operates on a simple principle:

  1. Suppliers (Lenders): Deposit assets into lending pools and receive interest-bearing tokens (e.g., aTokens on Aave, cTokens on Compound)
  2. Borrowers: Deposit collateral and borrow assets up to a certain loan-to-value (LTV) ratio
  3. Interest Rates: Dynamically adjust based on supply and demand (utilization rate)
  4. Liquidations: Automated when collateral value falls below liquidation threshold

3.2.2 Over-Collateralization

Unlike traditional loans, DeFi loans are over-collateralized. This means borrowers must deposit collateral worth more than the loan amount. Why?

  • No Credit History: Wallets are pseudonymous; no credit scores exist
  • Price Volatility: Crypto assets are highly volatile; extra collateral protects lenders
  • Instant Liquidation: Over-collateralization enables automated, trustless liquidations

Example: Borrowing on Aave V4

Starting Position:

  • You own 10 ETH at $2,000 each = $20,000 value
  • You want USDC for expenses but don't want to sell ETH

Borrowing Process:

  • Deposit 10 ETH as collateral into Aave
  • Aave gives you aETH tokens (receipt representing your deposit)
  • ETH has 80% max LTV on Aave V4
  • Maximum borrow: $20,000 × 80% = $16,000 USDC
  • Safe borrow (to avoid liquidation): ~$12,000 USDC (60% LTV)

Result:

  • You now have $12,000 USDC to spend
  • Your 10 ETH continues earning supply APY
  • You pay borrow APY on the $12,000 USDC
  • Your health factor is ~1.33 (safe zone)

3.3 Key Concepts and Metrics

3.3.1 Loan-to-Value (LTV) Ratio

LTV = Borrowed Value / Collateral Value × 100%

Maximum LTV varies by asset:

  • Blue Chip Assets (ETH, WBTC): 75-80% max LTV
  • Major Altcoins: 50-70% max LTV
  • Stablecoins: 80-90% max LTV (when borrowing other stablecoins)
  • Volatile/Risky Assets: 30-50% max LTV or not supported

3.3.2 Health Factor

Health Factor = (Collateral × Liquidation Threshold) / Borrowed Value

The health factor indicates how close a position is to liquidation:

  • HF > 1.5: Safe zone, low liquidation risk
  • HF = 1.2-1.5: Moderate risk, monitor closely
  • HF = 1.0-1.2: High risk, consider adding collateral or repaying debt
  • HF < 1.0: Position is being liquidated
Critical Warning: During extreme volatility (like March 2020 or May 2021), prices can crash faster than you can respond. Always maintain a health factor above 1.5, preferably above 2.0, especially during uncertain market conditions.

3.3.3 Interest Rate Models

DeFi protocols use utilization-based interest rate models. As more of a pool's assets are borrowed (higher utilization), rates increase to incentivize more supply.

Utilization Rate = Total Borrowed / Total Supplied

Typical interest rate curves:

  • 0-80% Utilization: Gradual rate increase
  • 80-90% Utilization: Steeper increase (optimal zone)
  • 90-100% Utilization: Exponential increase (kink model)

This prevents liquidity crises by making it very expensive to borrow when reserves are low, ensuring suppliers can always withdraw.

3.4 Aave V4: The Leading Lending Protocol

Aave has evolved from ETHLend (2017) to become the dominant DeFi lending protocol with $12+ billion TVL in 2026. Aave V4 represents the cutting edge of decentralized lending.

3.4.1 Aave V4 Key Features

  • Multi-Chain Support: Ethereum, Arbitrum, Optimism, Base, Polygon, Avalanche, and 10+ other chains
  • Isolation Mode: New or risky assets can be listed with strict caps, protecting the core protocol
  • E-Mode (Efficiency Mode): Higher LTV (up to 97%) for correlated assets (e.g., borrow USDC against USDT)
  • Supply/Borrow Caps: Risk mitigation through maximum exposure limits per asset
  • Siloed Assets: Certain assets can only be used as collateral in isolation
  • Liquidation Mechanisms:
    • Standard liquidations: Liquidators repay debt for discounted collateral
    • Dutch auction: Price decreases until liquidator bids
    • Backstop module: Protocol-owned reserves as last resort

3.4.2 Flash Loans: DeFi's Innovation

Flash loans are uncollateralized loans that must be borrowed and repaid within a single transaction. If the loan isn't repaid, the entire transaction reverts, making them risk-free for lenders.

Flash Loan Use Cases:

  1. Arbitrage: Exploit price differences across DEXs instantly
  2. Collateral Swap: Change collateral type without repaying loan
  3. Liquidation: Liquidate positions without upfront capital
  4. Refinancing: Move debt between protocols in one transaction

3.4.3 Credit Delegation

Aave's credit delegation enables users to delegate borrowing power to others. This creates a peer-to-peer credit market on top of the pool-based model:

  • Depositor (Delegator) maintains collateral in Aave
  • Borrower (Delegatee) can borrow against delegator's collateral
  • Typically requires off-chain legal agreement or social trust
  • Enables institutional credit lines and DAOs to borrow

3.5 Other Major Lending Protocols

3.5.1 Compound v3

Compound pioneered DeFi lending in 2018 and evolved to v3 with significant improvements:

  • Single Borrow Asset: Each market has one base asset (e.g., USDC) that can be borrowed
  • Multiple Collateral: Support various collateral types (ETH, WBTC, COMP, etc.)
  • Improved Capital Efficiency: Higher LTVs with better risk management
  • Lower Gas Costs: Optimized contracts reduce transaction fees

3.5.2 Morpho Blue

Morpho is a next-generation protocol that improves capital efficiency by peer-to-peer matching:

  • When lenders and borrowers match, they get better rates than pool average
  • Falls back to underlying pools (Aave, Compound) when no match
  • Lenders earn higher APY, borrowers pay lower APY
  • Seamlessly integrated - users don't need to manage matches

3.5.3 Euler Finance

Euler focuses on permissionless listing and risk-isolated markets:

  • Any ERC20 token can create a lending market
  • Risk-adjusting collateral factors algorithmically
  • Reactive interest rates respond to market volatility
  • MEV-resistant liquidations via Dutch auctions

3.6 Liquidations: Protecting the Protocol

Liquidations are automatic processes that protect lenders when borrowers' collateral falls below safe levels.

3.6.1 Liquidation Process

  1. Health Factor Drops Below 1: Position becomes liquidatable
  2. Liquidator Identifies Position: Bots constantly monitor for unhealthy positions
  3. Partial Liquidation: Liquidator repays portion of debt (typically 50%)
  4. Liquidation Bonus: Liquidator receives collateral + bonus (typically 5-10% discount)
  5. Position Stabilizes: Health factor returns above 1, or full liquidation occurs

3.6.2 Avoiding Liquidation

Best Practices:

  • Maintain health factor above 2.0 (some use 3.0+ for peace of mind)
  • Set up monitoring alerts (email, Telegram, Discord bots)
  • Keep extra funds available to add collateral quickly
  • Consider stable assets less prone to volatility
  • Understand that "black swan" events can cause rapid liquidations
  • Use E-Mode only with truly correlated assets
Real-World Example: During the March 2020 crash, ETH dropped 50% in hours. Many highly leveraged positions were liquidated, with some users losing 30-50% of their collateral to liquidation penalties and gas wars. Network congestion prevented many from adding collateral in time.

3.7 Interest Rate Strategies

3.7.1 For Lenders

Maximize yield while managing risk:

  • Stablecoin Lending: Lower but stable returns (2-8% APY typically), minimal price risk
  • ETH/WBTC Lending: Medium returns (1-5% APY) plus asset appreciation potential
  • Altcoin Lending: Higher APY (5-20%+) but increased smart contract and asset risk
  • Rate Shopping: Compare rates across Aave, Compound, Morpho, and other protocols
  • Auto-Compounding: Use yield aggregators or manually claim and reinvest

3.7.2 For Borrowers

Strategic borrowing approaches:

  • Cash Flow: Borrow stablecoins for expenses while keeping crypto exposure
  • Leverage: Borrow to increase crypto position (high risk - use cautiously)
  • Short Position: Borrow and sell an asset you believe will decrease
  • Yield Farming: Borrow at low rates, deploy to higher-yielding strategies
  • Tax Optimization: Borrowing isn't a taxable event (unlike selling), useful for accessing liquidity without capital gains tax

3.8 Advanced Strategies

3.8.1 Leveraged Yield Farming

Loop Strategy Example

  1. Deposit 10 ETH in Aave (earning 3% supply APY)
  2. Borrow $15,000 USDC (paying 5% borrow APY)
  3. Swap USDC to ETH, get ~7.5 ETH
  4. Deposit 7.5 ETH in Aave
  5. Borrow more USDC and repeat

Result: 2-3x leverage on ETH price movements + amplified APY earnings

Risk: 2-3x leverage on losses too; liquidation risk significantly increased

3.8.2 Recursive Borrowing

Some protocols enable recursive borrowing within the same asset:

  • Deposit USDC, borrow USDC, redeposit, repeat
  • Each cycle increases total positions and liquidity mining rewards
  • Net APY depends on spread between supply/borrow rates + incentives
  • Capital intensive and gas expensive, but can be profitable

3.9 Risks in DeFi Lending

  • Smart Contract Risk: Bugs can lead to loss of funds (Euler hack: $200M in 2023)
  • Liquidation Risk: Volatility can trigger unexpected liquidations
  • Oracle Failure: Manipulated or failed oracles can cause incorrect liquidations
  • Bank Run Risk: High utilization may prevent withdrawals temporarily
  • Governance Risk: Malicious governance proposals could change protocol parameters
  • Regulatory Risk: Future regulations may restrict lending protocols

3.10 Future of DeFi Lending (2026+)

Emerging trends shaping the future:

  • Under-Collateralized Loans: Using zero-knowledge proofs for credit scores without revealing identity
  • Real-World Asset Lending: Using tokenized real estate, invoices, or equipment as collateral
  • AI Risk Management: Machine learning models for dynamic risk parameters
  • Cross-Chain Lending: Collateral on one chain, borrow on another via bridges
  • Insurance Integration: Built-in coverage against smart contract exploits and liquidations
  • Institutional Adoption: Compliance features enabling traditional finance participation
