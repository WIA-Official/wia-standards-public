Chapter 7: Blue Carbon and Climate

弘益人間 (Hongik Ingan) - "Benefit All Humanity"

Blue carbon—the carbon captured by coastal and marine ecosystems—represents one of nature's most powerful climate solutions. Mangroves sequester carbon at rates 4-5 times higher than terrestrial forests, offering triple benefits: climate mitigation, coastal protection, and sustainable financing for conservation.

7.1 Blue Carbon Fundamentals

Blue carbon ecosystems—mangroves, seagrass meadows, and salt marshes—are disproportionately important for global carbon cycling despite covering less than 2% of ocean area.

Blue Carbon Ecosystem Comparison

Ecosystem Global Area (km²) Carbon Burial (tons C/ha/year) Total Carbon Stock (tons C/ha) Contribution to Marine Burial
Mangroves 142,500 3-5 400-1,200 10-15%
Seagrass Meadows 300,000 1-3 200-500 10-18%
Salt Marshes 54,000 2-4 300-700 2-5%
Total Blue Carbon ~500,000 - - 25-40%
Tropical Rainforest (comparison) ~6,000,000 0.7-1.5 100-250 N/A

Key Insight: Despite occupying less than 0.5% of the seafloor, blue carbon ecosystems contribute 50% of all carbon burial in marine sediments. Mangroves alone bury an estimated 22-24 million tons of carbon annually—equivalent to removing 80-90 million tons of CO₂ from the atmosphere each year.

Why Mangroves Are Carbon Champions

Unique Carbon Sequestration Mechanisms:

High Primary Productivity:
├── Net primary production: 10-25 tons dry matter/ha/year
├── Year-round growth in tropical climate
├── Efficient CO₂ uptake through leaves
└── Rapid biomass accumulation

Below-Ground Dominance:
├── 60-70% of total carbon in soil/sediment
├── Anaerobic conditions prevent decomposition
├── Continuous sediment accretion buries carbon
└── Stable storage for centuries to millennia

Tidal Pump Mechanism:
├── Tides import organic matter from ocean
├── Export of dissolved organic carbon (DOC)
├── Net carbon trapping in sediments
└── Calcium carbonate precipitation (minor)

Longevity:
├── Carbon storage stability: 1,000+ years in deep sediments
├── Minimal methane emissions (unlike freshwater wetlands)
├── Resistant to fire (unlike terrestrial forests)
└── Continuous accumulation over time

7.2 Measuring Mangrove Carbon Stocks

Accurate carbon accounting is essential for climate reporting, project certification, and carbon credit generation.

Carbon Pool Inventory Methods

Carbon Pool % of Total Measurement Method Sampling Effort
Soil Organic Carbon (0-100cm) 50-70% Core sampling + LOI or elemental analysis 15-20 cores per stratum
Above-Ground Biomass 20-30% Allometric equations from DBH, height All trees in 10-20 plots (100m²)
Below-Ground Biomass (roots) 15-25% Root:shoot ratios or excavation Derived or 5-10 root cores
Dead Wood 2-5% Line-intercept or quadrat sampling 10-15 transects or quadrats
Litter 1-2% Quadrat collection and weighing 15-20 quadrats (0.25m²)

Soil Carbon Sampling Protocol (Detailed)

Standard Soil Sampling for Blue Carbon Projects:

Equipment:
├── Soil corer: Russian peat corer or push corer (1m length, 5cm diameter)
├── Sample bags: Ziplock bags labeled with depth intervals
├── Measuring tape: For depth measurements
├── GPS: For core location coordinates
├── Field datasheet: Record metadata
└── Cooler: Keep samples cool until processing

Sampling Design:
├── Stratification: By vegetation type, elevation, disturbance history
├── Sample size: Minimum 15 cores per stratum (for 10% precision at 95% CI)
├── Core depth: 0-100cm (or to refusal)
├── Core replication: Triplicate cores at each location
└── Spacing: Systematic or stratified random

Field Procedure:
1. Navigate to sampling point (pre-determined GPS coordinates)
2. Clear surface vegetation and litter
3. Insert corer vertically, rotate and extract
4. Measure and record total core length
5. Section core into depth intervals:
   ├── 0-15 cm
   ├── 15-30 cm
   ├── 30-50 cm
   ├── 50-75 cm
   └── 75-100 cm
6. Place sections in labeled bags
7. Record observations: color, texture, roots, shells
8. Store samples in cooler
9. Repeat for triplicate cores (2-5 meters apart)

Laboratory Analysis:

1. Sample Preparation:
   ├── Dry at 60°C for 48-72 hours until constant weight
   ├── Weigh dried sample (for bulk density calculation)
   ├── Homogenize by grinding or sieving
   └── Subsample for carbon analysis

2. Bulk Density Calculation:
   BD (g/cm³) = Dry weight (g) / Volume (cm³)
   Volume = π × r² × depth
   where r = core radius (2.5cm for 5cm diameter core)

3. Organic Carbon Analysis:

   Method A: Loss-on-Ignition (LOI)
   ├── Weigh 10-20g dried sample
   ├── Combust at 450°C for 4-6 hours
   ├── Reweigh to determine mass loss
   ├── Organic Matter % = (Mass loss / Initial mass) × 100
   └── Organic Carbon % ≈ Organic Matter % × 0.4 (conversion factor)

   Method B: Elemental Analysis (more accurate)
   ├── Remove carbonates if present (HCl treatment)
   ├── Combust sample in elemental analyzer
   ├── Directly measure carbon content (%)
   └── Preferred for carbon credit projects

4. Carbon Stock Calculation:
   Carbon Stock (tons C/ha) = BD × Depth × %C × 100

   Example:
   BD = 0.5 g/cm³
   Depth = 30 cm (0-30cm layer)
   %C = 15%
   Carbon Stock = 0.5 × 30 × 0.15 × 100 = 225 tons C/ha

7.3 Carbon Sequestration Rates and Accretion

Understanding carbon sequestration rates—the annual increase in carbon stocks—is critical for projecting climate benefits and generating carbon credits.

Typical Sequestration Rates by Mangrove Age

Carbon Accumulation Timeline (Restored Mangrove):

Year 1-5 (Establishment Phase):
├── Above-ground biomass: 1-3 tons C/ha/year
├── Below-ground biomass: 0.5-1.5 tons C/ha/year
├── Soil carbon: 0.5-1.5 tons C/ha/year
└── Total sequestration: 2-6 tons C/ha/year (7-22 tons CO₂/ha/year)

Year 5-15 (Rapid Growth Phase):
├── Above-ground biomass: 3-6 tons C/ha/year
├── Below-ground biomass: 1-2 tons C/ha/year
├── Soil carbon: 1-2.5 tons C/ha/year
└── Total sequestration: 5-10 tons C/ha/year (18-37 tons CO₂/ha/year)

Year 15-30 (Maturation Phase):
├── Above-ground biomass: 1-3 tons C/ha/year (slowing)
├── Below-ground biomass: 0.5-1 tons C/ha/year
├── Soil carbon: 2-4 tons C/ha/year (dominant pool)
└── Total sequestration: 3.5-8 tons C/ha/year (13-29 tons CO₂/ha/year)

Year 30+ (Mature Forest Equilibrium):
├── Above-ground biomass: ~0 (turnover equilibrium)
├── Below-ground biomass: ~0 (equilibrium)
├── Soil carbon: 2-5 tons C/ha/year (continuous burial)
└── Total sequestration: 2-5 tons C/ha/year (7-18 tons CO₂/ha/year)

Note: Rates highly variable depending on species, climate, tidal regime,
sediment supply, and site history.

7.4 Blue Carbon Markets and Finance

Carbon markets provide financial mechanisms to incentivize mangrove restoration and protection through the sale of verified emission reductions (carbon credits).

Carbon Market Landscape

Market Type Regulation Credit Prices (USD/ton CO₂) Standards Project Types
Compliance Markets Government-mandated (cap-and-trade) $15-$80 EU ETS, California, Regional Limited coastal wetlands eligibility
Voluntary Markets Corporate/individual choice $5-$50 (avg $15-25) Verra VCS, Gold Standard, ACR Mangrove restoration, conservation
Nature-Based Solutions Premium Voluntary, high-quality projects $20-$100+ Verified + co-benefits Mangroves with community benefits

Verra VCS VM0033 Methodology Overview

VM0033: Methodology for Tidal Wetland and Seagrass Restoration is the primary methodology for mangrove restoration carbon projects.

Eligible Activities:
├── Restoration of degraded/converted mangroves
├── Hydrological restoration enabling natural regeneration
├── Active planting on suitable sites
└── Avoided conversion (protection component)

Crediting Period:
├── Options: 30 years (fixed) or 20 years (renewable twice)
├── Total maximum: 60 years with renewals

Baseline Scenarios:
├── Business-as-usual: Continued degradation or no regrowth
├── Baseline carbon stocks: Pre-restoration inventory
├── Without-project scenario: Required demonstration

Carbon Pools (Monitored):
├── Above-ground biomass (trees, shrubs)
├── Below-ground biomass (roots)
├── Soil organic carbon (minimum 0-30cm, ideally 0-100cm)
├── Dead wood (optional if <10% of total)
└── Litter (optional if <5% of total)

Monitoring Requirements:
├── Above/below-ground biomass: Every 5 years minimum
├── Soil carbon: Every 10 years minimum
├── Plot-based sampling: Statistical rigor (90% CI, 10% precision)
└── Quality assurance/quality control (QA/QC) procedures

Leakage Assessment:
├── Activity-shifting leakage: Displaced degradation activities
├── Market leakage: Negligible for most projects
└── Deduction: Typically 10-20% of gross removals

Permanence/Buffer:
├── Risk assessment: Low to high risk categories
├── Buffer contribution: 10-30% of credits withheld
└── Pooled buffer account: Insurance against reversals

Credit Issuance:
Net Verified Carbon Units (VCUs) =
  (Actual Removals - Baseline - Leakage) × (1 - Buffer %)

Developing a certified blue carbon project requires systematic planning, rigorous methodology application, and third-party verification.

Phase Activities Duration Cost (USD)

Minimum Project Size: Due to high upfront costs, projects typically require minimum 50-100 hectares and projected 10,000-20,000 credits over 10 years to be financially viable. Smaller projects should consider aggregation or simplified standards.

7.6 Revenue Projections and Economics

Understanding potential revenue streams and economic viability guides project design and benefit-sharing arrangements.

Example Project Financial Model (100 hectares, 30 years)

Project Parameters:
├── Area: 100 hectares
├── Baseline: Degraded land, minimal carbon
├── Restoration approach: Hydrological restoration + supplemental planting
├── Implementation cost: $5,000/ha = $500,000 total
├── Monitoring & verification: $50,000 every 5 years
└── Credit price: $20/ton CO₂ (conservative)

Carbon Removals (tons CO₂):
Year 1-5:   100 ha × 15 tons CO₂/ha/year × 5 years = 7,500
Year 6-10:  100 ha × 25 tons CO₂/ha/year × 5 years = 12,500
Year 11-15: 100 ha × 22 tons CO₂/ha/year × 5 years = 11,000
Year 16-20: 100 ha × 18 tons CO₂/ha/year × 5 years = 9,000
Year 21-25: 100 ha × 15 tons CO₂/ha/year × 5 years = 7,500
Year 26-30: 100 ha × 13 tons CO₂/ha/year × 5 years = 6,500
Total: 54,000 tons CO₂

Deductions:
├── Leakage (15%): -8,100 tons CO₂
├── Buffer (15%): -6,885 tons CO₂
└── Net Credits Issued: 39,015 VCUs

Revenue (@ $20/VCU):
├── Total Revenue: 39,015 × $20 = $780,300
├── Development & Verification Costs: -$350,000
├── Implementation Costs: -$500,000
└── Net Revenue: -$69,700 (negative without co-benefits!)

Alternative Scenario (@ $35/VCU with premium):
├── Total Revenue: 39,015 × $35 = $1,365,525
├── Costs: -$850,000
└── Net Revenue: $515,525

Revenue Distribution (example):
├── 40%: Community (livelihoods, infrastructure)
├── 30%: Project operations and maintenance
├── 20%: Implementing organization
└── 10%: Reserve fund for contingencies

Financial Viability Factors:

  • Credit prices significantly impact viability; nature-based solutions premiums ($30-50/ton) often necessary
  • Larger projects (500-1,000+ ha) achieve better economics through economies of scale
  • Combining restoration with avoided deforestation/degradation increases credit volume
  • Stacking revenues (carbon + biodiversity credits + tourism) improves viability
  • Low-cost implementation (natural regeneration) critical for positive returns

Chapter Summary

Blue carbon ecosystems, particularly mangroves, are disproportionately important for global carbon cycling, storing 400-1,200 tons C/ha and sequestering 3-5 tons C/ha/year—rates 4-5 times higher than terrestrial forests. Despite covering <0.5% of the seafloor, they contribute 10-15% of marine carbon burial, sequestering 22-24 million tons carbon annually (80-90 million tons CO₂ equivalent).

Carbon accounting requires systematic measurement of five pools: soil organic carbon (50-70% of total), above-ground biomass (20-30%), below-ground biomass (15-25%), dead wood (2-5%), and litter (1-2%). The Verra VM0033 methodology provides the framework for certification, requiring monitoring every 5 years for biomass and 10 years for soil, with deductions for leakage (10-20%) and buffer contributions (10-30%).

Financial viability depends critically on credit prices (nature-based solutions premiums of $30-50/ton often necessary), project scale (minimum 50-100 ha economically viable), and implementation costs (natural regeneration preferred). Development costs total $150,000-400,000+ over first 10 years. Equitable benefit-sharing models allocate 30-80% of revenue to communities through direct payments, infrastructure, and stewardship support. Beyond carbon, mangroves provide critical climate adaptation benefits including coastal protection, sea-level rise resilience, and fisheries support. With 71 countries including blue carbon in NDCs and markets projected to grow from $300M to $2-5B by 2030, mangrove restoration offers increasingly viable climate finance.
