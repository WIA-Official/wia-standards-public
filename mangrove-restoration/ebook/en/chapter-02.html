Chapter 2: Importance of Mangroves

弘益人間 (Hongik Ingan) - "Benefit All Humanity"

Mangroves are not merely coastal trees—they are life-support systems for coastal communities, biodiversity hotspots, climate regulators, and economic engines. Their importance to human well-being and planetary health cannot be overstated.

2.2 Fisheries and Food Security

Mangroves are the nursery grounds for the ocean, supporting coastal fisheries that feed hundreds of millions of people and provide livelihoods for coastal communities worldwide.

Fisheries Dependency

  • Global Impact: 80% of global fish catch is directly or indirectly dependent on mangrove ecosystems
  • Commercial Species: Shrimp, crabs, fish (snappers, groupers, mullet, barramundi)
  • Productivity: Mangrove-associated fisheries yield 300-500 kg/ha/year
  • Economic Value: Commercial fisheries dependent on mangroves worth over $40 billion annually

Case Study: Philippines Mangrove-Fisheries Relationship

Research in the Philippines demonstrated that for every hectare of mangrove lost, commercial fish catch declined by 5-8% in adjacent waters. Conversely, mangrove restoration projects showed:

  • 30% increase in fish abundance within 5 years
  • 40% increase in commercial catch value within 7 years
  • Return on investment of 4:1 for restoration costs vs. fisheries benefits
  • Enhanced food security for 45 fishing families per 25 hectares restored

2.3 Coastal Protection and Climate Resilience

Mangroves serve as natural coastal defenses, protecting communities and infrastructure from storms, erosion, and sea-level rise. Their protective value becomes increasingly critical in the face of climate change.

Wave Attenuation Capacity

Wave Height Reduction Through Mangrove Stands:

100m mangrove width:  50-66% wave height reduction
200m mangrove width:  70-85% wave height reduction
500m mangrove width:  90-100% wave height reduction

Factors Affecting Attenuation:
├── Forest width and density
├── Tree height and diameter
├── Root structure complexity
├── Wave characteristics (height, period)
└── Water depth and tidal stage

Storm Protection Benefits

Mangrove Width Storm Surge Reduction Infrastructure Protection Value

500 meters 50-75 cm reduction $120,000/km coastline/year
1,000 meters 100-150 cm reduction $250,000/km coastline/year

Real-World Protection Examples

2004 Indian Ocean Tsunami:

  • Villages behind dense mangroves experienced significantly less damage and casualties
  • Mangrove areas reduced wave energy by 30-50%
  • Mortality rates were 1.7 times lower in mangrove-protected areas
  • Economic damages reduced by $83,000 per kilometer of mangrove

Hurricane Irma (2017) - Florida:

  • Mangroves prevented $1.5 billion in property damages
  • Protected over 600,000 people from flooding
  • Average reduction in property damage: $725 per hectare of mangrove

2.4 Blue Carbon: Climate Change Mitigation

Mangroves are among the most carbon-dense ecosystems on Earth, storing more carbon per unit area than terrestrial forests and playing a crucial role in global climate regulation.

Carbon Storage Comparison

Ecosystem Type Total Carbon Storage (tons C/ha) Sequestration Rate (tons CO₂/ha/year)
Mangroves 400-1,200 10-15
Tropical Rainforest 100-250 3-5
Temperate Forest 80-150 2-4
Seagrass Meadows 200-500 4-8
Salt Marshes 300-700 6-10

Blue Carbon Mechanisms

Mangrove Carbon Pools:

Above-ground Biomass: 30-40% of total carbon
├── Trunks and branches: 60-80 tons C/ha
├── Leaves and canopy: 5-15 tons C/ha
└── Wood debris: 5-10 tons C/ha

Below-ground Biomass: 25-35% of total carbon
├── Living roots: 40-80 tons C/ha
└── Dead root matter: 10-20 tons C/ha

Soil Carbon: 30-40% of total carbon (largest pool)
├── Surface sediment (0-30cm): 100-300 tons C/ha
├── Deep sediment (30-100cm): 200-500 tons C/ha
└── Peat deposits (where present): 500-2,000+ tons C/ha

Global Climate Impact:

  • Mangroves sequester 22-24 million tons CO₂ annually (current extent)
  • Store an estimated 20 billion tons of carbon globally
  • Deforestation releases 0.02-0.12 Pg C/year to atmosphere
  • Restoration potential: 0.5 Gt CO₂ additional sequestration by 2050

2.6 Water Quality and Filtration Services

Mangroves act as natural water treatment systems, filtering pollutants and nutrients from terrestrial runoff before they reach coral reefs and seagrass beds.

Economic Value of Water Filtration

A mangrove forest of 100 hectares can provide water filtration services valued at:

  • $200,000-$800,000 per year in avoided water treatment costs
  • Protection for downstream coral reefs worth $2-5 million in tourism value
  • Seagrass bed preservation supporting $500,000 in fisheries annually

2.7 Timber and Forest Products

Sustainable mangrove management provides valuable timber and non-timber forest products for local communities, supporting livelihoods when properly regulated.

Commercial Products from Mangroves

Product Category Specific Products Annual Value (USD/ha)
Timber Construction poles, charcoal, fuelwood $300-$1,500
Non-Timber Forest Products Honey, wax, tannins, dyes, medicines $150-$800
Fisheries Products Crabs, shrimp, fish, mollusks $2,000-$8,000
Tourism Ecotourism, recreation, education $500-$3,000

Sustainable Harvesting Guidelines:

  • Maximum annual timber harvest: 10-15% of standing stock
  • Minimum cutting diameter: 10-15 cm DBH (diameter at breast height)
  • Rotation period: 15-25 years for poles, 30-40 years for timber
  • Selective cutting maintaining forest structure and species diversity
  • Community-based management with benefit-sharing mechanisms

2.8 Cultural and Spiritual Significance

For many coastal communities, mangroves hold profound cultural, spiritual, and traditional significance beyond their economic value.

Traditional Knowledge and Uses

  • Sacred Sites: Many cultures designate specific mangrove areas as sacred or spiritually significant
  • Traditional Medicine: Over 50 mangrove species used in traditional healing practices
  • Cultural Identity: Mangrove-dependent communities maintain traditional fishing, harvesting, and navigation knowledge
  • Educational Value: Living laboratories for environmental education and ecological research

Indigenous Mangrove Management Examples:

  • Australia: Aboriginal communities manage mangroves using traditional burning and harvesting practices spanning 40,000+ years
  • Pacific Islands: Customary marine tenure systems protect mangroves as communal resources
  • Southeast Asia: Community forestry models sustainably harvest mangroves while maintaining ecosystem integrity

Chapter Summary

Mangroves provide extraordinary economic, ecological, and social value to coastal communities and the planet. Their economic value ranges from $20,000-$90,000 per hectare annually, with global benefits exceeding $9.3 billion per year. Critical services include fisheries support (80% of global catch dependency), coastal protection ($4.3B annually), blue carbon sequestration (22-24 million tons CO₂/year), and water quality improvement.

Beyond economics, mangroves support exceptional biodiversity as habitat for thousands of species, including many endangered animals. They serve as natural coastal defenses, reducing wave heights by 50-100% and preventing billions in storm damage. Their blue carbon capacity stores 400-1,200 tons C/ha, far exceeding terrestrial forests.

Mangroves also provide sustainable livelihoods through fisheries, timber, and tourism while holding profound cultural significance for coastal communities. As natural laboratories, they advance scientific understanding of coastal ecology and climate adaptation. The multifaceted importance of mangroves makes their conservation and restoration a global priority for sustainable development and climate resilience.
