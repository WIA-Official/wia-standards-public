Nutritional Requirements by Species

2.1 Canine Nutrition

Dogs (Canis familiaris) are facultative carnivores with considerable dietary flexibility developed through thousands of years of domestication. While their wolf ancestors were obligate carnivores, domestic dogs have evolved the ability to digest and utilize a wider variety of nutrients, including plant-based carbohydrates.

2.1.3 Breed-Specific Considerations

While AAFCO provides general guidelines, certain breeds have unique nutritional needs due to size, metabolism, and genetic predispositions.

Large and Giant Breeds (>25 kg adult weight):

  • Controlled Growth: Excess calcium (>1.5% DM) during puppyhood increases risk of developmental orthopedic disease
  • Joint Support: Glucosamine (500-1000 mg/day) and chondroitin sulfate (400-800 mg/day) may benefit joint health
  • Dilated Cardiomyopathy (DCM) Risk: Ensure adequate taurine (dogs on grain-free diets may require supplementation)
  • Lower Energy Density: 3,200-3,600 kcal/kg prevents overfeeding and rapid growth

Small and Toy Breeds (<10 kg adult weight):

  • Higher Metabolic Rate: Require 1.2-1.4× calories per kg compared to large breeds
  • Dental Health: Smaller kibble sizes and dental-specific formulations address dental disease prevalence
  • Hypoglycemia Risk: Puppies need frequent small meals (4-6 times daily) to maintain blood glucose

Brachycephalic Breeds (Bulldogs, Pugs, etc.):

  • Kibble Shape: Specially designed shapes facilitate grasping and chewing despite shortened muzzles
  • Weight Management: Obesity exacerbates breathing difficulties; strict calorie control essential

Adult Dogs (1-7 years, varies by breed):

  • Maintenance Focus: Balanced nutrition to maintain ideal body condition
  • Activity-Based Adjustment: Working dogs may require 2-5× RER, while sedentary pets need 1.2-1.4× RER
  • Neutered/Spayed Considerations: ~20% reduction in metabolic rate post-surgery; reduce calories accordingly

Senior Dogs (7+ years for large breeds, 10+ for small breeds):

  • Protein Maintenance: Higher protein (25-30% DM) helps preserve lean muscle mass despite lower activity
  • Joint Support: Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA+DHA 300-500 mg/day) for anti-inflammatory effects
  • Antioxidants: Enhanced vitamin E, vitamin C, and beta-carotene combat oxidative stress
  • Phosphorus Restriction: 0.2-0.4% DM for dogs with early kidney disease (consult veterinarian)

2.2 Feline Nutrition

Cats (Felis catus) are obligate carnivores with unique metabolic requirements that distinguish them fundamentally from dogs. Their evolution as desert-dwelling hunters has resulted in specific nutritional dependencies that must be met through diet.

2.2.1 Unique Feline Nutritional Requirements

Unlike dogs, cats lack certain metabolic pathways and have absolute dietary requirements for nutrients they cannot synthesize:

Taurine: An amino sulfonic acid essential for cardiac function, vision, and reproduction. Cats cannot synthesize adequate amounts from methionine and cysteine.

  • Requirement: Minimum 0.1% DM (dry food), 0.2% DM (canned food)
  • Deficiency Signs: Dilated cardiomyopathy, central retinal degeneration, reproductive failure
  • Heat Sensitivity: Processing reduces taurine content; manufacturers must compensate

Arginine: Essential for ammonia detoxification via the urea cycle. Even a single arginine-deficient meal can cause hyperammonemia.

  • Requirement: Minimum 1.04% DM (growth), 1.0% DM (adult)
  • Deficiency Signs: Lethargy, vomiting, neurological signs within hours

Arachidonic Acid: An omega-6 fatty acid that cats cannot synthesize from linoleic acid due to limited delta-6-desaturase activity.

  • Requirement: Minimum 0.02% DM
  • Sources: Animal tissues only; absent in plant materials

Vitamin A (Retinol): Cats lack the enzyme to convert beta-carotene to active vitamin A.

  • Requirement: Minimum 5,000 IU/kg DM
  • Sources: Liver, fish oils, fortified foods

Niacin (Vitamin B3): Cats have inefficient conversion of tryptophan to niacin.

  • Requirement: Minimum 60 mg/kg DM (4× dog requirement)

2.2.3 Feline Energy Metabolism and Protein Catabolism

Cats exhibit unique metabolic characteristics:

Obligate Gluconeogenesis: Even when fed high-carbohydrate diets, cats maintain elevated hepatic gluconeogenic enzyme activity. They preferentially catabolize amino acids for glucose production, making high protein intake essential.

Protein Sparing Effect Minimal: Unlike omnivores, cats do not significantly reduce protein catabolism when fed adequate non-protein calories. This necessitates continuous dietary protein provision.

Limited Amylase Activity: Cats have lower pancreatic amylase secretion compared to dogs, resulting in reduced starch digestion efficiency. Diets should limit carbohydrates to <10% DM for optimal health.

Adult Cats (1-10 years):

  • High Protein Maintenance: Minimum 26% DM, but 30-40% optimal for carnivorous metabolism
  • Moderate Fat: 15-25% DM provides energy density and palatability
  • Low Carbohydrate: <10% DM reduces obesity and diabetes risk
  • Hydration Support: Wet food (75-80% moisture) helps prevent urinary tract disease in cats with low thirst drive

Senior Cats (10+ years):

  • Enhanced Protein: 35-45% DM to offset age-related muscle wasting (unless kidney disease contraindicates)
  • Omega-3 Fatty Acids: EPA+DHA 200-400 mg/day for joint health and cognitive function
  • Phosphorus Moderation: 0.3-0.6% DM to reduce kidney workload
  • Antioxidant Support: Vitamin E (100-150 IU/kg), vitamin C, lutein for cellular health

2.2.5 Common Feline Dietary-Responsive Conditions

Feline Lower Urinary Tract Disease (FLUTD):

  • Target urine pH: 6.2-6.4 to prevent struvite crystal formation
  • Magnesium restriction: <0.1% DM
  • Increased moisture: Wet food preferred (75%+ moisture)
  • Moderate sodium: 0.3-0.6% DM to promote drinking

Diabetes Mellitus:

  • High protein: >40% DM to reduce carbohydrate-induced hyperglycemia
  • Low carbohydrate: <10% DM maximizes glycemic control
  • High fiber: 10-15% DM (if low-carb not feasible) to slow glucose absorption
  • Consistent feeding: Same time and amount daily to coordinate with insulin

Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD):

  • Moderate protein restriction: 26-32% DM (controversial; consult nephrologist)
  • Phosphorus restriction: 0.3-0.5% DM critical for slowing progression
  • Omega-3 supplementation: EPA+DHA 300-600 mg/day
  • Potassium supplementation: If hypokalemic (common in CKD cats)

2.3 Avian Nutrition

Pet birds represent diverse species with highly variable nutritional needs. Unlike manufactured diets for dogs and cats, many bird owners still rely on seed-based diets that are nutritionally inadequate.

2.3.1 Psittacine (Parrot Family) Nutrition

Parrots, including budgerigars, cockatiels, African greys, macaws, and cockatoos, are predominantly granivores with frugivorous tendencies.

Dietary Problems with Seed-Only Diets:

  • Vitamin A Deficiency: Most seeds lack carotenoids; leads to squamous metaplasia, respiratory infections
  • Calcium Deficiency: Seeds have inverted Ca:P ratio (~1:10); causes metabolic bone disease, egg binding
  • Amino Acid Imbalances: Deficient in lysine and methionine
  • Obesity: Sunflower seeds high in fat (>50%); selective feeding on fatty seeds common

Balanced Parrot Diet Composition:

  • 60-80% high-quality pelleted diet (formulated for species)
  • 15-30% fresh vegetables (dark leafy greens, carrots, sweet potato)
  • 5-10% fresh fruits (berries, apple, mango)
  • 0-10% healthy seeds/nuts (limited due to fat content)

2.3.2 Passerine (Songbird) Nutrition

Finches, canaries, and other small songbirds have different requirements:

  • Higher Protein During Breeding: 16-18% protein when raising chicks vs. 12-14% maintenance
  • Calcium Supplementation: Cuttlebone or mineral block essential for egg-laying females
  • Seed-Based Acceptable: Small passerines tolerate seed-based diets better than parrots when supplemented

2.4 Rabbit Nutrition

Rabbits (Oryctolagus cuniculus) are hindgut fermenters with a digestive system optimized for high-fiber, low-energy plant material. Their nutritional management differs fundamentally from carnivorous and omnivorous pets.

2.4.1 Fiber Requirements

Fiber is the most critical nutrient for rabbits, essential for gastrointestinal motility and preventing stasis:

  • Crude Fiber (Minimum): 18-22% DM
  • Acid Detergent Fiber (ADF): >20% DM preferred
  • Neutral Detergent Fiber (NDF): >28% DM optimal
  • Hay: Should constitute 80-90% of diet; timothy, orchard grass, or brome preferred

2.4.2 Unique Rabbit Digestive Physiology

Cecotrophy: Rabbits practice coprophagy, consuming cecotropes (soft, nutrient-rich fecal pellets) directly from the anus. These provide:

  • B vitamins synthesized by cecal bacteria
  • Microbial protein (high biological value)
  • Volatile fatty acids for additional energy

Calcium Metabolism: Unlike other mammals, rabbits absorb calcium proportional to dietary intake rather than based on need, then excrete excess via urine. This unique physiology means:

  • High calcium diets (>1.5% DM) can cause urolithiasis (bladder stones)
  • Alfalfa hay (1.2-1.5% calcium) should be limited in adult rabbits
  • Calcium carbonate precipitation in urine is normal (cloudy, beige urine)

2.5 Exotic Pet Nutrition

2.5.1 Reptile Nutrition Overview

Reptile nutritional requirements vary dramatically by species (herbivorous, carnivorous, omnivorous) and are complicated by ectothermic metabolism.

Herbivorous Reptiles (Iguanas, Tortoises):

  • High fiber: 18-25% crude fiber
  • Moderate protein: 15-20% (young), 10-15% (adult)
  • Ca:P ratio: 2:1 to 3:1 critical for shell and bone health
  • Vitamin D3: Requires UVB light exposure for synthesis (or dietary supplementation)

Carnivorous Reptiles (Snakes, Monitor Lizards):

  • Whole prey feeding preferred (provides complete nutrition including bones, organs)
  • High protein: 40-60% DM
  • Moderate fat: 10-30% DM depending on species and prey
  • Feeding frequency: Varies widely (weekly to monthly depending on size and species)

Chapter Summary

Five Key Takeaways:

  1. Species-Specific Metabolism: Dogs are facultative carnivores with dietary flexibility, while cats are obligate carnivores requiring taurine, arachidonic acid, preformed vitamin A, and high protein (26%+ DM) due to unique metabolic pathways.
  2. Life Stage Variations: Nutritional requirements change dramatically across life stages—puppies need 22.5%+ protein and controlled calcium for large breeds, while senior dogs benefit from higher protein (25-30%) to preserve muscle mass despite lower caloric needs.
  3. Breed Considerations: Large breed dogs require growth restriction (1.0-1.5% calcium max) to prevent orthopedic disease, while small breeds have 1.2-1.4× higher metabolic rates and risk hypoglycemia without frequent feeding.
  4. Herbivore Digestive Physiology: Rabbits and herbivorous birds require 18-22% fiber for gastrointestinal health through cecal fermentation, fundamentally different from carnivore digestive strategies optimized for protein catabolism.
  5. WIA-PET-009 Implementation: The standard's species-specific data models capture these diverse requirements through flexible schemas accommodating macronutrient ranges, micronutrient profiles, and specialized considerations (e.g., taurine for cats, fiber for rabbits).
