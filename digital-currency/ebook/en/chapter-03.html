CHAPTER 3

Stablecoins & Asset-Backed Currencies

Understanding the mechanisms, risks, and opportunities of cryptocurrencies designed to maintain stable value through various collateralization and algorithmic approaches.

3.1 The Stablecoin Revolution

Stablecoins represent one of the most important innovations in cryptocurrency, solving a fundamental problem: volatility. While Bitcoin and other cryptocurrencies have proven revolutionary as technologies and stores of value, their price volatility makes them impractical for everyday transactions and business operations.

A merchant cannot price goods in Bitcoin when the currency might fluctuate 10% in a day. Employees don't want salaries paid in assets that could lose 20% of their value before rent is due. Lenders cannot issue loans denominated in cryptocurrencies with unpredictable purchasing power.

Stablecoins solve this by pegging their value to stable assets—typically the US dollar, but also other fiat currencies, commodities, or baskets of assets. This stability enables stablecoins to function as:

  • Medium of exchange: Reliable pricing and payment settlement
  • Unit of account: Consistent value measurement
  • Store of value: Short to medium-term wealth preservation
  • DeFi collateral: Stable assets for lending and derivatives
  • Trading pairs: Cryptocurrency trading without fiat conversion

3.2 Types of Stablecoins

3.2.1 Fiat-Collateralized Stablecoins

The most straightforward approach: back each stablecoin token 1:1 with fiat currency held in reserve. For every $1 of stablecoin issued, $1 (or equivalent value) is held in a bank account or short-term securities.

How They Work

  1. Issuance: User deposits $100 USD → Issuer mints 100 stablecoin tokens
  2. Redemption: User burns 100 tokens → Issuer returns $100 USD
  3. Reserve Management: Issuer maintains reserves in cash, government bonds, or money market funds
  4. Audits: Regular attestations to verify reserves match outstanding tokens

Major Fiat-Collateralized Stablecoins

Stablecoin Issuer Market Cap Reserves
USDT (Tether) Tether Ltd ~$90B Cash, T-bills, corporate bonds
USDC Circle ~$25B Cash, short-term US Treasuries
BUSD Paxos (for Binance) ~$16B Cash, cash equivalents (NY regulated)
TUSD TrustToken ~$3B USD in segregated accounts

Advantages:

  • Simple, understandable mechanism
  • Stable peg maintenance (when properly reserved)
  • Fastest to redeem for fiat
  • Lower volatility risk

Risks:

  • Centralization: Single entity controls reserves
  • Counterparty risk: Trust in issuer's solvency and honesty
  • Regulatory risk: Potential for regulatory action against issuer
  • Reserve composition: Quality and liquidity of reserve assets matter
  • Auditing: Need for transparent, regular reserve verification

⚠️ The Tether Controversy

Tether (USDT), the largest stablecoin, has faced scrutiny over reserve transparency. Initially claiming 100% backing by USD in bank accounts, it later revealed reserves included commercial paper, secured loans, and other assets. In 2021, Tether paid $41M to settle CFTC charges over reserve misrepresentations. This highlighted the critical importance of transparent auditing and regulatory oversight for fiat-collateralized stablecoins.

3.2.2 Crypto-Collateralized Stablecoins

These stablecoins are backed by cryptocurrency collateral rather than fiat. To account for crypto volatility, they typically use over-collateralization—requiring $150-200 worth of cryptocurrency to mint $100 in stablecoins.

DAI: The Flagship Example

DAI, created by MakerDAO, is the most successful crypto-collateralized stablecoin. It maintains a peg to the US dollar through a system of smart contracts, collateralized debt positions (CDPs), and algorithmic incentives.

How DAI Works:

  1. Lock Collateral: User deposits $150 worth of ETH into a Maker Vault
  2. Mint DAI: User can mint up to $100 DAI (maintains 150% collateralization ratio)
  3. Pay Stability Fee: Interest accrues on borrowed DAI
  4. Repay & Unlock: User returns 100 DAI + fees to unlock their ETH
  5. Liquidation: If ETH price drops and collateralization falls below threshold (e.g., below 130%), vault is liquidated to maintain system solvency

Peg Stability Mechanisms:

  • Stability Fee (Interest Rate): Raised when DAI > $1 (reduces supply), lowered when DAI < $1 (increases supply)
  • DAI Savings Rate: Interest paid to DAI holders, creating demand when raised
  • Liquidations: Automatic collateral sales maintain system solvency
  • Emergency Shutdown: Can freeze system and allow proportional claim redemption

Advantages:

  • Decentralized: No single entity controls reserves
  • Transparent: All collateral visible on blockchain
  • Censorship-resistant: Cannot be frozen or seized
  • Composable: Easily integrated into DeFi applications

Risks:

  • Crypto volatility: Extreme market crashes can break the peg
  • Smart contract risk: Bugs or exploits could drain collateral
  • Governance risk: DAO votes could make poor parameter choices
  • Complexity: More difficult for average users to understand
  • Capital inefficiency: Requires over-collateralization

3.2.3 Commodity-Backed Stablecoins

These stablecoins are backed by physical commodities, typically gold. Each token represents ownership of a specific amount of physical commodity held in secure vaults.

Examples

  • Pax Gold (PAXG): Each token = 1 troy ounce of gold in Paxos vaults
  • Tether Gold (XAUT): Each token = 1 troy ounce of gold
  • Digix Gold (DGX): Each token = 1 gram of gold

Advantages:

  • Backed by tangible, time-tested store of value
  • Easier than buying/storing physical gold
  • Divisible: Can buy fractional gold ownership
  • Global transferability of gold value

Challenges:

  • Storage costs and insurance for physical commodity
  • Auditing: Need to verify physical commodity exists
  • Redemption complexity: Converting tokens to physical commodity
  • Commodity price volatility (though less than crypto)

3.2.4 Algorithmic Stablecoins

Algorithmic stablecoins attempt to maintain their peg through algorithms and smart contracts that adjust supply and demand, without traditional collateral backing. They are the most experimental and have seen spectacular failures alongside innovative successes.

Mechanisms

Rebase Mechanisms (Elastic Supply):

The protocol automatically adjusts the supply of tokens in all holders' wallets:

  • If price > $1: Increase supply (dilute everyone), reducing price
  • If price < $1: Decrease supply (contract everyone's holdings), increasing price

Example: Ampleforth (AMPL)

Seigniorage Shares:

Two-token systems with a stablecoin and a volatile "share" token:

  • Stablecoin above $1: System mints new stablecoins, distributes to share holders
  • Stablecoin below $1: System issues bonds to buy and burn stablecoins

Example: Empty Set Dollar (ESD), Basis Cash

Hybrid Algorithmic:

Combines partial collateral with algorithmic mechanisms.

Example: Frax (fractionally collateralized, algorithmically stabilized)

The Terra/Luna Collapse (2022)

The most spectacular algorithmic stablecoin failure was Terra's UST, which collapsed in May 2022, losing over $60 billion in value and triggering a broader crypto market crash.

⚠️ Case Study: Terra/Luna Death Spiral

Terra's UST was algorithmic, backed by LUNA token. Users could always trade $1 of LUNA for 1 UST and vice versa. When UST lost its peg:

  1. UST dropped below $1, arbitrageurs bought cheap UST to redeem for $1 of LUNA
  2. Protocol minted massive amounts of LUNA to meet redemptions
  3. LUNA supply exploded, price collapsed from $80 to $0.00001
  4. With LUNA worthless, UST had no backing, completing the death spiral
  5. Result: $60B+ in value destroyed, thousands of investors ruined

Lessons:

  • Algorithmic stablecoins without collateral are fundamentally fragile
  • Crypto-backing crypto creates circular dependency risks
  • Incentive mechanisms can reverse catastrophically under stress
  • Regulatory oversight is necessary for consumer protection

Current Status:

After Terra's collapse, pure algorithmic stablecoins are viewed with skepticism. Most surviving projects either maintain partial collateral or serve niche experimental purposes rather than mainstream use.

3.3 Reserve Management & Transparency

3.3.1 Reserve Composition Best Practices

The quality and liquidity of reserves are critical to stablecoin safety:

Ideal Reserve Assets:

  • Cash: Most liquid, zero credit risk
  • Government securities: T-bills, short-term government bonds (highly liquid, minimal risk)
  • Repo agreements: Overnight or very short-term
  • Money market funds: Government-only funds with daily liquidity

Assets to Avoid or Minimize:

  • Commercial paper: Corporate credit risk
  • Long-term bonds: Interest rate risk and potential illiquidity
  • Corporate bonds: Credit risk and potential illiquidity
  • Secured loans: Difficult to value and liquidate quickly

3.3.2 Attestation and Auditing

Transparency builds trust. Stablecoin issuers should provide:

  • Monthly attestations: Independent accounting firms verify reserve balances
  • Reserve composition disclosure: Detailed breakdown of reserve asset types
  • Real-time proof of reserves: Cryptographic proofs of on-chain backing
  • Full audits: Annual comprehensive audits by reputable firms
  • Public reporting: Accessible information for all users

WIA-FIN-004 Reserve Standards

Stablecoins complying with WIA-FIN-004 must:

  • Maintain minimum 100% collateralization (110% for crypto-backed)
  • Publish reserve composition monthly
  • Obtain independent attestations at least quarterly
  • Provide API access to reserve data for programmatic verification
  • Implement proof-of-reserve protocols where technologically feasible
  • Maintain insurance coverage for custody risks

3.4 Regulatory Landscape

3.4.1 United States

Stablecoins face fragmented regulation across multiple agencies:

  • SEC: May classify some stablecoins as securities
  • CFTC: Oversees stablecoins used as commodities or in derivatives
  • OCC: Banks can custody stablecoins and use them for payment activities
  • State regulators: Money transmitter licenses required in most states
  • Federal Reserve: Proposed regulatory framework treating stablecoins like bank deposits

Legislative Proposals:

  • Require stablecoin issuers to be insured banks or special-purpose trusts
  • Mandatory reserve requirements (100% liquid, high-quality assets)
  • Regular audits and public reporting
  • Prohibition on lending out reserves
  • Federal oversight by OCC or Fed

3.4.2 European Union - MiCA Regulation

The Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA) regulation creates comprehensive framework:

  • Asset-Referenced Tokens (ARTs): Stablecoins pegged to baskets or commodities
  • E-Money Tokens (EMTs): Stablecoins pegged to single fiat currency
  • Authorization: Required for issuance and service provision
  • Capital requirements: Based on operational risks and token circulation
  • Reserve requirements: Segregated, liquid, low-risk assets
  • Redemption rights: At par value, anytime, cost-free
  • Limits on use: Restrictions if stablecoin becomes systemically important

3.4.3 Asia-Pacific Approaches

Varied approaches across the region:

  • Singapore (MAS): Licensing framework, reserve requirements, disclosure rules
  • Hong Kong (HKMA): Mandatory licensing for fiat-backed stablecoins, reserve audits
  • Japan: Treating stablecoins as digital money, requiring bank licenses
  • China: Banned private stablecoins, promoting e-CNY as alternative

3.6 Future of Stablecoins

Stablecoins are evolving rapidly with several key trends:

  • Regulatory clarity: Global frameworks bringing legitimacy and consumer protection
  • Bank integration: Traditional banks issuing their own stablecoins
  • CBDC competition: Stablecoins adapting to compete with sovereign digital currencies
  • Cross-border corridors: Specialized stablecoins for specific remittance markets
  • Programmable features: Smart contract integration for conditional payments
  • Real-world asset backing: Tokenized real estate, commodities, securities as collateral
  • Interoperability standards: Seamless exchange between different stablecoins
"Stablecoins represent a bridge between traditional finance and the decentralized future—preserving stability while embracing innovation." - WIA Stablecoin Working Group
