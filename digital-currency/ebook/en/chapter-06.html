CHAPTER 6

Regulatory Compliance & AML/KYC

Global regulatory frameworks, anti-money laundering requirements, know-your-customer procedures, and compliance best practices for digital currency operations.

6.1 The Regulatory Landscape

Digital currencies operate at the intersection of technology, finance, and law. Navigating this complex regulatory environment is essential for legal operations and long-term success.

6.1.1 Why Regulation Matters

  • Consumer Protection: Safeguarding users from fraud, theft, and platform failures
  • Financial Stability: Preventing systemic risks to the broader economy
  • Crime Prevention: Stopping money laundering, terrorism financing, and illicit activities
  • Tax Enforcement: Ensuring proper tax collection on digital currency transactions
  • Market Integrity: Preventing manipulation, insider trading, and unfair practices
  • Legitimacy: Regulatory compliance brings institutional acceptance and mainstream adoption

6.2 Anti-Money Laundering (AML)

6.2.1 The Money Laundering Process

Money laundering typically occurs in three stages:

  • Placement: Introducing illicit funds into the financial system
  • Layering: Obscuring the source through complex transactions
  • Integration: Funds appear legitimate and re-enter the economy

Digital currency risks:

  • Pseudonymous transactions can obscure identity
  • Cross-border transfers without intermediaries
  • Rapid movement of large sums
  • Privacy-focused cryptocurrencies (Monero, Zcash)
  • Decentralized exchanges without KYC

6.2.2 AML Program Requirements

Financial institutions handling digital currencies must implement comprehensive AML programs:

  • Risk Assessment: Identify and assess money laundering risks
  • Internal Controls: Policies, procedures, and systems to detect and prevent ML
  • Compliance Officer: Designated person responsible for AML program
  • Training: Regular employee education on AML obligations
  • Independent Testing: Regular audits of AML program effectiveness

6.2.3 Transaction Monitoring

Continuous monitoring for suspicious patterns:

  • Large transactions: Exceeding threshold amounts (e.g., $10,000 USD)
  • Structuring: Breaking large amounts into smaller transactions to avoid thresholds
  • Rapid movement: Funds quickly moving through multiple accounts
  • Unusual patterns: Transactions inconsistent with customer profile
  • High-risk jurisdictions: Transfers to/from sanctioned or high-risk countries
  • Mixing services: Use of tumblers or mixers to obscure transaction history

Red Flags for Suspicious Activity

  • Customer reluctant to provide information or documents
  • Use of multiple accounts without clear business purpose
  • Transactions involving known high-risk entities
  • Rapid conversion between cryptocurrencies and fiat
  • Inconsistent transactions relative to stated business activity
  • Customer requests unusual level of secrecy
  • Source of funds unclear or implausible

6.2.4 Suspicious Activity Reporting (SAR)

When suspicious activity is detected, financial institutions must file reports:

  • United States: FinCEN SAR within 30 days of detection
  • European Union: Reports to national FIU (Financial Intelligence Unit)
  • UK: SARs to National Crime Agency
  • Confidentiality: Cannot tip off customer about SAR filing
  • Safe harbor: Good faith reporting protected from liability

6.3 Know Your Customer (KYC)

6.3.1 KYC Requirements

KYC procedures verify customer identity and assess risk:

Individual Customers

  • Full name: Legal name as appears on ID documents
  • Date of birth: For age verification and identity matching
  • Address: Residential address verification
  • Identification: Government-issued ID (passport, driver's license, national ID)
  • Source of funds: Origin of funds to be deposited
  • Purpose of account: Intended use of digital currency services

Business/Corporate Customers

  • Company registration: Certificate of incorporation, business license
  • Beneficial ownership: Individuals owning 25%+ (varies by jurisdiction)
  • Directors and officers: Identification of key personnel
  • Business address: Physical location verification
  • Nature of business: Description of business activities
  • Financial statements: For larger accounts

6.3.2 Customer Due Diligence (CDD) Levels

Level Risk Requirements Limits
Simplified Low Basic information, limited verification Low transaction limits (e.g., $1,000)
Standard Medium Full KYC, ID verification, address proof Standard limits (e.g., $50,000/day)
Enhanced (EDD) High Additional documentation, source of funds, ongoing monitoring Higher or no limits, close supervision

6.3.3 Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD)

Required for high-risk customers:

  • Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs): Government officials, close associates
  • High-risk jurisdictions: Countries with weak AML controls or sanctions
  • High-value customers: Large transaction volumes or balances
  • Complex ownership: Convoluted corporate structures
  • Cash-intensive businesses: Higher money laundering risk

Additional EDD measures:

  • Senior management approval for account opening
  • Source of wealth documentation
  • Purpose of relationship and anticipated activity
  • More frequent monitoring and review
  • Additional background checks and references

6.4 Sanctions Compliance

6.4.1 Sanctions Programs

Must screen customers and transactions against sanctions lists:

  • OFAC (US): Office of Foreign Assets Control sanctions lists
  • UN Sanctions: Security Council consolidated list
  • EU Sanctions: European Union consolidated financial sanctions
  • UK Sanctions: HM Treasury consolidated list
  • Country-specific: National sanctions programs

6.4.2 Screening Requirements

  • Screen all customers at onboarding
  • Regular rescreening (at least annually, preferably real-time)
  • Transaction screening for counterparties
  • Blockchain address screening against known illicit addresses
  • Immediate action on matches (block transaction, freeze assets, report)

6.5 Cross-Border Compliance

6.5.1 Travel Rule

FATF Travel Rule requires VASPs (Virtual Asset Service Providers) to share information on cross-border transfers exceeding thresholds:

  • Originator information: Name, account number, address
  • Beneficiary information: Name, account number
  • Threshold: USD/EUR 1,000 (varies by jurisdiction)
  • Challenge: Decentralized systems lack intermediaries to transmit info

Solutions being developed:

  • InterVASP Messaging Standard
  • Travel Rule Universal Solution Technology (TRUST)
  • Blockchain-based Travel Rule solutions

6.5.2 Cross-Border Reporting

  • CTR (Currency Transaction Report): US requirement for transactions > $10,000
  • FBAR (Foreign Bank Account Report): US persons with foreign accounts > $10,000
  • EU Cross-Border Payments Regulation: Reporting requirements

6.6 Licensing and Registration

6.6.1 United States

  • FinCEN Registration: As Money Services Business (MSB)
  • State Money Transmitter Licenses: Required in most states (expensive, complex)
  • BitLicense: New York's specific cryptocurrency license
  • Securities Registration: If offering tokens deemed securities

6.6.2 European Union (MiCA)

  • CASP License: Crypto-Asset Service Provider authorization
  • Passporting: Single license valid across EU member states
  • Capital requirements: Based on activity type and scale
  • Ongoing obligations: Reporting, audits, consumer protection

6.6.3 Asia-Pacific

  • Singapore: MAS licensing under Payment Services Act
  • Japan: Virtual Currency Exchange Service Provider registration
  • Hong Kong: SFC licensing for trading platforms
  • Australia: AUSTRAC registration, AFS license for certain activities

6.7 Tax Compliance

6.7.1 Tax Treatment of Digital Currencies

  • US (IRS): Property for tax purposes, capital gains on disposition
  • UK (HMRC): Generally capital gains tax, some cases income tax
  • EU: Varies by country, often VAT-exempt for cryptocurrency exchanges
  • Reporting: Form 1099-B, 1099-K requirements in US

6.7.2 Information Reporting

  • Exchanges must report customer transactions to tax authorities
  • Cost basis tracking for capital gains calculations
  • Withholding on certain payments to foreign persons
  • International information sharing (FATCA, CRS)

6.8.2 Compliance Best Practices

  • Implement automated screening and monitoring systems
  • Maintain audit trails of all compliance activities
  • Regular staff training on AML/KYC procedures
  • Engage external legal and compliance consultants
  • Stay updated on regulatory changes across jurisdictions
  • Participate in industry compliance initiatives
  • Build relationships with regulators
  • Prepare for regulatory examinations and audits

⚠️ Consequences of Non-Compliance

  • Regulatory enforcement: Fines, penalties, business restrictions
  • Criminal liability: Potential prosecution for willful violations
  • License revocation: Loss of ability to operate
  • Reputational damage: Loss of customer trust and business relationships
  • Bank derisking: Loss of banking relationships

Example: BitMEX paid $100M in 2021 for AML violations. Binance paid $4.3B in 2023 for compliance failures.
