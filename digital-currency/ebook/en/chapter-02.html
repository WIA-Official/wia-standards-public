CHAPTER 2

Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs)

Exploring how central banks worldwide are developing sovereign digital currencies, the technological and policy considerations, and the transformative impact on monetary systems.

2.1 Understanding CBDCs

Central Bank Digital Currency (CBDC): A digital form of central bank money that is different from balances in traditional reserve or settlement accounts. It is a new form of money, issued digitally by a central bank and intended to serve as legal tender.

CBDCs represent perhaps the most significant evolution in monetary systems since the abandonment of the gold standard. Unlike cryptocurrencies, CBDCs are state-issued, centrally controlled, and carry the full faith and credit of their issuing government. They combine the efficiency of digital payments with the stability and regulatory oversight of traditional fiat currency.

2.1.1 Why Central Banks Are Exploring CBDCs

The surge in CBDC development stems from multiple converging factors:

  • Declining Cash Usage: In many advanced economies, physical cash usage has fallen dramatically. Sweden, for example, has seen cash transactions drop to less than 10% of retail payments. Central banks are concerned about maintaining monetary sovereignty and universal access to central bank money as cash fades.
  • Cryptocurrency Competition: The rise of Bitcoin, stablecoins, and potential corporate digital currencies (like Facebook's Libra project) posed challenges to monetary sovereignty. Central banks recognized the need to offer a modern, digital alternative to prevent private money from displacing sovereign currency.
  • Payment System Innovation: Existing payment infrastructure can be slow, expensive, and exclusionary. CBDCs offer opportunities to modernize payment systems, reduce costs, and improve financial inclusion.
  • Monetary Policy Tools: CBDCs could enable new monetary policy mechanisms, including direct distribution of stimulus payments, programmable money with expiration dates, and potentially negative interest rates on digital holdings.
  • Financial Inclusion: CBDCs accessible via simple mobile phones could provide banking services to billions of unbanked individuals globally.
  • Cross-Border Payments: International transfers remain slow and expensive. CBDC infrastructure could enable faster, cheaper cross-border payments through interoperable systems.

2.2 CBDC Architecture Models

Central banks are exploring various architectural approaches for CBDCs, each with distinct implications for privacy, efficiency, disintermediation risk, and monetary policy.

2.2.1 Retail vs. Wholesale CBDCs

Retail CBDC

Retail CBDCs are designed for general public use, functioning as digital cash for everyday transactions. They would be accessible to consumers and businesses, potentially through digital wallets on smartphones or payment cards.

Key characteristics:

  • Available to all citizens and residents
  • Used for retail payments (shopping, bill payments, peer-to-peer transfers)
  • May earn interest or be designed as non-interest bearing like cash
  • Requires robust infrastructure for mass adoption
  • Raises significant privacy and surveillance concerns

Examples: China's Digital Yuan (e-CNY), Bahamas' Sand Dollar, Eastern Caribbean DCash

Wholesale CBDC

Wholesale CBDCs are restricted to financial institutions and used for interbank settlements, securities transactions, and cross-border payments between central banks.

Key characteristics:

  • Limited to banks and regulated financial institutions
  • Used for large-value transactions and securities settlement
  • Can improve efficiency of existing Real-Time Gross Settlement (RTGS) systems
  • Lower implementation risk than retail CBDCs
  • Can incorporate DLT for transparency and atomicity of transactions

Examples: Project Jasper (Canada), Project Ubin (Singapore), Stella (ECB-Bank of Japan)

2.2.2 Direct vs. Indirect (Two-Tier) Models

Direct Model (Single-Tier)

In a direct model, the central bank maintains all CBDC accounts and handles all transactions directly with end users. The central bank becomes the sole payment service provider.

Advantages:

  • Central bank has complete control and oversight
  • No intermediary risk or counterparty exposure
  • Simplified system architecture
  • Direct monetary policy transmission

Disadvantages:

  • Central bank must handle customer service, KYC/AML, dispute resolution
  • Massive operational burden on central bank
  • Disintermediates commercial banks entirely
  • Requires central bank to build extensive retail infrastructure
  • Limited innovation in services and user interfaces

Indirect Model (Two-Tier)

The two-tier model preserves the existing financial system structure. The central bank issues CBDCs to commercial banks and Payment Service Providers (PSPs), who then distribute them to customers. This is the most common approach being explored globally.

Advantages:

  • Leverages existing banking infrastructure and relationships
  • Banks handle KYC/AML, customer service, compliance
  • Encourages innovation in wallet apps and services
  • Reduces operational burden on central bank
  • Maintains role of commercial banks in financial system

Disadvantages:

  • More complex system architecture
  • Requires regulatory framework for intermediaries
  • Potential for reduced transparency
  • Central bank has less direct control over user experience

🏛️ The Two-Tier Model: How It Works

Tier 1: Central bank issues CBDC and manages the core ledger, tracking which intermediaries hold how much CBDC.

Tier 2: Commercial banks and PSPs distribute CBDC to customers, maintain customer accounts, provide wallets, and handle customer transactions.

Example: When Alice pays Bob 100 digital yuan, her bank debits her account and Bob's bank credits his account. Behind the scenes, the banks settle on the central bank's CBDC ledger.

2.2.3 Account-Based vs. Token-Based Models

Account-Based CBDC

Account-based CBDCs function similarly to traditional bank accounts. Identity verification is required to open an account and conduct transactions. The system tracks account balances rather than individual currency units.

Characteristics:

  • Strong identity verification (KYC) required
  • Easier to implement AML/CFT controls
  • Transaction history easily tracked
  • Can have different tiers with varying limits and verification requirements
  • More privacy concerns due to identity linkage

Token-Based (Value-Based) CBDC

Token-based CBDCs function more like digital cash. The holder of a token (cryptographically secured digital object) owns the value it represents. Verification focuses on the token's validity rather than the holder's identity.

Characteristics:

  • Can enable greater anonymity (similar to cash)
  • Requires robust cryptographic security to prevent counterfeiting
  • Can work offline in some implementations
  • More complex to implement AML/CFT controls
  • Lost tokens mean lost money (like lost cash)

2.2.4 Technology Platforms

Central banks are exploring various technology platforms for CBDCs:

Centralized Database

Traditional database systems managed entirely by the central bank, similar to existing payment systems.

  • Pros: Proven technology, high performance, full control, easier privacy management
  • Cons: Single point of failure, less transparent, traditional infrastructure limitations

Distributed Ledger Technology (DLT) / Blockchain

Decentralized or federated ledger systems providing transparency, resilience, and programmability.

  • Pros: Transparent, resilient, programmable, innovative features
  • Cons: Scalability concerns, energy consumption (depends on consensus), technological complexity

Hybrid Systems

Combining centralized and distributed elements to leverage strengths of both approaches.

  • Pros: Flexibility, can optimize for different use cases, balanced approach
  • Cons: Complex architecture, integration challenges

2.3 Global CBDC Initiatives

2.3.1 China's Digital Yuan (e-CNY)

China leads the world in CBDC implementation. The People's Bank of China (PBOC) began researching digital currency in 2014 and launched pilots in 2020.

Key Features:

  • Two-tier distribution model through commercial banks
  • Account-based with tiered anonymity (different levels based on KYC)
  • Supports offline transactions via NFC technology
  • Controlled anonymity: small transactions have privacy, large ones require identity
  • Programmability features for targeted stimulus and monetary policy

Implementation Status:

  • Over $250 billion in transactions processed (as of 2024)
  • Deployed in major cities including Beijing, Shanghai, Shenzhen
  • Used in 2022 Beijing Winter Olympics
  • Over 260 million wallets opened
  • Expanding to cross-border payments via mBridge project

2.3.2 European Digital Euro

The European Central Bank (ECB) is developing a digital euro to complement cash and ensure European monetary sovereignty.

Design Principles:

  • Complement cash, not replace it
  • Privacy-preserving design (anonymous for low-value transactions)
  • Offline capability for resilience
  • Pan-European availability
  • Free basic services for users
  • Programmable but not surveillance-enabling

Timeline:

  • Investigation phase: 2021-2023
  • Preparation phase: 2023-2026
  • Decision on launch expected mid-2026
  • If approved, gradual rollout from 2027-2028

2.3.3 Bank of England - Digital Pound

The UK is exploring a "digital pound" through extensive consultation and technical research.

Key Considerations:

  • Platform model: BoE provides core infrastructure, private sector builds services
  • Privacy-first approach with legal protections
  • Limits on holdings to prevent bank disintermediation (proposed £10,000-20,000 cap)
  • Interoperability with private payment systems
  • Decision on implementation expected 2025-2026

2.3.4 Federal Reserve - Digital Dollar Research

The United States is taking a cautious, research-focused approach to CBDCs.

Current Status:

  • Research ongoing through Boston Fed and MIT (Project Hamilton)
  • No decision on whether to issue a digital dollar
  • Focus on technological research and policy analysis
  • Requires Congressional authorization for implementation
  • Emphasis on privacy, inclusion, and maintaining dollar dominance

📊 Global CBDC Status (2024)

  • 130+ countries exploring CBDCs (representing 98% of global GDP)
  • 3 fully launched: Bahamas (Sand Dollar), Eastern Caribbean (DCash), Nigeria (eNaira)
  • 20+ in pilot: Including China, India, South Korea, Sweden
  • 40+ in development: Including EU, UK, Australia, Brazil, Russia
  • Research phase: US, Canada, Japan, Switzerland

2.4 CBDC Implementation Considerations

2.4.1 Privacy vs. Transparency Trade-offs

One of the most contentious issues in CBDC design is balancing privacy rights with legitimate regulatory needs for transparency.

Privacy Concerns:

  • Government surveillance of all transactions
  • Potential for political control (freezing accounts, censoring transactions)
  • Data breaches exposing financial histories
  • Chilling effect on free expression and association

Transparency Needs:

  • AML/CFT compliance and criminal investigation
  • Tax enforcement
  • Sanctions compliance
  • Consumer protection

Proposed Solutions:

  • Tiered anonymity: Small transactions private, large ones require identity (e-CNY approach)
  • Zero-knowledge proofs: Prove transaction validity without revealing details
  • Privacy-preserving KYC: Identity verification without central database of all identities
  • Regulatory access controls: Law enforcement access only with judicial oversight
  • Offline transactions: Cash-like privacy for in-person payments

2.4.2 Financial Stability and Bank Disintermediation

A major concern is that CBDCs could trigger bank runs during financial crises as people convert deposits to risk-free central bank money.

Risks:

  • Rapid conversion from bank deposits to CBDC during stress
  • Reduced bank deposits means less lending capacity
  • Weakened monetary policy transmission through banks
  • Need for banks to rely more on costly wholesale funding

Mitigation Strategies:

  • Holding limits: Cap amount individuals can hold (£10-20K in UK proposal)
  • Tiered remuneration: No or low interest on CBDC to keep deposits attractive
  • Conversion limits: Restrict how quickly deposits can be converted to CBDC
  • Two-tier model: Maintain bank intermediation role

2.4.3 Monetary Policy Implications

CBDCs create new monetary policy opportunities and challenges:

New Policy Tools:

  • Negative interest rates: Directly apply negative rates to CBDC holdings to stimulate spending
  • Helicopter money: Direct distribution of stimulus to citizens' CBDC wallets
  • Programmable expiration: Stimulus payments that expire if not spent within timeframe
  • Targeted policies: Different interest rates for different regions or demographics

Challenges:

  • Cash substitution undermines negative rate effectiveness
  • Changing bank intermediation affects credit transmission
  • International spillovers from CBDC policies
  • Need for coordination with other central banks

2.4.4 Cross-Border Payments and Currency Competition

CBDCs could revolutionize cross-border payments but also create currency competition challenges.

Opportunities:

  • Instant cross-border settlements through CBDC bridges
  • Lower costs than correspondent banking networks
  • 24/7 availability vs. business hours for SWIFT
  • Programmable foreign exchange and atomic swaps

Projects:

  • mBridge: Multi-CBDC platform linking China, Hong Kong, Thailand, UAE
  • Project Dunbar: Multi-CBDC platform for international settlements
  • Project Jura: Cross-border securities settlement with wholesale CBDC

Currency Competition Risks:

  • Dollarization risk: Citizens in unstable economies holding digital dollars
  • Monetary sovereignty challenges for smaller nations
  • Capital flight facilitated by easy CBDC conversions
  • Need for capital controls in some jurisdictions

2.5.2 Interoperability Requirements

For CBDCs to function in a global economy, interoperability is essential:

  • Message format standardization: ISO 20022 compliance for payment messages
  • API standards: RESTful APIs for CBDC operations (specified in WIA-FIN-004 Phase 2)
  • Settlement protocols: Atomic swap protocols for cross-CBDC exchanges
  • Identity standards: Portable digital identity for cross-border KYC
  • Compliance data: Standardized AML/CFT reporting formats

2.6 Future of CBDCs

The next decade will see widespread CBDC adoption, fundamentally transforming the monetary system. Key trends include:

  • Mass adoption by 2030: Majority of major economies expected to have operational CBDCs
  • Cross-border integration: Multi-CBDC platforms enabling seamless international payments
  • Programmable money: Smart contracts and conditional payments becoming standard
  • Privacy innovations: Advanced cryptographic techniques protecting user privacy while enabling compliance
  • Hybrid systems: CBDCs coexisting with cash, commercial bank money, and private stablecoins
  • Offline capabilities: Robust offline payment mechanisms for resilience
"CBDCs represent not merely a technological upgrade, but a fundamental reimagining of the social contract around money—balancing individual privacy, state responsibility, and collective prosperity." - WIA Digital Currency Working Group
