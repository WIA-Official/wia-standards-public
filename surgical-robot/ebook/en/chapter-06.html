Phase 3: Protocol

6.1 Introduction to Surgical Workflow Protocols

Phase 3 of WIA-ROB-006 establishes standardized protocols for surgical workflows, safety procedures, verification checklists, and emergency response. These protocols ensure consistent, safe operation across all certified robotic systems, reducing human error and improving patient outcomes through systematic, validated procedures.

6.3 Safety Interlock Mechanisms

Hardware Interlocks

  • Foot Pedal Enabling: Instrument motion only when surgeon foot pedal depressed
  • Dead-man Switch: Master manipulator sensors detect surgeon hand presence
  • Emergency Stop Buttons: Hard-wired E-stop accessible from console, patient-side cart, and OR walls
  • Clutch Mechanism: Physical disengagement separating master from slave when activated
  • Workspace Boundaries: Hard stops preventing instrument exit from safe workspace volume

6.4 Emergency Stop and Failsafe Protocols

E-Stop Activation Sequence

  1. Immediate Action (< 100ms): Cut power to all motors, engage mechanical brakes
  2. Position Hold: Maintain current instrument positions via brakes
  3. Alert Generation: Visual and audible alarms throughout OR
  4. Event Logging: Timestamp, triggering condition, system state recorded
  5. Notification: Alert surgical team, biomedical engineering, manufacturer if applicable
  6. State Assessment: System diagnostics determine recovery path
  7. Recovery or Conversion: Resume if safe, otherwise convert to open surgery

Failsafe Behaviors

  • Power Loss: Engage mechanical brakes, maintain instrument positions, activate battery backup for critical systems
  • Network Failure: Freeze current state, prevent new commands, maintain video stream if possible
  • Software Crash: Activate watchdog timer, safe state transition, automatic system restart if appropriate
  • Sensor Failure: Disable affected subsystem, alert operator, switch to degraded mode or stop
  • Instrument Malfunction: Disable faulty instrument, maintain other instruments, support transition to alternative tools

6.5 Sterile Field Maintenance Procedures

Draping Protocol

  • Robot arms draped with sterile covers before entering surgical field
  • Sterile adapter interfaces for instrument attachment
  • Camera and light source covered with sterile sleeves
  • Cable management preventing contamination of sterile field
  • Defined sterile boundary zones marked visually

Instrument Exchange Procedure

  1. Surgeon requests instrument change via console menu
  2. System transitions to INSTRUMENT_EXCHANGE state
  3. Affected arm retracts to safe position outside patient
  4. Scrub nurse/tech performs sterile instrument exchange
  5. System verifies new instrument attachment and type
  6. Calibration check confirms proper function
  7. System returns to ACTIVE state, surgeon resumes control

6.8 Quality Metrics and Documentation

Mandatory Outcome Metrics

  • Operative Time: Total procedure duration, setup time, console time
  • Blood Loss: Estimated blood loss volume
  • Complications: Intra-operative and post-operative events
  • Conversion Rate: Frequency of conversion to open surgery
  • Technical Failures: System malfunctions, instrument failures
  • E-Stop Events: Emergency stop activations and reasons
  • Near-Miss Events: Close calls that didn't result in harm
  • Patient Outcomes: Length of stay, pain scores, return to activity

6.9 Training and Competency Validation

Surgeon Training Requirements

  1. Didactic Education: 8-16 hours covering robotic surgery principles, system operation, complications
  2. Simulation Training: 20-40 hours on certified simulators achieving proficiency benchmarks
  3. Animal Lab (Optional): 5-10 procedures on animal models for complex cases
  4. Clinical Observation: Observe 5-10 robotic procedures by experienced surgeon
  5. Proctored Cases: Perform 10-20 supervised procedures with expert proctor
  6. Independent Practice: 30-50 cases to achieve full proficiency
  7. Ongoing Assessment: Annual competency evaluation and outcomes review

OR Team Training

  • Scrub Nurses: Instrument handling, sterile technique, troubleshooting (8-16 hours)
  • Circulating Nurses: System setup, documentation, emergency procedures (4-8 hours)
  • Anesthesiologists: Patient positioning, physiological effects, communication (2-4 hours)
  • Surgical Technicians: Equipment maintenance, troubleshooting, sterile processing (4-8 hours)

Key Takeaways

  • Surgical workflow state machines define nine primary states (SETUP → TIMEOUT → ACTIVE → CLOSURE → SHUTDOWN) with clear transition criteria and required actions.
  • Safety interlocks include hardware mechanisms (foot pedals, E-stop, clutch) and software checks (collision detection, force limits, communication monitoring).
  • Emergency stop protocols ensure < 100ms response time with mechanical brakes, position hold, comprehensive logging, and safe recovery or conversion.
  • WHO Surgical Safety Checklist extended with robotic-specific verification items covering system status, calibration, workspace boundaries, and emergency plans.
  • Alarm management defines four priority levels (Critical, High, Medium, Low) with specific response times and actions for each category.
  • Mandatory outcome metrics include operative time, complications, conversion rate, technical failures, and patient outcomes for continuous quality improvement.
  • Comprehensive training requirements for surgeons (80-150 hours total) and OR teams ensure competent, safe robotic surgery practice.
