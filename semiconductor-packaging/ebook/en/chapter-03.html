2.5D and 3D Integration Technologies

18 min readLast updated: January 2026WIA-SEMI-PKG-001

3.1 Understanding Multi-Dimensional Integration

The evolution from traditional 2D packaging to 2.5D and 3D integration represents one of the most significant advances in semiconductor packaging technology. These approaches enable unprecedented levels of integration density, bandwidth, and performance by extending the interconnect architecture into the third dimension.

The terminology can be confusing, as different companies and organizations use varying definitions. In this chapter, we adopt the industry-consensus definitions: 2.5D integration refers to the use of an interposer (silicon or organic) to connect multiple dies side-by-side, while 3D integration involves vertical stacking of dies with through-silicon vias (TSVs) providing electrical connections between layers.

Key Benefits of Multi-Dimensional Integration

Multi-dimensional integration addresses several critical challenges facing modern semiconductor systems. The primary benefits include:

  • Increased Bandwidth: By placing dies in close proximity with high-density interconnects, 2.5D/3D integration enables bandwidths exceeding 1 TB/s between components.
  • Reduced Power: Shorter interconnect distances significantly reduce the energy required for data movement, with some implementations achieving sub-pJ/bit energy efficiency.
  • Smaller Form Factor: Vertical stacking and dense integration reduce the overall package footprint, critical for mobile and edge devices.
  • Heterogeneous Integration: Different process technologies can be combined optimally, with compute dies on advanced nodes and analog/IO on mature nodes.
  • Improved Latency: The short, direct connections between dies minimize signal propagation delays compared to package-level routing.

3.2 Silicon Interposer Technology

Silicon interposers form the foundation of 2.5D integration, providing a high-density interconnect platform for multiple dies. Unlike organic substrates, silicon interposers can support fine-pitch wiring (sub-1 micrometer lines/spaces) and through-silicon vias that enable vertical connections to the package substrate below.

Interposer Manufacturing Process

Silicon interposer fabrication shares many process steps with standard CMOS manufacturing, though typically at relaxed design rules (65nm to 130nm equivalent). The key manufacturing steps include:

  1. TSV Formation: Deep reactive ion etching (DRIE) creates via holes typically 5-10 micrometers in diameter and 50-100 micrometers deep.
  2. Liner and Barrier Deposition: Silicon dioxide isolation and tantalum/tantalum nitride barrier layers are deposited to prevent copper diffusion.
  3. Copper Fill: Electrochemical deposition (ECD) fills the TSVs with copper, followed by chemical-mechanical polishing (CMP).
  4. Redistribution Layers (RDL): Multiple metal layers provide routing between die bumps and TSV locations. Modern interposers may have 4-8 RDL layers.
  5. Wafer Thinning: The interposer wafer is thinned to expose TSVs from the backside, typically to 50-100 micrometers thickness.
  6. Backside Processing: Under-bump metallization and solder bumps are formed on the interposer backside for substrate attachment.

Interposer Design Considerations

Designing an effective silicon interposer requires careful attention to several factors:

  • Thermal Expansion Matching: The coefficient of thermal expansion (CTE) of silicon (2.6 ppm/K) differs significantly from organic substrates (15-20 ppm/K), requiring careful stress management.
  • Power Delivery: Large interposers require robust power distribution networks with appropriate decoupling capacitance placement.
  • Signal Integrity: High-speed signals must be routed with controlled impedance, and crosstalk between adjacent signals must be minimized.
  • Thermal Management: Heat from dies must conduct through the interposer; thermal vias and careful TSV placement can improve thermal performance.

Industry Trend: Glass Interposers

Glass substrates are emerging as an alternative to silicon interposers, offering lower cost, better electrical properties (lower dielectric constant and loss), and the ability to create larger panels. Intel, Samsung, and specialized substrate manufacturers are investing in glass interposer technology for next-generation applications.

3.3 Through-Silicon Via (TSV) Technology

Through-silicon vias are the enabling technology for both 2.5D interposers and true 3D stacking. These vertical electrical connections pass completely through a silicon die or interposer, providing the shortest possible path for signals and power between vertically integrated components.

TSV Categories

TSVs are categorized by when they are formed in the manufacturing process:

  • Via-First: TSVs are created before front-end-of-line (FEOL) processing. Offers highest integration but requires compatible CMOS processing.
  • Via-Middle: TSVs are formed after FEOL but before back-end-of-line (BEOL) metal layers. Most common approach for logic dies.
  • Via-Last: TSVs are created after all CMOS processing is complete. Enables TSV addition to existing designs but limited in density.

TSV Electrical Characteristics

Understanding the electrical behavior of TSVs is essential for high-speed design. A TSV can be modeled as a combination of resistance, inductance, and capacitance:

TSV Reliability Considerations

TSV reliability is influenced by the significant CTE mismatch between copper (17 ppm/K) and silicon (2.6 ppm/K). During thermal cycling, this mismatch creates stress that can lead to several failure modes:

  • Copper Pumping: Copper extrusion from TSVs due to thermal stress, potentially damaging connected devices.
  • Interfacial Delamination: Separation between copper and barrier/liner layers.
  • Keep-Out Zone Effects: Stress fields around TSVs affect nearby transistor performance, requiring exclusion zones of 5-20 micrometers.
  • Electromigration: High current densities in small TSVs can cause metal migration over time.

3.4 3D Stacking Architectures

True 3D integration involves the vertical stacking of active dies, connected through TSVs or hybrid bonding. This approach offers the ultimate in integration density but presents significant challenges in thermal management, testing, and manufacturing yield.

Face-to-Face (F2F) Stacking

In face-to-face stacking, two dies are bonded with their active surfaces facing each other. This approach offers the shortest possible interconnect path between dies and does not require TSVs through the active die. However, it limits the stack to two dies unless combined with face-to-back stacking for additional layers.

Face-to-Back (F2B) Stacking

Face-to-back stacking connects the active surface of one die to the back (inactive) side of another. This requires TSVs through the upper die(s) and enables stacking of multiple dies. Most HBM (High Bandwidth Memory) implementations use F2B stacking with up to 12 DRAM dies plus a base logic die.

High Bandwidth Memory (HBM)

HBM represents the most commercially successful application of 3D stacking technology. Developed by JEDEC and first introduced by SK Hynix in 2013, HBM uses wide I/O architecture with TSV-enabled vertical stacking to achieve memory bandwidths impossible with traditional DRAM packaging.

3.5 Integration Challenges and Solutions

Multi-dimensional integration presents unique challenges that require innovative solutions across the design, manufacturing, and test domains.

Thermal Management Challenges

Heat dissipation becomes increasingly difficult as dies are stacked or placed in close proximity. In a 3D stack, inner dies can only dissipate heat through neighboring dies, creating thermal bottlenecks. Solutions include:

  • Thermal TSVs: Dedicated TSVs for heat conduction, not electrical signals
  • Integrated Heat Spreaders: Metal layers within the stack to distribute heat laterally
  • Microfluidic Cooling: Channels for liquid coolant between die layers (research stage)
  • Power Management: Dynamic workload distribution to prevent hot spots

Test and Known-Good-Die (KGD)

3D integration amplifies the importance of die-level testing. When multiple dies are stacked, a single defective die can render the entire stack unusable. Known-good-die testing ensures each component die is fully functional before integration:

  • Wafer-Level Probing: Testing dies at wafer level before singulation
  • Burn-In: Stress testing to identify infant mortality failures
  • Built-In Self-Test (BIST): On-die test circuits for comprehensive testing
  • Repair Mechanisms: Redundancy and repair for yield enhancement

Yield Mathematics for 3D Stacking

For a stack of N dies, each with yield Y, the compound yield is approximately Y^N. If individual die yield is 90%, an 8-die stack has only 43% compound yield. This illustrates why KGD testing is essential for 3D integration economics. Achieving 99% individual die yield improves the 8-die stack yield to 92%.

Warpage and Stress Management

CTE mismatch between materials causes warpage during thermal processing and can create residual stress that affects device performance. Warpage management strategies include:

  • Symmetric stack structures that balance stress
  • Underfill materials with matched CTE properties
  • Process temperature optimization
  • Compensating structures in the package design

3.6 Applications and Case Studies

2.5D and 3D integration technologies have enabled breakthrough products across multiple market segments. Understanding these real-world applications provides insight into how these technologies are deployed.

AI Accelerators

NVIDIA's H100 and successor accelerators exemplify the power of 2.5D integration. The H100 uses TSMC's CoWoS-S platform to integrate a massive GPU die (814 mm²) with six HBM3 stacks, achieving over 3 TB/s of memory bandwidth. This configuration enables training of large language models that would be impossible with traditional packaging approaches.

High-Performance Computing

AMD's MI300X accelerator combines multiple GPU chiplets, CPU chiplets, and HBM3 stacks using a combination of 2.5D and 3D integration. The 3D V-Cache technology stacks additional L3 cache on top of the compute chiplets, dramatically improving performance for cache-sensitive workloads.

Mobile Applications

Apple's M-series chips use TSMC's InFO technology to integrate multiple dies with high-bandwidth LPDDR memory. The package-on-package approach, combined with advanced substrate technology, enables desktop-class performance in thin, thermally constrained mobile devices.

Summary

2.5D and 3D integration technologies represent a fundamental shift in semiconductor packaging, enabling levels of performance and integration that cannot be achieved with traditional approaches. Silicon interposers provide high-density interconnects for multi-die systems, while TSV technology enables true vertical integration with minimal signal delay and power consumption.

Key Takeaways

  • 2.5D integration uses silicon interposers to connect dies side-by-side with high-bandwidth links
  • 3D integration stacks dies vertically using TSVs for shortest interconnect paths
  • HBM memory is the most commercially successful 3D integration application
  • Thermal management and testing are critical challenges for multi-dimensional integration
  • AI accelerators and HPC systems are driving demand for 2.5D/3D packaging
