Chiplet Architecture and UCIe 3.0 Standard

20 min read Last updated: January 2026 WIA-SEMI-PKG-001

2.1 The Chiplet Revolution

The chiplet architecture represents one of the most significant paradigm shifts in semiconductor design since the invention of the integrated circuit. Rather than designing and manufacturing a single monolithic die containing all system functions, the chiplet approach disaggregates the system into multiple smaller, specialized dies that are integrated within a single package. This modular approach offers compelling advantages in yield, cost, time-to-market, and design flexibility.

AMD pioneered the commercial adoption of chiplet architecture with their Zen 2 processor family in 2019, demonstrating that multiple small compute chiplets could be combined with a larger I/O die to create high-performance processors at competitive costs. This approach enabled AMD to offer 64-core server processors while maintaining competitive yields and pricing, something that would have been extremely challenging with a monolithic approach.

Advantages of Chiplet Design

The chiplet approach offers several fundamental advantages that become more pronounced as transistor densities increase and manufacturing costs escalate at advanced nodes:

Advantage Description Impact
Improved Yield Smaller dies have exponentially better yield 40-60% cost reduction possible
Process Flexibility Each chiplet uses optimal process node Better performance/watt, lower cost
Design Reuse Validated chiplets used across products Faster time-to-market
Product Scalability Same chiplets, different configurations Broader product portfolio
Supplier Flexibility Chiplets from different vendors Reduced supply chain risk

Chiplet Categories

Chiplets can be categorized by their function and the technology requirements for their implementation. Understanding these categories helps system architects select the right combination of chiplets for their target applications:

  • Compute Chiplets: CPU cores, GPU compute units, and AI accelerators. These typically require the most advanced process nodes (3nm, 2nm) to maximize performance and energy efficiency.
  • Memory Interface Chiplets: DDR5, LPDDR5X, and HBM controllers. Often implemented on mature nodes (7nm, 12nm) due to the analog-intensive nature of memory PHYs.
  • I/O Chiplets: PCIe 6.0, CXL 3.0, Ethernet, and USB controllers. These integrate high-speed SerDes and typically use mature nodes for cost efficiency.
  • Accelerator Chiplets: Specialized functions like video codecs, security engines, and DSP blocks. Node selection depends on performance requirements.
  • Passive Interposers: Silicon or organic substrates providing interconnect between active chiplets without containing active circuitry.

2.2 Universal Chiplet Interconnect Express (UCIe)

The Universal Chiplet Interconnect Express (UCIe) specification represents a watershed moment for the semiconductor industry, providing the first comprehensive open standard for die-to-die interconnects. Announced in March 2022 by a consortium of industry leaders including AMD, ARM, ASE, Google, Intel, Meta, Microsoft, Qualcomm, Samsung, and TSMC, UCIe aims to create an ecosystem where chiplets from different vendors can interoperate seamlessly.

Prior to UCIe, each company developed proprietary die-to-die interfaces, making it impossible to mix chiplets from different sources. AMD's Infinity Fabric, Intel's AIB and EMIB, and TSMC's LIPINCON all used different electrical specifications, protocols, and physical implementations. UCIe addresses this fragmentation by defining a comprehensive standard covering the physical layer, protocol layer, and software stack.

UCIe 3.0 Release - August 2025

UCIe 3.0, ratified in August 2025, introduces significant enhancements including 64 GT/s per lane signaling (up from 32 GT/s in 2.0), advanced FEC for improved reliability, and enhanced security features. The specification also introduces support for photonic interconnects, enabling optical die-to-die communication for future high-bandwidth applications.

Physical Layer

The UCIe physical layer defines the electrical characteristics, signaling protocols, and physical interface specifications for die-to-die communication. UCIe supports two packaging options: standard package (longer reach, ~25mm) and advanced package (shorter reach, ~2mm), each with different bump pitch and electrical requirements.

Parameter Standard Package Advanced Package
Bump Pitch 100-130 μm 25-55 μm
Reach Up to 25mm Up to 2mm
Data Rate (UCIe 3.0) 32 GT/s 64 GT/s
Bandwidth Density 28 GB/s/mm 1350 GB/s/mm
Energy Efficiency 0.5 pJ/bit 0.25 pJ/bit

Die-to-Die Adapter Layer

The adapter layer bridges between the protocol layer and physical layer, handling link training, parameter negotiation, and error recovery. It provides a consistent interface to the protocol layer regardless of the underlying physical implementation, enabling the same protocol IP to work with both standard and advanced packaging options.

Protocol Layer

UCIe supports multiple protocols over the same physical interface, enabling flexible system architectures. Native protocol support includes:

  • PCIe 6.0: For legacy compatibility and I/O expansion
  • CXL 3.0: For cache-coherent memory expansion and pooling
  • Streaming Protocol: For raw data streaming between chiplets
  • Custom Protocols: Vendor-specific protocols for specialized applications

2.3 UCIe 3.0 Technical Specifications

UCIe 3.0 represents a significant advancement over previous versions, addressing the demands of next-generation AI accelerators and high-performance computing systems. The specification introduces several key enhancements:

Signaling Enhancements

UCIe 3.0 doubles the per-lane data rate to 64 GT/s for advanced packaging, achieved through PAM4 (4-level pulse amplitude modulation) signaling combined with advanced equalization techniques. This enables bandwidth densities exceeding 1.3 TB/s per millimeter of die edge, critical for memory-bandwidth-hungry AI workloads.

// UCIe 3.0 Lane Configuration Example typedef struct { uint8_t lane_count; // 16, 32, or 64 lanes uint8_t data_rate; // 32 or 64 GT/s uint8_t modulation; // NRZ or PAM4 uint16_t flit_size; // 256 bytes typical bool fec_enabled; // RS-FEC for reliability bool retry_enabled; // Link-level retry } ucie_config_t; // Bandwidth calculation // 64 lanes × 64 GT/s × 0.9375 (encoding) = 3.84 TB/s raw // With FEC overhead: ~3.5 TB/s effective bandwidth

Forward Error Correction (FEC)

UCIe 3.0 mandates Reed-Solomon FEC for advanced package configurations operating at 64 GT/s. The FEC implementation provides a coding gain of approximately 6 dB, essential for maintaining bit error rates below 10^-15 at the elevated data rates. The specification supports multiple FEC modes to balance latency and reliability:

  • Bypass Mode: No FEC, lowest latency, suitable for short reaches
  • Light FEC: RS(544,514), 5.8% overhead, moderate correction
  • Strong FEC: RS(544,480), 13.3% overhead, maximum correction

Security Features

UCIe 3.0 introduces comprehensive security features to protect against physical attacks and ensure data integrity across chiplet boundaries:

Feature Description Use Case
IDE (Integrity and Data Encryption) AES-256-GCM encryption for data-in-motion Confidential computing
Authentication SPDM-based chiplet authentication Supply chain security
Link Integrity MAC-based integrity verification Tamper detection
Key Management Secure key provisioning and rotation Lifecycle management

Implementation Consideration

Security features add latency to the die-to-die link. IDE encryption typically adds 10-20 nanoseconds of latency, which may impact performance-critical paths. System architects should carefully evaluate which links require security features and which can operate in bypass mode based on threat models.

2.4 Industry Implementations

Several major semiconductor companies have announced or shipped products utilizing chiplet architectures with various die-to-die interconnects. While UCIe adoption is growing, proprietary solutions continue to play important roles in the transition period.

AMD EPYC and Ryzen (Infinity Fabric)

AMD's Infinity Fabric has been the most commercially successful chiplet interconnect to date. Used across EPYC server processors, Ryzen desktop CPUs, and Instinct accelerators, Infinity Fabric enables AMD to scale from 8-core desktop chips to 192-core server processors using the same compute chiplet designs.

The fourth generation Infinity Fabric (IF4) in EPYC Genoa and Turin provides 32 GB/s per link with support for cache coherency across chiplets. AMD has committed to UCIe adoption for future products, enabling interoperability with third-party chiplets.

Intel Ponte Vecchio and Falcon Shores

Intel's Ponte Vecchio GPU, used in the Aurora supercomputer, represents one of the most complex chiplet designs ever produced. The package integrates 47 active tiles (chiplets) using Intel's EMIB (Embedded Multi-die Interconnect Bridge) and Foveros technologies. The successor Falcon Shores architecture is designed for UCIe compatibility.

NVIDIA Grace Hopper and Blackwell

NVIDIA's Grace Hopper superchip combines a Grace CPU with a Hopper GPU using NVLink-C2C (chip-to-chip) technology. The Blackwell architecture extends this with a 10 TB/s coherent interconnect between GPU dies. NVIDIA has joined the UCIe consortium, signaling future adoption of the standard.

UCIe Ecosystem Partners

The UCIe ecosystem extends beyond processor vendors to include IP providers, EDA tools, and packaging houses:

  • IP Providers: Synopsys, Cadence, Rambus, and Alphawave Semi offer UCIe PHY and controller IP
  • EDA Tools: Integrated design flows from Synopsys and Cadence support chiplet-based design
  • OSATs: ASE, Amkor, and JCET provide UCIe-compatible packaging services
  • Foundries: TSMC, Samsung, and Intel Foundry offer UCIe-compatible packaging technologies

2.5 Design Considerations for Chiplet Systems

Designing a chiplet-based system introduces unique challenges that differ significantly from monolithic IC design. System architects must consider partitioning strategies, interconnect bandwidth requirements, power delivery, thermal management, and test strategies.

System Partitioning

Effective chiplet partitioning requires careful analysis of data flows and communication patterns within the system. The goal is to minimize inter-chiplet bandwidth while maximizing the benefits of disaggregation. Key partitioning considerations include:

  • Bandwidth Analysis: Identify high-bandwidth internal interfaces that should remain within a chiplet
  • Latency Sensitivity: Time-critical paths may require co-location on the same chiplet
  • Process Optimization: Group functions with similar process requirements
  • Reuse Potential: Design chiplets for use across multiple product SKUs
  • Yield Considerations: Balance chiplet size against yield improvements

Power Delivery Network

Chiplet systems present unique power delivery challenges. Each chiplet requires its own power delivery network (PDN), and the package substrate must distribute power efficiently to all chiplets while maintaining acceptable voltage droop. Advanced packaging technologies like CoWoS and EMIB include dedicated power distribution layers to address these challenges.

Thermal Management

The thermal challenge in chiplet systems stems from the concentration of heat sources across multiple dies within a single package. Hot spots can develop at chiplet boundaries and high-power regions. Thermal solutions must consider:

  • Non-uniform heat distribution across chiplets
  • Thermal interface between multiple dies and heat spreader
  • Different thermal requirements for different chiplet types
  • Impact of silicon interposer on vertical heat conduction

Best Practice: Chiplet Thermal Modeling

Early thermal modeling is essential for chiplet system success. Create detailed thermal models including all chiplets, interposer, substrate, and cooling solution. Validate models with silicon measurements and iterate the design to ensure all chiplets operate within thermal specifications under worst-case workloads.

2.6 Future Directions

The chiplet ecosystem continues to evolve rapidly, with several emerging trends shaping the future of heterogeneous integration:

Optical Interconnects

UCIe 3.0 introduces support for optical die-to-die communication, enabling dramatically higher bandwidth over longer distances with lower power consumption. Co-packaged optics (CPO) and on-die photonic integration are active research areas that could transform chiplet interconnects.

Chiplet Marketplaces

The UCIe standard enables the emergence of chiplet marketplaces where companies can source pre-validated chiplets from multiple vendors. This model, similar to the IP licensing model that transformed ASIC design, could democratize access to advanced semiconductor capabilities.

Advanced Bonding Technologies

Hybrid bonding and other advanced bonding technologies are pushing interconnect density beyond what bump-based approaches can achieve. Sub-micron pitch interconnects enable effective monolithic integration of separately manufactured dies, blurring the line between chiplet integration and wafer-level manufacturing.

Summary

Chiplet architecture and the UCIe standard represent a fundamental shift in how the semiconductor industry approaches system design. By enabling the integration of specialized dies from multiple sources, these technologies extend the benefits of Moore's Law through architectural innovation rather than transistor scaling alone.

UCIe 3.0, with its 64 GT/s signaling, enhanced security features, and optical interconnect support, provides the foundation for next-generation AI accelerators, high-performance computing systems, and data center infrastructure. As the ecosystem matures and more companies adopt the standard, the vision of a vibrant chiplet marketplace moves closer to reality.

Key Takeaways

  • Chiplet architecture enables better yield, process optimization, and design reuse
  • UCIe 3.0 provides standardized die-to-die interconnects at 64 GT/s
  • The layered protocol stack supports PCIe, CXL, and custom protocols
  • Security features protect against physical attacks and ensure data integrity
  • Future directions include optical interconnects and chiplet marketplaces
