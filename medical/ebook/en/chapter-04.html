Electronic Health Records Systems

Electronic Health Records (EHRs) have transformed how healthcare organizations manage patient information, moving from paper-based records to sophisticated digital systems that support clinical workflows, decision support, and data-driven healthcare delivery.

EMR vs. EHR: Understanding the Distinction

The terms Electronic Medical Record (EMR) and Electronic Health Record (EHR) are often used interchangeably, but they represent different concepts in health IT. Understanding this distinction is important for discussions about healthcare interoperability and the scope of different systems.

Electronic Medical Record (EMR)

An EMR is a digital version of the paper charts in a clinician's office. It contains the medical and treatment history of patients within one practice. EMRs are used primarily by providers for diagnosis and treatment. The information in an EMR doesn't travel easily out of the practice—patients may need to request printed copies of their records to share with other providers.

Electronic Health Record (EHR)

An EHR is designed to be a more comprehensive record that can be shared across healthcare organizations. EHRs are built to share information with other providers, laboratories, specialists, and emergency facilities. They focus on the total health of the patient, going beyond standard clinical data collected in the provider's office to include a broader view of patient care.

Major EHR Vendors

The EHR market has consolidated significantly over the past decade, with a handful of vendors dominating different market segments. Understanding the major players and their approaches is essential for healthcare IT professionals and anyone involved in system selection or integration.

Epic Systems

Epic Systems, headquartered in Verona, Wisconsin, is the largest EHR vendor in the United States by market share in the acute care segment. Epic's integrated platform covers inpatient, ambulatory, revenue cycle, and numerous specialty modules. The company is known for its comprehensive functionality, strong clinical workflows, and extensive implementation support—but also for high costs and long implementation timelines.

Epic's "Care Everywhere" network enables data sharing between Epic installations without explicit interface development, though critics argue this creates a proprietary ecosystem that disadvantages non-Epic organizations. Epic has also invested heavily in FHIR APIs through its "App Orchard" marketplace.

Oracle Health (formerly Cerner)

Oracle acquired Cerner Corporation in 2022 for $28.3 billion, creating Oracle Health. Cerner's Millennium platform has been a major competitor to Epic in the acute care market, with particular strength in large health systems and federal healthcare (the Department of Veterans Affairs and Department of Defense). The Oracle acquisition brings cloud infrastructure and AI capabilities to the platform.

MEDITECH

MEDITECH (Medical Information Technology, Inc.) has been developing healthcare software since 1969, making it one of the oldest vendors in the market. MEDITECH Expanse, their current platform, serves community hospitals and health systems. The company is known for more affordable pricing compared to Epic and Cerner, making it attractive to smaller organizations.

EHR Certification

EHR certification in the United States is administered by the Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology (ONC). Certification ensures that EHR systems meet specific technical capabilities, security requirements, and interoperability standards. Healthcare providers using certified EHR technology (CEHRT) can participate in federal incentive programs and avoid penalties under the Medicare and Medicaid programs.

ONC Health IT Certification Program

The ONC Health IT Certification Program, established by the 21st Century Cures Act, sets criteria for EHR systems to be certified as interoperable, secure, and capable of supporting quality measurement. Certification criteria are updated regularly through rulemaking, with recent updates focusing on information blocking prevention, FHIR API requirements, and patient access.

2015 Edition Cures Update (HTI-1) Key Requirements:
  • FHIR R4 API: Support for HL7 FHIR Release 4 APIs
  • US Core v3.1.1: Conformance with US Core Implementation Guide
  • SMART App Launch: Support for third-party applications
  • Bulk FHIR: Export capability for population-level data
  • Information Blocking: Prevention of practices that impede data access
  • USCDI v1: Support for US Core Data for Interoperability

Meaningful Use and Promoting Interoperability

The Meaningful Use program, later renamed Promoting Interoperability (PI), provided incentive payments to eligible professionals and hospitals that demonstrated "meaningful use" of certified EHR technology. The program progressed through three stages, each with increasing requirements for data capture, exchange, and use of EHRs to improve care quality.

Stage Focus Key Requirements Timeline
Stage 1 Data Capture Demographics, vitals, problem lists, medications 2011-2012
Stage 2 Health Information Exchange Clinical summaries, transitions of care 2014-2016
Stage 3 Improved Outcomes APIs, patient engagement, quality reporting 2017-present

EHR Implementation Considerations

Implementation Approaches

EHR implementations are among the most complex IT projects healthcare organizations undertake. The approach chosen—whether "big bang" (all modules at once) or phased (sequential module deployment)— depends on organizational risk tolerance, resources, and strategic priorities.

  • Big Bang: All modules go live simultaneously. Higher risk but shorter overall timeline.
  • Phased by Module: Deploy modules sequentially (e.g., registration, then clinical, then revenue cycle).
  • Phased by Location: Deploy complete system to one facility, then expand to others.
  • Hybrid: Combination of approaches based on organizational structure and priorities.

Critical Success Factors

EHR Implementation Success Factors

  • Executive Sponsorship: Active leadership support and resource commitment
  • Physician Champions: Clinical leaders who advocate for the system
  • Workflow Analysis: Detailed documentation and optimization of clinical processes
  • Training: Comprehensive, role-based training with ongoing support
  • Data Migration: Careful planning for legacy data conversion
  • Change Management: Proactive communication and user support
  • Go-Live Support: At-the-elbow support during initial deployment
  • Optimization: Ongoing system refinement based on user feedback

EHR Integration Architecture

No EHR operates in isolation. Healthcare organizations must integrate their EHR with numerous other systems, including laboratory information systems, pharmacy systems, radiology (PACS), medical devices, billing systems, and external partners. Understanding integration architecture is critical for healthcare IT professionals.

Integration Engine

An integration engine (also called an interface engine) serves as the central hub for healthcare data exchange. The engine receives messages from source systems, transforms them as needed, and routes them to destination systems. Popular integration engines include InterSystems HealthShare, Rhapsody, Mirth Connect, and Microsoft Azure Health Data Services.

Clinical Decision Support

Clinical Decision Support (CDS) is one of the most valuable capabilities of modern EHR systems. CDS systems analyze patient data and provide clinicians with alerts, reminders, guidelines, and recommendations to support evidence-based care delivery.

Types of Clinical Decision Support

  • Drug-Drug Interaction Alerts: Warning when medications may interact
  • Drug-Allergy Alerts: Preventing prescriptions that conflict with documented allergies
  • Dosing Recommendations: Age, weight, and renal function-based dosing
  • Care Gaps: Identifying overdue preventive services
  • Order Sets: Evidence-based bundles of orders for specific conditions
  • Documentation Templates: Structured prompts ensuring complete documentation
  • Risk Scores: Calculated assessments (e.g., sepsis, falls, readmission risk)

CDS Challenges

While CDS has significant potential, healthcare organizations struggle with "alert fatigue"—clinicians becoming desensitized to warnings due to high volumes of low-value alerts. Effective CDS requires careful tuning to maximize signal (clinically important alerts) while minimizing noise (overrides that add cognitive burden without improving care).

Patient Engagement

Modern EHR systems include patient engagement tools—portals, mobile applications, and communication platforms—that give patients access to their health information and enable interaction with their care team.

Patient Portal Features

  • Health Records Access: View lab results, medications, immunizations
  • Appointment Scheduling: Book, cancel, or reschedule visits
  • Secure Messaging: Communicate with care team
  • Prescription Refills: Request medication renewals
  • Bill Pay: View and pay statements
  • Form Completion: Pre-visit questionnaires and consent forms
  • Proxy Access: Caregivers accessing dependent records

Summary

Key Takeaways

  • EMR vs. EHR: EMRs serve single practices; EHRs are designed for data sharing across organizations.
  • Market Consolidation: Epic, Oracle Health, and MEDITECH dominate the acute care market; athenahealth and eClinicalWorks lead ambulatory.
  • 96% Adoption: Nearly all US hospitals now use certified EHR technology, driven by HITECH incentives.
  • ONC Certification: Required for federal program participation; includes FHIR API mandates.
  • Meaningful Use Evolution: Progressed from data capture (Stage 1) to health information exchange (Stage 2) to improved outcomes (Stage 3).
  • Integration Critical: EHRs must integrate with numerous systems through HL7v2, FHIR, and other standards.
  • CDS Value: Clinical decision support improves care quality but requires careful management of alert fatigue.
