Clinical Terminology and Coding

Standardized clinical terminology is the foundation of semantic interoperability in healthcare. Without common vocabularies to represent clinical concepts, exchanged data loses meaning as it moves between systems, rendering technical interoperability meaningless.

The Importance of Standardized Terminology

Healthcare generates vast amounts of clinical data—diagnoses, procedures, laboratory tests, medications, observations, and more. When this data is represented using proprietary or local codes, it cannot be meaningfully exchanged between organizations or aggregated for analytics and research. Standardized terminologies solve this problem by providing common vocabularies with precisely defined meanings.

Consider a simple example: one hospital might record "Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus" while another uses "Adult-onset diabetes" and a third uses "Non-insulin dependent diabetes mellitus." While clinicians understand these are the same condition, computers cannot make this connection without a mapping to a common code. Standardized terminologies provide this common reference.

SNOMED CT

SNOMED CT (Systematized Nomenclature of Medicine - Clinical Terms) is the most comprehensive clinical terminology in healthcare. Developed and maintained by SNOMED International, it provides over 350,000 concepts covering clinical findings, procedures, body structures, organisms, substances, and more. SNOMED CT is designed for detailed clinical documentation and is the preferred terminology for encoding clinical information in electronic health records.

SNOMED CT Structure

SNOMED CT is organized as a poly-hierarchy where each concept can have multiple parent concepts. The terminology uses description logic to define concepts precisely, enabling automated reasoning and classification. Each concept has a unique numeric identifier (SCTID), a fully specified name, and one or more synonyms.

Component Description Example
Concept A clinical meaning 73211009 | Diabetes mellitus |
Description Human-readable term "Diabetes mellitus", "DM"
Relationship Link between concepts Is-a, Finding site, Causative agent
Hierarchy Top-level classification Clinical finding, Procedure, Body structure

SNOMED CT Hierarchies

SNOMED CT organizes concepts into 19 top-level hierarchies, each representing a major domain of clinical information:

  • Clinical finding: Observations about health status (diagnoses, symptoms)
  • Procedure: Diagnostic and therapeutic interventions
  • Body structure: Anatomical locations
  • Organism: Causative organisms (bacteria, viruses)
  • Substance: Chemical substances including drugs
  • Pharmaceutical/biologic product: Medications
  • Observable entity: Things that can be observed or measured
  • Event: Occurrences excluding procedures
  • Qualifier value: Values used to qualify other concepts
  • Specimen: Samples collected for testing

Post-Coordination

SNOMED CT supports post-coordination, allowing users to combine concepts to create more specific meanings not explicitly defined in the terminology. For example, "fracture of left femur" could be expressed by combining concepts for fracture, left, and femur, rather than requiring a pre-defined concept for every possible combination.

LOINC

Logical Observation Identifiers Names and Codes (LOINC) is the standard for identifying laboratory tests, clinical observations, and other measurements. Developed and maintained by the Regenstrief Institute, LOINC provides universal codes that enable the exchange of laboratory and clinical results.

LOINC Structure

Each LOINC code identifies a specific observation using six axes that together precisely define what is being measured:

Axis Description Example
Component What is measured Glucose, Hemoglobin A1c
Property Characteristic measured Mass concentration, Number
Time Interval of measurement Point in time, 24 hours
System Specimen type Serum, Plasma, Urine
Scale Type of scale Quantitative, Ordinal, Narrative
Method Measurement technique Immunoassay, Culture

ICD-10 (International Classification of Diseases)

The International Classification of Diseases (ICD) is the global standard for reporting diseases, health conditions, and causes of death. Published by the World Health Organization (WHO), ICD is used worldwide for mortality and morbidity statistics, reimbursement, and public health surveillance.

ICD-10-CM and ICD-10-PCS

In the United States, ICD-10 has been adapted into two code sets:

  • ICD-10-CM (Clinical Modification): For diagnosis coding in all healthcare settings. Contains approximately 72,000 codes.
  • ICD-10-PCS (Procedure Coding System): For inpatient procedure coding. Contains approximately 87,000 codes.

ICD-10-CM Structure

ICD-10-CM codes are alphanumeric with 3-7 characters. The structure provides increasing specificity:

Position Content Example (E11.65)
1 Category (letter) E = Endocrine, nutritional, metabolic
2-3 Category (numbers) 11 = Type 2 diabetes mellitus
4 Subcategory 6 = With other specified complications
5-7 Laterality, episode, extension 5 = With hyperglycemia

ICD-11

ICD-11, released by WHO in 2019, is the latest version of the classification and represents a significant modernization. ICD-11 is designed for electronic use, supports post-coordination, and includes detailed extensions for traditional medicine, functioning assessment, and more. The United States is evaluating transition timelines, with full adoption expected in the coming years.

CPT (Current Procedural Terminology)

Current Procedural Terminology (CPT) codes, developed and maintained by the American Medical Association (AMA), are the standard for reporting medical, surgical, and diagnostic procedures in the United States. CPT codes are required for professional billing and are used alongside ICD-10-CM diagnosis codes.

CPT Categories

Category Code Range Description
Category I 00100-99499 Standard procedures and services
Category II 0001F-9999F Performance measurement (optional)
Category III 0001T-9999T Emerging technology and services

RxNorm

RxNorm, produced by the National Library of Medicine (NLM), provides normalized names and identifiers for clinical drugs. It serves as a "Rosetta Stone" connecting drug vocabularies from different sources, enabling medication interoperability across healthcare systems.

RxNorm Relationships

RxNorm provides relationships between drug concepts, enabling navigation from ingredients to clinical drugs to branded products. It also maps to external vocabularies like NDC (National Drug Code), making it essential for translating between coding systems used by different organizations.

Terminology Mapping and Translation

Because different terminologies serve different purposes (SNOMED CT for clinical documentation, ICD-10 for billing, LOINC for laboratory), healthcare organizations must frequently map between code systems. This mapping, when done correctly, preserves semantic meaning; when done poorly, it introduces errors that can affect patient care and reimbursement.

Mapping Challenges

  • Granularity Differences: SNOMED CT is more detailed than ICD-10, so many SNOMED concepts map to a single ICD code.
  • Purpose Differences: Clinical terminologies capture clinical reality; billing codes reflect reimbursement logic.
  • Many-to-Many: Mappings are rarely one-to-one, requiring context to select the correct target code.
  • Maintenance: Both source and target terminologies update, requiring continuous map maintenance.

Summary

Key Takeaways

  • Semantic Interoperability: Standardized terminologies enable meaningful data exchange by ensuring shared understanding of clinical concepts.
  • SNOMED CT: Comprehensive clinical terminology with 350K+ concepts for detailed clinical documentation.
  • LOINC: Standard for laboratory tests and observations using six-axis structure for precise identification.
  • ICD-10: International disease classification used for diagnosis coding (ICD-10-CM) and procedures (ICD-10-PCS).
  • CPT: American Medical Association's procedural terminology for professional billing.
  • RxNorm: NLM's drug terminology that connects different medication vocabularies.
  • Mapping Complexity: Translation between terminologies requires careful attention to granularity and purpose differences.
