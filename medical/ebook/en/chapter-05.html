Chapter 5

Data Privacy and Security (HIPAA)

Healthcare data is among the most sensitive information that exists, and its protection is governed by a complex regulatory framework. In the United States, the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) establishes national standards for the protection of health information.

Introduction to HIPAA

The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996 (HIPAA) was enacted by the U.S. Congress to improve the portability and continuity of health insurance coverage, combat fraud and abuse in health insurance and healthcare delivery, and promote the use of medical savings accounts. While the law addressed multiple issues, its most enduring impact has been the establishment of national standards for protecting the privacy and security of health information.

HIPAA's privacy and security provisions, implemented through regulations issued by the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS), apply to "covered entities" (health plans, healthcare clearinghouses, and most healthcare providers) and their "business associates" (organizations that handle protected health information on behalf of covered entities).

HIPAA Privacy Rule

The HIPAA Privacy Rule (45 CFR Part 160 and Subparts A and E of Part 164) establishes national standards for the protection of individually identifiable health information held or transmitted by covered entities and their business associates. The Privacy Rule addresses the use and disclosure of Protected Health Information (PHI), individual rights regarding their health information, and administrative requirements for covered entities.

Protected Health Information (PHI)

PHI is individually identifiable health information that is created, received, maintained, or transmitted by a covered entity or business associate. PHI includes demographic information, medical histories, test results, insurance information, and any other data that can be used to identify an individual and relates to their past, present, or future physical or mental health condition, healthcare provision, or payment for healthcare.

18 HIPAA Identifiers:
  • Names
  • Geographic data (smaller than state)
  • Dates (except year)
  • Phone numbers
  • Fax numbers
  • Email addresses
  • Social Security numbers
  • Medical record numbers
  • Health plan beneficiary numbers
  • Account numbers
  • Certificate/license numbers
  • Vehicle identifiers
  • Device identifiers
  • Web URLs
  • IP addresses
  • Biometric identifiers
  • Full-face photographs
  • Any other unique identifier

Permitted Uses and Disclosures

The Privacy Rule permits covered entities to use and disclose PHI without individual authorization for certain purposes, while requiring authorization for others. Understanding these distinctions is critical for compliance.

Category Authorization Required? Examples
Treatment No Sharing records with specialist, consulting physicians
Payment No Billing insurance, obtaining prior authorization
Healthcare Operations No Quality improvement, training, compliance
Public Health No Disease reporting, vital statistics, FDA reports
Law Enforcement Sometimes Court orders, subpoenas, certain investigations
Research Usually (unless waived) Clinical trials, retrospective studies
Marketing Yes Promotional communications, sale of PHI

Minimum Necessary Standard

The Privacy Rule requires covered entities to make reasonable efforts to limit PHI disclosed to the minimum necessary to accomplish the intended purpose. This standard applies to most uses, disclosures, and requests for PHI, with exceptions for treatment purposes, disclosures to the individual, and disclosures required by law.

Individual Rights

The Privacy Rule grants individuals specific rights regarding their health information:

  • Right of Access: Individuals can access and obtain copies of their PHI, with limited exceptions.
  • Right to Amendment: Individuals can request corrections to inaccurate PHI.
  • Right to Accounting: Individuals can receive a list of certain disclosures of their PHI.
  • Right to Restriction: Individuals can request restrictions on certain uses and disclosures.
  • Right to Confidential Communications: Individuals can request alternate means of communication.
  • Right to Notice: Individuals must receive notice of privacy practices.

HIPAA Security Rule

The HIPAA Security Rule (45 CFR Part 160 and Subparts A and C of Part 164) establishes national standards for protecting electronic Protected Health Information (ePHI). While the Privacy Rule addresses all PHI regardless of form, the Security Rule specifically addresses the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of ePHI.

Administrative Safeguards

Administrative safeguards are policies and procedures designed to manage security measures and the conduct of workforce members. These include:

Safeguard Standard Implementation
Security Management Required Risk analysis, risk management, sanctions, review
Assigned Security Responsibility Required Designated security official
Workforce Security Required Authorization, supervision, clearance, termination
Information Access Management Required Access authorization, establishment, modification
Security Awareness Training Required Reminders, malicious software, login monitoring, password management
Security Incident Procedures Required Response and reporting
Contingency Plan Required Data backup, disaster recovery, emergency mode
Evaluation Required Periodic technical and non-technical evaluation

Physical Safeguards

Physical safeguards address physical access to electronic information systems and the facilities where they are housed:

  • Facility Access Controls: Contingency operations, facility security plan, access control, maintenance records
  • Workstation Use: Policies for appropriate workstation use
  • Workstation Security: Physical safeguards for workstations accessing ePHI
  • Device and Media Controls: Disposal, media re-use, accountability, data backup/storage

Technical Safeguards

Technical safeguards involve the technology and policies for protecting ePHI:

Safeguard Standard Specifications
Access Control Required Unique user ID, emergency access, automatic logoff, encryption
Audit Controls Required Hardware, software, procedural audit mechanisms
Integrity Required Mechanisms to authenticate ePHI, protect from improper alteration
Person/Entity Authentication Required Verify identity of those accessing ePHI
Transmission Security Required Integrity controls, encryption for ePHI in transit

HITECH Act and Breach Notification

The Health Information Technology for Economic and Clinical Health (HITECH) Act of 2009 significantly expanded HIPAA's scope and enforcement. HITECH extended HIPAA requirements directly to business associates, increased penalties for violations, and established breach notification requirements.

Breach Notification Requirements

HITECH requires covered entities and business associates to notify affected individuals, the Secretary of HHS, and in some cases the media, following a breach of unsecured PHI. The notification requirements depend on the number of individuals affected.

Breach Notification Timelines:
  • Individual Notice: Within 60 days of discovery
  • HHS Notice (500+ individuals): Within 60 days of discovery
  • HHS Notice (<500 individuals): Within 60 days of calendar year end
  • Media Notice (500+ in state/jurisdiction): Within 60 days of discovery

Business Associate Agreements

A Business Associate Agreement (BAA) is a contract between a covered entity and a business associate that establishes the permitted and required uses and disclosures of PHI by the business associate. BAAs are required whenever a covered entity engages a business associate that will have access to PHI.

Required BAA Provisions

  • Describe permitted uses and disclosures of PHI
  • Prohibit uses and disclosures not permitted by the agreement
  • Require appropriate safeguards
  • Require reporting of unauthorized uses, disclosures, and security incidents
  • Ensure subcontractors agree to same restrictions
  • Make PHI available to individual for access rights
  • Make PHI available for amendment
  • Provide accounting of disclosures
  • Make practices available for HHS compliance review
  • Return or destroy PHI at termination

De-identification

De-identified health information is not subject to HIPAA requirements because it cannot be used to identify an individual. The Privacy Rule provides two methods for achieving de-identification:

Safe Harbor Method

Remove all 18 specified identifiers (listed earlier) and have no actual knowledge that remaining information could be used to identify an individual.

Expert Determination Method

A qualified statistical or scientific expert determines that the risk of identification is "very small" and documents the methods and results of the analysis.

International Privacy Frameworks

While HIPAA governs healthcare privacy in the United States, healthcare organizations operating globally must comply with other regulatory frameworks.

GDPR (European Union)

The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) provides comprehensive data protection for EU residents. Health data is considered "special category" data requiring explicit consent for processing. GDPR grants broader individual rights than HIPAA, including the right to erasure ("right to be forgotten") and data portability.

Aspect HIPAA GDPR
Scope US healthcare entities All EU data processors
Consent Not always required Generally required for health data
Right to Erasure No general right Yes (with exceptions)

Summary

Key Takeaways

  • HIPAA Foundation: Enacted in 1996, HIPAA establishes national standards for protecting health information privacy and security.
  • Privacy Rule: Addresses use and disclosure of PHI, individual rights, and requires minimum necessary standard.
  • 18 Identifiers: PHI includes any health information combined with 18 specified identifiers.
  • Security Rule: Requires administrative, physical, and technical safeguards for electronic PHI.
  • HITECH Enhancement: Extended requirements to business associates, increased penalties, added breach notification.
  • BAA Required: Covered entities must have Business Associate Agreements with vendors handling PHI.
  • De-identification: Safe Harbor (remove 18 identifiers) or Expert Determination removes HIPAA requirements.
  • Global Compliance: Organizations must also consider GDPR and other international frameworks.
