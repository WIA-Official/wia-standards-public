Family Reunification

The heart of WIA-UNI-001 - enabling separated families to reconnect across the DMZ

👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 The Heart of the Standard

WIA-UNI-001 was created for one primary purpose: to reunite Korean families separated by the war. While the standard enables many types of data exchange, family reunification receives special priority, enhanced privacy protections, and dedicated support resources.

Real Story: Lee Family Reunion

In November 2023, 87-year-old Lee Keum-seom from Seoul met her son Ri Sung-chol, 65, from Pyongyang through a WIA-UNI-001 video call. They had been separated for 70 years. Lee thought her son had died in the war. Ri thought his mother had fled to the South and forgotten him.

Through tears, Lee showed her son photos of his father (who passed in 1987), his siblings, and his nieces and nephews. Ri showed his mother photos of his own children - her grandchildren she never knew existed. They talked for 3 hours. Lee passed away peacefully 6 months later, having seen her son's face one last time.

"Technology gave me my son back," she said. "I can die in peace now."

This is why WIA-UNI-001 exists. Not for politics. Not for economics. For moments like these.

The Family Reunification Process

Step 1: Registration & Verification

Families can register through Red Cross offices in both North and South Korea. The process is designed to be accessible even to elderly individuals with no technical experience:

  • In-Person Registration: Red Cross volunteers help families register
  • Minimal Documentation: Only basic information needed (name, birth year, last known location)
  • Photo Upload: Old family photos help verify identities
  • Voice Recording: "This is your mother/father/son/daughter" - family members recognize voices
  • Privacy Protection: All information encrypted, shared only with verified matches

Step 2: Database Matching

WIA-UNI-001 maintains a distributed database of separated families. Advanced matching algorithms consider multiple factors:

  • Name matching (accounting for different romanizations and dialects)
  • Age/birth year correlation
  • Last known location before separation
  • Family relationships (parent, child, sibling, spouse)
  • Physical descriptions and photos
  • Historical records and war-time documentation
interface FamilyRecord {
  id: string;
  registeredBy: 'south' | 'north';
  personalInfo: {
    familyName: string;
    givenName: string;
    birthYear: number;
    lastKnownLocation: string;
  };
  searchingFor: {
    relationship: 'parent' | 'child' | 'sibling' | 'spouse' | 'other';
    name: string;
    estimatedAge: number;
  };
  verificationData: {
    photos: EncryptedFile[];
    voiceRecording: EncryptedFile;
    additionalInfo: string;
  };
  privacyLevel: 'public' | 'verified-only' | 'private';
}

async function findMatches(record: FamilyRecord): Promise<Match[]> {
  const potentialMatches = await searchDatabase(record);

  return potentialMatches
    .map(match => ({
      ...match,
      confidence: calculateMatchConfidence(record, match)
    }))
    .filter(match => match.confidence > 0.7)
    .sort((a, b) => b.confidence - a.confidence);
}

Step 3: Initial Contact

When a potential match is found, both families are notified through their registered Red Cross offices. Initial contact is facilitated by trained counselors who help families prepare emotionally for reunion:

  • Counseling Support: Psychologists help families process 70+ years of separation
  • Language Assistance: Korean dialects have diverged; translators help bridge gaps
  • Technical Training: Volunteers teach elderly family members how to use the system
  • Privacy Briefing: Families informed of their rights and system capabilities

Step 4: First Exchange

The first exchange is always a letter, not a video call. This gives families time to process emotions and decide how they want to proceed:

First Letter Template (provided to families):

"Dear [name],

This is your [relationship]. I have been searching for you since [year]. I want you to know that I never forgot you. I thought about you every day. I want to know: Are you healthy? Are you happy? Do you have a family? I have so much to tell you...

With all my love,
[Your name]"

Step 5: Ongoing Communication

After initial contact, families can communicate as often as they wish. WIA-UNI-001 provides multiple channels tailored to different needs and technical abilities:

  • Text Messages: Simple, accessible to elderly users
  • Voice Calls: Hearing a loved one's voice after decades
  • Video Calls: Seeing faces, sharing daily life
  • Photo Sharing: Exchanging family photos spanning generations
  • Document Sharing: Birth certificates, family trees, historical records

Special Features for Families

Family Archive

Every family gets a private, encrypted archive where they can store:

  • All messages and recordings
  • Family photos from both North and South
  • Family tree (automatically generated from conversations)
  • Historical documents and war-time records
  • Video messages for future generations

Archives are preserved for 100 years and can be passed to children and grandchildren.

Translation & Interpretation

After 75+ years of separation, Korean language in North and South has diverged. Many elderly people struggle to understand each other:

  • Automatic Dialect Translation: North-South Korean dialect conversion
  • Historical Term Mapping: Words that changed meaning since 1950
  • Human Interpreters: Available for complex or emotional conversations
  • Cultural Context Notes: Explaining references unfamiliar to the other side

Legacy Planning

Many separated family members are elderly. WIA-UNI-001 includes features for legacy planning:

  • Final Messages: Record messages to be delivered after death
  • Archive Transfer: Designate who inherits your family archive
  • Memorial Pages: Families can create memorials for deceased members
  • Cross-Border Inheritance: Legal framework for passing information across the DMZ

Success Stories

The Park Sisters

Park Young-ja (South) and Park Young-soon (North) were separated at ages 7 and 9. Through WIA-UNI-001, they reconnected at ages 82 and 84. They now video call every Sunday morning. Young-ja teaches Young-soon's grandchildren about Seoul. Young-soon shares recipes from their childhood that Young-ja had forgotten.

The Kim Brothers

Kim Min-ho (South) and Kim Min-su (North) were 5 and 3 when separated. Neither remembered the other. DNA testing through Red Cross verified their relationship. Now 78 and 76, they're getting to know the brother they never knew. Min-ho's children call Min-su "uncle." Min-su's grandchildren are learning about their family in Seoul.
