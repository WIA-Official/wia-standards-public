5.1 Geothermal Energy Fundamentals

Geothermal energy taps into Earth's internal heat, utilizing thermal energy stored in rocks and fluids beneath the surface. This heat originates from two sources: residual energy from planetary formation 4.5 billion years ago, and ongoing radioactive decay of isotopes including uranium-238, thorium-232, and potassium-40 within Earth's mantle and crust. The continuous flow of geothermal heat to the surface - estimated at 44 terawatts globally - provides a vast, largely untapped renewable energy resource.

Unlike solar and wind energy, geothermal resources provide baseload power generation - continuous, dispatchable electricity independent of weather or time of day. Geothermal power plants typically operate at capacity factors exceeding 90%, comparable to nuclear facilities and far surpassing solar (15-25%) and wind (25-50%). This reliability makes geothermal energy exceptionally valuable for grid stability as variable renewable energy sources scale.

Geothermal resources concentrate in tectonically active regions where Earth's crust is thinner or fractured, allowing heat to reach shallower depths. The "Ring of Fire" encircling the Pacific Ocean contains the world's most abundant high-temperature geothermal resources. Iceland, Philippines, El Salvador, and New Zealand generate significant portions of electricity from geothermal sources, demonstrating the technology's potential where resources are favorable.

5.2 Geothermal Technologies

Conventional Geothermal Power

Conventional geothermal power requires hydrothermal resources - underground reservoirs containing hot water or steam at temperatures exceeding 150°C. These resources are accessed through wells drilled 1-3 kilometers deep. Three primary power generation technologies serve different resource conditions:

Dry steam plants utilize high-temperature steam (above 235°C) directly from geothermal reservoirs to drive turbines. This is the simplest and most efficient geothermal technology but requires the rarest resource type. The Geysers in California, the world's largest geothermal field, employs dry steam technology across 1,500 MW of installed capacity.

Flash steam plants, the most common geothermal technology, handle high-pressure hot water (above 182°C) from reservoirs. As this water reaches the surface and pressure drops, a portion "flashes" to steam, which drives turbines. Remaining hot water may undergo secondary flashing to extract additional energy. Flash steam plants achieve thermal-to-electrical conversion efficiencies of 10-15%, limited by the relatively low temperature heat source compared to fossil fuel combustion.

Binary cycle plants enable power generation from lower-temperature resources (100-180°C). These systems use geothermal fluid to heat a secondary working fluid with a low boiling point (such as isobutane or pentane) via heat exchangers. The vaporized working fluid drives turbines, then condenses for reuse in a closed loop. Binary plants emit essentially zero greenhouse gases, as geothermal fluid never leaves the closed system.

Enhanced Geothermal Systems

Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS) expand geothermal potential beyond naturally occurring hydrothermal resources by creating artificial reservoirs in hot dry rock. EGS involves drilling into crystalline basement rock at depths of 3-5 kilometers where temperatures exceed 200°C, then hydraulically fracturing the rock to create permeability. Water is injected through one well, circulates through the fractured rock network absorbing heat, and returns to the surface through production wells.

EGS could unlock geothermal energy across most continental areas, not just tectonically active zones. Assessments suggest EGS could provide hundreds of gigawatts of baseload generation capacity in the United States alone. However, EGS faces technical challenges including induced seismicity concerns, maintaining reservoir permeability, and high drilling costs. Several demonstration projects globally are advancing EGS technology toward commercial viability.

Direct Use Applications

Geothermal direct use applications harness low-to-medium temperature resources (20-150°C) for heating purposes: district heating, greenhouse heating, aquaculture pond warming, industrial process heat, and spa/balneology. Direct use achieves much higher energy efficiencies (up to 70%) than electricity generation, as energy is not lost in thermal-to-electrical conversion.

District heating systems circulate hot geothermal water through insulated pipe networks, supplying space heating and hot water to buildings. Reykjavik, Iceland's capital, heats 95% of buildings with geothermal district energy. Geothermal heat pumps, using stable ground temperatures of 10-20°C found a few meters underground, provide highly efficient space conditioning globally, even in areas without conventional geothermal resources.

5.3 Biomass Energy Fundamentals

Biomass energy converts organic materials - plant matter, agricultural residues, forestry waste, and organic municipal solid waste - into heat, electricity, or transportable fuels. Biomass serves as a renewable energy source because the carbon dioxide released during combustion or conversion was recently captured from the atmosphere through photosynthesis, creating a shorter carbon cycle than fossil fuels formed over millions of years.

The sustainability of biomass energy depends critically on sourcing and land use practices. Biomass from agricultural and forestry residues that would otherwise decompose offers clear climate benefits. Purpose-grown energy crops must be evaluated carefully, considering land use change, fertilizer inputs, biodiversity impacts, and competition with food production. Sustainable biomass certification schemes provide frameworks for ensuring environmental and social responsibility.

5.4 Biomass Technologies

Direct Combustion

Direct combustion of biomass for heat and power generation represents the oldest and most widespread biomass energy technology. Modern biomass combustion facilities achieve high efficiencies through advanced combustion control, heat recovery, and emissions controls. Dedicated biomass power plants generate electricity at utility scales up to 50 MW or more. Co-firing - blending biomass with coal in existing power plants - provides a pathway for reducing emissions from the existing coal fleet while utilizing established infrastructure.

Gasification

Biomass gasification converts solid biomass into a combustible gas mixture (primarily carbon monoxide, hydrogen, and methane) through partial oxidation at high temperatures (700-1000°C). This synthesis gas or "syngas" can fuel gas turbines or engines for electricity generation, provide process heat for industry, or serve as feedstock for producing liquid fuels and chemicals. Gasification offers higher conversion efficiencies and cleaner combustion than direct burning.

Anaerobic Digestion

Anaerobic digestion employs microorganisms to break down organic matter in oxygen-free conditions, producing biogas - primarily methane (50-70%) and carbon dioxide. Biogas from agricultural waste, wastewater treatment, and landfills can be combusted for heat and electricity, upgraded to pipeline-quality biomethane, or compressed as vehicle fuel. Anaerobic digestion provides the additional benefit of producing nutrient-rich digestate suitable for fertilizer, closing nutrient cycles sustainably.

Biofuels

Liquid biofuels - including ethanol, biodiesel, and advanced fuels - offer renewable alternatives for transportation. First-generation biofuels derive from food crops: ethanol from corn or sugarcane fermentation, and biodiesel from vegetable oils or animal fats. While these fuels reduce fossil fuel dependence, they raise concerns about food security and land use change.

Second-generation biofuels use non-food feedstocks including agricultural residues, dedicated cellulosic crops, and algae. Cellulosic ethanol production from crop residues, switchgrass, or forestry waste avoids food competition while utilizing marginal lands unsuitable for food production. Algae-based biofuels offer extremely high yields per hectare and can grow in saltwater or wastewater, though commercial-scale production remains under development.

5.5 Environmental and Sustainability Considerations

Geothermal Environmental Impacts

Geothermal power generation offers minimal environmental impacts compared to fossil fuels. Binary cycle plants produce near-zero air emissions, while flash steam plants emit small amounts of carbon dioxide and hydrogen sulfide from geothermal fluids - typically less than 5% of equivalent fossil fuel generation. Modern facilities reinjection all non-condensable gases and excess fluids to maintain reservoir pressure and minimize surface impacts.

Induced seismicity - earthquakes triggered by fluid injection or extraction - has occurred at some geothermal sites, particularly EGS projects. Careful reservoir management, including controlled injection rates, seismic monitoring, and traffic light protocols that modify operations if seismic activity exceeds thresholds, mitigates these risks. Land use requirements for geothermal facilities are modest: a 1 MW geothermal plant occupies approximately 400 square meters, less than solar or wind installations.

Biomass Sustainability

Biomass sustainability requires holistic assessment of the entire production chain. Sustainable forestry practices ensure harvest rates do not exceed regrowth, maintaining forest carbon stocks. Agricultural residue collection must leave sufficient organic matter to maintain soil health and prevent erosion. Purpose-grown energy crops should utilize marginal lands, employ appropriate species, and avoid monocultures that harm biodiversity.

Life cycle greenhouse gas emissions from biomass vary enormously depending on feedstock, production practices, and reference fossil fuel. Well-designed biomass systems can achieve emissions reductions of 70-90% compared to fossil fuels. Poor practices - including conversion of forests to energy crops or inefficient combustion - may produce higher emissions than fossil alternatives. Robust sustainability standards and certification schemes are essential for ensuring biomass energy delivers promised climate benefits.

Key Takeaways

  • Geothermal energy provides baseload renewable power with exceptional reliability
  • Multiple geothermal technologies serve different resource temperatures and applications
  • Enhanced Geothermal Systems could expand geothermal potential dramatically
  • Biomass energy converts organic materials into heat, electricity, and fuels
  • Sustainability of biomass depends critically on sourcing and land management
  • Both technologies offer unique advantages complementing variable renewable sources
