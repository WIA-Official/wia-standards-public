4.1 Fundamentals of Plasma Etching

Plasma etching is the cornerstone of pattern transfer in semiconductor manufacturing, enabling the creation of nanoscale features with extraordinary precision. The process combines chemical and physical mechanisms to selectively remove material while preserving critical dimensions and feature profiles.

Plasma Chemistry

Plasma etching relies on reactive species generated in a low-pressure gas discharge. The plasma environment contains:

  • Neutral Radicals: Chemically reactive species (F, Cl, O atoms) responsible for chemical etching
  • Ions: Charged particles (CF3+, Cl+) providing directional physical etching
  • Electrons: High-energy particles sustaining plasma discharge
  • Photons: UV/VUV light enabling endpoint detection

Etch Mechanisms

Mechanism Description Profile Result Example
Chemical Etching Radical-driven reactions Isotropic (undercut) F + Si → SiF4
Physical Sputtering Ion bombardment Anisotropic Ar+ bombardment
Ion-Enhanced Ion + radical synergy Highly anisotropic Cl2/BCl3 poly etch
Inhibitor-Based Sidewall passivation Vertical profiles C4F8/O2 oxide etch

4.3 High Aspect Ratio Etching

High aspect ratio (HAR) etching is among the most challenging processes in semiconductor manufacturing, particularly critical for 3D NAND memory and advanced DRAM. These structures require etching features with depth-to-width ratios exceeding 100:1.

3D NAND Channel Hole Etch

Modern 3D NAND devices require channel holes penetrating through 200+ alternating oxide/nitride layers:

Parameter 176L NAND 232L NAND 300L+ NAND
Layer Count 176 232 300+
Stack Height ~8 um ~10 um ~14 um
Hole CD ~100nm ~90nm ~80nm
Aspect Ratio ~80:1 ~110:1 ~175:1
Etch Time ~3 hours ~4 hours ~6+ hours

HAR Etch Challenges

  • Ion Trajectory Distortion: Charging effects bend ion paths in deep features
  • Etch Stop/Twisting: Loss of verticality at extreme depths
  • Bottom CD Control: Maintaining dimensions at feature bottom
  • Polymer Buildup: Passivation accumulation limiting etch
  • Mask Erosion: Maintaining selectivity over extended etch times

HAR Etch Solutions

  • Pulsed Plasma: Alternating etch and deposition phases for profile control
  • Cryogenic Etch: Low temperature for enhanced selectivity and passivation
  • Mixed Frequency Excitation: Independent control of ion energy and flux
  • Process Gas Pulsing: Synchronized chemistry for depth-dependent optimization

4.4 Conductor Etch for Advanced Logic

Conductor etching creates the metal gates, contacts, and interconnects in logic devices. At 2nm and below, conductor etch must achieve atomic-scale precision while managing diverse material stacks.

Metal Gate Etch

Modern metal gate stacks consist of multiple thin layers that must be etched with extreme selectivity:

Layer Material Thickness Etch Chemistry
Hard Mask TiN, SiN 20-50nm Cl2/BCl3
Fill Metal W, Al 50-100nm Cl2/O2/BCl3
Work Function TiN, TiAl 2-5nm Cl2/Ar
Barrier TaN 1-2nm Cl2/BCl3
High-k HfO2 1-2nm BCl3/Ar

GAA Nanosheet Etch

Gate-All-Around transistors in TSMC N2 and Samsung 2nm require precise etching of nanosheet channel release:

Nanosheet Release Process

  1. Si/SiGe Superlattice: Alternating Si and SiGe layers deposited epitaxially
  2. Fin Formation: Patterning and etching to create fin structure
  3. Selective SiGe Removal: Isotropic etch removes sacrificial SiGe
  4. Nanosheet Release: Si nanosheets remain suspended
  5. Gate Wrap: GAA gate structure surrounds each nanosheet

Contact and Via Etch

Contact-level etching creates the connections between transistors and the first metal layer:

  • Contact CD: 12-20nm for advanced nodes
  • Aspect Ratio: 10-15:1
  • Selectivity: Greater than 20:1 to silicide
  • Profile: Vertical with slight taper for fill

4.5 Atomic Layer Etching (ALE)

Atomic Layer Etching represents the ultimate in etch precision, removing material one atomic layer at a time through self-limiting surface reactions. ALE is the etch counterpart to ALD, enabling the atomic-scale control required for sub-3nm devices.

ALE Process Steps

ALE Cycle:

Step 1: Surface Modification
        Reactive gas adsorbs on surface
        Forms modified layer (~1 atomic layer)
        
Step 2: Purge
        Remove excess reactants
        
Step 3: Removal
        Low-energy ions remove modified layer
        Stops at unmodified material
        
Step 4: Purge
        Remove etch products
        
Result: 0.3-0.5 nm removed per cycle

ALE Materials and Chemistries

Material Modification Step Removal Step EPC (nm)
Si Cl2 adsorption Ar+ bombardment 0.3-0.4
SiO2 C4F8 fluorocarbon Ar+ bombardment 0.4-0.5
Si3N4 CH3F/O2 plasma Ar+ bombardment 0.3-0.4
TiN Cl2 adsorption Ar+ bombardment 0.2-0.3

ALE Applications

  • FinFET Fin Trimming: Precise CD control for threshold voltage tuning
  • Spacer Definition: Atomic-scale spacer thickness control
  • Surface Smoothing: Reducing roughness for improved carrier mobility
  • Damage Removal: Removing plasma-damaged surface layers
  • 3D Structure Definition: Creating GAA inner spacers

4.6 Dielectric Etch

Dielectric etching creates vias, trenches, and contact holes in oxide and low-k dielectrics. These processes are critical for the back-end-of-line (BEOL) interconnect structure.

Dual Damascene Etch

The dual damascene process creates via and trench structures simultaneously:

  • Via-First Approach: Via etched first, then trench
  • Trench-First Approach: Trench etched first, then via
  • Aspect Ratio: Combined AR up to 10:1
  • Profile Control: Smooth sidewalls for barrier coverage

Low-k Dielectric Etch Challenges

Low-k materials (k < 3.0) used in advanced interconnects present unique etch challenges:

  • Plasma Damage: Carbon depletion increases k value
  • Profile Distortion: Pattern dependent etch loading
  • Line Edge Roughness: Critical for resistance control
  • Selectivity: Stopping on barrier without damage

4.7 Advanced Etch Process Control

Achieving the precision required for advanced nodes demands sophisticated process control systems that can respond to variations in real-time.

In-Situ Metrology

  • Scatterometry: CD and profile measurement during etch
  • Ellipsometry: Film thickness monitoring
  • OES: Plasma composition and endpoint detection
  • Plasma Probes: Ion flux and energy characterization

Advanced Process Control (APC)

APC Implementation

  • Run-to-Run Control: Recipe adjustment based on prior wafer results
  • Fault Detection: Real-time identification of process excursions
  • Virtual Metrology: Predicting results without physical measurement
  • Chamber Matching: Maintaining consistency across multiple chambers

4.8 WIA-SEMI-EQP-001 Etch Standards

The WIA-SEMI-EQP-001 standard defines comprehensive requirements for etch equipment integration:

Data Interface Specification

// WIA-SEMI-EQP-001 Etch Equipment Interface
interface EtchEquipment {
    // Equipment identification
    equipmentId: string;
    etchType: 'CONDUCTOR' | 'DIELECTRIC' | 'ALE' | 'HAR';
    vendor: string;
    model: string;
    
    // Real-time monitoring
    getPlasmaParameters(): PlasmaData;
    getEndpointSignals(): EndpointData;
    getChamberCondition(): ChamberStatus;
    
    // Process control
    executeRecipe(recipe: EtchRecipe): Promise<EtchResult>;
    adjustParameter(param: string, value: number): void;
    triggerEndpoint(): void;
    
    // Quality metrics
    getEtchDepth(): number;
    getCD(): CDMeasurement;
    getSelectivity(): number;
    getUniformity(): UniformityMap;
}

interface PlasmaData {
    rfForward: number;
    rfReflected: number;
    dcBias: number;
    pressure: number;
    gasFlows: GasFlow[];
    oesSpectrum: SpectralData;
}

Integration Requirements

  • Recipe Format: Standardized XML/JSON for recipe exchange
  • Alarm Handling: Common alarm codes and escalation procedures
  • Maintenance Data: Chamber condition and PM scheduling
  • Metrology Integration: Inline measurement data correlation
