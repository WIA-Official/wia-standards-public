6.1 Fundamentals of Ion Implantation

Ion implantation is the primary method for introducing dopant atoms into semiconductor materials with precise control over concentration and depth. The process involves accelerating ionized dopant atoms and directing them into the silicon substrate, where they modify the electrical properties of the semiconductor.

Ion Implantation Physics

When energetic ions enter the silicon lattice, they undergo a series of collisions that determine their final distribution:

  • Nuclear Stopping: Elastic collisions with silicon nuclei dominate at low energies
  • Electronic Stopping: Inelastic interactions with electrons dominate at high energies
  • Projected Range (Rp): Average depth of implanted ions
  • Straggle (deltaRp): Statistical variation in implant depth
  • Lateral Straggle: Horizontal spreading of dopant profile

Ion Source

The ion source generates the dopant ions from gaseous or solid precursors:

  • Freeman Source: Traditional hot cathode source for most species
  • Bernas Source: Higher beam currents for high-dose implants
  • IHC Source: Indirectly heated cathode for improved lifetime
  • Multicusp Source: For producing molecular and cluster ions

Mass Analyzer

The mass analyzer separates the desired dopant species from contaminants:

  • Magnetic Sector: Bends ion beam by mass/charge ratio
  • Resolution: M/deltaM greater than 100 for isotope selection
  • Contamination: Less than 0.1% unwanted species

Beam Line and Scanner

  • Electrostatic Scanner: For beam scanning across wafer
  • Mechanical Scanner: Wafer movement for hybrid scanning
  • Parallelism: Better than 0.5 degree across wafer
  • Uniformity: Better than 0.5% (1-sigma) dose uniformity

Advanced Implant Techniques

Cluster and Molecular Ion Implantation

For ultra-shallow junctions, molecular and cluster ions enable lower effective energy per dopant atom:

  • B18H22: 18 boron atoms at once, effective energy = beam energy/18
  • C7H7: Carbon cluster for co-implant
  • Benefit: Reduced channeling, shallower junctions
  • Challenge: Complex source chemistry, beam current limitations

Hot Implantation

Elevated temperature implantation reduces damage and enables dynamic annealing:

  • Temperature Range: 300-500 degrees C during implant
  • Benefit: Reduced defect density, less TED
  • Application: High-dose implants, SiC devices

6.4 Thermal Processing Systems

Thermal processing activates implanted dopants, repairs lattice damage, and drives diffusion. The thermal budget must be carefully controlled to achieve the desired dopant profile while minimizing unintended diffusion.

Thermal Process Categories

Process Type Temperature Time Scale Application
Furnace Anneal 800-1100 C Minutes-hours Oxidation, diffusion
RTP (Rapid Thermal) 900-1100 C Seconds Dopant activation
Spike Anneal 1000-1100 C Sub-second USJ activation
Millisecond Anneal 1200-1400 C 0.1-10 ms Advanced nodes
Laser Anneal Melt (1400+ C) Microseconds Contact silicide

Rapid Thermal Processing (RTP)

RTP systems heat wafers quickly using lamp-based radiation:

RTP System Specifications

  • Ramp Rate: 50-400 degrees C/second
  • Temperature Range: 200-1200 degrees C
  • Uniformity: Better than 2 degrees C (3-sigma)
  • Throughput: 80-120 wafers per hour
  • Gas Ambient: N2, O2, H2, forming gas

Millisecond Annealing

For advanced nodes, millisecond annealing (MSA) enables high activation with minimal diffusion:

  • Flash Lamp Anneal: Xe flash lamps, 0.5-20ms pulse
  • Laser Spike Anneal: CO2 or diode laser, millisecond scan
  • Peak Temperature: 1200-1400 degrees C
  • Thermal Budget: 10x lower than spike RTP

6.5 Furnace Systems

Batch furnace systems remain important for processes requiring long thermal soaks or precise ambient control.

Furnace Types

Type Orientation Batch Size Application
Horizontal Boat horizontal 100-200 wafers Oxidation, diffusion
Vertical Boat vertical 100-150 wafers LPCVD, oxidation
Mini-Batch Vertical 25-50 wafers Fast turnaround

Oxidation Processes

Thermal oxidation grows high-quality SiO2 for gate dielectrics and isolation:

  • Dry Oxidation: O2 ambient, slow growth, best quality
  • Wet Oxidation: H2O ambient, faster growth, higher defects
  • HCl Gettering: HCl addition for metal gettering
  • Temperature: 800-1100 degrees C

6.6 Doping Challenges for GAA Transistors

Gate-All-Around nanosheet transistors present unique doping challenges due to their 3D structure and nanoscale dimensions.

GAA Doping Requirements

Key Challenges

  • Conformal Doping: Uniform doping around all nanosheet surfaces
  • Junction Abruptness: Sub-3nm/decade profiles required
  • Dose Control: Precise doping in nanoscale volumes
  • Damage Mitigation: Minimizing implant damage in thin channels
  • Thermal Budget: Preserving nanosheet integrity during anneal

Alternative Doping Methods

Method Mechanism Advantage Limitation
Plasma Doping (PLAD) Low-energy plasma immersion Conformal, low damage Dose control
Gas Phase Doping Thermal diffusion from gas Highly conformal Profile control
Solid Source Diffusion from deposited layer Very conformal Additional steps
Monolayer Doping Self-assembled dopant layer Atomic precision Development stage

In-Situ Doping

For epitaxially grown source/drain regions, dopants can be incorporated during growth:

  • B doped SiGe: For PMOS raised S/D
  • P doped Si: For NMOS raised S/D
  • Concentration: Up to 3x10^21 /cm3 achievable
  • Benefit: No implant damage, abrupt profiles

6.8 WIA-SEMI-EQP-001 Standards

The WIA-SEMI-EQP-001 standard defines integration requirements for ion implantation and thermal processing equipment:

Data Interface Specification

// WIA-SEMI-EQP-001 Implant/Thermal Interface
interface ImplantEquipment {
    // Equipment identification
    equipmentId: string;
    implantType: 'HIGH_CURRENT' | 'MEDIUM_CURRENT' | 'HIGH_ENERGY' | 'PLASMA';
    
    // Recipe parameters
    getRecipe(): ImplantRecipe;
    setRecipe(recipe: ImplantRecipe): void;
    
    // Real-time monitoring
    getBeamCurrent(): number;
    getDose(): number;
    getUniformity(): UniformityMap;
    
    // Maintenance
    getSourceLifetime(): number;
    getFilamentCondition(): FilamentStatus;
}

interface ThermalEquipment {
    equipmentId: string;
    processType: 'RTP' | 'FURNACE' | 'MSA' | 'LASER';
    
    // Temperature control
    getTemperatureProfile(): TemperatureData[];
    setRecipe(recipe: ThermalRecipe): void;
    
    // Process monitoring
    getUniformity(): number;
    getGasFlows(): GasFlow[];
}

Integration Requirements

  • Dose Monitoring: Real-time dose integration and feedback
  • Temperature Control: Closed-loop temperature uniformity
  • Recipe Management: Standard format for recipe exchange
  • Predictive Maintenance: Source and filament lifetime tracking
