💎 Photonic Quantum Processors

Light-Based Quantum Computing at Room Temperature

4.1 Photonic Quantum Computing Overview

Photonic quantum computing encodes quantum information in properties of light—such as polarization, path, time-bin, or optical mode. Unlike superconducting and trapped ion systems, photonic qubits can operate at room temperature and are naturally suited for quantum communication due to light's ability to travel long distances with minimal decoherence. Companies like Xanadu, PsiQuantum, and QuiX are pursuing this approach with billion-dollar investments.

The photonic approach offers unique advantages: photons don't interact with each other (low noise), travel at the speed of light (natural for networking), and can be generated and detected with high fidelity. However, the lack of photon-photon interactions makes deterministic two-qubit gates challenging, leading to alternative computational models like measurement-based quantum computing and boson sampling.

4.2 Photonic Qubit Encodings

Multiple degrees of freedom of photons can encode quantum information. The choice of encoding affects available operations, noise sensitivity, and scalability.

🔄 Polarization Encoding

|0⟩ = horizontal, |1⟩ = vertical polarization. Simple to manipulate with wave plates. Limited by birefringence in optical fibers. Best for free-space and short fiber links.

🛤️ Path Encoding

|0⟩ and |1⟩ correspond to different spatial paths. Implemented with beam splitters and phase shifters. Scales well on integrated photonic chips. Used by Xanadu.

⏱️ Time-Bin Encoding

|0⟩ = early pulse, |1⟩ = late pulse in same spatial mode. Robust to polarization drift in fiber. Requires interferometric stability. Used for long-distance quantum communication.

🌊 Continuous Variable (CV)

Information in quadratures of optical field (amplitude/phase). Uses squeezed light states. Native Gaussian operations. Xanadu's approach with GBS (Gaussian Boson Sampling).

4.3 Key Components

  • Single-Photon Sources: Quantum dots, spontaneous parametric down-conversion (SPDC), or four-wave mixing generate individual photons. Indistinguishability and purity are critical metrics. Best sources: >99% purity, >90% indistinguishability.
  • Integrated Photonic Circuits: Waveguides, beam splitters, and phase shifters on silicon nitride, silicon, or lithium niobate chips. Programmable mesh networks implement arbitrary linear operations. Xanadu's X-Series uses silicon photonics.
  • Single-Photon Detectors: Superconducting nanowire single-photon detectors (SNSPDs) offer >95% efficiency and <50 ps timing resolution. Operate at 2-4 K. Critical for measurement-based computation.
  • Squeezed Light Sources: Optical parametric oscillators (OPOs) generate squeezed vacuum states for CV quantum computing. Xanadu demonstrated 8-mode entangled squeezed states.
  • Quantum Memories: Store photonic quantum states for synchronization. Implementations: atomic ensembles, rare-earth crystals, optical cavities. Storage times: μs to ms demonstrated.

🔬 Xanadu's Borealis Processor

Xanadu's Borealis demonstrated quantum computational advantage using Gaussian Boson Sampling with 216 squeezed-state qubits in 2022. The system uses time-multiplexing to achieve large mode numbers with compact hardware. It solved problems related to graph structure and molecular simulation that would take classical supercomputers thousands of years.

4.4 Computational Models

Measurement-Based Quantum Computing (MBQC)

MBQC starts with a highly entangled resource state (cluster state) and performs computation through adaptive single-qubit measurements. The measurement outcomes determine subsequent measurement bases, implementing arbitrary quantum circuits. This model converts the challenge of deterministic gates into the challenge of creating large entangled states.

Linear Optical Quantum Computing (LOQC)

The KLM protocol (Knill-Laflamme-Milburn) showed universal quantum computing is possible with linear optics, single-photon sources, and detectors. Two-qubit gates are probabilistic but can be made near-deterministic using resource states and feedforward.

Boson Sampling and GBS

Boson sampling, introduced by Aaronson and Arkhipov, is a non-universal quantum computation where photons pass through a linear optical network and are measured at outputs. Computing the output distribution is classically hard (#P-hard). Gaussian Boson Sampling (GBS) uses squeezed states and has applications in molecular simulation and optimization.

🏭 PsiQuantum's Manufacturing Strategy

PsiQuantum has raised over $700 million to build a million-qubit photonic quantum computer using standard semiconductor manufacturing (GlobalFoundries' 300mm fabs). Their fusion-based approach generates entanglement probabilistically but uses error correction to achieve fault tolerance. The company claims this is the only path to commercially useful quantum computers at scale.

4.6 Advantages and Challenges

✅ Advantages

Room temperature: No cryogenics needed (except detectors).

Low decoherence: Photons don't interact with environment.

Networking: Natural for quantum communication.

Manufacturing: Leverages silicon photonics industry.

❌ Challenges

Photon loss: Exponential resource overhead.

Deterministic gates: Photon-photon interaction weak.

Source quality: Need identical, pure photons.

Detection: SNSPDs require cryogenics.

4.7 Chapter Summary

📋 Key Takeaways

• Photonic qubits encode information in light properties: polarization, path, time-bin, or CV

• Computational models: MBQC, LOQC, Boson Sampling, GBS

• Key components: single-photon sources, integrated circuits, SNSPDs, squeezed light

• Xanadu's Borealis demonstrated quantum advantage with 216-mode GBS

• PsiQuantum targets million-qubit system using semiconductor manufacturing

• Natural fit for quantum networking and communication
