Chapter 8: Compliance and Certification

8.1 ISO 10218 — Industrial Robot Safety

ISO 10218-1:2011 (Robots for industrial environments — Safety requirements — Part 1: Robots) and ISO 10218-2:2011 (Part 2: Robot systems and integration) define fundamental safety requirements for industrial robot installations. Key WIA-ROBOT requirements derived from ISO 10218-1:

  • §5.4.2 — Emergency stop function: Category 0 or Category 1 stop; response time not to exceed the time required to reach a safe state (WIA target: 10 ms for E-Stop signal propagation).
  • §5.6 — Speed and force limiting: collaborative operation shall limit TCP speed to 250 mm/s and contact force per ISO 15066.
  • §5.7 — Limiting device: hardware over-travel protection independent of software control.
  • §5.8 — Safety-rated monitored stop: robot holds position without removing power when human enters collaboration zone.

8.2 ISO 15066:2016 — Collaborative Robots

ISO 15066:2016 specifies safety requirements for collaborative robot systems operating in direct cooperation with humans. Four collaborative operation modes:

Safety-rated monitored stopRobot halts when human enters0 (stopped)

Speed and separation monitoringRobot slows as human approachesProportional to separation distance

ISO 15066 Table A.1 body region force limits (partial): head/neck — 65 N transient, 25 N quasi-static; chest/back — 140 N transient, 60 N quasi-static; hand — 140 N transient, 70 N quasi-static.

8.3 ISO 13482:2014 — Personal Care Robots

ISO 13482:2014 (Robots and robotic devices — Safety requirements for personal care robots) covers mobile servant robots, physical assistant robots, and person carrier robots. Relevant to WIA care robots, exoskeletons, and mobility aids:

  • Risk assessment per ISO 12100 (risk estimation and evaluation methodology).
  • Stability requirements: robot must not tip over on slopes up to 3° (Type B mobile servant) or 6° (Type A physical assistant).
  • Entrapment prevention: no limb-entrapment gaps between 4 mm and 25 mm.
  • Load capacity: exoskeleton must support 125% of rated body weight for 1 minute without failure.

8.4 IEC 61508 — Functional Safety

IEC 61508:2010 (Functional Safety of E/E/PE Safety-related Systems) defines Safety Integrity Levels (SIL). WIA-ROBOT targets:

Robot TypeTarget SILPFH (failures/hour)
Exoskeleton / RehabilitationSIL 210⁻⁷ to 10⁻⁶
Surgical RobotSIL 2–310⁻⁸ to 10⁻⁷
Care Robot / Mobility AidSIL 110⁻⁶ to 10⁻⁵

SIL 2 compliance requires systematic capability (SC) 3 for the software development process — covering requirements specification, design, implementation, testing, and configuration management. WIA-ROBOT software uses ROS 2 REP-2004 Quality Level 1 packages exclusively in the safety-critical path.

FDA Digital Health Center of Excellence (DHCoE) guidance on AI/ML-based software as a Medical Device (SaMD) applies to any WIA robot using machine learning for patient-facing decisions (gait control, fall prediction).

8.6 EU MDR 2017/745 (Europe)

In the EU, medical robots are regulated under the Medical Device Regulation 2017/745 (MDR), which replaced the MDD 93/42/EEC. Classification:

  • Exoskeleton/rehabilitation robot — typically Class IIa (Rule 9: active therapeutic device) or IIb if implant-interfacing.
  • Surgical robot — Class III (Rule 8: surgically invasive) or IIb (non-implantable instruments).
  • Requires CE marking via Notified Body (NB) audit + Technical Documentation (IEC 62304 software lifecycle, ISO 14971 risk management).

8.8 Korea Robot Industry Standards

In Korea, assistive robots are regulated by the Ministry of Trade, Industry and Energy (MOTIE) under the Robot Industry Promotion Act (로봇산업진흥법) and the Intelligent Robot Development and Dissemination Promotion Act. KITECH (Korea Institute of Industrial Technology) operates the Korea Robot Industry Association (KRIA) testing infrastructure. KS B ISO 13482 is the Korean mirror of ISO 13482:2014.
