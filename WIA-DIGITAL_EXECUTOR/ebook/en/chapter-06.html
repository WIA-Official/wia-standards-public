CHAPTER 6

Implementation Guide

This chapter provides practical guidance for implementing the WIA-DIGITAL_EXECUTOR standard in various contexts, from individual users setting up personal digital estate plans to platform providers integrating estate planning features, and service providers building comprehensive digital executor services.

6.1 Individual User Implementation

For individuals seeking to establish their digital estate plans, implementation follows a structured process that can be completed over several sessions, typically requiring 4-8 hours of initial setup and periodic updates.

6.1.1 Quick Start Guide (First Hour)

Begin with high-priority assets and basic planning. Install a password manager with emergency access features such as 1Password, Bitwarden, or LastPass. Create an initial asset inventory template using spreadsheet or dedicated tools. Designate at least one emergency contact or preliminary executor. Document the location of critical information (safe combinations, key documents). Set up an initial backup of important photos and documents.

6.1.2 Comprehensive Implementation (4-8 Hours)

A complete implementation addresses all aspects of digital estate planning systematically. Conduct full digital asset discovery using techniques from Chapter 4. Transfer all passwords to password manager with strong master password. Configure emergency access in password manager (typically 7-30 day waiting period). Create detailed asset inventory with instructions for each account. Designate specific beneficiaries for different asset categories. Set up two-factor authentication backup codes stored securely. Document cryptocurrency wallet details and key storage locations. Create archival backups of critical data (photos, documents, emails). Draft letter of intent explaining your wishes and reasoning. Discuss plans with family members and designated executors. Store documentation securely with copies in multiple locations.

6.1.3 Maintenance and Updates

Digital estate plans require periodic maintenance as accounts change, assets grow, and life circumstances evolve. Quarterly reviews (15-30 minutes) should verify password manager is updated with new accounts, confirm emergency contacts are still appropriate, and check that backup codes remain accessible. Annual reviews (1-2 hours) should update asset inventory with new accounts and closed accounts, review beneficiary designations, update cryptocurrency holdings and key locations, verify executor is still willing and able, refresh data backups, and review and update instructions.

Life event triggers requiring immediate updates include marriage or divorce, birth or adoption of children, death of designated executor or beneficiary, significant asset changes (large cryptocurrency holdings, business acquisition), and relocation to different jurisdiction.

6.2 Platform Provider Integration

Online platforms and service providers can implement WIA-DIGITAL_EXECUTOR support to provide better estate planning features for their users. Integration levels range from basic to comprehensive.

6.2.1 Basic Integration: Legacy Contact API

The minimum integration allows users to designate legacy contacts through their account settings. This requires implementing legacy contact designation endpoints, notification protocols when estate execution is triggered, secure credential or data transfer mechanisms, and memorialization or closure options.

6.2.2 Standard Integration: Full WIA-DIGITAL_EXECUTOR Support

Comprehensive integration implements the complete WIA-DIGITAL_EXECUTOR specification including standardized data formats for estate planning, API endpoints for executor authentication, automated account status monitoring, data export in WIA-standard formats, integration with external estate planning services, and support for executor verification processes.

6.3 Digital Executor Service Implementation

Organizations providing comprehensive digital executor services must implement the full WIA-DIGITAL_EXECUTOR standard while addressing additional business and operational considerations.

6.3.1 Core Service Components

A complete digital executor service requires multiple integrated systems. The User Management System handles account registration, subscription management, and user authentication. The Asset Registry stores encrypted asset inventories with secure credential vaulting. The Beneficiary Database maintains verified beneficiary information with multi-factor identity verification. The Execution Engine monitors triggers, manages workflows, and coordinates transfers. The Integration Layer connects to external platforms and services. The Legal Compliance Module ensures regulatory compliance and generates required documentation. The Security Infrastructure provides encryption, access control, and audit logging.

6.3.2 Operational Requirements

Service providers must establish 24/7 monitoring for system health and security, customer support with estate planning expertise, legal partnerships for complex cases, geographic redundancy to prevent data loss, disaster recovery procedures, succession planning for the service itself (what happens if the service provider goes out of business?), insurance coverage for errors and omissions, and regulatory compliance in operating jurisdictions.

Critical Question: Digital executor services must answer "Who executes the executor?" If the service provider ceases operations, users' estate plans must remain accessible. Solutions include data escrow arrangements, portable export formats, partnership networks for service continuity, and blockchain-based backup protocols.

6.3.3 Business Models and Pricing

Various business models support digital executor services. Subscription models charge monthly or annual fees ($5-50/month depending on features), providing predictable revenue and ongoing service funding but requiring long-term user commitment. One-time setup fees ($200-1000) are paid for initial estate plan creation with optional maintenance subscriptions, lower barrier to entry but less predictable revenue.

Freemium models offer basic features free with premium tiers for advanced features ($10-30/month for premium), maximize user adoption but require strong conversion to premium. Enterprise plans provide service to employees as benefit ($5-15 per employee), delivered through employers as wellness/financial planning benefit. Estate value-based models charge percentage (0.5-2%) of digital estate value, align pricing with value but require asset valuation.

6.4 Open Source Implementation

The WIA-DIGITAL_EXECUTOR standard encourages open source implementations to maximize accessibility and enable community innovation. Reference implementations are available at https://github.com/WIA-Official/digital-executor for core protocol libraries, example applications, and integration guides.

6.4.1 Self-Hosted Setup

Technical users can deploy their own digital executor infrastructure using the open source reference implementation. Minimum requirements include Linux server (Ubuntu 22.04+ recommended), 2GB RAM, 20GB storage, and public IP or domain name. The Docker-based deployment simplifies installation and management with PostgreSQL for data storage, Redis for session management, and Nginx as reverse proxy.

# Quick Start - Docker Compose Deployment git clone https://github.com/WIA-Official/digital-executor.git cd digital-executor # Configure environment cp .env.example .env nano .env # Edit configuration # Generate encryption keys ./scripts/generate-keys.sh # Start services docker-compose up -d # Initialize database docker-compose exec app npm run db:migrate docker-compose exec app npm run db:seed # Create admin account docker-compose exec app npm run create-admin # Access at https://your-domain.com

6.4.2 Security Hardening

Self-hosted implementations require careful security configuration including SSL/TLS certificates (Let's Encrypt recommended), firewall rules (only expose ports 80, 443), regular security updates, encrypted backups to multiple locations, strong password policies, rate limiting on API endpoints, and intrusion detection monitoring.

🔑 Key Takeaways

  • Individual implementation follows a structured process from quick start (1 hour covering critical assets, password manager, emergency contact) to comprehensive setup (4-8 hours for full asset discovery, documentation, beneficiary designation, and family communication), with ongoing maintenance through quarterly reviews (15-30 min) and annual updates (1-2 hours).
  • Platform provider integration offers two levels: Basic Integration (legacy contact API allowing users to designate contacts with configurable access) and Standard Integration (full WIA-DIGITAL_EXECUTOR support with standardized data formats, executor authentication APIs, automated monitoring, and external service integration).
  • Digital executor service providers must implement seven core components: User Management, Asset Registry, Beneficiary Database, Execution Engine, Integration Layer, Legal Compliance Module, and Security Infrastructure—with 24/7 operations, legal partnerships, geographic redundancy, and service continuity planning essential.
  • Business models for digital executor services include subscription ($5-50/month), one-time setup fees ($200-1000), freemium (basic free, premium $10-30/month), enterprise plans ($5-15 per employee), and estate value-based pricing (0.5-2% of assets)—each with different revenue predictability and user adoption patterns.
  • Open source self-hosted implementations enable technical users to deploy their own infrastructure using the reference implementation at https://github.com/WIA-Official/digital-executor with minimum requirements of 2GB RAM, 20GB storage, Docker-based deployment, and comprehensive security hardening including SSL/TLS, firewalls, encrypted backups, and intrusion detection.
