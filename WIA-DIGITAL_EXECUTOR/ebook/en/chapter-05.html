CHAPTER 5

Phase 3 & 4: Verification and Execution

After planning goals and cataloging assets, the WIA-DIGITAL_EXECUTOR standard addresses the critical phases of identity verification and automated execution. These phases transform static planning into dynamic action when the time comes to transfer digital assets to beneficiaries.

5.1 Phase 3: Identity Verification and Authorization

Identity verification is perhaps the most legally and ethically sensitive aspect of digital estate management. The system must confirm with high confidence that individuals claiming to be beneficiaries are who they claim to be, while also preventing fraudulent claims and identity theft targeting deceased persons' estates.

5.1.1 Multi-Factor Verification Framework

The WIA-DIGITAL_EXECUTOR standard implements a comprehensive multi-factor verification framework that combines several independent verification methods to establish identity with high confidence. This framework recognizes that no single verification method is foolproof, and combining multiple factors dramatically increases security.

Verification Factor Method Strength Implementation
Knowledge Information only the legitimate beneficiary knows Medium Secret questions, shared memories, family details
Possession Physical items the beneficiary possesses High Government ID, inherited documents, access codes
Inherence Biometric characteristics of the beneficiary Very High Fingerprints, facial recognition, voice analysis
Location Geographic or network location verification Medium IP address verification, GPS confirmation
Legal Documentation Court orders, death certificates, legal attestations Very High Official documents from recognized authorities
Third-Party Attestation Trusted individuals or organizations confirm identity High Attorneys, notaries, family witnesses

5.1.2 Graduated Verification Levels

The standard defines four verification levels, with requirements increasing based on asset value and sensitivity. Level 1 (Basic) requires email verification plus one additional factor, suitable for low-value sentimental assets like social media posts or shared photos. Level 2 (Standard) requires government ID verification plus two additional factors, appropriate for most personal digital assets including email, cloud storage, and personal documents.

Level 3 (Enhanced) demands government ID, biometric verification, video interview, and legal documentation, necessary for financial accounts, cryptocurrency holdings, and valuable business assets. Level 4 (Legal) requires court orders, attorney attestation, multiple witness confirmations, and notarized documents, reserved for disputed estates, high-value assets, or situations involving legal challenges.

5.1.3 Death Verification Protocols

Before any estate execution begins, the deceased's death must be verified through authorized channels. The standard accepts official death certificates issued by government authorities, medical examiner reports in cases of unusual death, court declarations of death for missing persons, and in some jurisdictions, sworn affidavits from multiple parties.

Fraud Prevention: Death verification is cross-referenced against public death registries, obituaries, and Social Security Death Index records. The system implements waiting periods (typically 30-90 days) before irreversible actions to allow for fraud detection and error correction.

5.1.4 Inactivity Monitoring Alternative

Not all estate execution requires death verification. The standard supports inactivity-based triggers where prolonged account inactivity initiates a verification process to determine if the account holder is deceased or simply inactive. The system monitors login patterns, sends graduated notifications (email, SMS, phone calls), contacts emergency contacts after extended inactivity, and provides ample opportunity for the account holder to confirm they're alive before any execution proceeds.

Inactivity thresholds are configurable, typically ranging from 90 days to 2 years depending on the asset type and user preferences. High-value assets generally have longer inactivity periods and more aggressive notification protocols.

5.2 Phase 4: Automated Execution Protocols

Once verification is complete, Phase 4 implements the actual transfer of digital assets to beneficiaries. This phase must be carefully orchestrated to honor the deceased's wishes while navigating platform policies, legal requirements, and technical constraints.

5.2.1 Execution Workflow Architecture

Standard Execution Flow

Step 1: Trigger Detection
System detects death certificate submission or inactivity threshold breach. Begins verification process with all relevant parties notified of pending execution.
Step 2: Verification Period (30-90 days)
Identity verification of executors and beneficiaries. Death confirmation through multiple sources. Legal documentation review. Opportunity for disputes or corrections.
Step 3: Asset Assessment
System inventories all registered assets, determines which can be automatically transferred, identifies assets requiring manual intervention, and generates transfer plan.
Step 4: Credential Transfer
Encrypted credentials are decrypted and securely transferred to authorized beneficiaries through encrypted channels. Access codes, recovery information, and documentation provided.
Step 5: Platform Coordination
For platforms supporting WIA-DIGITAL_EXECUTOR, automated account transfer or memorialization. For non-supporting platforms, executor receives instructions and credentials for manual processing.
Step 6: Confirmation and Closure
Beneficiaries confirm receipt of assets. System generates completion report. Audit trail sealed and archived. Estate marked as settled.

5.2.2 Platform-Specific Execution Strategies

Different platforms require different execution approaches. The standard defines three strategic categories for handling various platforms.

Platform Category Characteristics Execution Strategy Examples
WIA-Compatible Platforms implementing WIA-DIGITAL_EXECUTOR APIs Automated API-driven transfer with full system integration Future-forward platforms, digital estate services
Legacy-Aware Platforms with existing legacy/memorial features Hybrid approach using platform tools plus WIA coordination Google, Facebook, Apple with legacy contacts
Manual-Required Platforms with no estate planning features Credential transfer to executor for manual processing Most smaller platforms and services
Non-Transferable Platforms prohibiting account transfer Data archival before account closure; documentation provided Some streaming services, gaming platforms

5.2.3 Cryptocurrency Execution Mechanisms

Cryptocurrency execution requires specialized handling due to its unique technical characteristics. The standard supports multiple execution mechanisms for different security and trust models.

Simple Transfer uses pre-signed transactions that become valid upon trigger condition. The deceased creates and signs transactions in advance, with executors broadcasting them when authorized. This is simple and reliable but inflexible. Multi-Signature Wallets require multiple parties to authorize transactions. The deceased holds some keys, executors or beneficiaries hold others, and death triggers release of the deceased's keys to complete the necessary signature set.

Time-Locked Transactions use Bitcoin's or Ethereum's time-lock features, making transactions valid only after a specific date or block height, with executors broadcasting pre-signed time-locked transactions when the time comes. Smart Contract Executors implement estate execution logic on blockchain platforms like Ethereum, with code automatically transferring assets upon verifiable trigger conditions, offering maximum automation but requiring technical expertise.

Dead Man's Switch protocols require periodic confirmation of continued life, with assets automatically transferring to beneficiaries if confirmations cease for a configured period. This is useful for inactivity-based execution but requires ongoing attention from the account holder.

5.2.4 Data Archival and Preservation

Before closing or transferring accounts, the system archives data according to the deceased's instructions. This includes email archives in standard formats (MBOX, PST), cloud storage downloads organized by file type and date, social media content exports (posts, photos, messages where permitted), database exports for business applications, website full backups including databases and files, and cryptocurrency transaction histories.

Archives are encrypted and distributed to designated beneficiaries, with multiple copies stored in different locations for redundancy. The standard specifies open, non-proprietary formats to ensure long-term accessibility regardless of changing technology platforms.

5.3 Execution Safeguards and Dispute Resolution

Estate execution is irreversible for many asset types, making safeguards essential to prevent errors and fraud.

5.3.1 Waiting Periods and Notifications

The standard mandates waiting periods between trigger detection and irreversible execution. During these periods, all interested parties receive notifications: executors, beneficiaries, emergency contacts, potentially affected parties, and legal representatives if designated. Notifications include what action is pending, when it will execute, how to dispute or correct if needed, and contact information for questions.

Standard waiting periods are 30 days for low-value sentimental assets, 60 days for standard personal accounts, 90 days for financial assets and cryptocurrency, and 120+ days for high-value or disputed estates.

5.3.2 Dispute Resolution Framework

When disputes arise about beneficiary identity, asset ownership, transfer instructions, or executor authority, the standard provides a structured resolution process. Initial dispute filing halts execution pending resolution. Evidence submission from all parties is reviewed by neutral arbitrator or legal authority. Waiting period extends until resolution, and final decision by court or arbitration governs execution.

Principle: The system prioritizes accuracy over speed. Better to delay execution while resolving uncertainties than to execute incorrectly and cause irreversible harm or legal complications.

5.3.3 Fraud Detection and Prevention

The system implements multiple fraud detection mechanisms including cross-referencing death certificates with public records, monitoring for duplicate or conflicting claims, analyzing beneficiary verification patterns for anomalies, geo-location checks for suspicious access attempts, and machine learning models trained on fraudulent claim patterns.

When fraud is suspected, execution is automatically halted, law enforcement may be notified, additional verification is required, and legal review is triggered before proceeding.

🔑 Key Takeaways

  • Phase 3 (Verification) implements a multi-factor framework combining six independent verification factors: knowledge (secret information), possession (physical items), inherence (biometrics), location (geographic verification), legal documentation (official certificates), and third-party attestation (trusted confirmations)—no single factor is sufficient for high-value assets.
  • The standard defines four graduated verification levels from Basic (email + one factor for sentimental assets) to Legal (court orders + attorney attestation + multiple witnesses for disputed high-value estates), with requirements scaling based on asset value, sensitivity, and potential for disputes.
  • Death verification requires official documentation (death certificates, medical examiner reports, court declarations) cross-referenced against public registries, with 30-90 day waiting periods before irreversible actions to allow for fraud detection and error correction—inactivity monitoring provides an alternative trigger with graduated notifications.
  • Phase 4 (Execution) follows a six-step workflow from trigger detection through verification period, asset assessment, credential transfer, platform coordination, to final confirmation and closure—with different strategies for WIA-compatible platforms (automated), legacy-aware platforms (hybrid), manual-required platforms (credential transfer), and non-transferable platforms (archival before closure).
  • Cryptocurrency execution uses specialized mechanisms including simple transfer (pre-signed transactions), multi-signature wallets (distributed key control), time-locked transactions (blockchain time-locks), smart contract executors (automated code), and dead man's switch protocols (periodic life confirmation required)—each offering different trade-offs between security, flexibility, and automation.
