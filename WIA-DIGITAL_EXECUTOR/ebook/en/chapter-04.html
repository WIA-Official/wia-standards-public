CHAPTER 4

Phase 1 & 2: Planning and Asset Inventory

Digital estate planning begins with two foundational phases: establishing your planning goals and creating a comprehensive inventory of your digital assets. These phases lay the groundwork for all subsequent activities and determine the effectiveness of your overall digital estate plan.

4.1 Phase 1: Estate Planning Goals

Before cataloging assets or designating beneficiaries, you must clarify your goals for your digital estate. Different people have different priorities, and your plan should reflect your personal values, family situation, and specific concerns.

4.1.1 Defining Your Objectives

Digital estate planning objectives typically fall into several categories. Asset preservation focuses on ensuring valuable digital assets (financial, sentimental, or commercial) are transferred to intended beneficiaries. Memory preservation emphasizes saving photos, videos, messages, and other content that document your life and relationships. Business continuity ensures digital businesses, websites, or professional activities continue operating or are properly wound down. Privacy protection involves preventing unauthorized access to private information and communications. Legacy management determines how your online presence and reputation are managed after death. Financial settlement covers proper handling of cryptocurrency, online accounts, and digital revenue streams.

Planning Goals Assessment

□ Identify digital assets with financial value requiring transfer
□ Determine which memories and content to preserve
□ Assess business assets requiring continuity planning
□ Define privacy boundaries for posthumous access
□ Decide on social media and online presence preferences
□ Consider charitable or educational uses for digital assets
□ Document special instructions or wishes

4.1.2 Family Communication Strategy

Digital estate planning requires open communication with family members and designated executors. Discussing death and asset distribution can be uncomfortable, but it's essential for preventing conflicts and ensuring your wishes are understood. Consider holding a family meeting to discuss your digital estate plans, explaining why you've made specific decisions, sharing information about where important documents are stored, introducing family members to your designated digital executor, and addressing questions and concerns from family members.

Documentation should include a letter of intent explaining your wishes, contact information for your digital executor, the location of asset inventories and access credentials, and guidance on which assets have priority for preservation or transfer.

4.2 Phase 2: Digital Asset Discovery and Inventory

Creating a comprehensive inventory of digital assets is perhaps the most time-consuming aspect of digital estate planning, but it's absolutely essential. Assets you don't document may be lost forever when you die.

4.2.1 Asset Discovery Techniques

Most people dramatically underestimate how many digital accounts they maintain. Systematic discovery techniques help ensure comprehensive coverage. Email archaeology involves searching your email for registration confirmations, password reset emails, and account notifications. Browser password managers contain stored credentials for many sites. Credit card and bank statements reveal subscription services and account charges. Mobile device apps indicate accounts that may not be accessed via web browsers. Social media connections may be linked to other services via single sign-on.

4.2.2 Documentation Standards

For each discovered asset, comprehensive documentation should include the asset type and category (email, social media, financial, etc.), platform or service provider name, account identifier (username, email, account number), access URL or application name, estimated value (financial or sentimental), current status (active, dormant, closed), location of access credentials, two-factor authentication details, recovery information (backup codes, recovery email/phone), special instructions for handling, designated beneficiaries, and last verification date.

Security Note: Never store actual passwords in your asset inventory document. Instead, reference where passwords can be found (password manager, encrypted file, safe deposit box, etc.). The inventory itself should be treated as sensitive but not catastrophic if compromised.

4.2.3 Password Vault Management

Modern digital estate planning requires a password manager or vault. Manual password management is impractical when dealing with dozens or hundreds of accounts. When selecting a password vault solution, consider security features (encryption strength, zero-knowledge architecture), emergency access capabilities (designated emergency contacts who can request access after waiting period), cross-platform support, password generation and analysis tools, secure sharing capabilities, and export functionality for portability.

Popular password managers include 1Password (offers family plans with estate planning features), LastPass (emergency access with configurable waiting periods), Bitwarden (open-source, self-hosting option available), Dashlane (secure sharing and emergency features), and KeePass (completely local, maximum control but requires manual backup and sharing).

4.3 Cryptocurrency and Digital Asset Special Considerations

Cryptocurrency requires special attention due to its unique technical characteristics and high value. Unlike traditional accounts where legal authority can restore access, cryptocurrency is controlled solely by private keys.

4.3.1 Private Key Management

Private keys must be handled with extreme care. They provide complete, irrevocable control over cryptocurrency holdings. Best practices include using hardware wallets (Ledger, Trezor) that keep keys offline, implementing multi-signature wallets requiring multiple keys to authorize transactions, creating encrypted backups of seed phrases (the human-readable form of private keys), storing backups in multiple secure physical locations, never storing private keys digitally without strong encryption, and creating clear documentation about key locations without revealing the keys themselves.

Storage Method Security Level Accessibility Best For
Hardware Wallet Very High Medium Active holdings, regular transactions
Paper Wallet High (if stored securely) Low Long-term holdings, inheritance
Metal Backup Very High Low Disaster-proof backup of seed phrases
Multi-Signature Very High Medium Shared control, inheritance planning
Shamir's Secret Sharing Extreme Low Maximum security, distributed trust
Exchange Custody Medium High Small holdings, frequent trading

4.3.2 NFT and Digital Collectible Inventory

Non-fungible tokens (NFTs) and digital collectibles present unique inventory challenges. They're stored on blockchains like Ethereum, but accessing them requires knowing which blockchain networks to check, which wallet addresses you control, how to use blockchain explorers to view holdings, and which marketplaces (OpenSea, Rarible, etc.) list your collections.

Your inventory should document the blockchain network (Ethereum, Solana, Polygon, etc.), wallet addresses holding NFTs, marketplace accounts where NFTs are listed, significant individual NFTs by name and token ID, private keys or seed phrases controlling access, and any royalty arrangements for creator NFTs.

4.4 Beneficiary Designation (Phase 2 Extension)

Parallel to asset inventory, you must designate beneficiaries who will receive access to or ownership of digital assets. This process requires careful thought about who should receive what, and under what conditions.

4.4.1 Beneficiary Selection Criteria

Not all beneficiaries are appropriate for all assets. Consider technical competency (can they manage what they inherit?), emotional readiness (are they prepared for potentially sensitive content?), legal authority (are they old enough, legally authorized?), trustworthiness (will they honor restrictions and privacy preferences?), and practical capability (do they have time and resources to manage inherited assets?).

For complex estates, you might designate different beneficiaries for different asset categories: a technically skilled family member for cryptocurrency and technical accounts, a spouse or partner for personal communications and photos, an business partner for professional accounts and business assets, and a trusted friend for social media memorialization.

4.4.2 Conditional Beneficiary Rules

The WIA-DIGITAL_EXECUTOR standard supports sophisticated conditional rules for beneficiary designation. You can specify age conditions (beneficiary must reach certain age before receiving access), survival conditions (if primary beneficiary is deceased, assets go to secondary), achievement conditions (educational milestones, responsible behavior), time-delayed access (immediate for some assets, delayed for others), and shared access (multiple beneficiaries with different permission levels).

🔑 Key Takeaways

  • Phase 1 (Planning Goals) requires defining clear objectives across six categories: asset preservation, memory preservation, business continuity, privacy protection, legacy management, and financial settlement—with open family communication essential for preventing future conflicts.
  • Phase 2 (Asset Inventory) demands systematic discovery using email archaeology, password managers, credit card statements, mobile apps, and social media connections to identify all digital accounts—most people have 80-100+ passwords and significantly underestimate their digital footprint.
  • Comprehensive asset documentation must include 12 key elements for each asset: type, platform, identifier, access URL, value, status, credential location, 2FA details, recovery info, handling instructions, beneficiaries, and last verification date—never store actual passwords in the inventory itself.
  • Cryptocurrency requires special handling because private keys provide complete, irrevocable control—best practices include hardware wallets, multi-signature wallets, encrypted seed phrase backups in multiple physical locations, and clear documentation about key locations without revealing the keys themselves.
  • Beneficiary designation should match recipients to appropriate assets based on technical competency, emotional readiness, legal authority, trustworthiness, and practical capability—with conditional rules supporting age requirements, survival conditions, time delays, and shared access for complex inheritance scenarios.
