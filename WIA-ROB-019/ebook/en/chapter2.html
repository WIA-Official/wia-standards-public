2.1 Anthropometric Design Principles

The mechanical design of humanoid robots begins with understanding human biomechanics and anthropometry. WIA-ROB-019 establishes design guidelines based on human proportions, ensuring robots can navigate human environments and use human tools effectively.

2.1.1 Standard Dimensions

The standard defines three size categories to accommodate different applications and design constraints:

Category Height Range Weight Range Applications
Compact 100-140 cm 15-40 kg Research, education, child interaction, confined spaces
Standard 150-180 cm 40-80 kg Service, healthcare, general applications
Industrial 180-210 cm 80-150 kg Heavy manipulation, construction, disaster response

Within each category, proportions should approximate human ratios to maintain natural appearance and functional compatibility:

  • Head height: 1/7 to 1/8 of total height
  • Shoulder width: 2.5 to 3 head widths
  • Arm length: Approximately equal to total height
  • Leg length: 45-50% of total height
  • Torso length: 30-35% of total height
  • Hand length: 10-11% of total height
  • Foot length: 14-15% of total height

⚖️ Mass Distribution

Proper mass distribution is critical for balance and energy efficiency. The center of mass should be located approximately at the hip joint level (50-55% of total height from ground) with the robot in a neutral standing position. This placement minimizes the energy required to maintain balance and facilitates natural walking dynamics.

2.2 Structural Materials and Design

2.2.1 Material Selection Criteria

Material selection for humanoid robots involves balancing multiple competing requirements: strength, weight, cost, manufacturability, and aesthetic considerations. WIA-ROB-019 provides guidelines for appropriate material choices:

Structural Components

  • Aluminum Alloys (6061, 7075): Excellent strength-to-weight ratio, good machinability, moderate cost. Widely used for primary structural elements. Typical yield strength: 250-500 MPa, density: 2.7 g/cm³.
  • Carbon Fiber Composites: Superior strength-to-weight ratio, design flexibility, higher cost. Used in high-performance applications and weight-critical components. Tensile strength: 500-3000 MPa, density: 1.5-1.6 g/cm³.
  • Titanium Alloys (Ti-6Al-4V): Exceptional strength and corrosion resistance, high cost. Reserved for high-stress components in advanced systems. Yield strength: 880-950 MPa, density: 4.4 g/cm³.
  • Engineering Plastics (PEEK, PEI, Nylon): Low weight, moderate strength, good for secondary structures and housings. Cost-effective for non-critical components. Tensile strength: 50-100 MPa, density: 1.1-1.3 g/cm³.

Transmission and Actuation

  • Steel (4340, 17-4 PH): High strength for gears, shafts, and load-bearing components. Surface treatments (nitriding, carburizing) enhance wear resistance.
  • Beryllium Copper: Non-magnetic, high conductivity, good for electrical components near sensors.
  • Composite Belts and Cables: Kevlar, Spectra, Dyneema for tension elements in cable-driven systems.

2.2.2 Finite Element Analysis Requirements

All structural components must undergo FEA to verify they meet safety factors under expected loading conditions. WIA-ROB-019 requires:

  • Minimum safety factor of 3.0 for static loads
  • Minimum safety factor of 5.0 for dynamic/impact loads
  • Fatigue analysis for components experiencing cyclic loading (>10⁶ cycles expected)
  • Modal analysis to identify resonant frequencies (must be >5× control frequency)
  • Thermal analysis for components near heat sources (motors, electronics)

Testing Protocol

After FEA, critical components require physical testing to validate simulation results. This includes static load testing to 150% of maximum expected load, dynamic testing with representative load profiles, and accelerated fatigue testing. Documentation of all test results is required for WIA-ROB-019 certification.

2.3 Joint Design and Actuation

2.3.1 Joint Architecture

Robot joints must provide controlled motion, support structural loads, and integrate sensors for position and force feedback. WIA-ROB-019 recognizes three primary joint architectures:

Direct Drive

Motor directly connected to joint without gearing. Advantages include zero backlash, high bandwidth, and excellent force control through current sensing. Disadvantages are lower torque and higher cost due to larger motors. Primarily used in research platforms and applications requiring high-fidelity force control.

Design requirements:

  • Frameless motors with high torque constant (>0.1 Nm/A)
  • High-resolution encoders (>17 bits, <10 arc-second accuracy)
  • Integrated torque sensing (±0.5 Nm accuracy)
  • Continuous torque: 5-50 Nm depending on joint

Geared Transmission

Most common architecture, using harmonic drives, planetary gears, or cycloidal reducers to amplify motor torque. Reduction ratios typically 50:1 to 200:1. Harmonic drives offer zero backlash and high reduction ratios in compact packages but have limited backdrivability.

Design requirements:

  • Harmonic drive: >90% efficiency, <1 arc-minute backlash
  • Planetary gears: >95% efficiency, <5 arc-minute backlash
  • Rated torque: 20-300 Nm depending on joint and robot size
  • Position sensing: post-reducer, 14-bit minimum resolution

Series Elastic Actuation

Compliant element (spring) placed between motor/gearbox and joint output. The spring deflection is measured to determine applied torque, enabling precise force control and impact absorption. Particularly valuable for safe human-robot interaction.

Design requirements:

  • Spring constant: selected to provide 5-30° deflection at maximum torque
  • Deflection sensing: optical encoders, capacitive sensors, or strain gauges
  • Force accuracy: ±1% of full scale
  • Bandwidth: >10 Hz for force control loop

2.4 Hand and Gripper Design

2.4.1 Grasp Taxonomy

Human hands perform diverse grasping strategies. WIA-ROB-019 identifies essential grasp types that humanoid robot hands should support:

  • Power Grasp: Object held against palm with fingers wrapped around. Used for tools, handles, containers. Requires 20-100 N grip force.
  • Precision Grasp: Object held between fingertips. Used for small objects, writing, delicate manipulation. Requires 1-20 N grip force with high position accuracy (±1 mm).
  • Tripod Grasp: Object held between thumb and two fingers. Common for pens, small tools. Intermediate force and precision requirements.
  • Hook Grasp: Fingers hook around object (bag handles, loops). Passive grip strength, minimal control needed.
  • Lateral Grasp: Object held between thumb and side of index finger. Used for cards, keys, thin objects.

2.4.2 Hand Configurations

The standard recognizes three hand complexity levels:

Level 1: Basic Gripper

Simple parallel-jaw or adaptive gripper with 1-2 DOF. Suitable for structured environments with known objects. Cost-effective, reliable, limited versatility.

Specifications:

  • DOF: 1-2
  • Grip force: 20-100 N
  • Opening: 80-120 mm
  • Weight: 300-800 g

Level 2: Adaptive Hand

Underactuated design with 3-5 active DOF and passive compliance. Conforms to object shapes automatically. Good balance of capability, simplicity, and cost.

Specifications:

  • DOF: 3-5 active, 8-12 passive
  • Grip force: 50-150 N (power), 5-30 N (precision)
  • Finger length: 80-120 mm
  • Weight: 500-1200 g
  • Sensors: Force/torque at wrist, contact sensors on fingertips

Level 3: Dexterous Hand

Anthropomorphic design with 12-20 active DOF enabling human-like manipulation. High cost and complexity, suitable for research and applications requiring fine manipulation.

Specifications:

  • DOF: 12-20 active
  • Grip force: Variable 1-150 N with force control
  • Finger length: Anthropomorphic proportions
  • Weight: 800-2000 g
  • Sensors: Joint torque, tactile arrays (>100 taxels per finger), IMUs
  • Control frequency: >100 Hz

2.5 Foot and Balance Mechanism Design

2.5.1 Foot Structure

The foot is critical for maintaining balance during standing and walking. WIA-ROB-019 specifies requirements for foot design:

Geometric Requirements

  • Length: 14-16% of robot height
  • Width: 35-45% of foot length
  • Plantar surface area: Minimum 150 cm² for standard-size robots
  • Toe clearance in swing phase: Minimum 30 mm

Force Distribution

The foot must incorporate force-torque sensors to measure ground reaction forces. Sensor placement options include:

  • Ankle-mounted 6-axis F/T sensor: Measures total foot force/torque. Simple, accurate, but doesn't provide force distribution information. Required minimum accuracy: ±0.5 N, ±0.1 Nm.
  • Multi-point force sensing: Array of force sensors under foot (typically 4-8 points at heel, midfoot, toe regions). Provides center of pressure calculation. Required accuracy: ±1 N per sensor.
  • Tactile array: Dense array of pressure sensors (16×16 or higher). Complete pressure distribution, higher cost and complexity. Required resolution: 5-10 mm, pressure range: 0-500 kPa.

2.5.2 Ankle Compliance

Some degree of ankle compliance improves stability and reduces impact forces. Three approaches are recognized:

  • Rigid ankle: No compliance, relies entirely on active control for balance. Simple mechanically but requires high-bandwidth control and powerful actuators.
  • Passive compliance: Spring elements (mechanical or rubber) provide limited compliance (5-10°). Improves disturbance rejection and energy efficiency.
  • Active compliance: Controlled impedance through actuator control (series elastic or torque-controlled). Adjustable stiffness, highest performance but more complex.

2.6 Torso and Spine Design

The torso houses critical components (batteries, computers, power electronics) while providing structural support and flexibility for natural motion.

2.6.1 Structural Layout

WIA-ROB-019 recognizes two primary torso configurations:

Rigid Torso

Single rigid structure, simple and mechanically robust. Suitable for applications not requiring torso articulation. All body motion achieved through hip, shoulder, and neck joints.

Articulated Torso

Multiple segments (typically 2-3) connected by spine joints enabling:

  • Forward/backward bending (flexion/extension): ±30-45°
  • Side bending (lateral flexion): ±20-30°
  • Rotation (axial rotation): ±20-30°

Articulated torsos extend workspace, improve balance recovery, and enable more natural human-like motion. Trade-offs include added complexity, weight, and cost.

2.6.2 Component Integration

The torso must efficiently package heavy components while maintaining proper mass distribution:

  • Battery Pack: Located near center of mass (typically in pelvis/lower torso). Weight: 3-15 kg depending on robot size and capacity. Quick-swap capability recommended.
  • Computing Modules: Upper torso near sensors to minimize cable runs. Thermal management essential—fans or heat pipes to dissipate 50-150 W.
  • Power Electronics: Motor drivers distributed near actuators to reduce wiring. Centralized DC bus distribution.
  • Communication Hub: Central location with star topology to all subsystems. Redundant networking recommended for safety-critical systems.

2.7 Head and Neck Design

2.7.1 Sensor Platform

The head serves primarily as a sensor platform housing cameras, microphones, and communication interfaces. Design priorities include:

  • Camera placement: Stereo cameras with 60-80 mm baseline at approximate human eye height
  • Microphone array: Minimum 4 microphones for sound localization
  • Display (optional): Screen or expressive eyes for communication
  • Lighting: Indicator LEDs for status communication
  • Weight budget: Maximum 1.5-3 kg including sensors and housing

2.7.2 Neck Articulation

The neck enables camera pointing and enhances social interaction through head gestures. Minimum requirements:

  • Pan (yaw): ±90° range, 120°/s speed
  • Tilt (pitch): -45° to +30° range, 90°/s speed
  • Roll (optional): ±15° range for advanced systems
  • Pointing accuracy: ±2° for visual servoing tasks

2.8 Cable Management and Routing

Proper cable management is essential for reliability and maintenance. WIA-ROB-019 requires:

  • Strain relief at all connection points
  • Cable routing that avoids joint singularities and excessive bending
  • Minimum bend radius: 10× cable diameter for power, 15× for data
  • Separation of power and signal cables to minimize EMI
  • Accessible cable routes for maintenance and replacement
  • Color coding and labeling per WIA standards
  • Modular connectors enabling subsystem replacement

2.9 Biomechanical Modeling

2.9.1 Kinematic Models

Accurate kinematic models are essential for motion planning and control. Forward kinematics calculate end-effector position from joint angles using Denavit-Hartenberg parameters or equivalent representations. Inverse kinematics solve for joint angles to achieve desired end-effector poses—often with multiple solutions requiring optimization to select preferred configurations.

2.9.2 Dynamic Models

Dynamic models relate joint torques to motion, incorporating inertial properties, gravity, velocity-dependent effects (Coriolis, centrifugal), and friction. The equations of motion for an n-DOF robot: 

M(q)q̈ + C(q,q̇)q̇ + G(q) + F(q̇) = τ

Where:

  • M(q): Mass/inertia matrix (n×n)
  • C(q,q̇): Coriolis and centrifugal terms (n×n)
  • G(q): Gravitational torques (n×1)
  • F(q̇): Friction and damping (n×1)
  • τ: Applied joint torques (n×1)
  • q, q̇, q̈: Joint positions, velocities, accelerations

These models enable model-based control, simulation, and performance prediction. WIA-ROB-019 requires that dynamic models be validated through physical testing, with prediction accuracy within 10% of measured values for torques and 5% for energy consumption.

2.10 Chapter Summary

This chapter covered the mechanical design and biomechanical principles underlying humanoid robot development according to WIA-ROB-019. We examined anthropometric design guidelines, material selection, joint architecture, and specialized subsystems including hands, feet, torso, and head mechanisms.

Proper mechanical design is fundamental to achieving the performance, safety, and reliability required for real-world humanoid robot deployment. The specifications and guidelines in WIA-ROB-019 reflect accumulated best practices from decades of research and development.
