7.1 Environmental Health and Smart Textiles

Environmental factors profoundly affect human health, yet personal exposure to air pollution, UV radiation, temperature extremes, and hazardous substances remains largely invisible until symptoms appear. Smart textiles bring environmental sensing to individual level, creating personal exposure profiles rather than relying on regional monitoring stations that may not reflect actual individual exposure. The WIA-IND-002 standard defines specifications for environmental sensors integrated into clothing, enabling continuous awareness and protective action embodying 弘益人間 by democratizing environmental health protection for all.

7.2 Air Quality Monitoring

Air pollution causes millions of premature deaths annually through cardiovascular disease, respiratory illness, and cancer. Particulate matter (PM2.5, PM10), nitrogen oxides (NOx), ozone (O3), carbon monoxide (CO), and volatile organic compounds (VOCs) represent major pollutants. Traditional monitoring relies on stationary stations providing city-wide averages, missing hyperlocal variations and personal exposure patterns.

Smart textiles incorporate miniaturized air quality sensors measuring pollutant concentrations at breathing level. Particulate sensors use optical scattering or electronic sensing to detect particle concentration and size distribution. Electrochemical sensors measure gas concentrations. Continuous monitoring reveals exposure patterns: commuting routes, indoor air quality, workplace exposures, and exercise locations. This information enables protective actions—choosing less polluted routes, avoiding outdoor exercise during high-pollution periods, or improving indoor ventilation.

7.3 UV Radiation Detection

Ultraviolet radiation causes skin cancer, photoaging, and immune suppression, yet determining safe exposure limits proves challenging given varying UV intensity, skin types, and protection methods. UV sensors integrated into clothing measure actual UV exposure in real-time. Photodiodes sensitive to UVA and UVB wavelengths quantify exposure, while algorithms account for skin type, sunscreen protection, and cumulative exposure.

Smart UV monitoring provides personalized alerts before reaching harmful exposure levels. Athletes training outdoors, outdoor workers, and beach-goers particularly benefit. Tracking cumulative UV exposure over days and weeks reveals patterns enabling long-term skin cancer risk assessment. For phototherapy patients requiring controlled UV exposure, smart textiles ensure therapeutic doses without harmful excess. The WIA-IND-002 standard specifies UV sensor spectral response, dynamic range, and angular response ensuring accurate exposure measurement regardless of sun angle or body orientation.

7.4 Temperature and Heat Stress Monitoring

Heat stress causes illness and death, particularly affecting outdoor workers, athletes, and vulnerable populations during heat waves. Heat stress depends not just on air temperature but also humidity, radiation, wind speed, and individual factors like acclimatization, fitness, and clothing. Traditional heat indices based on regional weather data fail to capture individual exposure and physiological responses.

Smart textiles measure microclimate conditions at skin level: temperature, humidity, and radiant heat. Physiological monitoring (heart rate, core temperature, sweat rate) reveals individual heat strain responses. Integrating environmental and physiological data enables personalized heat stress assessment. Workers receive warnings before reaching dangerous heat strain, enabling rest breaks, hydration, or cooling interventions. Automated work-rest cycles based on real-time heat stress optimize productivity while protecting health—embodying 弘益人間 by valuing worker wellbeing.

7.5 Occupational Hazard Detection

Workers in construction, manufacturing, mining, agriculture, and emergency response face exposure to hazardous substances: toxic gases, volatile solvents, heavy metals, and carcinogenic compounds. Traditional safety protocols rely on area monitoring and time-based exposure limits that may not reflect individual exposure.

Smart textiles incorporating gas sensors detect hydrogen sulfide, carbon monoxide, ammonia, chlorine, and other toxic gases, alerting workers to dangerous concentrations. Volatile organic compound sensors identify solvent vapors. Colorimetric sensors change appearance when exposed to specific chemicals, providing visual warnings. Dosimetry functions accumulate exposure over time, ensuring regulatory limits aren't exceeded. Real-time alerts enable immediate protective action—donning respirators, evacuating areas, or activating ventilation systems before harmful exposure occurs.

7.6 Allergen and Pollution Mapping

Allergies affect hundreds of millions globally, yet predicting exposure to triggers like pollen, mold, or specific pollutants proves difficult. Smart textiles enable participatory environmental monitoring—many individuals wearing sensor-equipped clothing create high-resolution pollution and allergen maps. Data aggregation reveals spatial and temporal patterns: pollution hotspots, pollen concentration variations, or indoor air quality across buildings.

Individuals access personalized maps showing allergenic pollen in their neighborhoods, high-pollution commute routes to avoid, or indoor spaces with poor air quality. Community-generated environmental data empowers collective action: advocating for pollution controls, improving building ventilation, or creating low-pollution zones. This collaborative approach to environmental monitoring embodies 弘益人間 by pooling individual data for community benefit.

7.7 Indoor Air Quality

People spend 90% of time indoors where air quality may exceed outdoor pollution levels due to inadequate ventilation, offgassing from materials, combustion appliances, or biological contaminants. Indoor air pollutants including formaldehyde, radon, carbon dioxide, and particulates from cooking or combustion affect health and cognitive function.

Smart textiles monitor indoor air quality continuously as individuals move through buildings. Carbon dioxide sensors indicate ventilation adequacy—elevated CO2 levels impair cognition and cause drowsiness. VOC sensors detect harmful volatile compounds from furniture, carpets, or cleaning products. Particulate sensors reveal cooking emissions or infiltration of outdoor pollution. Temperature and humidity monitoring ensures comfort and prevents mold growth. This continuous indoor air quality awareness enables interventions: increasing ventilation, removing pollution sources, or using air purifiers strategically.

7.8 Water Quality and Moisture Detection

Water exposure in occupational contexts (firefighters, divers, aquatic workers) or recreational activities (swimming, surfing) may involve contamination risks. Smart textiles incorporating water quality sensors detect contaminants: bacteria, heavy metals, or chemical pollutants. Alerts prevent exposure to contaminated water.

Moisture detection in clothing serves safety and comfort functions. Excessive moisture from rain, humidity, or sweat compromises thermal insulation, causing hypothermia risk in cold environments. Moisture sensors trigger alerts prompting protective action: adding insulation, seeking shelter, or increasing heat production through activity. For water sports, moisture ingress detection in protective suits prevents dangerous exposure. The WIA-IND-002 standard specifies moisture sensor placement, sensitivity, and response time ensuring reliable detection across diverse scenarios.

7.9 Radiation Monitoring

Workers in nuclear facilities, medical imaging, research laboratories, or high-altitude aviation face ionizing radiation exposure requiring careful monitoring. Traditional dosimeters measure cumulative exposure but don't provide real-time awareness enabling protective action during exposure events.

Smart textiles incorporating radiation detectors (Geiger counters, scintillation detectors, or solid-state dosimeters) measure exposure rates continuously. Real-time alerts when dose rates exceed safe limits enable immediate protective action. Cumulative dose tracking ensures regulatory exposure limits aren't exceeded. For emergency responders entering contaminated areas, radiation mapping via textile sensors helps identify hotspots and plan safe routes. Integration with dose management systems ensures accurate records for regulatory compliance and long-term health surveillance.

7.10 Environmental Data Integration and Action

Environmental sensor data achieves maximum value when integrated with contextual information and decision support systems. Location data from smartphones links exposures to specific places, building environmental exposure maps. Weather data provides context for outdoor exposures. Activity data distinguishes resting from exercise periods—pollution's health impact increases during exercise due to increased ventilation rate.

Machine learning identifies personal exposure patterns and risk factors. Predictive models forecast future exposures based on weather, time, and location, enabling proactive protective measures. Personalized recommendations suggest actions: optimal commute routes minimizing pollution exposure, exercise timing avoiding high-pollution periods, or indoor activities during poor outdoor air quality. Integration with smart home systems automates responses: activating air purifiers when indoor pollution detected, adjusting ventilation based on CO2 levels, or triggering alerts for UV exposure during outdoor activities. This comprehensive environmental awareness and automated response truly embodies 弘益人間 by making environmental health protection accessible and actionable for everyone.
