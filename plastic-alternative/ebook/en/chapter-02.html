Understanding Bio-based Plastics

Bio-based plastics represent a fundamental shift in how we source and manufacture materials. Instead of extracting crude oil from deep underground—a finite resource formed over millions of years—bio-based plastics derive from renewable biological sources that can be replanted and regrown in months or years.

Key Terminology

Bio-based: Material derived from renewable biological resources (plants, algae, bacteria) rather than fossil fuels. A material can be bio-based without being biodegradable.

Biodegradable: Capable of being broken down by microorganisms into natural substances like water, CO2, and biomass. A material can be biodegradable without being bio-based.

Bioplastic: An umbrella term for plastics that are either bio-based, biodegradable, or both. The ideal bioplastic is both fully bio-based AND fully biodegradable.

The Bioplastic Universe

Not all bioplastics are created equal. Understanding the landscape is crucial for making informed choices:

Category Bio-based? Biodegradable? Examples
Bio-based & Biodegradable ✓ Yes ✓ Yes PLA, PHA, PBS, Starch blends
Bio-based but NOT Biodegradable ✓ Yes ✗ No Bio-PE, Bio-PET
NOT Bio-based but Biodegradable ✗ No ✓ Yes PBAT, PCL (fossil-derived)
Conventional Plastics ✗ No ✗ No Standard PE, PP, PET, PS

For maximum environmental benefit, we focus on materials that are both bio-based AND biodegradable. These represent the true circular solution—renewable sourcing and natural end-of-life degradation.

Major Bio-based Plastic Types

1. PLA (Polylactic Acid)

PLA is currently the most widely produced and commercially successful bioplastic. Its success stems from excellent clarity, good mechanical properties, and established manufacturing infrastructure.

Production Process

  1. Feedstock Selection: Typically corn, sugarcane, cassava, or sugar beet
  2. Sugar Extraction: Starch is converted to simple sugars (glucose)
  3. Fermentation: Bacteria ferment sugars into lactic acid
  4. Purification: Lactic acid is purified to pharmaceutical grade
  5. Polymerization: Lactic acid molecules link to form long polymer chains
  6. Processing: PLA resin formed into pellets for manufacturing

Material Properties

Property PLA Value Comparable To
Tensile Strength 50-70 MPa PET (similar)
Melting Temperature 150-160°C Processing-friendly
Glass Transition 55-60°C Not for hot liquids
Density 1.24 g/cm³ Slightly denser than PET
Clarity Excellent Matches polystyrene
Biodegradation 180-360 days Industrial composting only
Bio-based Content 95-100% Fully renewable

Applications

  • Food Service: Cold drink cups, salad containers, clamshell packaging
  • Retail Packaging: Blister packs, film wraps, rigid trays
  • Agriculture: Plant pots, nursery containers
  • 3D Printing: Most popular filament material
  • Medical: Biodegradable sutures, drug delivery systems, tissue engineering scaffolds
  • Textiles: Performance fabrics, nonwoven materials

Advantages

  • Excellent optical clarity
  • Good printability
  • FDA approved for food contact
  • Cost-competitive ($1.50-2.50/kg)
  • Widely available
  • Mature production technology
  • Low odor and taste transfer

Limitations

  • Requires industrial composting
  • Poor heat resistance
  • Brittle (low impact strength)
  • Not marine biodegradable
  • Slow degradation in landfills
  • Limited flexibility
  • Feedstock competition concerns

Innovation: CPLA (Crystallized PLA)

CPLA is heat-treated PLA with improved temperature resistance up to 85°C. This makes it suitable for hot beverages and food applications. The crystallization process aligns polymer chains, creating a more stable structure. CPLA is used in compostable coffee cup lids and hot food containers.

2. PHA (Polyhydroxyalkanoates)

PHA represents the holy grail of bioplastics—a material that is 100% bio-based, biodegrades in virtually any environment (including oceans), and is produced through natural biological processes.

How Nature Makes Plastic

PHA is produced by bacteria as an energy storage mechanism, similar to how humans store fat. When bacteria have excess nutrients but lack other essential elements (like nitrogen or phosphorus), they convert sugars into PHA granules inside their cells.

Production Steps:

  1. Bacterial Cultivation: Select high-PHA-producing bacterial strains
  2. Fermentation: Feed bacteria sugars, vegetable oils, or even waste materials
  3. Stress Conditions: Limit nitrogen to trigger PHA accumulation (cells can be 80% PHA by weight)
  4. Harvesting: After 48-72 hours, bacterial cells are harvested
  5. Extraction: PHA extracted from cells using solvents or enzymatic methods
  6. Purification: Refined to remove bacterial residues

PHA Family Members

PHA is actually a family of polymers with different properties based on composition:

PHBHHx Highly flexible, elastic, soft Films, bags, flexible packaging

Marine Biodegradability: The PHA Advantage

PHA is one of the only plastics that truly biodegrades in ocean conditions. Marine microorganisms recognize PHA as a natural food source (since marine bacteria also produce PHA). This means:

  • No persistent pollution if it enters marine environments
  • Safe for marine life (non-toxic degradation)
  • Ideal for applications near water (fishing gear, coastal packaging)
  • Addresses the ocean plastic crisis directly

Testing data: PHA shows 70-90% degradation in seawater within 6-12 months at ambient ocean temperatures (ASTM D7081 standard).

Applications

  • Flexible Packaging: Films, bags, pouches
  • Marine Applications: Fishing line, nets, buoys, aquaculture equipment
  • Agriculture: Mulch films, seed coatings, controlled-release fertilizer encapsulation
  • Medical: Surgical implants, drug delivery, tissue engineering (biocompatible)
  • Personal Care: Microbeads (biodegradable alternative), packaging
  • Food Service: Straws, cutlery, food containers

Advantages

  • Biodegrades in ALL environments
  • Marine-safe and ocean-degradable
  • Home compostable
  • 100% bio-based
  • Good flexibility and elasticity
  • Can be produced from waste feedstocks
  • Non-toxic degradation

Limitations

  • Higher cost ($4-6/kg)
  • Limited production capacity
  • Processing can be challenging
  • Fewer suppliers than PLA
  • Lower tensile strength than PLA
  • Thermal instability during processing

Case Study: Danimer Scientific

Danimer Scientific (USA) produces Nodax® PHA at commercial scale. Their material is used by major brands including:

  • PepsiCo: Developing biodegradable snack bags
  • Bacardi: Creating biodegradable spirits bottles
  • Mars: Sustainable candy wrappers

Danimer's PHA is certified home compostable and marine biodegradable, representing a true end-to-end sustainable solution.

3. PBS (Polybutylene Succinate)

PBS bridges the gap between performance and sustainability. This biodegradable polyester combines excellent mechanical properties with good processability.

Material Characteristics

Property PBS Value Notes
Tensile Strength 30-40 MPa Good strength
Elongation at Break 200-800% Highly flexible
Melting Point 90-120°C Easy processing
Biodegradation 180-365 days Compost and soil
Bio-based Content 50-100% Depends on feedstock

Primary Applications

  • Agricultural Films: Mulch films that can be tilled into soil
  • Shopping Bags: Strong, flexible, compostable
  • Food Packaging: Films and containers
  • Disposable Tableware: Plates, cups, cutlery

4. Starch-based Plastics

Starch-based materials are among the most economical bioplastics, utilizing one of the world's most abundant natural polymers.

Types of Starch Plastics

Thermoplastic Starch (TPS)

Pure starch processed with plasticizers (glycerol, sorbitol) to create moldable material. Used in loose-fill packaging (biodegradable packing peanuts), disposable cutlery, and agricultural applications.

Starch Blends

Starch combined with other biodegradable polymers (PLA, PBAT, PCL) to improve mechanical properties. Common in bags, films, and injection-molded products.

Starch-based Foams

Expanded starch materials that replace styrofoam. Used in protective packaging and food service containers.

Advantages

  • Lowest cost bioplastic option ($1-1.50/kg)
  • Abundant, non-toxic feedstock
  • Rapid biodegradation
  • Can use waste/non-food grade starch

Limitations

  • Water sensitive (swells and weakens)
  • Lower mechanical strength
  • Poor barrier properties
  • Limited shelf life

Feedstock Considerations

A critical question for bio-based plastics: what should we grow them from?

First Generation Feedstocks

Sources: Food crops (corn, sugarcane, potatoes)

Pros: Established agriculture, high yields, existing infrastructure

Cons: Food vs. fuel debate, land use concerns, water consumption

Second Generation Feedstocks

Sources: Agricultural waste (corn stover, bagasse, wheat straw)

Pros: Waste valorization, no food competition, lower environmental impact

Cons: More complex processing, less established technology

Third Generation Feedstocks

Sources: Algae, seaweed, bacteria

Pros: No arable land needed, high productivity, CO2 capture

Cons: Early stage technology, higher costs, scaling challenges

The Food vs. Fuel Debate

Critics argue that using food crops for bioplastics competes with food production. However:

  • Current bioplastic production uses <0.02% of global agricultural land
  • Many producers use non-food-grade or waste materials
  • Second and third generation feedstocks eliminate this concern
  • The bigger issue is conventional plastic's fossil fuel consumption and pollution

As technology advances, the industry is shifting toward waste-based and non-food feedstocks, making this concern increasingly obsolete.

Future Developments

CO2-based PHA

Companies like Newlight Technologies are producing PHA directly from captured CO2, creating carbon-negative plastic. This transforms a greenhouse gas into a valuable material.

Methane-based Bioplastics

Methanotrophic bacteria can convert methane (from landfills or agriculture) into PHA, reducing a potent greenhouse gas while producing sustainable plastic.

Genetically Engineered Crops

Research into crops engineered to produce higher starch yields or even produce PHA directly in plant tissues could dramatically reduce costs and environmental impact.

Looking Ahead

Bio-based plastics are not a distant dream—they're a present reality with significant room for growth. As production scales and technology improves, costs will continue to decrease while performance improves.
