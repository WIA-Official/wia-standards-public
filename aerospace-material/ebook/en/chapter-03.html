Chapter 3: Titanium Alloys

Titanium alloys offer an exceptional combination of high strength, low density, and excellent corrosion resistance, making them essential for aerospace applications where both structural performance and weight savings are critical. This chapter covers the metallurgy, processing, and applications of titanium alloys in accordance with the WIA-AEROSPACE_MATERIAL standard.

3.1 Titanium Metallurgy

Crystal Structure and Alloy Types

Titanium Alloy Classification
=============================

Alpha (α) Phase: HCP structure, stable at room temperature
  Stabilizers: Al, O, N, C
  Properties: Good creep resistance, weldable
  Examples: CP Ti, Ti-5Al-2.5Sn

Beta (β) Phase: BCC structure, stable at high temperature
  Stabilizers: V, Mo, Nb, Ta, Fe
  Properties: Good formability, higher strength
  Examples: Ti-15V-3Cr-3Al-3Sn

Alpha-Beta (α+β): Mixture of both phases
  Processing: Can be heat treated for strength
  Properties: Best combination of properties
  Examples: Ti-6Al-4V, Ti-6Al-2Sn-4Zr-2Mo

Beta Transus Temperature: α → β transformation
  CP Ti: 882°C
  Ti-6Al-4V: 995°C
  Ti-17: 885°C

3.4 Processing Challenges

Titanium Processing Considerations

  • Reactivity: Reacts with oxygen, nitrogen above 500°C; requires vacuum/inert processing
  • Galling: High friction coefficient; lubrication and coatings required
  • Spring-back: Low modulus leads to significant elastic recovery in forming
  • Machinability: Poor thermal conductivity, work hardening; low speeds, sharp tools
  • Welding: Excellent weldability but strict shielding required

3.8 Fatigue and Damage Tolerance

Titanium Fatigue Behavior

Titanium alloys exhibit good fatigue strength but are sensitive to surface condition, microstructure, and texture. The presence of alpha case (oxygen-enriched surface layer) significantly reduces fatigue life.

Fatigue Considerations:
  - S-N curve: No true endurance limit
  - Surface condition: Critical for fatigue life
  - Alpha case: Must be removed by machining/chemical
  - Shot peening: Improves fatigue 20-40%
  - Fretting: Susceptible, requires coatings
  - Crack growth: Lower than aluminum at high ΔK

Chapter Summary

This chapter covered titanium alloy metallurgy, processing, and aerospace applications. The unique properties of titanium—high strength-to-weight ratio, corrosion resistance, and temperature capability—make it essential for both airframe and engine applications. The WIA-AEROSPACE_MATERIAL standard provides comprehensive guidance for titanium selection and use.
