Chapter 1: Introduction to Aerospace Materials

Aerospace materials form the foundation of every aircraft, spacecraft, and engine. The selection of appropriate materials is critical to achieving performance, safety, durability, and cost objectives. This chapter establishes the fundamental principles of aerospace material science, including selection criteria, design requirements, and the historical evolution of materials used in aviation and space applications.

1.1 Material Selection Principles

Aerospace material selection balances multiple competing requirements: strength, weight, cost, producibility, and environmental resistance. The unique demands of flight—cyclic loading, extreme temperatures, corrosive environments—require materials engineered specifically for aerospace applications.

│   ├── Formability
│   ├── Machinability

│
└── Economics
    ├── Material cost
    ├── Processing cost
    ├── Lifecycle cost
    └── Availability

1.4 Historical Development

Aerospace Materials Evolution Timeline
======================================

1903: Wright Flyer - Wood, fabric, steel wire
1915: All-metal aircraft - Aluminum alloys emerge
1930s: 2024-T3 aluminum dominates airframes
1940s: 7075-T6 for higher strength applications
1950s: Titanium alloys for jet engine compressors
1960s: Superalloys enable higher turbine temperatures
1970s: Composite materials enter secondary structures
1980s: Carbon fiber in primary structures (737/757)
1990s: Aluminum-lithium alloys for weight reduction
2000s: GLARE hybrid material (A380)
2010s: Composite fuselage (787, A350)
2020s: Additive manufactured components, CMCs in engines

1.5 Material Categories

Aerospace Material Families

  • Aluminum Alloys: 2xxx, 7xxx series for structures; dominant in airframes
  • Titanium Alloys: High strength-to-weight, corrosion resistant; engine and structure
  • Steels: High strength for landing gear, fasteners, bearings
  • Nickel Superalloys: High-temperature engine components
  • Composites: CFRP for weight-critical structures
  • Ceramics: Thermal protection, CMC engine components

1.7 Material Property Dependencies

Factor Effect on Properties Consideration
Temperature Strength decreases at elevated temps Use elevated temp allowables
Loading Rate Higher rate = higher apparent strength Static vs dynamic testing
Environment Corrosion, hydrogen embrittlement Protective measures required
Thickness Properties vary with section size Use appropriate allowables
Orientation Anisotropic behavior (rolled products) Test in all directions
