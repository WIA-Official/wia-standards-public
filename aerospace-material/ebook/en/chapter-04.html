Chapter 4: Steel and High-Strength Alloys

Steel alloys remain essential in aerospace applications where the highest strength levels are required. Landing gear, fasteners, bearings, and engine components demand the exceptional mechanical properties that only steel can provide. This chapter covers aerospace steels including low-alloy high-strength, stainless, and maraging steels according to the WIA-AEROSPACE_MATERIAL standard.

4.1 Steel Classifications

Aerospace Steel Categories

Aerospace Steel Classification
==============================

Low-Alloy High-Strength Steels:
├── 4130: General purpose, weldable
├── 4340: Highest strength, landing gear
├── 300M: Modified 4340, better fracture toughness
└── D6AC: Deep hardening, large sections

Stainless Steels:
├── Martensitic: 440C (bearings), 17-4PH (fasteners)
├── Austenitic: 321, 347 (high temp)
├── Precipitation Hardening: 15-5PH, 17-7PH, A286
└── Duplex: 2205 (corrosion resistance)

Maraging Steels:
├── 18Ni(200): Low strength grade
├── 18Ni(250): Medium strength
├── 18Ni(300): High strength
└── 18Ni(350): Ultra-high strength

Tool and Bearing Steels:
├── M50: Turbine engine bearings
├── M50NiL: Case hardened bearings
└── 52100: Standard bearing steel

4.4 Maraging Steels

Maraging Steel Characteristics

  • Composition: 18% Ni, 7-9% Co, 3-5% Mo, <0.03% C
  • Mechanism: Age hardening of soft iron-nickel martensite
  • Advantage: Machinable soft, then age hardened
  • Welding: Excellent, weld zone can be re-aged
  • Applications: Rocket motor cases, tooling, aircraft components
// Maraging Steel Processing

4.7 Corrosion Protection

Steel Protection Systems

  • Cadmium plating: Traditional, sacrificial, toxic concerns
  • IVD aluminum: Ion vapor deposited, replacement for Cd
  • Zinc-nickel: Alloy plating, good corrosion protection
  • Primers: Chromate (legacy), non-chromate (new)
  • Topcoats: Polyurethane, epoxy systems
