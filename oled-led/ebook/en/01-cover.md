### Historical Context

The evolution of display illumination has been remarkable:

**1960s-1980s: CRT Era**
- Cathode ray tubes dominate with electron beam excitation
- Self-emissive but bulky and power-hungry

**1990s-2000s: LCD + CCFL Era**
- Liquid crystal displays with cold cathode fluorescent lamps
- Thin and efficient but limited contrast
- CCFL backlights containing mercury

**2005-2015: LCD + LED Era**
- White LEDs replace CCFL backlights
- Edge-lit and direct-lit configurations
- Mercury-free, more efficient, and thinner
- Local dimming begins with direct-lit arrays

**2010-Present: OLED Era**
- Self-emissive OLED displays in smartphones and premium TVs
- Infinite contrast, perfect blacks, fast response
- Burn-in concerns and premium pricing

**2020-Present: Advanced Technologies**
- WOLED (LG) vs QD-OLED (Samsung/Sony) competition
- Mini-LED (TCL, Samsung) offering LCD+LED performance boost
- Tandem OLED promising extended lifetime
- Micro-LED emerging for ultra-premium applications

### Key Technologies Explained

**OLED (Organic Light-Emitting Diode)**
Organic materials emit light when current flows through them. Each pixel is self-emissive, allowing:
- Perfect blacks by turning pixels completely off
- Infinite contrast ratio
- Wide viewing angles
- Fast response time (<0.1ms)
- Thin form factor without backlight

Challenges include:
- Lifetime limitations (50,000-100,000 hours to LT95)
- Burn-in risk with static content
- Lower peak brightness than LED (typically 600-1500 Cd/m²)
- Higher manufacturing cost

**LED Backlit LCD**
Liquid crystal layer modulates light from LED backlight:
- High brightness capability (1000-5000+ Cd/m²)
- Long lifetime (>100,000 hours)
- No burn-in risk
- Cost-effective manufacturing

Challenges include:
- Limited contrast due to backlight leakage
- Slower response time (5-10ms)
- More complex optical stack

**Mini-LED**
Miniaturized LEDs (100-500μm) enable thousands of local dimming zones:
- Dramatically improved contrast vs traditional LED
- High peak brightness
- Reduced blooming with finer zone control
- No burn-in risk
- Cost competitive with OLED

Challenges include:
- Still has some backlight leakage
- More complex backlight driver electronics
- Thicker than OLED due to backlight

### Measurement Standards

Understanding key metrics:

**Luminance (Cd/m²)**
- Brightness of a surface as perceived by the human eye
- Measured in candelas per square meter (Cd/m²) or nits (equivalent)
- Typical ranges: smartphone 500-1000, TV 100-2000, monitor 200-500

**Luminous Efficacy (lm/W)**
- Efficiency of converting electrical power to visible light
- Measured in lumens per watt
- Modern white LEDs: 130-200 lm/W
- OLED typically lower: 50-100 lm/W

**Color Gamut**
- Range of colors a display can reproduce
- Common standards: sRGB (100% baseline), DCI-P3 (digital cinema), Rec.2020 (UHDTV)
- QD-OLED leads with near 100% DCI-P3, approaching Rec.2020

**Contrast Ratio**
- Ratio of brightest white to darkest black
- OLED achieves "infinite" contrast (truly off pixels)
- Mini-LED reaches 100,000:1 or higher
- Standard LED LCD: 1,000:1 to 5,000:1

**Response Time**
- Speed of pixel state change (gray-to-gray)
- OLED: <0.1ms
- LED LCD: 5-12ms typically
- Critical for motion clarity in gaming and sports
