Chapter 5

Modeling and Prediction

Climate Models, Scenario Analysis, and Projections

Overview

Predicting future ocean acidification requires sophisticated models spanning from global Earth System Models to regional biogeochemical simulations to machine learning approaches. This chapter explores the modeling tools used to project acidification trajectories, assess ecosystem impacts, and support decision-making for mitigation and adaptation strategies.

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand Earth System Models and their ocean components
  • Learn about IPCC emission scenarios and their acidification projections
  • Explore regional downscaling and coastal modeling approaches
  • Examine ecosystem impact models and biological responses
  • Discover machine learning applications in acidification prediction
  • Assess model uncertainties and validation strategies

Earth System Models (ESMs)

Earth System Models are the primary tools for projecting future ocean acidification under different emission scenarios. These complex models simulate interactions among atmosphere, ocean, land, and ice.

Model Components

Coupled Model Architecture

  • Atmospheric Component: CO₂ emissions, transport, chemistry
  • Ocean Circulation: Physical ocean model (temperature, salinity, currents)
  • Ocean Biogeochemistry: Carbon cycle, carbonate chemistry, nutrients
  • Sea Ice: Coverage, thickness, gas exchange impacts
  • Land Surface: Terrestrial carbon cycle, runoff
  • Coupling: Air-sea CO₂ flux, heat exchange, freshwater inputs

IPCC Emission Scenarios and Projections

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) uses Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSPs) combined with Representative Concentration Pathways (RCPs) to project future climate and ocean conditions.

Key Scenarios and Ocean pH Projections

Scenario Description 2100 CO₂ (ppm) 2100 pH Change 2100 Ω_aragonite (tropical)
SSP1-1.9 (Low) Net zero by 2050, strong mitigation ~450 -0.15 units ~2.5 (marginal viability)
SSP1-2.6 (Low-Med) Strong mitigation, Paris targets met ~500 -0.19 units ~2.2 (stressed)
SSP2-4.5 (Medium) Moderate mitigation, current pledges ~600 -0.25 units ~1.9 (critical)
SSP3-7.0 (High) Limited cooperation, high emissions ~800 -0.35 units ~1.4 (ecosystem collapse)
SSP5-8.5 (Very High) Fossil fuel intensive, no mitigation ~1100 -0.44 units ~1.0 (mass extinction risk)

⚠️ Current Trajectory

As of 2025, global emissions track closest to SSP3-7.0 (high emissions scenario), despite international climate commitments. Even under the most optimistic scenarios (SSP1-2.6), significant ocean acidification is unavoidable due to:

  • Committed warming: Already-emitted CO₂ continues to be absorbed
  • Ocean inertia: Decades to centuries for pH to stabilize after emissions stop
  • Deep ocean lag: Surface changes penetrate slowly to depth
  • Irreversibility: Recovery timescales of 1000s of years even after stabilization

Regional Downscaling and Coastal Models

Global ESMs have coarse resolution (~100 km), missing critical coastal and estuarine dynamics. Regional models provide fine-scale projections needed for local management.

Regional Ocean Modeling System (ROMS)

High-Resolution Coastal Modeling

Capabilities:

  • Resolution: 1-10 km (captures coastal features)
  • Processes: Upwelling, tides, river discharge, mixing
  • Biogeochemistry: Coupled carbon cycle, nutrients, oxygen
  • Forcing: Boundary conditions from global ESMs + local observations

Applications:

  • California Current upwelling and acidification extremes
  • Chesapeake Bay eutrophication-acidification interactions
  • Puget Sound pH variability and shellfish impacts
  • Gulf of Maine warming and acidification synergies

Coastal Carbon Cycle Models

  • River inputs: Terrestrial carbon, nutrients, alkalinity
  • Benthic processes: Sediment respiration, carbonate dissolution
  • Tidal mixing: Vertical and horizontal exchange
  • Biological production: Phytoplankton blooms, seagrass metabolism
  • Anthropogenic inputs: Wastewater, agricultural runoff

Ecosystem and Biological Impact Models

Translating pH projections into ecosystem impacts requires models that link chemistry to biology.

Dynamic Energy Budget (DEB) Models

Individual Organism Responses

DEB models simulate energy allocation in individual organisms (growth, reproduction, maintenance) under varying environmental conditions including pH:

  • Species: Oysters, mussels, sea urchins, corals
  • Inputs: pH, temperature, food availability, size
  • Outputs: Growth rates, reproduction, mortality
  • Applications: Aquaculture planning, population projections
  • Example: Pacific oyster DEB predicts 30% growth reduction at pH 7.7

Ecosystem Models (NPZD and Beyond)

Model Type Complexity Components OA Integration
NPZD (Basic) Low Nutrients, Phytoplankton, Zooplankton, Detritus pH effects on plankton rates
NEMURO Medium Multiple plankton types, nutrients Calcifier vs non-calcifier shifts
Ecopath/Ecosim Medium-High Full food web, fisheries Bottom-up OA impacts on fish yield
Atlantis Very High Spatial, age-structured, fisheries management Multi-stressor scenarios (OA + warming + fishing)

Coral Reef Models

Reef Trajectory Projections

Model Components:

  • Coral calcification as function of Ω_aragonite and temperature
  • Bleaching mortality based on degree heating weeks
  • Bioerosion rates (parrotfish, urchins, boring organisms)
  • Recruitment and recovery dynamics
  • Competition with macroalgae

Key Outputs:

  • Net calcification rates (reef growth vs erosion)
  • Coral cover trajectories under scenarios
  • Tipping points to algae-dominated states
  • Ecosystem service loss timelines

Major Finding: Most models project shift from net accretion to net erosion by 2050-2070 under SSP2-4.5 or higher scenarios.

Data Assimilation

🔄 Merging Models with Observations

Data assimilation techniques blend model predictions with real-world observations to create optimal estimates of ocean state:

  • Kalman Filters: Sequential updating as new data arrives
  • Variational Methods: Optimize model-data fit over time windows
  • Particle Filters: Ensemble approach for nonlinear systems
  • Applications: Nowcasting, reanalysis products, forecast initialization
  • Example: Copernicus Marine Service biogeochemical reanalysis (1993-present)

Uncertainty Quantification

All models have uncertainties. Understanding and communicating these uncertainties is crucial for decision-making.

Sources of Uncertainty

Source Type Magnitude Mitigation Strategies
Emission Scenarios Scenario Uncertainty Large (dominant) Evaluate multiple scenarios, risk-based planning
Model Structure Epistemic Moderate-Large Multi-model ensembles, model comparison
Parameter Values Epistemic Moderate Sensitivity analysis, ensemble methods
Internal Variability Aleatoric Small-Moderate Large ensembles, probabilistic forecasts
Initial Conditions Aleatoric Small (short-term) Multiple initializations, ensemble

Multi-Model Ensembles

📊 Strength in Numbers

The IPCC uses ensembles of 20-40 different ESMs to assess future projections. Benefits include:

  • Robust trends: Agreement across models = higher confidence
  • Uncertainty ranges: Model spread indicates projection uncertainty
  • Emergent constraints: Observations can narrow future ranges
  • Regional patterns: Identify where models agree/disagree

Example: All CMIP6 models agree on 0.3-0.5 pH unit decline by 2100 under SSP5-8.5, but regional patterns vary by ±0.05 pH units.

Model Validation and Skill Assessment

How do we know if models are reliable? Validation against observations is essential.

Validation Approaches

  • Historical Hindcasts: Can models reproduce observed pH trends (1990-2020)?
  • Spatial Patterns: Do models capture regional chemistry distributions?
  • Seasonal Cycles: Amplitude and timing of seasonal pH variability
  • Event Reproduction: Upwelling events, extreme low-pH episodes
  • Multi-Variable Consistency: pH, DIC, TA, pCO₂ all coherent?

✅ Model Successes

  • Global trends: ESMs accurately reproduce observed pH decline at time-series stations (±20%)
  • Spatial patterns: Carbonate system distribution well-captured (R² > 0.9 for DIC, TA)
  • Seasonal cycles: Moderate skill in open ocean (R² ~0.6-0.8)

⚠️ Model Challenges

  • Coastal variability: Models struggle with complex, high-frequency coastal dynamics
  • Extremes: Underestimate amplitude of pH excursions in upwelling zones
  • Biological feedbacks: Ecosystem responses poorly constrained
  • Carbon cycle feedbacks: Uncertainty in how biology responds to changing chemistry

Operational Forecasting

Beyond century-scale projections, operational forecasts provide near-term (days to months) predictions useful for marine industries and resource managers.

Seasonal to Subseasonal Forecasting

Near-Term Prediction Systems

Time Scales:

  • Nowcast: Present state estimate (data assimilation)
  • Short-term (1-7 days): Weather-driven upwelling, mixing events
  • Medium-term (1-3 months): Seasonal transitions, ENSO impacts

Applications:

  • Shellfish hatchery water intake decisions
  • Aquaculture harvest timing optimization
  • Reef tourism advisories (combined with bleaching forecasts)
  • Research cruise planning

Example Systems:

  • NOAA CarbonTracker (atmospheric CO₂ + ocean uptake)
  • Copernicus Marine Service biogeochemical forecasts
  • Regional models (California Current, Puget Sound)

Chapter Summary

Modeling ocean acidification spans from global Earth System Models projecting century-scale changes under different emission scenarios to high-resolution coastal models simulating local variability, to machine learning approaches filling observational gaps and providing operational forecasts.

IPCC scenarios reveal stark differences: strong mitigation (SSP1-2.6) limits pH decline to ~0.19 units by 2100, while business-as-usual (SSP5-8.5) leads to catastrophic 0.44 unit drops. Regional models show polar regions and upwelling zones face the most severe and rapid changes.

Ecosystem models translate chemistry into biology, projecting coral reef collapse, pteropod habitat loss, and fisheries declines. Machine learning offers new tools for prediction and pattern detection. However, all models carry uncertainties—from emission scenarios to ecosystem responses—making ensemble approaches and rigorous validation essential.

These modeling capabilities inform the WIA Ocean Acidification Response Standard's prediction and alert systems, enabling proactive rather than reactive management of ocean acidification impacts.
