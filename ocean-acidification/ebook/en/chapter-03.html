Chapter 3

Ecosystem Impacts

Coral Reefs, Shellfish, and Marine Food Chains

Overview

Ocean acidification creates cascading impacts throughout marine ecosystems, from microscopic plankton to apex predators, from shallow coral reefs to deep-sea communities. This chapter examines how changing ocean chemistry affects individual species, critical habitats, and entire food webs, with far-reaching consequences for ocean health and human wellbeing.

Coral Reefs: Rainforests of the Sea Under Threat

Coral reefs are among the most vulnerable ecosystems to ocean acidification. These biodiversity hotspots support 25% of all marine species despite covering less than 0.1% of the ocean floor.

Coral Calcification and Growth

Reef-building corals (scleractinian corals) create their calcium carbonate skeletons through a process that is highly sensitive to carbonate chemistry:

Reduced Calcification Rates CRITICAL

  • Current impact: 15-30% reduction in calcification rates since pre-industrial times
  • Mechanism: Lower CO₃²⁻ availability reduces CaCO₃ precipitation
  • Threshold: Below Ω_aragonite = 3.0, calcification becomes severely impaired
  • 2050 projection: 50-70% reduction in calcification at current emission rates
  • Consequence: Weaker skeletons, reduced reef growth, increased storm vulnerability

Erosion vs. Accretion Imbalance CRITICAL

  • Natural balance: Coral growth normally exceeds bioerosion by boring organisms
  • Acidification effect: Erosion accelerates while growth slows
  • Tipping point: When erosion exceeds growth, reefs begin net dissolution
  • Caribbean reefs: Some already experiencing net erosion
  • Timeline: Many reefs could shift to net erosion by 2050

Coral-Algae Symbiosis Disruption

Acidification interacts with coral bleaching (temperature stress), creating synergistic impacts:

Stressor Combination Impact on Corals Recovery Potential Time to Death
Acidification alone (pH 7.8) 30% calcification reduction Moderate (decades) Gradual decline (50-100 years)
Warming alone (+2°C) Bleaching, 50% mortality Good if brief (months) Rapid if prolonged (weeks)
Acidification + Warming 90% mortality, failed recovery Very poor Rapid (days-weeks)
Acidification + Warming + Pollution Near-complete mortality None Very rapid (hours-days)

⚠️ Reef Ecosystem Services at Risk

Coral reefs provide critical services worth an estimated $375 billion annually:

  • Fisheries: Supporting 1 billion people's protein needs
  • Coastal protection: $100+ billion in storm/erosion protection
  • Tourism: $36 billion directly, $100+ billion including multiplier effects
  • Biodiversity: Genetic resources, pharmaceuticals, cultural heritage
  • Carbon storage: Significant though less than open ocean

By 2050, most coral reefs could cease to exist as functional ecosystems, representing one of the greatest losses of biodiversity and ecosystem services in human history.

Shellfish and Mollusks: Commercial and Ecological Crises

Mollusks (oysters, clams, mussels, scallops) and other shelled organisms are experiencing some of the most immediate and economically significant impacts of ocean acidification.

Larval Shell Development Failure

The Larval Bottleneck CRITICAL

The earliest life stages are most vulnerable to acidification:

  • Pacific oyster larvae: 50-80% mortality at pH 7.7 (common in upwelling zones)
  • Critical period: First 48 hours when shell development initiates
  • Mechanism: Insufficient carbonate ions to begin shell precipitation
  • Hatchery impacts: $110 million losses in Pacific Northwest (2005-2009)
  • Wild populations: Recruitment failures observed in multiple species

Case Study: Pacific Northwest Oyster Crisis

🦪 Real-World Acidification Impacts

Background:

  • Pacific Northwest supports $278 million oyster industry
  • Natural upwelling brings deep, CO₂-rich water to surface
  • Anthropogenic acidification makes upwelling water more corrosive

The Crisis (2005-2009):

  • Hatcheries experienced 70-80% larval mortality
  • pH during upwelling events: 7.6-7.8 (Ω_aragonite < 1.0)
  • Production crashes threatened industry collapse

The Response:

  • Installation of real-time pH monitoring systems
  • Water intake modifications to avoid low-pH water
  • Buffering treatments to raise pH of hatchery water
  • Selective breeding for acid-tolerant strains

Outcomes:

  • Partial recovery through adaptation strategies
  • Industry remains vulnerable to extreme upwelling events
  • Demonstrated urgent need for monitoring and early warning systems
  • Model for WIA Ocean Acidification Standard implementation

Pteropods: Canaries in the Coal Mine

Pteropods (sea butterflies and sea angels) are planktonic snails with aragonite shells that are extremely sensitive to acidification. They serve as indicator species for ocean health.

Shell Dissolution in Living Pteropods CRITICAL

  • Observation: Wild pteropods collected in Southern Ocean show active shell dissolution
  • Threshold: Ω_aragonite < 1.0 causes rapid shell degradation
  • Arctic status: Already reaching undersaturation seasonally
  • Southern Ocean: Projected chronic undersaturation by 2050
  • Ecological role: Critical food for salmon, mackerel, whales, seabirds
  • Consequences: Food web collapse scenarios in polar ecosystems

Coccolithophores and Marine Phytoplankton

Microscopic phytoplankton form the foundation of marine food webs and play a critical role in the global carbon cycle.

Coccolithophores: The Chalk Makers

These single-celled algae produce calcite plates (coccoliths) and are responsible for much of the calcium carbonate production in the open ocean:

  • Current role: ~1.5 gigatons of CaCO₃ produced annually
  • Acidification impact: Malformed coccoliths, reduced calcification (20-40% at pH 7.8)
  • Photosynthesis: May increase under high CO₂ (fertilization effect)
  • Trade-off: More biomass but less calcium carbonate (shifting C/Ca ratio)
  • Climate feedback: Reduced carbonate production alters ocean carbon pump
  • Ecosystem impact: Changes in phytoplankton community structure

Diatoms vs. Coccolithophores: Community Shifts

Characteristic Diatoms (Silica) Coccolithophores (Calcite) Acidification Winner
Shell Material Silica (SiO₂) Calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) Diatoms (unaffected)
CO₂ Response Positive (photosynthesis boost) Mixed (photosynthesis up, calcification down) Diatoms
Nutrient Requirements Silicon, nitrogen, phosphorus Nitrogen, phosphorus Context-dependent
Carbon Export High (dense shells sink fast) Moderate (ballasting effect) Depends on community shift
Food Web Quality High (fatty acids, nutrition) Lower nutritional value Diatoms

🔬 The Phytoplankton Shift Hypothesis

Acidification may drive a global shift from calcifying to non-calcifying phytoplankton communities. Consequences include:

  • Altered carbon export: Changes in biological carbon pump efficiency
  • Food web impacts: Different nutritional quality for grazers
  • Climate feedback: Reduced ocean CO₂ sequestration capacity
  • Regional extinctions: Some calcifying species may disappear

Echinoderms: Urchins, Starfish, and Sea Cucumbers

Echinoderms build their skeletons from high-magnesium calcite, one of the most soluble forms of calcium carbonate, making them particularly vulnerable.

Sea Urchin Development

Larval Abnormalities HIGH

  • Purple sea urchin: 50% developmental abnormalities at pH 7.7
  • Skeletal malformations: Smaller, weaker larval rods
  • Delayed development: Slower growth to metamorphosis
  • Mortality increase: 2-3x higher at acidified conditions
  • Adult impacts: Thinner tests (shells), reduced spine strength

Ecosystem Role Implications

  • Herbivory: Urchins control algae; population changes alter kelp forest dynamics
  • Predator-prey: Weakened spines may increase predation vulnerability
  • Commercial species: Sea urchin roe (uni) industry worth $100M+ globally
  • Regional impacts: Coastal ecosystem phase shifts possible

Fish: Beyond Calcification

Fish don't build calcium carbonate structures, but acidification affects them through surprising pathways, particularly neurological and behavioral impacts.

Sensory System Disruption

Olfactory Impairment HIGH

  • Mechanism: Elevated CO₂ alters GABA-A neurotransmitter receptor function
  • Clownfish studies: Cannot distinguish predator odors at pH 7.8
  • Salmon homing: Impaired ability to find natal streams
  • Settlement behavior: Larval fish make poor habitat choices
  • Predator avoidance: Reduced response to threat cues (increased mortality)

Hearing and Laterline Function MODERATE

  • Otolith growth: Ear stones grow larger in acidified water (aragonite structures)
  • Hearing sensitivity: Changes in auditory thresholds
  • Schooling behavior: Impaired coordination and communication
  • Navigation: Potential disruption of magnetic sense orientation

Regional Ecosystem Projections

🌍 Major Ecosystem Transformations by 2100

  • Coral reefs (tropical): 70-90% loss, shift to algae-dominated systems
  • Polar ecosystems: Pteropod collapse, food web reorganization
  • Kelp forests: Expansion in some areas (urchin decline), loss in others (warming)

  • Deep sea: Shoaling saturation horizons threaten deep-water corals
  • Coastal estuaries: Compound stressors create dead zones

Chapter Summary

Ocean acidification creates pervasive impacts across marine ecosystems, from individual organisms to entire food webs. Calcifying organisms—including corals, shellfish, pteropods, and many plankton—face direct threats to their ability to build and maintain shells and skeletons. Even non-calcifying organisms like fish experience surprising neurological and behavioral impacts that reduce survival.

These individual species impacts cascade through food webs, threatening to reorganize entire ecosystems and disrupt critical services including fisheries, coastal protection, and carbon sequestration. The differential sensitivity of species creates "winners and losers," potentially shifting oceans toward jellyfish-dominated systems with reduced biodiversity and productivity.

Understanding these ecosystem impacts is crucial for effective monitoring, early warning systems, and targeted conservation efforts—the foundation of the WIA Ocean Acidification Response Standard.
