Chapter 7

Adaptation Approaches

Resilient Species, Aquaculture Practices, and Protected Areas

Overview

While mitigation addresses the root causes of ocean acidification, adaptation strategies help ecosystems and human communities cope with acidification that is already occurring and locked in for coming decades. This chapter explores biological, technological, and social adaptation pathways, from assisted evolution of resilient species to redesigning aquaculture systems to transforming coastal economies.

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand natural vs. assisted adaptation mechanisms
  • Explore selective breeding and assisted evolution for key species
  • Learn aquaculture adaptation strategies and technologies
  • Examine marine protected area design for acidification resilience
  • Assess fisheries management adaptations
  • Evaluate community-based adaptation and livelihood transitions

Natural Adaptation Capacity

Marine organisms have evolutionary mechanisms for adaptation, but the rapid pace of acidification challenges their capacity to adapt naturally.

Evolutionary Response Timescales

Organism Type Generation Time Genetic Diversity Adaptation Potential
Phytoplankton Days-weeks Very high (large populations) High—rapid evolution possible
Zooplankton Weeks-months High Moderate-High—some adaptation likely
Shellfish (oysters) 1-3 years Moderate-High Moderate—selective breeding promising
Fish 1-5 years Moderate Low-Moderate—behavioral plasticity helps
Corals 5-20 years Moderate (declining) Low—too slow for current acidification rate
Marine mammals 10-30 years Low (small populations) Very Low—reliant on food web adaptation

⚠️ The Speed Mismatch

Ocean acidification is occurring at 10-100 times faster than most marine species' capacity for evolutionary adaptation. This speed mismatch means:

  • Long-lived species (corals, large fish, mammals) cannot evolve fast enough
  • Short-lived species (plankton) may adapt but create food web mismatches
  • Even successful adaptation comes at costs (energy allocation trade-offs)
  • Human assistance may be necessary for some key species survival

Phenotypic Plasticity: The First Line of Defense

🧬 Within-Generation Responses

Phenotypic plasticity allows organisms to adjust to environmental changes within their lifetime, without genetic changes:

  • Physiological acclimation: Adjusting metabolic rates, ion regulation
  • Behavioral changes: Habitat shifts, feeding adjustments
  • Limits: Plasticity has bounds—extreme conditions exceed adaptive capacity
  • Costs: Energy diverted from growth/reproduction to stress response

Examples:

  • Fish compensating for behavioral impairment by increased vigilance (energy cost)
  • Oysters up-regulating calcification genes (limited effectiveness)
  • Corals expelling symbionts and recruiting more tolerant strains

Selective Breeding and Assisted Evolution

For commercially and ecologically important species, human-assisted adaptation can accelerate the development of acidification-resistant populations.

Oyster and Shellfish Breeding Programs

Commercial Aquaculture Adaptation

Approach:

  • Expose larvae to acidified conditions (pH 7.5-7.8)
  • Select survivors with highest growth and survival rates
  • Breed selected individuals for multiple generations
  • Characterize genetic markers associated with tolerance

Success Stories:

  • Pacific oyster (Crassostrea gigas): 20-30% improvement in survival after 2-3 generations
  • Sydney rock oyster: Acid-tolerant lines commercially available (Australia)
  • Eastern oyster: Breeding programs underway (USA East Coast)

Timeline:

  • Proof of concept: 3-5 years
  • Commercial lines development: 10-15 years
  • Industry-wide deployment: 15-20 years

Limitations:

  • Trade-offs: Tolerance may reduce growth rates or disease resistance
  • Genetic diversity loss: Selection narrows gene pool
  • Limited to aquaculture: Wild populations still vulnerable
  • Not a permanent solution: Only buys time for emission reductions

Coral Assisted Evolution

Engineering Resilient Reefs

Methods:

  • Selective breeding: Cross acid-tolerant coral colonies
  • Symbiont manipulation: Inoculate corals with heat/acid-tolerant Symbiodiniaceae
  • Epigenetic conditioning: Pre-expose larvae to stressors to prime stress response
  • Microbiome engineering: Introduce beneficial bacteria that aid stress tolerance

Research Progress:

  • Australian Institute of Marine Science: 50+ coral crosses tested
  • SECORE International: Larval conditioning protocols developed
  • Hawaii Institute of Marine Biology: Symbiont shuffle experiments
  • Some corals show 10-20% improved calcification under acidification

Deployment Challenges:

  • Scale: Billions of coral larvae needed to restore reef systems
  • Genetic risks: Reduced diversity, outbreeding depression
  • Ethical questions: Is human intervention justified/wise?
  • Regulatory hurdles: GMO concerns, permitting delays
  • Cost: $100-$1000+ per coral transplant

Aquaculture System Adaptations

Beyond selective breeding, aquaculture operations can adapt infrastructure and practices to cope with acidification.

Water Quality Management

Engineering Solutions for Hatcheries and Farms

Real-Time Monitoring:

  • Continuous pH, pCO₂, temperature sensors
  • Automated alerts for low-pH events
  • Integration with weather/upwelling forecasts
  • Cost: $10,000-$100,000 per facility

Water Treatment:

  • CO₂ stripping: Aeration towers remove dissolved CO₂
  • Alkalinity addition: Sodium carbonate buffering
  • Selective intake timing: Pump water during high-pH periods
  • Deep vs. surface water blending: Optimize source water mix

Recirculating Aquaculture Systems (RAS):

  • Closed systems with controlled chemistry
  • Degassing and buffering integrated into design
  • Eliminates dependence on ocean water quality
  • Trade-off: High capital costs, energy intensive

Operational Adaptations

Adaptation Description Cost Effectiveness
Seasonal Shifting Time spawning to avoid low-pH periods (upwelling season) Low (operational) Moderate—limits production windows
Location Diversification Multiple sites in different oceanographic regimes High (capital) High—risk spreading
Species Diversification Add acid-tolerant species to portfolio Medium (new expertise) High—resilience improvement
Land-Based Systems Shift to RAS or pond-based culture Very High (infrastructure) Very High—full control

Marine Protected Area (MPA) Design for Resilience

Strategic design and management of MPAs can enhance ecosystem resilience to acidification.

Resilience-Based MPA Principles

🛡️ Protecting Against the Unavoidable

Key Design Elements:

  • Connectivity: Networks allowing larval exchange and recolonization
  • Representation: Protect diversity of habitats and pH microenvironments
  • Replication: Multiple sites reduce risk of total loss
  • Size: Large enough to maintain viable populations and ecological processes
  • Climate refugia: Protect areas naturally buffered from acidification

Identifying Climate Refugia

🗺️ Priority Areas for Protection

Refugia Characteristics:

  • Upwelling shadows: Areas sheltered from low-pH upwelling
  • High productivity zones: Photosynthesis raises daytime pH
  • Strong water exchange: Flushing prevents CO₂ accumulation
  • High alkalinity: Groundwater input, carbonate geology
  • Depth refuges: Shallow areas for calcifiers before saturation horizon rises

Mapping Tools:

  • High-resolution pH monitoring networks
  • Oceanographic models of current patterns
  • Remote sensing (chlorophyll, SST as proxies)
  • Species distribution models under future scenarios

MPA Management Strategies

Strategy Mechanism Examples
Reduce Local Stressors Eliminate overfishing, pollution, physical damage Great Barrier Reef Marine Park zoning
Active Restoration Coral gardening, assisted evolution, transplants Caribbean coral restoration projects
Herbivore Protection No-take zones for parrotfish, urchins (algae control) Bonaire National Marine Park
Adaptive Zoning Adjust boundaries as conditions change California Marine Protected Area network
Wastewater Management Control nutrient/pollutant inputs lowering pH Florida Keys National Marine Sanctuary

Fisheries Management Adaptations

Commercial and subsistence fisheries face disruptions from acidification impacts on target species and food webs. Adaptive management is essential.

Ecosystem-Based Fisheries Management (EBFM)

Managing for Resilience, Not Just Yield

Principles:

  • Consider entire food web, not just target species
  • Account for climate and acidification impacts in stock assessments
  • Maintain ecosystem structure and function
  • Precautionary approach given uncertainties

Specific Adaptations:

  • Lower quotas: Reduce harvest to buffer against acidification stress
  • Protect forage fish: Maintain food web base (herring, anchovies, pteropods)
  • Habitat protection: Close sensitive areas (nursery grounds, spawning sites)
  • Dynamic management: Adjust regulations as ocean conditions change

Species Portfolio Diversification

Current Fishery Acidification Risk Diversification Strategy
Oyster Fishery Very High (calcifier) Add finfish, seaweed aquaculture
Dungeness Crab High (larval vulnerability) Develop alternative crab species, seasonal shifting
Coral Reef Fisheries Critical (habitat collapse) Transition to pelagic fishing, aquaculture
Salmon Moderate (food web impacts) Hatchery supplementation, habitat restoration

Community and Livelihood Adaptations

For coastal communities dependent on vulnerable fisheries, adaptation may require economic transformation.

Livelihood Diversification

🏘️ Building Community Resilience

Options for Fishing-Dependent Communities:

  • Aquaculture transition: From wild harvest to farming (with adaptation measures)
  • Tourism development: Eco-tourism, dive operations (while reefs remain)
  • Seaweed farming: Low-tech, climate-resilient alternative
  • Value-added processing: Increase profit margins on reduced catches
  • Alternative livelihoods: Training for non-marine industries

Support Mechanisms:

  • Government buyback programs for displaced fishers
  • Retraining and education subsidies
  • Microfinance for livelihood transitions
  • Social safety nets during transition periods

Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK) Integration

🌊 Learning from Indigenous Communities

Indigenous and local communities possess centuries of experience adapting to environmental changes:

  • Seasonal knowledge: Timing of spawning, migrations, productivity shifts
  • Spatial knowledge: Identification of refugia, productive areas
  • Species ecology: Behavioral patterns, habitat preferences
  • Adaptive practices: Flexible harvest strategies, resource rotation

Co-Management Approaches:

  • Combine TEK with scientific monitoring
  • Indigenous-led MPAs and conservation areas
  • Participatory research programs
  • Benefit-sharing from adaptation innovations

Early Warning Systems and Preparedness

Effective adaptation requires advance notice of acidification events, enabling proactive rather than reactive responses.

Industry Alert Systems

Actionable Forecasts for Aquaculture

Components:

  • Real-time pH/pCO₂ monitoring at key locations
  • Oceanographic forecasts (upwelling, mixing events)
  • Alert thresholds customized for species sensitivity
  • Automated notifications (SMS, email, dashboard)

Operational Responses:

  • Hatcheries: Delay larval release, activate water treatment
  • Grow-out operations: Harvest early, move stock to protected sites
  • Wild fisheries: Adjust effort, target alternative species

Example: Pacific Coast Shellfish Growers Association (PCSGA) System

  • Network of pH sensors along Washington/Oregon coast
  • Integration with NOAA upwelling forecasts
  • Alerts sent 24-72 hours before low-pH events
  • Estimated savings: $2-5 million/year in avoided losses

Chapter Summary

Adaptation to ocean acidification requires strategies spanning biological, technological, and social dimensions. While some species show capacity for natural adaptation through evolution or phenotypic plasticity, the rapid pace of acidification often exceeds these capacities, necessitating human-assisted approaches.

Selective breeding and assisted evolution show promise for commercially important species like oysters and corals, though timelines are long (10-20 years) and approaches carry risks. Aquaculture systems can adapt through water treatment, monitoring, and operational changes. Marine protected areas designed for climate resilience—protecting refugia, maintaining connectivity, reducing local stressors—offer ecosystem- level adaptation benefits.

For fishing communities, adaptation may ultimately require livelihood diversification and economic transformation, supported by early warning systems, co-management with Indigenous knowledge, and social safety nets. All these adaptations buy time but cannot substitute for the fundamental solution: reducing CO₂ emissions to halt acidification at the source.
