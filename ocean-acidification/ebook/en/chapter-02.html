Chemistry and Processes

CO₂ Absorption, Carbonate System, and Ocean Buffering

Overview

To understand ocean acidification, we must dive deep into the complex chemistry of seawater and the carbonate system. This chapter explores how CO₂ enters the ocean, the chemical transformations that occur, the buffering capacity of seawater, and the critical concept of saturation states that determine whether marine organisms can build and maintain their calcium carbonate structures.

CO₂ Gas Exchange: From Atmosphere to Ocean

The journey of atmospheric carbon dioxide into ocean water begins at the air-sea interface, governed by fundamental physical chemistry principles.

Henry's Law and CO₂ Solubility

The dissolution of CO₂ in seawater follows Henry's Law, which states that the amount of gas dissolved in a liquid is proportional to its partial pressure in the atmosphere:

[CO₂(aq)] = K_H × pCO₂

Where:

  • [CO₂(aq)]: Concentration of dissolved CO₂ in seawater
  • K_H: Henry's Law constant (temperature-dependent)
  • pCO₂: Partial pressure of CO₂ in the atmosphere

🌡️ Temperature Dependency

CO₂ solubility is highly temperature-dependent. Cold water dissolves significantly more CO₂ than warm water:

  • 0°C water: ~1.8 times more soluble than at 25°C
  • Polar regions: Act as major CO₂ sinks due to cold temperatures
  • Warming oceans: Reduced CO₂ uptake capacity, creating positive feedback
  • Seasonal cycles: Winter uptake, summer outgassing in many regions

Factors Affecting Gas Exchange Rates

Factor Effect on CO₂ Uptake Mechanism
Wind Speed Increases uptake Enhances mixing, reduces boundary layer thickness
Wave Action Increases uptake Increases surface area, disrupts stratification
Temperature Decreases with warming Reduces CO₂ solubility (inverse relationship)
Salinity Decreases slightly Higher salinity = lower gas solubility ("salting out")
Surface Films Decreases uptake Organic films create diffusion barriers
Biological Activity Increases uptake Photosynthesis draws down CO₂, creates gradient

The Carbonate Chemistry System

Once CO₂ enters seawater, a cascade of chemical reactions occurs, collectively known as the carbonate system or dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) system.

The Reaction Cascade

Understanding ocean acidification requires mastering these interconnected equilibrium reactions:

Complete Carbonate System Reaction Pathway

Step 1: CO₂ Dissolution
─────────────────────────
CO₂(g) ⇌ CO₂(aq)
Atmospheric CO₂ dissolves in seawater


Step 2: Hydration
─────────────────────────
CO₂(aq) + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃
Dissolved CO₂ reacts with water to form carbonic acid
(This reaction is actually quite slow without catalysis)


Step 3: First Dissociation
─────────────────────────
H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻
Carbonic acid dissociates into hydrogen ion and bicarbonate
(Fast reaction, K₁ = 10⁻⁶·³⁵ at 25°C)


Step 4: Second Dissociation
─────────────────────────
HCO₃⁻ ⇌ H⁺ + CO₃²⁻
Bicarbonate dissociates into hydrogen ion and carbonate
(K₂ = 10⁻¹⁰·³³ at 25°C)


Net Effect:
─────────────────────────
CO₂ + H₂O + CO₃²⁻ ⇌ 2 HCO₃⁻
This is the key buffering reaction!

The Three Forms of Dissolved Inorganic Carbon

At any given moment, carbon in seawater exists in three chemical forms, with relative proportions determined by pH:

Species Formula % at pH 8.1 % at pH 7.8 Biological Significance
Dissolved CO₂ & Carbonic Acid CO₂(aq) + H₂CO₃ ~0.5% ~1.1% Substrate for photosynthesis
Bicarbonate HCO₃⁻ ~89% ~94% Dominant DIC form, buffer component
Carbonate CO₃²⁻ ~10.5% ~4.9% Critical for calcification!

⚠️ The Carbonate Ion Problem

Notice how the carbonate ion (CO₃²⁻) percentage drops dramatically with even a small pH decrease. This is the crux of the ocean acidification crisis: as pH falls from 8.1 to 7.8, carbonate availability is cut in half, making it much harder for marine organisms to build shells and skeletons.

Ocean Buffering Capacity

For millions of years, the ocean has resisted pH changes through its natural buffering system. However, this buffering capacity is not unlimited and is currently being overwhelmed.

The Bicarbonate Buffer System

The ocean's primary buffering mechanism relies on the bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) and carbonate (CO₃²⁻) equilibrium:

How the Buffer Works

When acid (H⁺) is added to seawater:

H⁺ + CO₃²⁻ → HCO₃⁻

Carbonate ions "consume" the excess H⁺, preventing large pH drops.

When base (OH⁻) is added to seawater:

OH⁻ + HCO₃⁻ → CO₃²⁻ + H₂O

Bicarbonate ions neutralize the base, maintaining pH stability.

The Revelle Factor: Buffering Capacity Measurement

The Revelle factor (β) quantifies how efficiently the ocean can absorb CO₂ without large pH changes:

β = (ΔpCO₂/pCO₂) / (ΔDIC/DIC)
Ocean Region Current Revelle Factor Buffering Capacity Future Trend
Tropical Surface 9-10 Moderate Decreasing (approaching 13 by 2100)
Temperate Surface 10-12 Lower Decreasing significantly
Polar Surface 12-14 Weak Critical—already near limits
Deep Ocean 8-10 Higher Slowly decreasing

⚠️ Diminishing Returns

As more CO₂ is absorbed, the Revelle factor increases (buffering capacity decreases). This creates a dangerous positive feedback: the more CO₂ we add, the less effectively the ocean can absorb it, leading to accelerated pH changes and more atmospheric CO₂ remaining airborne.

Total Alkalinity and Buffering

Total Alkalinity (TA) represents the ocean's acid-neutralizing capacity, primarily from bicarbonate and carbonate ions:

TA ≈ [HCO₃⁻] + 2[CO₃²⁻] + [OH⁻] - [H⁺] + minor species
  • Pre-industrial TA: ~2300 μmol/kg in surface waters
  • Current TA: Relatively unchanged (~2300 μmol/kg)
  • Key insight: Alkalinity stays constant, but DIC increases, shifting the balance
  • Result: More bicarbonate, less carbonate, lower pH

Saturation States: The Calcification Threshold

Perhaps the most critical concept for understanding biological impacts is the saturation state of calcium carbonate minerals.

Calcium Carbonate Minerals in the Ocean

Marine organisms build shells and skeletons from two main forms of calcium carbonate (CaCO₃):

Mineral Chemical Formula Crystal Structure Users Solubility
Aragonite CaCO₃ Orthorhombic Corals, pteropods, some mollusks More soluble (less stable)
Calcite CaCO₃ Trigonal Coccolithophores, foraminifera, mollusks Less soluble (more stable)
High-Mg Calcite CaCO₃ (with Mg) Modified trigonal Echinoderms, coralline algae Most soluble (least stable)

The Saturation State Equation

The saturation state (Ω, omega) determines whether calcium carbonate will precipitate or dissolve:

Ω = [Ca²⁺] × [CO₃²⁻] / Ksp

Where:

  • Ω > 1: Supersaturated—precipitation favored (calcification possible)
  • Ω = 1: Saturated—equilibrium (no net precipitation or dissolution)
  • Ω < 1: Undersaturated—dissolution favored (shells dissolve!)
  • Ksp: Solubility product constant (different for aragonite and calcite)

📊 Critical Omega Thresholds

Tropical Corals Aragonite > 4.0 < 3.0 Reduced calcification, weakened skeletons
Cold-water Corals Aragonite > 2.5 < 1.5 Severe calcification impairment
Pteropods Aragonite > 2.0 < 1.0 Shell dissolution begins
Coccolithophores Calcite > 4.0 < 2.5 Malformed plates, reduced growth
Oysters (larvae) Aragonite > 2.5 < 1.5 Larval shell development failure

Regional Chemistry Variations

Ocean chemistry is not uniform globally. Understanding regional variations is crucial for targeted monitoring and conservation efforts.

Depth Profile: Vertical Stratification

Depth Zone pH Range Ω_aragonite Key Characteristics

The Aragonite Saturation Horizon

The aragonite saturation horizon (ASH) is the depth at which Ω_aragonite = 1. Above this depth, aragonite can form; below it, aragonite dissolves.

🌊 Rising Saturation Horizons

  • Pre-industrial ASH depth: ~1000-1500m in tropical Pacific
  • Current ASH depth: ~200-500m (shoaling by 50-200m/decade)
  • Arctic Ocean: ASH already reached surface in some areas
  • 2100 projection: ASH could reach surface in 30-70% of Southern Ocean

This shoaling means the "safe zone" for calcifying organisms is shrinking from both top (acidification) and bottom (rising saturation horizon), creating a squeeze on habitat availability.

Temperature, Salinity, and Pressure Effects

Ocean chemistry doesn't occur in isolation. Physical parameters significantly influence carbonate chemistry.

Temperature Effects

  • CO₂ solubility: Decreases ~4% per 1°C warming
  • pH: Decreases ~0.015 units per 1°C warming (independent of acidification)
  • Saturation states: Decrease with warming (double jeopardy with acidification)
  • Metabolic rates: Increase with temperature, increasing CO₂ production
  • Stratification: Warming increases stratification, reducing deep water ventilation

Salinity Effects

  • Higher salinity: Increases total alkalinity and DIC proportionally
  • pH impact: Minimal direct effect (alkalinity and DIC cancel out)
  • Calcium concentration: Increases with salinity (more Ca²⁺ available)
  • Freshwater inputs: Can create localized low-pH zones near river mouths

Pressure (Depth) Effects

  • CO₂ solubility: Increases with pressure/depth
  • Apparent pH: Decreases with depth (increased acidity)
  • Saturation states: Decrease dramatically with depth
  • Deep sea organisms: Adapted to lower pH, but still vulnerable to rapid changes

Carbonate System Modeling

Calculating complete carbonate chemistry requires knowing at least two of four measurable parameters:

🔬 The Four Measurable Parameters

  1. pH: Measure of acidity (hydrogen ion activity)
  2. Total Alkalinity (TA): Acid-neutralizing capacity
  3. Dissolved Inorganic Carbon (DIC): Sum of CO₂, HCO₃⁻, CO₃²⁻
  4. pCO₂: Partial pressure of CO₂ in equilibrium with seawater

Common pairs used:

  • pH + TA (most common in lab settings)
  • pCO₂ + TA (useful for atmosphere-ocean exchange studies)
  • DIC + TA (high precision oceanographic work)

From any two parameters, we can calculate the other two plus saturation states using software like CO2SYS, seacarb, or PyCO2SYS.

Natural Analogs: Windows into the Future

Several natural systems provide insights into future ocean chemistry:

CO₂ Seep Sites

Volcanic CO₂ vents create localized low-pH zones:

  • Ischia, Italy: pH gradients from 8.1 down to 6.5
  • Papua New Guinea: Multiple vent sites with diverse ecosystems
  • Findings: Calcifying species disappear below pH 7.8, ecosystem shifts to algae and sea grass
  • Winner species: Fleshy algae, some fish, sea anemones
  • Loser species: Corals, coralline algae, urchins, mollusks

Upwelling Zones

Areas where deep, CO₂-rich water reaches the surface provide a preview of future conditions:

  • California Current System: pH as low as 7.6-7.7 during upwelling events
  • Oyster hatchery failures: Documented mortality linked to upwelling acidification
  • Seasonal patterns: Help predict timing of future chronic acidification

Chapter Summary

Ocean acidification is driven by a complex cascade of chemical reactions beginning with CO₂ dissolution and culminating in reduced pH and carbonate ion availability. The ocean's buffering system, while historically effective at maintaining stable pH, is being overwhelmed by unprecedented rates of CO₂ input.

Critical concepts include the carbonate system equilibria, the Revelle factor's measurement of diminishing buffering capacity, and saturation states that determine whether marine organisms can build their shells and skeletons. Regional variations in chemistry—from polar to tropical, surface to deep, coastal to open ocean—require sophisticated monitoring networks to track these changes effectively.

Understanding this chemistry is essential for interpreting monitoring data, predicting future changes, and designing effective interventions—topics we'll explore in subsequent chapters.
