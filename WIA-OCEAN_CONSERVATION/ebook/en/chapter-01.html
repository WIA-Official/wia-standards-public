1.1 Why Ocean Conservation Matters

The ocean is Earth's largest ecosystem, covering approximately 71% of the planet's surface and containing 97% of all water. This vast body of water is not just a geographical feature—it is the foundation of life on Earth. The ocean produces more than half of the world's oxygen, absorbs about 30% of carbon dioxide produced by humans, and regulates global climate patterns.

Beyond its atmospheric and climatic functions, the ocean directly sustains human civilization. Over 3 billion people depend on marine and coastal biodiversity for their livelihoods. The ocean provides food, medicine, raw materials, and serves as a highway for international trade—over 90% of global trade is carried by sea. The ocean economy is valued at over $2.5 trillion annually, with millions of jobs directly tied to marine industries including fishing, tourism, shipping, and renewable energy.

Yet despite its critical importance, the ocean faces unprecedented threats. Overfishing has depleted fish stocks by 90% in many regions. Plastic pollution has created massive garbage patches, with an estimated 8 million tonnes of plastic entering the ocean annually. Climate change is warming ocean waters, causing coral bleaching and disrupting marine ecosystems. Ocean acidification threatens shell-forming organisms and the food webs that depend on them.

1.2 The Current State of Our Oceans

1.2.1 Overfishing and Fisheries Collapse

Industrial fishing has expanded dramatically since the 1950s, with fishing capacity now far exceeding the ocean's ability to replenish fish stocks. The Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) reports that 34% of global fish stocks are overfished, 60% are maximally sustainably fished, and only 6% are underfished. Iconic species like Atlantic bluefin tuna, many shark species, and Pacific salmon have experienced dramatic population declines.

Destructive fishing practices compound the problem. Bottom trawling destroys seafloor habitats, including coral reefs and seagrass beds that serve as nurseries for countless species. Bycatch—the unintentional capture of non-target species—kills an estimated 40% of the global catch, including sea turtles, dolphins, seabirds, and juvenile fish. Illegal, unreported, and unregulated (IUU) fishing accounts for up to 26 million tonnes of fish annually, undermining conservation efforts and costing the global economy $23 billion per year.

1.2.2 Plastic Pollution Crisis

Plastic has become the signature pollutant of our age. Since mass production began in the 1950s, humanity has produced over 9 billion tonnes of plastic, much of which ends up in the ocean. Five massive garbage patches now circulate in the world's major ocean gyres, with the Great Pacific Garbage Patch alone covering an area twice the size of Texas.

Microplastics—particles smaller than 5mm—have infiltrated every marine environment, from the surface to the deepest trenches. Marine animals mistake plastic for food, leading to starvation, internal injuries, and toxic chemical exposure. Microplastics have been found in commercially important fish and shellfish, entering the human food chain with unknown health consequences.

1.2.3 Climate Change Impacts

The ocean has absorbed 93% of excess heat from global warming, leading to ocean temperatures rising faster than at any time in the past 10,000 years. This warming triggers cascading effects: coral bleaching events that have devastated reefs worldwide, shifts in species distributions disrupting ecosystems and fisheries, and intensification of storms and extreme weather events.

Ocean acidification, often called "climate change's evil twin," occurs as the ocean absorbs CO2, forming carbonic acid and lowering pH. Ocean acidity has increased by 30% since pre-industrial times, threatening organisms that build calcium carbonate shells and skeletons—including corals, mollusks, and crucial plankton species at the base of marine food webs.

1.2.4 Habitat Destruction

Coastal development, pollution, and destructive fishing practices have degraded critical marine habitats. Approximately 50% of coral reefs, 35% of mangrove forests, and 29% of seagrass beds have been lost globally. These ecosystems provide nursery habitat for commercially important fish, protect coastlines from storms and erosion, sequester vast amounts of carbon, and support incredible biodiversity.

1.4 Global Conservation Framework

1.4.1 United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS)

Often called the "Constitution for the Oceans," UNCLOS is the primary international agreement governing ocean use and conservation. Established in 1982 and entering into force in 1994, UNCLOS defines maritime zones, establishes rights and responsibilities of nations, and provides a framework for marine resource management and environmental protection.

Key UNCLOS provisions include the 12-nautical-mile territorial sea, 200-nautical-mile Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ), and regulations for the high seas—international waters beyond national jurisdiction that cover 64% of the ocean surface. UNCLOS also established principles for marine scientific research, protection of the marine environment, and peaceful resolution of disputes.

1.4.2 Sustainable Development Goal 14 (SDG 14)

The United Nations' SDG 14, "Life Below Water," aims to conserve and sustainably use the ocean, seas, and marine resources. Adopted in 2015 as part of the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development, SDG 14 includes ten specific targets addressing marine pollution, ocean acidification, overfishing, IUU fishing, marine protected areas, and small-scale fisheries.

Key SDG 14 targets include: preventing and reducing marine pollution (14.1), protecting at least 10% of coastal and marine areas (14.5), ending overfishing and IUU fishing (14.4 and 14.6), and increasing economic benefits from sustainable ocean use (14.7). Progress toward these targets varies globally, with significant work still needed to achieve the 2030 goals.

1.4.3 Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD)

The CBD's Aichi Biodiversity Targets, established in 2010, included ambitious goals for marine conservation including protecting 10% of coastal and marine areas by 2020. The post-2020 Global Biodiversity Framework, adopted in 2022, increased this target to 30% protection by 2030 ("30x30"), recognizing the urgent need for expanded marine protection.

1.4.4 Regional Fisheries Management Organizations (RFMOs)

RFMOs manage fisheries in international waters and coordinate conservation efforts among member nations. Organizations like the International Commission for the Conservation of Atlantic Tunas (ICCAT), the Western and Central Pacific Fisheries Commission (WCPFC), and the Commission for the Conservation of Antarctic Marine Living Resources (CCAMLR) set catch limits, establish protected areas, and combat illegal fishing in their respective regions.

1.5 The WIA-OCEAN_CONSERVATION Standard

The WIA-OCEAN_CONSERVATION standard provides a comprehensive, unified framework for marine conservation efforts worldwide. Developed in alignment with international agreements and incorporating best practices from leading conservation organizations, this standard addresses the full spectrum of ocean conservation challenges.

Key Components of the Standard

  • Standardized Data Formats: Consistent protocols for collecting and sharing marine observation data, enabling global collaboration and analysis.
  • API Integration: Seamless integration with satellite monitoring, marine databases, enforcement systems, and citizen science platforms.
  • Conservation Protocols: Evidence-based procedures for species monitoring, ecosystem assessment, MPA management, and restoration activities.
  • Technology Integration: Advanced tools for illegal fishing detection, pollution tracking, coral reef monitoring, and conservation effectiveness evaluation.
  • Stakeholder Engagement: Frameworks for involving communities, indigenous peoples, fishers, scientists, policymakers, and NGOs in conservation efforts.

1.6 The Philosophy: 弘益人間 (Benefit All Humanity)

The WIA-OCEAN_CONSERVATION standard is guided by the Korean philosophical principle of 弘益人間 (Hongik Ingan), meaning "Benefit All Humanity." This principle recognizes that ocean conservation is not just an environmental imperative but a moral obligation to present and future generations.

Healthy oceans benefit all of humanity through climate regulation, food security, economic opportunity, and intrinsic natural value. Ocean conservation must therefore be approached holistically, considering ecological, social, economic, and cultural dimensions. The principle of 弘益人間 reminds us that our actions today will echo through generations, and we have a responsibility to leave a thriving ocean for those who come after us.

1.7 A Vision for Ocean Conservation

Despite the challenges, there is hope. Marine Protected Areas are expanding globally, with numerous success stories of ecosystem recovery. Sustainable fishing practices are being adopted more widely, and fish stocks are rebounding in well-managed fisheries. Technologies like satellite monitoring and AI are revolutionizing our ability to detect and prevent illegal fishing. Coral restoration techniques are improving, with some reefs showing remarkable resilience.

The global community is increasingly recognizing the ocean's critical importance. The UN Decade of Ocean Science for Sustainable Development (2021-2030) is mobilizing scientific research and innovation. Major corporations are committing to ocean-friendly practices, and consumer awareness is driving demand for sustainable seafood. Youth movements worldwide are demanding ocean protection.

The coming decades will determine the fate of our ocean. With coordinated action, scientific innovation, political will, and public engagement, we can reverse the decline and restore ocean health. The WIA-OCEAN_CONSERVATION standard provides the tools and framework needed to make this vision a reality.
