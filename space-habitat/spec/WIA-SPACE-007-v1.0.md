# WIA-SPACE-007: Space Habitat Standard v1.0.0

**Status:** Draft  
**Version:** 1.0.0  
**Last Updated:** 2025-12-26  
**Category:** Space Infrastructure  
**Philosophy:** 弘益人間 (Benefit All Humanity)

## Abstract

This standard defines comprehensive requirements and guidelines for designing, building, and operating sustainable human habitats in space environments, including low Earth orbit stations, lunar bases, and Mars settlements. It covers structural design, life support systems, radiation protection, artificial gravity, closed ecosystems, construction methods, energy systems, and human factors.

## 1. Scope

### 1.1 Applicability

This standard applies to:
- Space stations in Earth orbit
- Lunar surface habitats and underground facilities
- Mars surface bases
- Free-flying space settlements
- Asteroid and other celestial body habitats

### 1.2 Objectives

- Ensure crew safety and long-term health
- Enable self-sufficiency and sustainability
- Minimize dependence on Earth resupply
- Support scientific research and economic activity
- Prepare for multi-generational space living

## 3. Terms and Definitions

### 3.1 General Terms

**Space Habitat:** A sealed environment designed to support human life in the vacuum of space or on celestial bodies.

**BLSS (Bioregenerative Life Support System):** A life support system using biological processes (plants, microorganisms) to recycle air, water, and waste.

**ISRU (In-Situ Resource Utilization):** The practice of using local materials from the Moon, Mars, or asteroids rather than transporting from Earth.

**Regolith:** The loose surface material covering solid rock on the Moon or Mars.

**EVA (Extravehicular Activity):** Activities performed by astronauts outside the pressurized habitat (spacewalks).

### 3.2 Radiation Terms

**GCR (Galactic Cosmic Rays):** High-energy particles from outside the solar system, difficult to shield.

**SPE (Solar Particle Event):** Sudden release of energetic particles from the Sun, requires storm shelter.

**Radiation Dose:** Measured in Sieverts (Sv) or millisieverts (mSv); career astronaut limit: 1 Sv.

### 3.3 Life Support Terms

**Oxygen Generation:** Production of breathable oxygen, typically through water electrolysis or plant photosynthesis.

**CO2 Removal:** Extraction of carbon dioxide from cabin air using chemical or biological methods.

**Water Recovery:** Recycling urine, sweat, humidity into potable water; target: >95% recovery.

## 4. Structural Requirements

### 4.1 Pressure Vessel Design

All habitable volumes SHALL:
- Maintain internal pressure of 55-101.3 kPa (0.54-1.0 atm)
- Withstand pressure differential without structural failure
- Use cylindrical or spherical geometry for optimal stress distribution
- Incorporate leak detection and automatic isolation capability

### 4.2 Radiation Shielding

Habitats SHALL provide radiation protection:
- LEO (Low Earth Orbit): 10-20 g/cm² equivalent shielding
- Lunar surface: 50-100 g/cm² using regolith covering
- Mars surface: 30-50 g/cm² (thin atmosphere provides partial shielding)
- Storm shelter: 100+ g/cm² for solar particle events

### 4.3 Micrometeorite Protection

Multi-layer shielding (Whipple shield) SHALL:
- Protect against impacts up to 1.5 cm diameter
- Include bumper layer, spacing, and rear wall
- Be inspected and repaired regularly

### 4.4 Thermal Control

Temperature SHALL be maintained:
- Internal: 18-27°C (64-81°F)
- Critical equipment: Within manufacturer specifications
- Passive systems (MLI, coatings) and active systems (fluid loops, radiators)

## 5. Life Support Systems

### 5.1 Atmosphere

Atmospheric composition SHALL:
- Oxygen: 19.5-23.5% by volume
- Carbon dioxide: <0.5% (5,000 ppm)
- Relative humidity: 30-70%
- Total pressure: 55-101.3 kPa

### 5.2 Water Systems

Water recycling SHALL achieve:
- Recovery rate: >93% (current ISS standard)
- Target: >98% for long-duration missions
- Potability: Meet WHO drinking water standards

### 5.3 Food Production

For long-duration missions (>2 years):
- Minimum 30% food from local cultivation
- Target: >70% self-sufficiency
- Hydroponic or aeroponic systems
- LED lighting optimized for plant growth

### 5.4 Waste Management

All waste SHALL be:
- Recycled where possible (target >80%)
- Converted to useful products (compost, building materials, fuel)
- Safely stored if not recyclable
- Never vented if avoidable

## 6. Artificial Gravity

### 6.1 Rotation Requirements

For rotating habitats providing artificial gravity:
- Rotation rate: ≤2 rpm (preferred ≤1 rpm for long-term)
- Radius: ≥100 m for 1g at 1 rpm
- Gradient: <10% difference between head and feet

### 6.2 Health Standards

Artificial gravity level SHALL:
- Prevent bone density loss (<1% per month)
- Maintain muscle mass
- Preserve cardiovascular function
- Minimum recommended: 0.3g (Mars-level)

## 7. Construction

### 7.1 3D Printing

Regolith-based construction SHALL:
- Use ≥80% local materials
- Achieve compressive strength ≥20 MPa
- Provide radiation shielding
- Be automatable by robots

### 7.2 Inflatable Modules

Inflatable habitats SHALL:
- Deploy reliably in vacuum
- Provide equivalent protection to rigid modules
- Include redundant pressure layers
- Be inspectable and repairable

### 7.3 Modular Design

All modules SHALL:
- Use standard docking interfaces (CBM, IDA, NDS)
- Function independently if isolated
- Support incremental expansion
- Be transportable in launch vehicles

## 8. Energy Systems

### 8.1 Solar Power

Solar arrays SHALL:
- Provide ≥50W per square meter (average)
- Include sun-tracking capability
- Have ≥15-year operational life
- Withstand micrometeorite damage

### 8.2 Nuclear Power

If nuclear reactors are used:
- Be sited ≥100m from habitation
- Include passive safety systems
- Provide 10+ years continuous operation
- Have dual containment

### 8.3 Energy Storage

Battery systems SHALL:
- Support ≥24 hours backup power
- Withstand ≥1,000 charge cycles
- Operate in temperature range -20°C to +50°C

## 9. Human Factors

### 9.1 Crew Selection

Crew members SHALL demonstrate:
- Psychological stability
- Teamwork and cooperation skills
- Stress management ability
- Cultural sensitivity

### 9.2 Habitat Design

Living spaces SHALL provide:
- ≥10 m³ personal volume per person
- Private sleeping quarters
- Common areas for social interaction
- Windows or viewports (where possible)

### 9.3 Psychological Support

Support systems SHALL include:
- Weekly psychological assessments
- Access to mental health professionals
- Earth communication (≥1 hour/week personal time)
- Recreation and entertainment options

### 9.4 Work-Life Balance

Schedules SHALL ensure:
- ≤8 hours work per day (except emergencies)
- ≥8 hours sleep opportunity
- ≥2 hours exercise daily
- Weekly rest days

## 10. Emergency Procedures

### 10.1 Fire Safety

Fire suppression SHALL:
- Detect fires within 10 seconds
- Suppress within 60 seconds
- Not deplete breathable atmosphere
- Include crew escape routes

### 10.2 Decompression

In case of cabin leak:
- Automatic isolation of affected module
- Crew alerted within 5 seconds
- Emergency suits accessible within 60 seconds
- Life support for ≥24 hours

### 10.3 Medical Emergencies

Medical capabilities SHALL include:
- Trauma care equipment
- Telemedicine with Earth
- Emergency evacuation plan
- Medical training for all crew

### 10.4 Evacuation

Escape vehicles SHALL:
- Be available at all times
- Accommodate entire crew
- Support ≥24 hours autonomous operation
- Be inspected monthly

## 11. Planetary Bases

### 11.1 Lunar Base

Lunar habitats SHALL:
- Be located near water ice deposits
- Utilize solar power (with nuclear backup)
- Provide ≥50 g/cm² radiation shielding
- Support ≥4 crew members continuously

### 11.2 Mars Base

Mars habitats SHALL:
- Use ISRU for water, oxygen, and fuel
- Employ MOXIE-type systems (CO2 → O2)
- Achieve ≥50% food self-sufficiency within 5 years
- Support expansion to 12+ crew

### 11.3 Terraforming Considerations

Long-term Mars development MAY include:
- Atmospheric modification (ethical review required)
- Introduction of photosynthetic organisms
- Climate warming strategies
- Multi-generational commitment

## 12. Compliance and Certification

### 12.1 Testing

All critical systems SHALL undergo:
- Design verification
- Prototype testing
- Integration testing
- Operational validation

### 12.2 Documentation

Required documentation:
- Design specifications
- Test reports
- Operations manual
- Emergency procedures
- Maintenance schedules

### 12.3 Inspections

Regular inspections SHALL occur:
- Daily: Critical systems (life support, power)
- Weekly: General habitat condition
- Monthly: Emergency equipment
- Annually: Structural integrity

## 13. Sustainability Metrics

Habitat sustainability SHALL be measured by:
- Water recycling rate (target: >98%)
- Oxygen closure (target: >95%)
- Food self-sufficiency (target: >70%)
- Material recycling (target: >80%)
- Energy independence (100% for Mars/Moon)

## 14. Ethical Guidelines

Space habitats SHALL:
- Respect potential extraterrestrial life
- Minimize environmental impact
- Ensure crew well-being
- Support international cooperation
- Benefit all humanity (弘益人間)

## Appendix A: Calculation Examples

### A.1 Artificial Gravity

For centrifugal acceleration a = ω²r:
- To achieve 1g (9.81 m/s²) at radius r=100m:
- ω = sqrt(a/r) = sqrt(9.81/100) = 0.313 rad/s = 3 rpm

### A.2 Water Budget

Daily water needs per person:
- Drinking/cooking: 3 liters
- Hygiene: 5 liters
- EVA suit cooling: 2 liters
- Total: ~10 liters/person/day

With 95% recovery: 0.5 liters/day resupply needed

### A.3 Oxygen Production

Oxygen needed per person: ~0.84 kg/day

Water electrolysis:
- 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂
- 1 kg O₂ requires 1.125 kg H₂O
- Daily: 0.945 kg water per person

Plant photosynthesis:
- 20-30 m² growing area per person
- Efficiency: 50-80% of oxygen needs
