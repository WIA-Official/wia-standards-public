Chapter 3

Wearable Sensors & Devices

Learning Objectives:

Understand the technology behind various health monitoring sensors including PPG, electrochemical sensors, accelerometers, and their applications in wearable devices. Learn about accuracy, limitations, and best practices for each sensor type.

3.1 Photoplethysmography (PPG) Sensors

PPG sensors are the most common technology in consumer wearables for heart rate monitoring. They work by shining light (typically green, red, or infrared) into the skin and measuring the amount of light that is reflected back. Blood absorbs more light than surrounding tissue, so variations in blood volume with each heartbeat cause fluctuations in light absorption.

3.1.1 PPG Technology Fundamentals

The PPG sensor consists of LEDs (light-emitting diodes) and photodetectors. During systole (heart contraction), blood volume in arteries increases, absorbing more light. During diastole (heart relaxation), blood volume decreases, reflecting more light back to the photodetector. By analyzing these periodic changes, the heart rate and other cardiovascular metrics can be calculated.

LED Color Wavelength Primary Use Advantages
Green 525-540 nm Heart rate monitoring Best absorption by hemoglobin, optimal for pulse detection
Red 660 nm SpO2 measurement Deep penetration, used with infrared for oxygen saturation
Infrared 880-940 nm SpO2 measurement Minimal absorption by tissue, essential for SpO2 calculations

3.1.2 Accuracy Factors

PPG accuracy depends on multiple factors:

  • Skin tone: Melanin affects light absorption; darker skin may require higher LED intensity and more sophisticated algorithms
  • Motion artifacts: Movement creates noise in the signal; advanced motion compensation algorithms are essential
  • Device fit: Loose-fitting devices allow ambient light interference and reduce signal quality
  • Tattoos: Ink, especially dark colors, can block LED light and interfere with measurements
  • Temperature: Cold skin reduces blood perfusion, weakening the PPG signal
  • Activity level: Accuracy decreases during vigorous exercise due to motion and reduced signal quality

3.2 Electrochemical Sensors for Glucose

Continuous glucose monitors use electrochemical sensors inserted subcutaneously to measure glucose in interstitial fluid. These sensors employ enzyme-based glucose oxidase or glucose dehydrogenase reactions that produce electrical currents proportional to glucose concentration.

3.2.1 CGM Sensor Technology

When glucose from interstitial fluid contacts the sensor's enzyme layer, an enzymatic reaction occurs producing hydrogen peroxide or an electron. This generates a small electrical current that the sensor measures and converts to a glucose value. Modern CGMs measure glucose every 1-5 minutes, providing 288-720 data points per day.

Interstitial vs Blood Glucose: CGM sensors measure interstitial fluid glucose, which lags behind blood glucose by 5-15 minutes. This lag is most apparent during rapid glucose changes and must be considered when interpreting CGM data for immediate treatment decisions.

3.3 Accelerometers and Activity Tracking

Accelerometers measure acceleration forces in three axes (X, Y, Z), enabling detection of body movement, orientation changes, and activity patterns. Modern wearables use MEMS (Micro-Electro-Mechanical Systems) accelerometers that are tiny, power-efficient, and highly accurate.

3.3.1 Activity Recognition

By analyzing acceleration patterns, algorithms can identify specific activities:

  • Walking: Regular, rhythmic up-and-down motion with consistent frequency
  • Running: Similar to walking but higher amplitude and frequency
  • Cycling: Minimal vertical acceleration, characteristic leg movement patterns
  • Swimming: Distinctive arm stroke patterns in water
  • Sleep: Extended periods of minimal movement with characteristic position changes

3.4 Pulse Oximetry for SpO2

Pulse oximetry uses two wavelengths of light (red and infrared) to determine the percentage of hemoglobin saturated with oxygen. Oxygenated hemoglobin (HbO2) absorbs more infrared light, while deoxygenated hemoglobin (Hb) absorbs more red light. By comparing absorption at both wavelengths, SpO2 can be calculated.

3.4.1 SpO2 Calculation

The ratio of red to infrared absorption correlates with oxygen saturation. Normal SpO2 levels are 95-100%. Values below 90% indicate hypoxemia and require medical attention. The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted the importance of home SpO2 monitoring for detecting "silent hypoxemia" where patients have dangerously low oxygen levels without obvious respiratory distress.

3.5 Electrocardiography (ECG/EKG)

Consumer wearables now offer single-lead ECG capabilities, typically using two electrodes to measure electrical activity of the heart. While less comprehensive than clinical 12-lead ECG, single-lead recordings can detect atrial fibrillation and other cardiac arrhythmias.

3.5.1 How Wearable ECG Works

To record an ECG with a smartwatch, the user places a finger on a button or crown, creating a complete circuit between the watch back (in contact with wrist) and the finger. This captures the electrical potential difference caused by the heart's electrical activity.

Clinical Limitations: Single-lead ECG from wearables is not a substitute for comprehensive clinical ECG. It's primarily useful for detecting AFib and should not be used to diagnose myocardial infarction or other complex cardiac conditions.

3.6 Device Integration Considerations

Successful health monitoring systems must integrate diverse devices with varying capabilities, accuracy levels, and data formats. Key considerations include:

3.6.1 Device Registration and Management

  • Unique device identification (manufacturer, model, serial number)
  • Capability discovery (which metrics the device can measure)
  • Firmware version tracking for algorithm changes
  • Battery level monitoring and low-battery handling
  • Sensor wear time tracking (for devices like CGM with limited lifespans)

3.6.2 Multi-Device Conflict Resolution

Users often wear multiple devices simultaneously (smartwatch + CGM + smart scale). Systems must handle conflicting data intelligently:

  • Prioritize medical-grade devices over consumer devices when both provide the same metric
  • Use quality scores to weight conflicting measurements
  • Present multiple sources transparently to users and clinicians
  • Implement device-specific correction factors based on validation studies

Key Takeaways

  • PPG sensors measure heart rate using light absorption changes with blood volume, affected by skin tone, motion, and device fit
  • Electrochemical CGM sensors measure glucose in interstitial fluid, which lags blood glucose by 5-15 minutes
  • Accelerometers enable activity recognition, step counting, and sleep tracking through pattern analysis
  • Pulse oximetry uses two wavelengths of light to calculate oxygen saturation, critical for respiratory monitoring
  • Single-lead ECG from wearables can detect atrial fibrillation but is not a substitute for comprehensive clinical ECG
  • Device integration requires handling multiple data sources, quality scores, and conflict resolution strategies
