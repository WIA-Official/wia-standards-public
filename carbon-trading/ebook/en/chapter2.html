CHAPTER 2

Carbon Markets Fundamentals

Understanding the structure, participants, and mechanics of compliance and voluntary carbon markets.

Compliance vs. Voluntary Markets

Compliance Markets

Compliance carbon markets are established by government regulations that mandate emission reductions. Companies operating in regulated sectors must hold sufficient allowances or credits to cover their emissions, or face significant penalties. These markets represent the majority of global carbon trading volume.

Key Characteristics:

  • Legally binding emission caps
  • Penalties for non-compliance (often €100+ per tonne)
  • Established by government regulation
  • Covers major industrial emitters
  • Declining cap over time drives emission reductions

Voluntary Markets

Voluntary carbon markets allow organizations and individuals to purchase carbon credits to offset their emissions beyond regulatory requirements. These markets are growing rapidly as companies make net-zero commitments and consumers demand climate action.

Key Characteristics:

  • No regulatory mandate
  • Driven by corporate commitments and consumer demand
  • Often requires higher quality standards
  • Supports diverse project types globally
  • Rapidly expanding market ($2B in 2023, projected $50B by 2030)

Types of Carbon Credits

Allowances

Carbon allowances (also called permits or quotas) are created by cap-and-trade systems. Governments issue a limited number of allowances, each representing the right to emit one tonne of CO2. As the cap decreases over time, allowances become scarcer and more valuable.

Example: In the EU ETS, a power company receives or purchases allowances. If it emits 1 million tonnes of CO2 but holds only 900,000 allowances, it must buy 100,000 allowances from the market or face penalties of €100 per tonne.

Offset Credits

Offset credits are generated by projects that reduce, remove, or avoid greenhouse gas emissions outside the capped sectors. These projects must demonstrate additionality - that the emission reductions would not have occurred without the carbon finance incentive.

Major Offset Standards:

  • Verra VCS: Verified Carbon Standard, largest voluntary registry
  • Gold Standard: High-quality credits with sustainable development benefits
  • CDM: Clean Development Mechanism under Kyoto Protocol
  • Climate Action Reserve: North America focused
  • American Carbon Registry: First private voluntary registry

Removal vs. Avoidance Credits

An important distinction exists between credits from emission avoidance/reduction versus actual carbon removal:

  • Avoidance/Reduction: Preventing emissions that would otherwise occur (renewable energy, energy efficiency, forest conservation)
  • Removal: Extracting CO2 from the atmosphere (reforestation, direct air capture, biochar, enhanced weathering)

Removal credits typically command premium prices ($50-200 per tonne) as they address legacy emissions and are essential for achieving net-zero, particularly for hard-to-abate sectors.

Major Carbon Trading Systems

EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS)

Launched in 2005, the EU ETS is the world's first and largest carbon market, covering about 40% of the EU's greenhouse gas emissions. It operates across 27 EU member states plus Iceland, Liechtenstein, and Norway.

Covered Sectors:

  • Power generation and heat production
  • Energy-intensive industries (steel, cement, chemicals)
  • Aviation within the European Economic Area
  • Maritime transport (from 2024)

Key Features:

  • Covers ~10,000 installations
  • Annual cap declining 2.2% per year (accelerating to 4.2%)
  • Mix of free allocation and auctioning
  • Market Stability Reserve to manage surplus allowances
  • Target: 62% reduction by 2030 vs. 2005

China National ETS

Launched in July 2021, China's national ETS is now the world's largest carbon market by coverage, encompassing over 5 billion tonnes of CO2 annually - about one-seventh of global emissions.

Current Scope:

  • Power sector only (initial phase)
  • ~2,200 power companies
  • Planned expansion to steel, cement, aluminum, chemicals
  • Free allocation based on benchmarks
  • Trading price: ~$8-12 per tonne (significantly below EU)

California Cap-and-Trade

California's program, launched in 2013 and linked with Quebec's system, is North America's most comprehensive carbon market. It covers about 75% of the state's emissions.

Program Details:

  • Covers electricity, large industrial facilities, fuel distributors
  • Quarterly allowance auctions
  • Price floor ($18.72 in 2024, rising annually)
  • Offset program limited to 4-6% of compliance
  • Target: Carbon neutrality by 2045

Pricing Mechanisms

Market-Based Pricing

In cap-and-trade systems, prices are determined by supply and demand dynamics. The cap creates scarcity, while factors like economic growth, energy prices, weather, and technological change affect demand. Prices can be volatile, requiring market stability mechanisms.

Price Floors and Ceilings

Many systems implement price controls to manage volatility:

  • Price Floor: Minimum auction price prevents collapse (California: $18.72)
  • Price Ceiling: Maximum price or safety valve releases extra allowances if prices spike too high
  • Price Collar: Combination of floor and ceiling

Factors Affecting Carbon Prices

  • Cap Stringency: Tighter caps drive higher prices
  • Economic Growth: Stronger economy increases demand for allowances
  • Energy Prices: High fossil fuel prices encourage switching, reducing demand
  • Technology Costs: Cheaper renewables reduce abatement costs
  • Weather: Cold winters increase heating demand
  • Policy Expectations: Anticipated future regulations affect current prices
Price Evolution: EU ETS prices have evolved from €5-15 (2013-2017) to €70-100 (2023-2024), driven by tighter caps, Market Stability Reserve, and stronger climate ambition. Analysts project €150+ by 2030.

Market Participants

Compliance Buyers

Companies in regulated sectors that must surrender allowances equal to their emissions. Major buyers include power generators, steel mills, cement plants, refineries, and airlines.

Voluntary Buyers

  • Corporations: Microsoft, Google, Amazon purchasing millions of credits annually for net-zero commitments
  • Financial Institutions: Banks offsetting operational emissions
  • Governments: Public sector offsetting programs
  • Individuals: Flight offsetting, lifestyle carbon footprint reduction

Project Developers

Organizations that develop emission reduction projects and generate carbon credits. Includes renewable energy developers, forestry companies, waste management firms, and specialized carbon project developers.

Financial Intermediaries

  • Brokers: Facilitate trades between buyers and sellers
  • Exchanges: Provide trading platforms (ICE, EEX, CME)
  • Banks: Provide financing, derivatives, risk management
  • Investment Funds: Carbon-focused funds and portfolios
  • Speculators: Provide liquidity through trading

Looking Forward

Carbon markets are expanding rapidly, with over 70 carbon pricing initiatives worldwide covering nearly a quarter of global emissions. As climate ambition increases and net-zero targets approach, these markets will play an increasingly central role in the global economy.
