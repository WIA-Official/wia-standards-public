Chapter 01: Introduction & The Seed Sovereignty Question

1.1 Why this book exists

WIA Smart Seed is the open standard for seed registration, certification, testing, and digital passport data exchange. This book is the practitioner introduction.

1.2 The seed sovereignty question

Seeds are the foundation of agriculture. Modern seed systems combine three intertwined concerns: variety registration (UPOV plant breeders rights, national variety listings), seed quality testing (ISTA international rules, AOSA US rules), and seed certification (OECD seed schemes for international trade). Seed companies, certification bodies, regulators, and farmers each interact with these systems through paper records and vendor-locked digital systems. Cross-border seed trade requires manual reconciliation of certification chains across multiple jurisdictions.

1.3 What WIA Smart Seed changes

The standard collapses paper records and vendor-locked digital systems into one open wire format. A seed lot produced in Argentina, certified to OECD seed scheme rules, tested by ISTA-accredited laboratories, and shipped to farmers in Brazil can carry a digital passport that retailers and farmers verify by QR code without contacting any single central database.

1.4 The 4-Phase architecture

Phase 1 (Data Format) defines JSON envelopes for variety registrations, seed lots, germination tests, digital passports, and traceability chain records. Phase 2 (API Interface) specifies the HTTP surface. Phase 3 (Federation Protocol) handles cross-organisation data flow. Phase 4 (Integration) bridges to UPOV, ISTA, AOSA, OECD seed schemes, FAO AgroVoc crop concept vocabulary, and the WIA family.

1.5 Farmer sovereignty as a baked-in primitive

Earlier seed standards have treated farmer use of saved seeds (from previous harvests) as either fully permitted (UPOV 1978 Convention) or restricted (UPOV 1991 Convention). WIA Smart Seed treats the farmer\x27s right to know what they are buying as a Phase 3 primitive: every digital passport MUST be verifiable without contacting a vendor system, so farmers retain independent verification capability.

1.6 How this book is organised

Chapter 2 covers seed quality. Chapter 3 introduces the standard at a glance. Chapters 4-7 walk the four phases. Chapter 8 closes with conformance and roadmap.

Implementer Notes

Three operational concerns recur in seed infrastructure and are baked into the standard. First, ISTA accreditation is the gating credential for international seed trade; the standard\x27s lab_id field MUST reference an active ISTA accreditation that downstream consumers can verify via WIA-OMNI-API. Second, passport revocation must propagate quickly when contamination or fraud is detected; the standard\x27s POST /ss/passport/{id}/revoke endpoint is the primary mechanism. Third, farmer-saved seed compatibility varies by jurisdiction; implementations MUST NOT make jurisdiction-specific judgements but MUST preserve the breeder PVR number so downstream consumers can apply local rules.

Korea-Specific Application Note

Korea\x27s seed system is coordinated by the Rural Development Administration (농촌진흥청) and the Korea Seed and Variety Service (국립종자원). Korean rice varieties (e.g., 일품벼, 추청벼, 호품벼) are registered through the national variety listing process. The standard\x27s Phase 4 UPOV bridge maps cleanly onto KSVS PVR records, and Korean ISTA-accredited laboratories (국립종자원 시험분석부 등) can submit germination test data directly. The standard\x27s farmer-sovereignty primitive aligns with Korean farmer expectations of independent verification of purchased seed quality.

Independent Verification Pattern

The standard's defining choice — public-readable digital passport endpoints — implements the farmer-sovereignty primitive at the API layer. A farmer scanning a QR code at the point of purchase resolves to a publicly-readable URL that returns the digital_passport envelope including signature, lab credentials, certification class, and traceability chain. The farmer can verify the signature against the certification body's published key (also public). No vendor authentication required. This pattern lifts cleanly to vendor-locked alternative systems: when a vendor offers a digital passport that requires their app to verify, the farmer should refuse to buy.
