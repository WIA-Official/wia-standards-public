Effective interventions transform degraded lands into productive ecosystems, restoring ecological function while improving livelihoods. 6.1 Intervention Planning and Prioritization

Successful intervention begins with strategic planning that matches interventions to specific degradation types, environmental conditions, socioeconomic contexts, and available resources. The standard provides frameworks for evidence-based intervention selection and adaptive implementation.

Prioritization Criteria

With limited resources, not all degraded areas can be addressed simultaneously. Prioritization considers multiple factors:

  • Degradation Severity: Severely degraded areas may require intervention before degradation becomes irreversible
  • Restoration Potential: Areas with moderate degradation often show better response to intervention than severely degraded areas
  • Strategic Value: Watersheds, riparian zones, and migration corridors have disproportionate ecosystem importance
  • Population Density: High population areas affect more people but may face greater future pressure
  • Cost-Effectiveness: Return on investment varies significantly across intervention types and locations
  • Community Engagement: Areas with strong community organization and motivation show better sustainability

6.2 Soil Conservation Interventions

Soil conservation forms the foundation of most restoration efforts, reducing erosion and improving moisture retention. Physical structures combined with biological measures provide comprehensive erosion control.

Physical Soil Conservation

Contour Bunding: Earth embankments constructed along contour lines slow runoff, reduce erosion, and increase infiltration. Bunds are typically 0.5-1.0m high, spaced 10-50m apart depending on slope. On agricultural land, bunds also define cultivation strips that follow contours rather than slope lines.

Terracing: More intensive than bunding, terracing creates level or reverse-slope benches on steep slopes. Bench terraces are common in mountainous areas, while broad-based terraces suit mechanized agriculture. Terracing requires significant labor but provides long-lasting erosion control and cultivation opportunities.

Check Dams: Small structures built across gullies and drainage lines slow water flow, trap sediment, and promote groundwater recharge. Loose rock check dams allow water passage while trapping sediment. Gabion check dams (wire mesh filled with rocks) provide more structured control. Check dams are cost-effective (typically $500-$3,000 each) and highly effective at controlling gully erosion.

Stone Lines and Bunds: Lines of stones placed along contours provide simpler erosion control than earth bunds. Stones slow runoff, trap sediment, and gradually form natural terraces as soil accumulates behind them. Stone lines are particularly suitable for rocky soils where earth bunding is difficult.

Biological Soil Conservation

Vetiver Grass Systems: Vetiver grass (Chrysopogon zizanioides) forms dense hedgerows with deep roots (up to 3m) that stabilize soil and slow runoff. Planted along contours, vetiver creates living barriers that trap sediment and eventually form natural terraces. Vetiver is drought-tolerant, non-invasive, and provides fodder and mulch.

Cover Crops and Green Manures: Growing cover crops during fallow periods protects soil, adds organic matter, and fixes nitrogen (with legumes). Common species include cowpea, mung bean, and lablab. Cover crops are particularly valuable in agricultural systems, improving soil while preventing erosion.

6.3 Vegetation Restoration Strategies

Restoring vegetation cover addresses multiple degradation aspects simultaneously: erosion control, soil improvement, carbon sequestration, biodiversity conservation, and livelihood support through products and services.

Assisted Natural Regeneration (ANR)

ANR accelerates natural forest and woodland recovery by protecting and managing naturally regenerating trees and shrubs. Rather than planting, ANR selectively protects desired species, removes competing vegetation, and applies appropriate pruning. ANR costs 10-30% of conventional tree planting while often achieving faster growth through established root systems. Success requires presence of viable root stocks, seeds, or seedlings—conditions common in many degraded areas.

Implementation involves:

  • Identifying and marking desired naturally regenerating stems
  • Clearing competing vegetation around selected stems
  • Protecting from fire and grazing during establishment
  • Pruning to promote straight growth and reduce branching
  • Thinning to appropriate density as regeneration progresses

Tree Planting Programs

Where natural regeneration is insufficient, direct tree planting restores woody vegetation. Success requires careful attention to:

Species Selection: Native species adapted to local conditions show highest survival and provide appropriate ecosystem services. Fast-growing species provide quick ground cover, while slower-growing species offer long-term structure. Multi-species mixtures support biodiversity and resilience.

Planting Timing and Methods: Planting at the start of the rainy season maximizes early growth. Proper planting technique (adequate hole size, correct depth, soil contact) significantly affects survival. In harsh conditions, enriched planting pits with compost or water-retention materials improve establishment.

Nursery Management: Quality seedlings produced in well-managed nurseries show 70-90% survival compared to 20-40% for poor-quality seedlings. Container-grown seedlings with well-developed root systems transplant more successfully than bare-root seedlings in drylands.

Grass Seeding and Establishment

Herbaceous cover establishes faster than trees, providing rapid erosion control and soil protection. Native perennial grasses offer the best combination of establishment, persistence, and ecological appropriateness. Seeding requires:

  • Seed collection from local populations to ensure adaptation
  • Soil preparation (light scarification, not deep tillage which increases erosion risk)
  • Timing to coincide with reliable rainfall
  • Appropriate seeding rates (typically 5-15 kg/hectare depending on species)
  • Protection from grazing for 1-2 growing seasons until establishment

6.4 Water Management Interventions

Water scarcity limits dryland productivity and resilience. Water harvesting, conservation, and efficient use interventions increase water availability for vegetation and agriculture.

Zai Pits: These small planting pits (20-40cm diameter, 10-20cm deep) are dug in degraded, crusted soils and filled with organic matter. Zai concentrate rainfall around plants, break soil crusts, and improve infiltration. Originating in Burkina Faso, zai have restored hundreds of thousands of hectares of abandoned farmland across the Sahel. While labor-intensive (requiring 250-400 person-hours per hectare), zai dramatically improve crop yields on degraded land.

Irrigation Efficiency

Where irrigation exists, improving efficiency reduces water consumption while maintaining or increasing productivity. Drip irrigation reduces water use by 30-70% compared to flood irrigation while improving yields through optimal water delivery. Drip systems cost $1,000-$3,000 per hectare but pay back within 3-5 years through water savings and increased production.

6.5 Sustainable Land Management Practices

Long-term land health requires transforming agricultural and pastoral practices toward sustainable systems that maintain productivity while preventing degradation.

Agroforestry Systems

Agroforestry integrates trees with crops or livestock, providing multiple benefits: soil improvement, erosion control, microclimate moderation, diversified products, and carbon sequestration. Common systems include:

Alley Cropping: Rows of nitrogen-fixing trees (e.g., Leucaena, Gliricidia) alternating with crop alleys. Trees are regularly pruned, providing mulch and green manure for crops while preventing competition.

Parkland Systems: Scattered trees maintained in agricultural fields provide shade, nitrogen fixation (with legumes), fodder, fruits, and other products. The Sahelian parkland system, featuring species like Faidherbia albida (which loses leaves during the growing season, avoiding competition), demonstrates agroforestry's potential.

Silvopastoral Systems: Integrate trees with livestock grazing, providing shade, fodder, and improved forage quality while controlling erosion and sequestering carbon.

Conservation Agriculture

Conservation agriculture combines three principles:

  • Minimum Soil Disturbance: Reduced or no-till planting minimizes soil structure disruption and erosion
  • Permanent Soil Cover: Maintaining crop residues or cover crops protects soil from erosion and sun
  • Crop Diversification: Rotating or intercropping different species improves soil health and pest management

Meta-analyses show conservation agriculture increases yields by 5-15% on average while reducing soil loss by 50-90%. However, adoption requires overcoming barriers including specialized equipment needs, short-term yield reductions during transition, and competing uses for crop residues (fodder, fuel).

Holistic Grazing Management

Sustainable livestock management maintains rangeland health while supporting pastoral livelihoods. Key strategies include:

Rotational Grazing: Dividing rangeland into paddocks grazed sequentially provides rest periods for vegetation recovery. Appropriate rotation cycles (typically 30-90 days rest between grazing) prevent overgrazing while maintaining productivity.

Stocking Rate Control: Matching livestock numbers to rangeland carrying capacity prevents degradation. Carrying capacity varies with rainfall, ranging from 1 animal per 50+ hectares in arid zones to 1 per 5-10 hectares in semi-arid regions.

Strategic Supplementation: Providing supplementary feed during dry seasons reduces rangeland pressure while maintaining livestock condition. Fodder production (improved pastures, fodder trees, crop residues) provides sustainable feed sources.

6.6 Implementation and Adaptive Management

Successful intervention requires more than technical excellence—it demands careful implementation, community engagement, and adaptive management that responds to experience and changing conditions.

Phased Implementation

Large-scale restoration proceeds in phases, typically:

  • Year 1: Site preparation, nursery establishment, initial structural interventions (check dams, bunds), pilot planting
  • Years 2-3: Main planting/seeding campaigns, expanding structural interventions, establishing grazing management
  • Years 3-5: Maintenance, gap filling, adaptive adjustments based on monitoring results
  • Years 5+: Monitoring and maintenance, handover to community management

Community Engagement

Community participation transforms intervention sustainability. Effective engagement includes:

  • Participatory planning ensuring interventions align with community needs and priorities
  • Clear benefit-sharing arrangements addressing tenure, product rights, and cost recovery
  • Capacity building through training in implementation and maintenance techniques
  • Local employment in intervention activities providing immediate livelihood benefits
  • Formal management agreements clarifying long-term responsibilities and rights

🌱 Key Takeaways

  • Intervention prioritization considers degradation severity, restoration potential, strategic value, population density, cost-effectiveness, and community engagement. The standard provides a comprehensive intervention menu organized by objectives, with typical costs ranging from $100-$3,000 per hectare depending on intensity and technique.
  • Soil conservation through physical structures (contour bunding, terracing, check dams) and biological measures (vetiver grass, cover crops) forms the foundation of most restoration efforts. Check dams are particularly cost-effective at $500-$3,000 each while controlling gully erosion and promoting groundwater recharge.
  • Vegetation restoration employs assisted natural regeneration (ANR), tree planting, and grass seeding depending on site conditions and regeneration potential. ANR costs 10-30% of conventional planting while often achieving faster growth through established root systems, making it highly cost-effective where applicable.
  • Water management interventions including rainwater harvesting structures, zai pits, and drip irrigation increase water availability and use efficiency. Zai pits have restored hundreds of thousands of hectares in the Sahel, while drip irrigation reduces water use by 30-70% with 3-5 year payback periods.
  • Sustainable land management through agroforestry, conservation agriculture, and holistic grazing management maintains long-term productivity while preventing future degradation. Conservation agriculture increases yields 5-15% while reducing soil loss 50-90%, though adoption faces barriers including equipment needs and competing residue uses.
