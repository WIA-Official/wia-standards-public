Chapter 02: Measuring UHI: LST, Air Temp, Heat Index

2.1 Land Surface Temperature (LST)

LST is measured by satellite thermal infrared (TIR) sensors. Landsat 8/9 Band 10 (~10.9 µm wavelength) provides 30-meter resolution thermal imagery; Sentinel-3 SLSTR provides 1-km resolution with daily revisit; ECOSTRESS (on the ISS) provides 70-meter with diurnal variation. LST measures the temperature of the ground surface, not air temperature; the difference matters for UHI assessment. The standard\x27s surface_temperature_observation envelope preserves the data source so consumers can apply appropriate atmospheric correction.

2.2 Ambient air temperature

Air temperature is measured at standard 2-meter height by weather stations. The WMO standard requires shielded thermometers in well-ventilated enclosures; most operational networks (NOAA US Climate Reference Network, EU Copernicus, KMA stations in Korea) follow this standard. Citizen-science networks (Weather Underground, Netatmo) follow varying standards; their data quality flags inform the standard\x27s data_quality field.

2.3 Heat index and apparent temperature

Heat index combines air temperature and humidity to estimate perceived temperature. NOAA\x27s formula uses Steadman\x27s 1979 model; EU Copernicus uses the Universal Thermal Climate Index (UTCI); WMO recommends UTCI for cross-country comparison. The standard\x27s heat_health_event envelope carries max_heat_index_c with the formula identifier so consumers can normalise across jurisdictions.

2.4 Tree canopy and impervious surface

Tree canopy coverage and impervious surface percentage are the two dominant UHI predictors at the neighbourhood scale. USGS NLCD (National Land Cover Database) provides US 30-meter resolution; EU Corine Land Cover provides European coverage; vendor satellite imagery (Planet Labs, Maxar) provides higher resolution at cost. The standard\x27s canopy_assessment envelope carries tree_canopy_pct and impervious_surface_pct with the data source for provenance.

2.5 UHI intensity

UHI intensity is the temperature difference between an urban location and a paired rural reference. The pairing matters: WMO recommends matching land cover type, elevation, and prevailing wind. The standard\x27s surface_temperature_observation envelope optionally carries a paired rural reference observation_id so downstream consumers can compute intensity without ambiguity.

2.6 Diurnal vs nocturnal UHI

UHI exhibits both daytime and nocturnal modes with different drivers. Nocturnal UHI is driven by stored heat in dark surfaces re-emitting after sunset; daytime UHI is driven by shading and evapotranspiration deficits. The standard\x27s observations preserve captured_at timestamps for downstream temporal analysis.

Implementer Notes

Three operational concerns recur in UHI infrastructure and are baked into the standard. First, satellite revisit cadence: Landsat revisits any spot every 16 days; Sentinel-3 daily but 1-km resolution; ECOSTRESS sub-daily but ISS orbital coverage variable. Hosts MUST document data-source revisit characteristics in the discovery document. Second, vulnerable population data: aggregate-only by Phase 1 §4.6 design; downstream consumers requesting individual identification MUST be refused. Third, heat advisory latency: the gap between NWS issuance and public dissemination is critical; the standard\x27s heat_health_event stream targets < 5 minute latency from issuance to subscriber receipt.

Korea-Specific Application Note

Korea's 2018 and 2024 heat waves drove formal heat-emergency response across municipal and national agencies. KMA (Korea Meteorological Administration) issues heat advisories aligned with NOAA NWS's structure but adapted for Korean climate; the Ministry of the Interior and Safety coordinates muders-shelter (무더위 쉼터) activation across cities. The standard's heat_health_event envelope maps cleanly onto KMA advisories via the data_source field, and the Korean municipal climate offices in Seoul, Daejeon, Daegu, and Gwangju have published municipal climate adaptation plans that align with the standard's mitigation_intervention vocabulary.

Vulnerable-Population-First Design

Every choice in this standard is filtered through: does this protect the people most at risk during heat events while supporting effective outreach, or does it build identification infrastructure that outlasts the emergency? When the two conflict, vulnerable-population data sovereignty wins. That is why the standard mandates aggregate-only vulnerable_population_area envelopes, refuses to allow individual identification even in emergency mode, and requires emergency-management workflows to operate on neighbourhood counts rather than personal lists.
