Chapter 01: Introduction & The Urban Heat Hazard

1.1 Why this book exists

WIA Urban Heat Island is the open standard for urban heat island monitoring and mitigation tracking. This book is the practitioner introduction.

1.2 The urban heat hazard

Heat waves are the deadliest weather hazard in many countries. The 2003 European heat wave killed an estimated 70,000 people; the 2022 European heat wave killed an estimated 60,000+; the Pacific Northwest 2021 heat dome killed hundreds despite occurring in a famously temperate region. Cities amplify the hazard: dark surfaces absorb solar radiation, reduced vegetation eliminates cooling evapotranspiration, waste heat from air conditioning + vehicles + industrial activity adds to the load. The Urban Heat Island (UHI) effect can push city temperatures 5-10°C above surrounding rural areas during heat waves.

1.3 Why open standards matter for UHI

UHI mitigation is a per-city policy decision but the data flows naturally cross jurisdictional boundaries: satellite thermal imagery covers metropolitan regions that span multiple cities and counties; vulnerable populations move between cities for work; heat advisories from national weather services apply across municipal lines; mitigation interventions in one city benefit (or shift heat to) neighbouring cities. WIA Urban Heat Island provides an open wire format so cross-city, cross-county, cross-state coordination is possible without per-jurisdiction bilateral agreements.

1.4 The 4-Phase architecture

Phase 1 (Data Format) defines JSON envelopes for surface temperature observations, canopy assessments, mitigation interventions, heat-health events, and vulnerable-population alert records. Phase 2 (API Interface) specifies the HTTP surface. Phase 3 (Federation Protocol) handles cross-jurisdiction data flow. Phase 4 (Integration) bridges to Landsat / Sentinel-3 thermal IR products, NOAA NWS heat advisories, EU Copernicus climate, USGS NLCD landcover, and the WIA family.

1.5 Vulnerable population protection as a baked-in primitive

Earlier UHI standards have treated vulnerable population identification (elderly, low-income, isolated, chronic illness) as a separate emergency-management concern. WIA Urban Heat Island treats it as a Phase 3 primitive: heat-health event envelopes carry a vulnerable_population_alert_count, and the audience matrix gives emergency-management agencies real-time visibility while keeping individual identities protected via aggregate-only counts.

Implementer Notes

Three operational concerns recur in UHI infrastructure and are baked into the standard. First, satellite revisit cadence: Landsat revisits any spot every 16 days; Sentinel-3 daily but 1-km resolution; ECOSTRESS sub-daily but ISS orbital coverage variable. Hosts MUST document data-source revisit characteristics in the discovery document. Second, vulnerable population data: aggregate-only by Phase 1 §4.6 design; downstream consumers requesting individual identification MUST be refused. Third, heat advisory latency: the gap between NWS issuance and public dissemination is critical; the standard\x27s heat_health_event stream targets < 5 minute latency from issuance to subscriber receipt.

Korea-Specific Application Note

Korea's 2018 and 2024 heat waves drove formal heat-emergency response across municipal and national agencies. KMA (Korea Meteorological Administration) issues heat advisories aligned with NOAA NWS's structure but adapted for Korean climate; the Ministry of the Interior and Safety coordinates muders-shelter (무더위 쉼터) activation across cities. The standard's heat_health_event envelope maps cleanly onto KMA advisories via the data_source field, and the Korean municipal climate offices in Seoul, Daejeon, Daegu, and Gwangju have published municipal climate adaptation plans that align with the standard's mitigation_intervention vocabulary.

Vulnerable-Population-First Design

Every choice in this standard is filtered through: does this protect the people most at risk during heat events while supporting effective outreach, or does it build identification infrastructure that outlasts the emergency? When the two conflict, vulnerable-population data sovereignty wins. That is why the standard mandates aggregate-only vulnerable_population_area envelopes, refuses to allow individual identification even in emergency mode, and requires emergency-management workflows to operate on neighbourhood counts rather than personal lists.
