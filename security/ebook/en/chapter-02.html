Chapter Overview

Cybersecurity is the practice of protecting systems, networks, and programs from digital attacks. These cyberattacks are usually aimed at accessing, changing, or destroying sensitive information; extorting money from users; or interrupting normal business processes. This chapter provides a comprehensive foundation in cybersecurity principles, threat landscapes, and defense mechanisms.

Understanding cybersecurity fundamentals is essential for anyone involved in information technology, security operations, or organizational leadership. The concepts covered here form the basis for more advanced topics in subsequent chapters.

The CIA Triad

The CIA Triad is a foundational model in information security that represents the three core objectives of any security program: Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability. These principles guide the development and implementation of security controls across all aspects of an organization.

Confidentiality

Confidentiality ensures that sensitive information is accessed only by authorized individuals. This principle prevents unauthorized disclosure of data and protects privacy. Key mechanisms for maintaining confidentiality include:

  • Encryption: Converting data into an unreadable format that can only be decrypted with the proper key.
  • Access Controls: Implementing authentication and authorization mechanisms to restrict access.
  • Data Classification: Categorizing data based on sensitivity levels to apply appropriate protections.
  • Physical Security: Protecting hardware and storage media from unauthorized physical access.
  • Network Segmentation: Isolating sensitive systems and data from general network traffic.

Integrity

Integrity ensures that data remains accurate, complete, and unaltered throughout its lifecycle. This principle protects against unauthorized modification, whether intentional or accidental:

  • Hash Functions: Creating digital fingerprints to detect any changes to data.
  • Digital Signatures: Verifying the authenticity and integrity of digital communications.
  • Version Control: Tracking changes and maintaining historical records of data modifications.
  • Input Validation: Ensuring data entered into systems meets expected formats and constraints.
  • Audit Trails: Logging all changes to sensitive data for accountability and forensics.

Availability

Availability ensures that systems, applications, and data are accessible to authorized users when needed. This principle focuses on maintaining operational continuity:

  • Redundancy: Implementing backup systems and failover mechanisms.
  • Load Balancing: Distributing traffic across multiple servers to prevent overload.
  • Disaster Recovery: Planning and testing recovery procedures for various failure scenarios.
  • DDoS Protection: Implementing controls to mitigate distributed denial-of-service attacks.
  • Maintenance Windows: Scheduling updates and maintenance to minimize service disruption.

Extended CIA Model

Modern security frameworks often extend the CIA Triad to include additional principles such as Authenticity (verifying identity), Non-repudiation (preventing denial of actions), and Accountability (tracking actions to individuals).

Common Attack Types

🎣

Phishing

Social engineering attacks that trick users into revealing sensitive information or installing malware through deceptive emails, websites, or messages.

🔒

Ransomware

Malware that encrypts victim's files and demands payment for decryption keys. Modern variants also exfiltrate data for double extortion.

🌊

DDoS Attacks

Distributed Denial of Service attacks overwhelm systems with traffic, making them unavailable to legitimate users.

💉

SQL Injection

Exploiting vulnerabilities in database-driven applications to execute unauthorized SQL commands, potentially exposing or modifying data.

👤

Man-in-the-Middle

Intercepting communications between two parties to eavesdrop or modify data in transit without detection.

🔑

Credential Stuffing

Using stolen username/password combinations from data breaches to gain unauthorized access to other accounts.

📦

Supply Chain Attacks

Compromising software supply chains to distribute malware through trusted update mechanisms and dependencies.

⚡

Zero-Day Exploits

Attacks targeting previously unknown vulnerabilities before patches are available, giving defenders zero days to prepare.

The Cyber Attack Lifecycle

Understanding how attacks unfold helps defenders anticipate and disrupt adversary operations. The Cyber Kill Chain, developed by Lockheed Martin, and the MITRE ATT&CK framework provide models for understanding attack progression.

Cyber Kill Chain Phases

// Cyber Kill Chain - Attack Progression Model

1. RECONNAISSANCE
   "Research, identification, and selection of targets"
   - Passive: OSINT, social media, public records
   - Active: Port scanning, vulnerability scanning
   
2. WEAPONIZATION
   "Coupling exploit with backdoor into deliverable payload"
   - Creating malware
   - Preparing exploit kits
   - Crafting phishing content
   
3. DELIVERY
   "Transmission of weapon to targeted environment"
   - Email attachments
   - Malicious websites
   - USB drops
   - Watering hole attacks
   
4. EXPLOITATION
   "Triggering exploit to gain code execution"
   - Exploiting software vulnerabilities
   - User execution of malware
   - Drive-by downloads
   
5. INSTALLATION
   "Installing backdoor or implant on victim system"
   - Malware installation
   - Rootkit deployment
   - Persistence mechanisms
   
6. COMMAND & CONTROL (C2)
   "Establishing channel for remote control"
   - Callback to attacker infrastructure
   - Encrypted communications
   - Domain generation algorithms
   
7. ACTIONS ON OBJECTIVES
   "Achieving original goals of the attack"
   - Data exfiltration
   - Data destruction
   - Lateral movement
   - Privilege escalation

Defense Strategy

The goal of defenders is to break the kill chain at the earliest possible stage. Detection and response capabilities at each phase provide multiple opportunities to stop an attack before it achieves its objectives.

Defense in Depth

Defense in depth is a security strategy that employs multiple layers of security controls throughout an IT system. If one layer fails, another layer is in place to prevent or detect the attack. This approach provides redundancy and comprehensive protection.

Security Layers

1

Physical Security

Protecting physical access to facilities, hardware, and storage media through locks, guards, surveillance, and environmental controls.

2

Perimeter Security

Network firewalls, DMZ architecture, intrusion detection/prevention systems, and web application firewalls to protect the network boundary.

3

Network Security

Internal firewalls, network segmentation, VLANs, network access control, and encrypted communications within the network.

4

Endpoint Security

Antimalware, endpoint detection and response (EDR), host-based firewalls, device encryption, and patch management.

5

Application Security

Secure coding practices, input validation, authentication, authorization, and application-level encryption.

6

Data Security

Encryption at rest and in transit, data loss prevention, data classification, and access controls for sensitive information.

7

User Security

Security awareness training, phishing simulations, acceptable use policies, and identity management.

Encryption Fundamentals

Encryption is the process of converting plaintext into ciphertext using an algorithm and a key, making data unreadable without the proper decryption key. It is fundamental to maintaining confidentiality and integrity in modern systems.

Types of Encryption

Symmetric Encryption

Uses the same key for both encryption and decryption. Fast and efficient for large data volumes, but requires secure key distribution.

  • AES (Advanced Encryption Standard): The gold standard for symmetric encryption, supporting 128, 192, and 256-bit keys.
  • ChaCha20: A modern stream cipher, often used with Poly1305 for authenticated encryption.
  • 3DES: Triple DES, legacy algorithm being phased out in favor of AES.

Asymmetric Encryption

Uses a pair of keys: a public key for encryption and a private key for decryption. Enables secure key exchange and digital signatures but is computationally intensive.

  • RSA: Widely used for secure data transmission, typically with 2048 or 4096-bit keys.
  • Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC): Provides equivalent security with smaller key sizes, improving performance.
  • Diffie-Hellman: Key exchange protocol enabling secure key agreement over insecure channels.
# Encryption Best Practices

Data at Rest
├── Full disk encryption (BitLocker, FileVault, LUKS)
├── Database encryption (TDE)
├── File-level encryption
└── Key management system (HSM recommended)

Data in Transit
├── TLS 1.3 for web traffic
├── IPSec for VPN tunnels
├── SSH for remote administration
└── Certificate management

Data in Use
├── Secure enclaves (Intel SGX, ARM TrustZone)
├── Homomorphic encryption (emerging)
├── Confidential computing
└── Memory encryption

Key Management
├── Secure key generation (CSPRNG)
├── Key rotation policies
├── Key escrow procedures
├── Hardware Security Modules (HSM)
└── Key lifecycle management

Encryption Standards in WIA-SEC-001

WIA-SEC-001 mandates AES-256 for symmetric encryption, RSA-2048 or ECC P-256 minimum for asymmetric operations, and SHA-256 or higher for hashing. TLS 1.2 is the minimum for communications, with TLS 1.3 recommended.

Security Control Categories

Security controls are safeguards or countermeasures designed to protect the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of systems and data. Controls are categorized by their function and implementation approach.

By Function

Control Type Purpose Examples
Preventive Stop security incidents before they occur Firewalls, access controls, encryption, training
Detective Identify security incidents when they occur IDS, SIEM, audit logs, monitoring
Corrective Minimize impact and restore systems after incidents Incident response, backup restoration, patching
Deterrent Discourage potential attackers Warning banners, security cameras, policies
Compensating Provide alternative protection when primary controls are insufficient Enhanced monitoring when patching is delayed
Recovery Restore capabilities after an incident Disaster recovery, business continuity plans

By Implementation

  • Administrative Controls: Policies, procedures, guidelines, and training that govern how an organization manages security.
  • Technical Controls: Hardware and software mechanisms that protect systems and data (firewalls, encryption, authentication).
  • Physical Controls: Tangible measures that protect facilities and equipment (locks, guards, badges, environmental controls).

Chapter Summary

This chapter established the fundamental concepts of cybersecurity that underpin all security programs. Key takeaways include:

  • The CIA Triad (Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability) forms the foundation of security objectives.
  • Understanding threat actors and their motivations helps predict and prepare for attacks.
  • Common attack types include phishing, ransomware, DDoS, injection attacks, and supply chain compromises.
  • The Cyber Kill Chain provides a model for understanding attack progression and defense opportunities.
  • Defense in depth employs multiple security layers to provide comprehensive protection.
  • Encryption is essential for protecting data at rest, in transit, and in use.
  • Security controls are categorized by function (preventive, detective, corrective) and implementation (administrative, technical, physical).
  • Core security operations include vulnerability management, patch management, log management, and change management.
