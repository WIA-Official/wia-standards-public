🌱 Chapter 4: Cultivation Techniques

This chapter covers the practical techniques for cultivating seaweed from spore collection through deployment and maintenance.

4.1 Spore Collection and Seeding

Most seaweed farming begins with collecting reproductive material (spores or gametophytes) from mature parent plants.

Kelp Spore Collection

  • Timing: Collect sori (spore-producing structures) from mature kelp blades when dark brown and releasing spores (typically late summer/fall)
  • Method: Excise small sections of fertile blade, rinse in clean seawater, place in spore collection vessel
  • Spore Release: Occurs within hours under proper light and temperature conditions
  • Settlement: Spores settle onto substrate (ropes, nets, or seed stock) within 24-48 hours

Nori Spore Collection

  • Conchospore Method: Harvest mature nori with reproductive structures, place with oyster shells in tanks
  • Shell Cultivation: Conchospores penetrate shells and develop into conchocelis phase (several months)
  • Conchospore Release: When environmental conditions trigger release, shells placed in nets for spore settlement

4.2 Nursery Management

Indoor Nursery Phase

  • Duration: 4-8 weeks for kelp, varies by species
  • Environment: Controlled temperature (10-15°C for kelp), moderate light (50-100 μmol/m²/s), flowing seawater
  • Seeding Density: Balance between competition and robust seedling development
  • Nutrients: Seawater may require enrichment (nitrogen and phosphorus) for optimal growth

Outdoor Nursery Phase

  • Hardening: Gradual acclimation to ocean conditions before final deployment
  • Location: Protected nearshore site with good water quality
  • Duration: 2-4 weeks
  • Monitoring: Check growth daily, assess readiness for deployment

4.3 Deployment

Longline Deployment

  • Timing: When seedlings reach 1-3 cm and water temperature appropriate (typically late fall for winter-growing kelp)
  • Method: Wind seeded line onto larger backbone rope or attach directly
  • Spacing: 50 cm - 1 meter between cultivation lines on backbone
  • Depth: Typically 0.5-2 meters below surface, adjusted for light and wave conditions

Net Deployment (Nori)

  • Net preparation: Stretch nets on frames for spore settlement in hatchery
  • Installation: Attach seeded nets to raft or floating frame systems
  • Positioning: Intertidal or shallow subtidal, adjusted by tidal cycle

4.4 Seasonal Management

Winter Growing Season (Cold-Water Species)

October-November: Deploy seeded lines as water temperature drops below 15°C

December-February: Peak growth period; monitor weekly for health and growth rate

March-April: Harvest window opens; optimal quality before reproductive maturity

May-June: Complete harvest before warm water temperatures cause deterioration

Summer Growing Season (Warm-Water Species)

April-May: Deploy tropical species seedlings as water warms

June-August: Rapid growth in warm, sunny conditions

September-October: Harvest mature biomass

4.5 Maintenance and Monitoring

Regular Inspections

  • Frequency: Weekly during growing season, bi-weekly during slow growth
  • Infrastructure check: Lines, buoys, anchors for wear or damage
  • Crop health: Color, texture, epiphyte growth, disease signs
  • Wildlife interactions: Grazing by urchins, fish, or birds

Growth Measurements

  • Length: Measure 10-20 individuals per line to track growth rate
  • Biomass: Weigh samples to estimate total farm biomass
  • Density: Count individuals per meter to calculate stocking density

Problem Management

  • Epiphytes: Remove heavily fouled sections; adjust deployment timing to avoid peak fouling periods
  • Grazers: Use exclusion devices or harvest earlier to avoid losses
  • Disease: Remove infected areas immediately; improve water flow; consider resistant varieties

4.6 Advanced Techniques

Selective Breeding

  • Choose fastest-growing, most robust individuals for spore collection
  • Multi-generation selection for desired traits (growth rate, disease resistance, quality)
  • Maintain genetic diversity to avoid inbreeding depression

Clonal Propagation

  • Vegetative reproduction from blade fragments or holdfasts
  • Maintains superior genotypes
  • Useful for species difficult to propagate from spores

Polyculture

  • Grow multiple species on same infrastructure for diversification
  • Sequential cropping: winter kelp followed by summer sea lettuce
  • Spatial arrangement: different species at different depths or zones

Success Tips

  • Start with certified seed stock from reputable hatcheries until you develop in-house capacity
  • Keep detailed records of deployment dates, growth rates, and harvest yields to optimize future cycles
  • Be flexible with deployment and harvest timing based on actual environmental conditions, not just calendar dates
  • Invest time in nursery management - healthy seedlings are the foundation of a successful crop
