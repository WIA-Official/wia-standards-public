The Carbon Cycle

CO₂ doesn't simply accumulate - it participates in the global carbon cycle:

  • Oceans absorb ~25% of human CO₂ emissions (causing acidification)
  • Land ecosystems absorb ~30% (forests, soils)
  • ~45% remains in atmosphere (the "airborne fraction")
  • This distribution varies year-to-year based on climate variability

Methane's Dual Nature

Methane presents both a challenge and an opportunity:

  • Challenge: 28x more potent than CO₂ over 100 years
  • Opportunity: Short atmospheric lifetime means rapid climate benefits from reduction
  • Reducing methane emissions is one of the fastest ways to slow near-term warming

Methane Super-Emitters

Satellite observations have revealed "super-emitter" events - individual sources emitting tons of methane per hour:

  • Oil & gas leaks: Faulty equipment, venting, flaring
  • Coal mine vents: Uncontrolled releases during extraction
  • Landfill hotspots: Poor waste management
  • These super-emitters represent low-hanging fruit for mitigation

The Nitrogen Paradox

Nitrogen fertilizers have enabled global food security, feeding billions. Yet their overuse creates environmental problems:

  • N₂O emissions from agriculture have increased 30% since 1980
  • N₂O is also an ozone-depleting substance
  • Only 50% of applied nitrogen is absorbed by crops
  • Better nitrogen use efficiency could reduce emissions 20-40%

2.4 Fluorinated Gases (F-gases) - Industrial Powerhouses

F-gases are entirely synthetic compounds with no natural sources. While present in tiny concentrations, their extreme warming potentials make them significant:

The Kigali Amendment

In 2016, the Montreal Protocol was amended to phase down HFCs:

  • Developed countries: 85% reduction by 2036 (baseline 2011-2013)
  • Developing countries: 80-85% reduction by 2045
  • Expected to prevent 0.4°C of warming by 2100
  • Transition to low-GWP alternatives (HFOs, natural refrigerants)

SF₆ - The Most Potent GHG

Lifetime3,200 years
Primary UseElectrical switchgear insulation

SF₆: Essential but Problematic

SF₆ is critical for high-voltage electrical equipment, but:

  • 1 kg SF₆ = warming impact of 23.5 tonnes CO₂
  • Electric grid expansion is increasing SF₆ use
  • Leakage rates can be 10-15% over equipment lifetime
  • Alternatives exist but are more expensive (SF₆-free switchgear)

2.7 Temporal Trends in Emissions

Historical Emission Growth

  • 1990-2000: +1.0% annual growth (globalization begins)
  • 2000-2010: +2.0% annual growth (China's rapid development)
  • 2010-2019: +1.2% annual growth (renewable energy uptake)
  • 2020: -5.4% (COVID-19 pandemic)
  • 2021: +5.9% (post-pandemic rebound)
  • 2022-2025: +0.5% (plateau beginning?)

2.9 Monitoring Priorities by Gas

WIA-ENE-051 Gas-Specific Monitoring Recommendations

CO₂:

  • High-precision background monitoring (±0.1 ppm)
  • Continuous emission monitoring systems (CEMS) for large point sources
  • Satellite column measurements for regional budgets

CH₄:

  • Super-emitter detection via satellite (TROPOMI, GHGSat)
  • Leak detection and repair (LDAR) programs for oil & gas
  • Isotopic analysis to distinguish sources (fossil vs. biogenic)

N₂O:

  • Agricultural soil flux chambers for direct measurement
  • Industrial stack monitoring for adipic/nitric acid plants
  • Regional atmospheric inversions

F-gases:

  • Equipment-level leak detection
  • Production and import/export tracking
  • Atmospheric monitoring at select remote stations

2.10 Future Source Trends

Emerging Emission Concerns

  • Permafrost thaw: Potential release of 50-100 GtC as CO₂ and CH₄
  • Arctic methane: Subsea clathrate destabilization
  • Tropical wetlands: Enhanced CH₄ under warming
  • Wildfire emissions: Increasing frequency and intensity
  • Data centers: Growing energy demand and potential refrigerant leaks

Key Takeaways

  • CO₂ accounts for ~65% of anthropogenic radiative forcing, primarily from fossil fuels
  • CH₄ is 28x more potent than CO₂ and offers rapid climate benefits if reduced
  • N₂O from agriculture is increasing due to fertilizer use
  • F-gases have extremely high GWPs but are being phased down under Kigali Amendment
  • Different sectors emit distinct gas portfolios requiring tailored monitoring
  • Regional emission patterns reflect development stage and energy systems
  • Emission intensity is decreasing, but absolute emissions remain too high
  • Climate feedbacks may create new natural emission sources
