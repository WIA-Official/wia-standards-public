3.1 Overview of Measurement Approaches

Greenhouse gas monitoring relies on three complementary measurement approaches, each with distinct advantages and limitations:

Approach Spatial Scale Temporal Resolution Precision Coverage
In-situ (ground) Point measurements Seconds to minutes Very high (±0.1 ppm CO₂) Sparse spatial
Remote sensing Column/profile Daily revisit Moderate (±0.5 ppm CO₂) Global
Inverse modeling Regional to global Weekly to monthly Moderate (depends on data) Complete

Complementary Nature

The ideal GHG monitoring system combines all three approaches:

  • Ground stations provide high-precision validation data
  • Satellites offer global spatial coverage
  • Models integrate observations to estimate fluxes

3.2 In-Situ Analyzers

Ground-based instruments that continuously measure GHG concentrations at a specific location.

Non-Dispersive Infrared (NDIR) Analyzers

NDIR Technology

Principle: CO₂ and other GHGs absorb specific infrared wavelengths

How it works:

  1. Infrared light source emits broad spectrum
  2. Light passes through sample cell containing air
  3. CO₂ molecules absorb light at 4.26 μm
  4. Detector measures transmitted light intensity
  5. Absorption is proportional to concentration (Beer-Lambert Law)

Advantages: Robust, low maintenance, field-deployable

Limitations: Moderate precision (±0.5 ppm), single gas

Cost: $3,000 - $10,000

Cavity Ring-Down Spectroscopy (CRDS)

CRDS Technology

Principle: Measures how quickly light decays in an optical cavity

How it works:

  1. Laser pulse injected into high-finesse optical cavity (mirrors >99.995% reflective)
  2. Light bounces back and forth thousands of times (~10 km effective path)
  3. GHG molecules absorb specific wavelengths, accelerating decay
  4. Measure "ring-down time" - how fast intensity drops
  5. Faster decay = higher concentration

Advantages: Very high precision (±0.1 ppm CO₂, ±1 ppb CH₄), multi-gas, drift-free

Limitations: Expensive, requires stable environment

Cost: $50,000 - $100,000

Example: Picarro G2401 (CO₂, CH₄, CO, H₂O)

Off-Axis Integrated Cavity Output Spectroscopy (OA-ICOS)

OA-ICOS Technology

Principle: Similar to CRDS but measures continuous wave absorption

Key difference: Laser enters cavity at slight angle, creating dense pattern of reflections

Advantages: High precision, lower cost than CRDS, good stability

Limitations: Slightly lower precision than CRDS

Cost: $30,000 - $70,000

Example: Los Gatos Research analyzers

Gas Chromatography (GC)

GC Technology

Principle: Separates gas mixture components, then detects individually

How it works:

  1. Air sample injected into carrier gas stream
  2. Mixture flows through separation column
  3. Different gases travel at different rates
  4. Detector identifies gases as they exit (retention time)
  5. Common detectors: FID (for CH₄), ECD (for N₂O, SF₆)

Advantages: Can measure many trace gases, very sensitive for halocarbons

Limitations: Not continuous (sample-based), requires calibration

Applications: F-gases, N₂O, isotope-specific measurements

3.3 Flask Sampling Networks

Air samples collected in flasks and analyzed in central laboratories - the gold standard for precision:

Flask Sampling Process

  1. Collection: Air drawn into evacuated glass flasks (typically paired)
  2. Pressurization: Sample compressed to 2-3 atmospheres
  3. Sealing: Valves closed to preserve sample
  4. Shipping: Flasks sent to central laboratory
  5. Analysis: Multi-instrument analysis (GC, CRDS, mass spec)
  6. Quality control: Comparison with reference standards

3.4 Tall Tower Networks

Towers extending 100-300+ meters measure vertical GHG profiles, capturing regional flux signals:

Tall Tower Advantages

  • Vertical sampling: Multiple inlets at different heights reveal atmospheric mixing
  • Regional signal: Upper levels sample air from larger areas (~100 km)
  • Boundary layer: Daytime sampling captures well-mixed regional emissions
  • Inverse modeling: Ideal data for estimating regional fluxes

Example Sites:

  • WLEF (Wisconsin, USA): 447m tower, 11, 30, 76, 122, 244, 396m inlets
  • Cabauw (Netherlands): 213m tower, European flux hotspot
  • Zotino (Siberia): 304m tower, vast boreal forest

3.5 Eddy Covariance Flux Measurements

Direct measurement of GHG exchange between ecosystems and atmosphere:

Eddy Covariance Principle

Concept: Measure vertical wind velocity and GHG concentration at high frequency (10-20 Hz)

Flux calculation:

Flux = ρ × cov(w', c')
where:
  ρ = air density
  w' = deviation of vertical wind from mean
  c' = deviation of GHG concentration from mean
  cov = covariance (correlation between w' and c')

Physical meaning: Updrafts carrying high CO₂ = net emission; updrafts with low CO₂ = net uptake

Requirements:

  • Fast-response analyzer (10-20 Hz)
  • 3D sonic anemometer for wind
  • Flat, homogeneous terrain (ideally)
  • Continuous operation

Applications:

  • Forest carbon uptake
  • Agricultural emissions (CH₄, N₂O)
  • Urban flux measurements
  • Wetland methane emissions

3.6 Ground-Based Remote Sensing (FTIR)

Fourier Transform Infrared Spectrometers measure total atmospheric column concentrations:

TCCON - Total Carbon Column Observing Network

Instrument: High-resolution FTIR spectrometer

Measurement: Solar absorption spectra (near-infrared)

Output: Column-averaged dry-air mole fractions (XCO₂, XCH₄, etc.)

How it works:

  1. Tracks the sun throughout the day
  2. Records absorption spectrum as sunlight passes through atmosphere
  3. Each gas has unique spectral fingerprint
  4. Retrieval algorithm inverts spectrum to concentrations
  5. Result: Total column amount divided by air column

Precision: ~0.25% (±1 ppm for XCO₂ of 410 ppm)

Sites: 28 globally (as of 2025)

Critical role: Primary validation for satellite missions

Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs)

UAV Platforms for GHG Monitoring

Small UAVs (< 25 kg):

  • Compact NDIR sensors
  • 0-1 km altitude
  • Plume mapping (landfills, oil & gas)
  • Cost-effective, flexible deployment

  • Miniaturized laser spectrometers
  • 0-3 km altitude
  • Extended flight time (hours)
  • Agricultural emissions, pipeline monitoring

Advantages:

  • Fill gap between ground and satellite
  • On-demand deployment
  • Dangerous area access
  • High spatial resolution

3.8 Isotopic Analysis

Measuring isotope ratios provides "fingerprints" to identify emission sources:

Radiocarbon (¹⁴C) for Fossil Fuel Attribution

Fossil fuels are millions of years old - all ¹⁴C has decayed (half-life: 5,730 years)

Application: Measure atmospheric ¹⁴CO₂. Decline indicates fossil fuel CO₂ addition.

Example: Urban CO₂ is 5-10% lower in ¹⁴C than background, revealing fossil fuel contribution

3.9 Continuous Emission Monitoring Systems (CEMS)

Regulatory-grade instruments for industrial stack monitoring:

CEMS Requirements

Regulatory context: EPA, EU ETS, and other programs require continuous monitoring of large emitters

Measured parameters:

  • CO₂ concentration (% or ppm)
  • Stack gas flow rate (m³/hr)
  • Temperature and pressure
  • O₂ or CO₂ for combustion efficiency

Emission calculation:

Emissions (tonnes CO₂/hr) =
    (Concentration × Flow Rate × MW_CO₂) / (R × T)

where:
    MW_CO₂ = 44 g/mol
    R = gas constant
    T = absolute temperature

Quality requirements:

  • Relative Accuracy Test Audit (RATA): < 10% error
  • Calibration drift check: quarterly
  • Linearity check: quarterly
  • Data availability: > 95%

3.11 Emerging Measurement Technologies

Next-Generation Instruments

Quantum Cascade Lasers (QCL):

  • Mid-IR lasers enabling compact, multi-gas analyzers
  • Target: sub-ppb precision in portable package
  • Applications: UAVs, low-cost networks

Photoacoustic Spectroscopy:

  • Detects sound waves from light absorption
  • Very small sample volumes
  • Miniaturization potential for IoT sensors

Dual-Comb Spectroscopy:

  • Ultra-fast, broadband spectroscopy
  • Measure many gases simultaneously
  • Open-path capability (no sampling required)

Low-Cost Electrochemical Sensors:

  • $50-200 per sensor
  • Dense urban networks
  • Moderate accuracy, needs calibration
  • Citizen science applications

3.12 Measurement Quality Assurance

WMO/GAW Quality Framework

Compatibility goals (WMO 2021):

  • CO₂: ±0.1 ppm (Northern Hemisphere), ±0.05 ppm (inter-laboratory)
  • CH₄: ±2 ppb
  • N₂O: ±0.1 ppb
  • SF₆: ±0.02 ppt

Traceability chain:

  1. Primary standards: Gravimetrically prepared at WMO Central Calibration Lab (CCL)
  2. Secondary standards: Distributed to network operators
  3. Working standards: Used for field instrument calibration
  4. Target gas: Intermediate checks

QA/QC procedures:

  • Daily automated calibrations
  • Weekly to monthly target gas checks
  • Annual comparison with traveling standards
  • Inter-comparison campaigns
  • Blind audits by independent teams

Key Takeaways

  • CRDS and OA-ICOS provide highest precision for in-situ monitoring
  • Flask sampling networks offer unmatched accuracy for long-term trends
  • Tall towers bridge point measurements and regional fluxes
  • Eddy covariance directly measures ecosystem-atmosphere exchange
  • TCCON ground-based FTIR is critical for satellite validation
  • Isotopic analysis enables source attribution (fossil vs. biogenic)
  • CEMS provides regulatory-grade industrial monitoring
  • Mobile systems enable flexible spatial surveys
  • Emerging technologies promise lower cost and wider deployment
  • Rigorous QA/QC with WMO traceability ensures data quality
