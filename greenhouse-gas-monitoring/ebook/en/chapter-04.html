4.1 Why Monitor GHGs from Space?

Satellite remote sensing revolutionized GHG monitoring by providing capabilities impossible from the ground:

Advantage Description Impact
Global Coverage Monitor entire planet, including oceans and remote areas No geographic blind spots
Consistency Single instrument, standardized methodology Comparable data across borders
Independence Objective data not controlled by individual nations Verify self-reported emissions
Systematic Sampling Regular revisit patterns Track temporal trends globally
Emission Detection Identify large point sources and plumes Find unreported emissions

Satellite vs. Ground Measurements: Complementary Strengths

Satellites excel at: Spatial coverage, source detection, regional budgets

Ground stations excel at: Precision, long-term trends, validation

Optimal approach: Combine both for comprehensive monitoring

4.2 Satellite Measurement Principles

Passive Remote Sensing

Most GHG satellites use passive spectrometers measuring reflected or emitted radiation:

How Passive Sensors Work

  1. Sunlight source: Sun illuminates Earth's surface
  2. Atmospheric path: Light passes through atmosphere (down and back up)
  3. Absorption: GHG molecules absorb specific wavelengths
  4. Reflection: Surface reflects remaining light
  5. Satellite detection: Spectrometer measures received spectrum
  6. Retrieval algorithm: Inverts spectrum to derive GHG concentration

Key concept: More absorption → less light reaching satellite → higher GHG concentration

Spectral Regions Used

Spectral Region Wavelength Gases Advantages Challenges
Near-Infrared (NIR) 1.6-2.4 μm CO₂, CH₄, H₂O Strong reflected signal, high sensitivity Requires sunlight, surface reflection
Shortwave IR (SWIR) 2.0-2.5 μm CO₂, CH₄ Strong absorption bands Requires sunlight
Thermal IR (TIR) 8-12 μm CO₂, CH₄, O₃ Day/night operation, vertical profiles Lower precision, less surface sensitivity

4.4 Satellite Data Products

Column-Averaged Dry-Air Mole Fraction (XGas)

Understanding XCO₂ and XCH₄

Definition: Total column amount of gas divided by total column of dry air

XCO₂ = (Total CO₂ column) / (Total dry air column)

Units: ppm (parts per million)

Example:
  Total CO₂ column: 3.5 × 10²² molecules/cm²
  Dry air column: 2.12 × 10²⁵ molecules/cm²
  XCO₂ = (3.5 × 10²²) / (2.12 × 10²⁵) × 10⁶ = 415 ppm

Why use XCO₂ vs. surface concentration?

  • Insensitive to surface pressure variations
  • Comparable to ground-based FTIR measurements
  • Integrates entire atmospheric column

Processing Levels

Level Description Contents Users
L0 Raw data Instrument digital counts, engineering data Mission teams
L1B Calibrated radiances Geolocated, calibrated spectra Algorithm developers
L2 Geophysical retrievals XCO₂, XCH₄ with quality flags and errors Scientists, advanced users
L3 Gridded products Spatial/temporal averages, gap-filled General users, modelers
L4 Model outputs Flux inversions, forecasts Policy makers, public

4.5 Retrieval Algorithms

Converting measured spectra to GHG concentrations is complex and non-trivial:

Retrieval Process Overview

  1. Forward model: Simulate expected spectrum given atmosphere state
  2. Comparison: Compare simulation with actual measurement
  3. Iteration: Adjust atmospheric state to minimize difference
  4. Convergence: Repeat until best fit achieved
  5. Output: Retrieved GHG profile or column amount

Key challenges:

  • Aerosol and cloud interference
  • Surface reflectance variability
  • Temperature and pressure profiles
  • Spectroscopic uncertainties
  • Instrument calibration drifts

Major Retrieval Algorithms

Algorithm Mission Approach Key Features
ACOS OCO-2/3 Optimal estimation Full physics, aerosol scattering
RemoTeC TROPOMI Phillips-Tikhonov Fast processing, operational
FOCAL OCO-2 Fast iterative Alternative to ACOS for validation
SRFP GOSAT Short-range forecast Japan operational algorithm

4.6 Plume Detection and Quantification

Satellites can now detect individual emission plumes from large sources:

Methane Plume Detection

Detection threshold: ~2-5 tonnes CH₄/hour (TROPOMI), ~100 kg/hr (GHGSat)

Detection algorithm:

1. Calculate anomaly: ΔCH₄ = Observed - Background
2. Identify contiguous enhanced pixels
3. Apply statistical threshold (e.g., > 3σ)
4. Compute integrated mass enhancement (IME)
5. Estimate emission rate using wind speed

Emission rate (kg/hr) ≈ IME × wind_speed / plume_width

Major sources detected:

  • Oil & gas infrastructure leaks
  • Coal mine ventilation shafts
  • Large landfills
  • Natural gas processing plants
  • Pipeline ruptures

Attribution to Facilities

5 km TROPOMI Cluster of facilities Oil field, industrial zone
10 km GOSAT City or region Urban emissions, large basin

4.7 Validation and Uncertainty

Validation Hierarchy

How Satellites Are Validated

  1. Pre-launch: Laboratory calibration, algorithm testing on synthetic data
  2. Commissioning: Initial on-orbit performance verification
  3. Ground-based FTIR: TCCON provides primary validation (XGas)
  4. Aircraft campaigns: Vertical profiles for retrieval validation
  5. In-situ networks: Surface and tower data for flux inversions
  6. Inter-satellite comparison: Cross-validation between missions

Uncertainty Sources

Source Typical Magnitude Mitigation
Instrument noise ~0.5 ppm XCO₂ Averaging, better SNR
Aerosol scattering 0.5-2 ppm bias Simultaneous aerosol retrieval
Cloud contamination Variable bias Strict cloud screening
Surface reflectance 0.3-1 ppm Improved surface models
Spectroscopy ~0.2 ppm Laboratory measurements
Calibration drift 0.1-0.5 ppm/year On-board calibration, vicarious

4.8 Inverse Modeling with Satellite Data

Satellite observations are used in inverse models to estimate surface fluxes:

Inverse Modeling Concept

Forward problem: Fluxes → Concentrations (atmospheric transport)

Inverse problem: Concentrations → Fluxes (infer sources/sinks)

Basic equation:

y = H × x + ε

where:
  y = observations (satellite XCO₂)
  x = state vector (fluxes at surface)
  H = observation operator (transport model)
  ε = observation error

Goal: Find x that best fits y

Solution approach (Bayesian):

x̂ = xb + BH^T(HBH^T + R)^-1(y - Hxb)

where:
  xb = prior fluxes (best guess)
  B = prior error covariance
  R = observation error covariance
  x̂ = optimized (posterior) fluxes

Applications of Flux Inversions

  • National emissions: Top-down verification of inventories
  • Regional budgets: Quantify sources/sinks at sub-continental scale
  • Seasonal cycles: Track biospheric uptake and release
  • Trend attribution: Separate fossil vs. biogenic contributions
  • Policy verification: Detect emission changes from climate policies

Key Takeaways

  • Satellites provide global, consistent GHG monitoring impossible from ground alone
  • Passive spectrometers measure absorption of reflected sunlight to infer concentrations
  • OCO-2 demonstrated policy-relevant CO₂ precision from space
  • TROPOMI enables detection of methane super-emitters at facility scale
  • Column-averaged mole fractions (XCO₂, XCH₄) are standard satellite products
  • TCCON ground-based FTIR provides primary satellite validation
  • Inverse models use satellite data to estimate surface fluxes
  • Next-generation missions offer higher resolution and operational capability
  • WIA-ENE-051 provides standardized APIs for multi-mission satellite data integration
