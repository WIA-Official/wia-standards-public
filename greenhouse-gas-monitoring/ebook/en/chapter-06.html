6.1 International Climate Governance

UNFCCC - The Foundation

United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change

Adopted1992 (Rio Earth Summit)
Entry into ForceMarch 21, 1994
Parties198 (nearly universal)
ObjectiveStabilize GHG concentrations to prevent dangerous interference with climate system

Core Principles:

  • Common but differentiated responsibilities (CBDR): All countries share responsibility, but developed nations should lead
  • Precautionary principle: Act even with scientific uncertainty
  • Right to sustainable development: Climate action must support development goals

Key Reporting Requirement:

  • National Communications: Comprehensive reports on emissions, policies, vulnerability
  • National Inventory Reports (NIRs): Detailed emission estimates

The Kyoto Protocol (1997-2020)

Feature Description Impact
Binding Targets Annex I countries: -5% below 1990 by 2008-2012 First legally binding emission limits
Flexible Mechanisms Emissions Trading, CDM, JI Cost-effective compliance pathways
Compliance System Expert review, adjustment procedures Enforcement of reporting quality
LimitationUSA never ratified, developing countries no targets Incomplete global coverage

The Paris Agreement (2015-Present)

Paris Agreement - Universal Climate Action

AdoptedDecember 12, 2015 (COP21)
Entry into ForceNovember 4, 2016
Parties195+ (virtually universal)
Temperature GoalWell below 2°C, pursue 1.5°C above pre-industrial

Key Innovations:

  • NDCs (Nationally Determined Contributions): Each country sets its own targets (bottom-up approach)
  • Universal participation: All countries have obligations, not just developed
  • Ratchet mechanism: Successive NDCs must represent progression
  • Global Stocktake: Every 5 years, assess collective progress
  • Enhanced Transparency Framework: Standardized reporting for all

Reporting Requirements:

  • Developed countries: Biennial Transparency Reports (BTRs) every 2 years
  • Developing countries: BTRs with flexibility based on capacity
  • All countries: National inventory of GHG emissions/removals

6.2 Regional Regulatory Systems

EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS)

World's Largest Carbon Market

Launched2005
CoverageEU 27 + Iceland, Liechtenstein, Norway
SectorsPower, industry, aviation (intra-EU)
Emissions Covered~40% of EU total GHGs
Installations~10,000 facilities

Cap-and-Trade Mechanism:

  1. Total emissions cap set and lowered annually (-2.2% per year)
  2. Allowances (EUAs) distributed via auction and free allocation
  3. Installations must monitor and report emissions
  4. Annual surrender of allowances matching emissions
  5. Trading allowed for cost-effective compliance

Monitoring Requirements:

  • Continuous Emission Monitoring Systems (CEMS) for large combustion
  • Calculation-based methods with uncertainty thresholds
  • Annual verified emission reports
  • ISO 14065 accredited verifiers

Penalties: €100 per tonne shortfall + obligation to surrender allowances next year

California Cap-and-Trade Program

Aspect Details
Launch 2013
Linked Markets California + Quebec (since 2014)
Coverage ~75% of CA emissions
Sectors Electric power, large industry, fuel distributors, natural gas
Cap Reduction ~4% per year (more aggressive than EU ETS)

China National ETS

World's Largest Emissions Trading System (by volume)

LaunchFebruary 2021
Phase 1 CoveragePower sector only (2,162 installations)
Emissions Covered~4.5 billion tonnes CO2/year
Threshold26,000 tonnes CO2/year
Future ExpansionSteel, cement, chemicals, aluminum sectors planned

Unique Features:

  • Intensity-based benchmarks (emissions per unit output)
  • Free allocation initially (auctions planned)
  • Regional pilot programs since 2013 (Beijing, Shanghai, etc.)

6.4 Corporate Disclosure Requirements

Mandatory Disclosure Regimes

EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD)

Effective: 2024 (phased implementation)

Scope: ~50,000 companies (all large EU companies + listed SMEs)

Required disclosures:

  • Scope 1, 2, and 3 GHG emissions
  • Climate transition plan
  • Sustainability targets and progress
  • Principal risks related to sustainability
  • Third-party assurance required

European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS): Detailed technical standards for implementation

SEC Climate Disclosure Rule (USA)

Proposed: 2022 (implementation pending legal challenges)

Scope: All publicly traded companies

Proposed requirements:

  • Scope 1 and 2 emissions (all companies)
  • Scope 3 if material or target set (large accelerated filers)
  • Climate-related risks and impacts
  • Governance and risk management
  • Financial statement disclosures of climate impacts
  • Attestation required for Scope 1 & 2 (phased)

Voluntary Disclosure Frameworks

Framework Operator Focus Adoption
CDP Carbon Disclosure Project Climate, water, forests 18,700+ companies (2024)
TCFD Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures Financial risks and opportunities 4,000+ supporters, increasingly mandatory
GRI Global Reporting Initiative Comprehensive sustainability (including GHGs) 10,000+ organizations
SASB Sustainability Accounting Standards Board Financially material sustainability issues Merged into IFRS Foundation

6.5 Verification and Accreditation

ISO 14064 Family

International Standards for GHG Accounting

ISO 14064-1:2018 - Organization-level quantification and reporting

  • Principles for GHG inventories
  • Scope 1, 2, 3 definitions
  • Quantification methodologies
  • Reporting requirements

ISO 14064-2:2019 - Project-level quantification

  • Baseline setting
  • Additionality demonstration
  • Monitoring plans
  • Emission reduction calculation

ISO 14064-3:2019 - Verification and validation

  • Verification process requirements
  • Evidence gathering
  • Materiality and risk assessment
  • Assurance levels (limited vs. reasonable)

Accreditation Bodies

Body Role Scope

6.6 Sectoral Regulations

Aviation - CORSIA

Carbon Offsetting and Reduction Scheme for International Aviation

OperatorICAO (International Civil Aviation Organization)
LaunchPilot phase 2024-2026
CoverageInternational flights between participating countries
MechanismOffset growth above 2019 baseline

Monitoring requirements:

  • Fuel consumption monitoring (all flights)
  • CO2 calculation using ICAO methodology
  • Verification by accredited entities
  • Annual emissions reporting
  • Purchase of CORSIA Eligible Fuels or carbon credits

Shipping - IMO GHG Strategy

International Maritime Organization Targets

  • 2030: 40% reduction in carbon intensity
  • 2050: Net zero GHG emissions from shipping

Regulations:

  • Energy Efficiency Existing Ship Index (EEXI)
  • Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) - annual rating A-E
  • EU ETS inclusion of maritime (from 2024)
  • IMO DCS: Data Collection System for fuel consumption

6.7 Compliance and Enforcement

Enforcement Mechanisms

Jurisdiction Non-Compliance Penalty Additional Consequences
EU ETS €100 per tonne shortfall Name published, allowances still owed, potential suspension
California $10,000 per day violation 4 allowances per tonne shortfall, program suspension
UK CRC £40 per tonne shortfall Compliance still required, public disclosure
UNFCCC No financial penalty (diplomatic pressure) Expert review, questions of implementation, reputation

Review and Adjustment Procedures

UNFCCC Review Process

  1. Initial Check: Automated CRF table consistency tests
  2. Expert Review Team (ERT): International experts review submission
  3. Draft Report: Findings and recommendations
  4. Country Response: Party clarifies issues
  5. Final Report: Published with conclusions
  6. Adjustment (if needed): Estimates corrected for compliance accounting

Common issues identified:

  • Methodological inconsistencies
  • Missing categories or gases
  • Insufficient uncertainty quantification
  • Lack of transparency in documentation

6.8 Emerging Regulatory Trends

Border Carbon Adjustments

EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM)

ImplementationTransitional 2023-2025, full from 2026
Covered ProductsCement, iron & steel, aluminum, fertilizers, electricity, hydrogen
MechanismImporters buy CBAM certificates matching embedded carbon

Monitoring requirement:

  • Report embedded emissions in imported goods
  • Quarterly CBAM declarations
  • Verification by accredited entities
  • Default values if actual emissions unknown

Objective: Prevent carbon leakage (production moving to countries with weaker climate policies)

Methane Regulations

Global Methane Pledge

Launched: COP26 Glasgow (2021)

Signatories: 150+ countries

Target: Reduce methane 30% by 2030 (vs. 2020)

Implementing regulations:

  • USA: EPA methane standards for oil & gas (2024)
  • EU: Methane Regulation (import requirements, OGMP 2.0)
  • Canada: 75% methane reduction by 2030 in oil & gas

Monitoring innovations:

  • Satellite detection of super-emitters
  • Mandatory leak detection and repair (LDAR)
  • Continuous monitoring systems
  • Third-party verification of reductions

6.10 Future Outlook

Regulatory Trends to Watch

  • Mandatory corporate climate disclosure: Trend toward SEC/CSRD-style rules globally
  • Scope 3 requirements: Increasing focus on supply chain emissions
  • Real-time monitoring mandates: Shift from annual reporting to continuous data
  • Satellite-based verification: Independent validation of self-reported data
  • Sectoral expansion: More industries covered by mandatory schemes
  • Harmonization efforts: Move toward global standards (ISSB, ISO)
  • Climate litigation: Courts increasingly requiring evidence-based emission data

Key Takeaways

  • UNFCCC provides the international framework; Paris Agreement requires universal transparency
  • Regional carbon markets (EU ETS, California, China) drive demand for precise monitoring
  • National climate laws increasingly include legally binding targets and monitoring provisions
  • Corporate disclosure is shifting from voluntary to mandatory (CSRD, SEC proposals)
  • ISO 14064 family provides technical standards for quantification and verification
  • Sectoral regulations (CORSIA aviation, IMO shipping) expand coverage
  • Enforcement includes financial penalties and reputational consequences
  • Border carbon adjustments (CBAM) require embedded emissions tracking
  • Methane regulations specifically target the second-largest GHG contributor
  • WIA-ENE-051 enables automated compliance checking across multiple frameworks
