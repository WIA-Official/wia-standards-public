Chapter 5

Informed Consent and Patient Autonomy

Patient autonomy—the right to make informed decisions about one's own healthcare—is a cornerstone of modern medical ethics. When AI systems influence clinical decisions, informed consent takes on new dimensions. Patients must understand not only the proposed treatment but also the role algorithms play in their care, what data is used, and what the implications of AI involvement mean for their health outcomes and personal information.

The Foundation of Informed Consent

Informed consent emerged from recognition that patients have the right to determine what happens to their own bodies. This principle, enshrined in law and medical ethics, requires that before any medical intervention, patients receive adequate information to make a voluntary and intelligent choice. The elements of informed consent—disclosure, comprehension, voluntariness, competence, and consent—were developed for human-delivered care. AI challenges each of these elements in new ways.

Traditional informed consent focuses on disclosing the nature of the proposed treatment, its risks and benefits, alternatives, and the consequences of refusing treatment. When AI is involved, the scope of disclosure expands: What role does AI play? How was it trained? What are its limitations? Can the patient opt out? How is their data used? The challenge is providing sufficient information without overwhelming patients or undermining their trust in the care they receive.

AI-Specific Disclosure Requirements

When AI participates in clinical care, patients may need information beyond traditional consent disclosures. The challenge is determining what must be disclosed, how to present complex technical concepts accessibly, and how to integrate AI disclosure into clinical workflows without creating consent fatigue or undermining trust.

Disclosure Element Traditional Consent AI-Enhanced Consent Rationale

What Must Be Disclosed?

There is no consensus on exactly what AI-related information must be disclosed for consent to be valid. Different stakeholders propose varying standards, from minimal disclosure (AI is used) to extensive disclosure (algorithm specifics, training data sources, performance metrics). The appropriate level likely depends on the AI's role, the stakes of the decision, and the patient's preferences.

Proposed Disclosure Standards

Standard Required Disclosure Proponents

The Right to Know About AI

A fundamental question in AI ethics is whether patients have a right to know when AI influences their care—even if that knowledge might cause anxiety or change their behavior in ways that affect outcomes. This tension between transparency and potential psychological impact mirrors debates in other disclosure contexts, such as informing patients of incidental findings.

Arguments for Disclosure

  • Autonomy: Patients have the right to know what influences their care to make informed decisions
  • Trust: Transparency about AI use builds trust; discovering undisclosed AI may undermine it
  • Accountability: Disclosure enables patients to seek recourse if AI causes harm
  • Dignity: Being subject to algorithmic assessment without knowledge may feel dehumanizing
  • Precedent: Medical ethics requires disclosure of material information; AI qualifies

Concerns About Disclosure

  • Anxiety: Learning AI is involved may cause unnecessary patient worry
  • Nocebo Effect: Negative expectations about AI may worsen outcomes
  • Consent Fatigue: Adding AI disclosure to already-lengthy consent processes may reduce comprehension
  • Inequitable Burden: Patients with less education may struggle to understand AI information
  • Workflow Disruption: Detailed AI explanations may burden clinical encounters

Balancing Transparency and Burden

Healthcare systems must find approaches that respect patient autonomy while avoiding consent overload. Potential strategies include:

  • Tiered disclosure: Basic information always provided; detailed information available on request
  • System-level consent: General consent for AI-assisted care at admission; specific consent for high-stakes AI applications
  • Visual aids: Infographics and videos that convey AI information efficiently
  • Patient portals: Detailed AI information available through digital platforms for interested patients
  • Decision aids: Tools that help patients understand AI role and make informed choices

Opt-Out Rights and Alternatives

If AI disclosure is required, should patients have the right to refuse AI-assisted care? The right to opt out raises practical and ethical questions: Is human-only assessment always available? What if AI-assisted care is demonstrably better? How do opt-out requests affect the patient-physician relationship?

When Opt-Out May Be Limited

There may be situations where opt-out rights are constrained. If AI is deeply integrated into standard diagnostic equipment, opting out may be impractical. In emergencies, obtaining specific AI consent may be impossible. Resource constraints may limit availability of human-only alternatives. These limitations should be disclosed and justified.

Data Consent for AI Training

Beyond consent for AI-assisted care, patients may need to consent to the use of their data for AI training and development. This secondary use of health data raises distinct ethical considerations about privacy, benefit-sharing, and control over personal information.

Data Use Consent Considerations

  • Scope: What data will be used? Clinical records, imaging, genomics, wearables?
  • Purpose: Research only? Commercial product development? Both?
  • Duration: One-time use or ongoing contribution to AI training?
  • De-identification: What anonymization measures are applied?
  • Sharing: Will data be shared with third parties? Commercially licensed?
  • Benefit sharing: Will patients share in any commercial value from their data?
  • Withdrawal: Can consent be withdrawn? What happens to models already trained?

Dynamic Consent Models

Traditional one-time consent may be inadequate for ongoing AI development. Dynamic consent models allow patients to make ongoing choices about how their data is used, updated as new AI applications emerge. While more respectful of autonomy, these models are more complex to implement and may create data availability challenges for researchers.

Consent Model Approach Advantages Challenges
Broad Consent General permission for future research Simple; enables research Limited patient control; may not cover novel uses
Specific Consent Separate consent for each AI project Precise patient control Impractical for large datasets; slows research
Tiered Consent Categories of uses with patient choices Balances control and practicality Categories may not match actual uses
Dynamic Consent Ongoing patient engagement and choices Continuous autonomy; updated preferences Technology requirements; engagement burden
Meta-Consent Consent to governance process rather than specific uses Scalable; trusts oversight bodies Indirect patient control; governance quality critical

Vulnerable Populations

Special considerations apply to obtaining consent from vulnerable populations who may face barriers to understanding AI or expressing preferences. Children, elderly patients, those with cognitive impairment, and individuals with limited health literacy require adapted consent approaches.

Considerations for Vulnerable Groups

  • Pediatric Patients: Parental consent required; age-appropriate assent from children; consider AI implications for long-term health records
  • Elderly Patients: May need simpler explanations; extra time for comprehension; avoid ageist assumptions about technology understanding
  • Cognitive Impairment: Capacity assessment; surrogate decision-makers; respect for patient preferences where possible
  • Limited Literacy: Visual and verbal explanations; teach-back methods; multilingual materials
  • Non-English Speakers: Qualified interpreters; translated materials; cultural sensitivity about AI attitudes
  • Incarcerated Individuals: Ensure voluntariness given power dynamics; protect against coercion

Implementation Approaches

Integrating AI Consent into Clinical Workflows

Healthcare institutions must develop practical approaches to obtaining AI-related consent that respect patient autonomy while remaining feasible within clinical operations. This requires balancing thoroughness with efficiency.

Implementation Best Practices

  • General AI Policy: Inform patients at registration that AI may be used throughout their care
  • Specific Consent Triggers: Obtain specific consent for high-stakes AI applications (autonomous diagnosis, treatment selection)
  • Staff Training: Train clinicians to explain AI in accessible terms and respond to questions
  • Written Materials: Develop clear, tested patient information sheets about AI
  • Digital Tools: Use interactive digital consent tools that adapt to patient understanding
  • Documentation: Record AI-related consent in the electronic health record
  • Opt-Out Processes: Establish clear workflows for honoring patient opt-out requests

Consent Documentation

Clear documentation of AI-related consent protects both patients and institutions. Documentation should record what information was provided, patient questions and concerns, the consent decision, and any opt-out requests.

Summary

Key Takeaways

  • Autonomy Foundation: Patient autonomy—the right to make informed healthcare decisions—is a core ethical principle that applies to AI-assisted care.
  • Expanded Disclosure: AI introduces new disclosure requirements beyond traditional consent, including AI role, limitations, and data use.
  • Right to Know: Patients have a prima facie right to know when AI influences their care, though disclosure methods must avoid overwhelming patients.
  • Opt-Out Rights: Patients should generally have the option to decline AI-assisted care, though practical limitations may constrain this right.
  • Data Consent: Secondary use of patient data for AI training requires appropriate consent, potentially through dynamic consent models.
  • Vulnerable Populations: Special consent approaches are needed for children, elderly, cognitively impaired, and other vulnerable groups.
  • Implementation Required: Healthcare institutions must develop practical workflows for AI consent that balance autonomy and operational feasibility.
