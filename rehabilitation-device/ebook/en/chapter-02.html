Chapter 2

Robotic Rehabilitation Systems

← Previous Contents Next →

Robotic rehabilitation systems deliver high-dose, high-intensity, and reproducible therapy that augments — not replaces — the licensed clinician. This chapter walks the standards stack a developer or rehab engineer needs when bringing a robotic upper-limb or lower-limb rehab platform to market: where the IEC 80601-2-78:2019 particular-requirements document fits, how the device is classified under FDA 21 CFR 860.3 and EU MDR Annex VIII, and the per-control-strategy options (impedance, admittance, assist-as-needed) you choose between for a given clinical phase.

2.1 Reference platforms

The reference catalogue spans Lokomat (gait), ArmeoSpring / ArmeoPower (upper limb), InMotion ARM/HAND (Bionik, formerly Interactive Motion Technologies), Bi-Manu-Track (Reha-Stim Medtec), Amadeo (Tyromotion), KineAssist, ZeroG, and pediatric variants. Each platform commits to specific kinematic and dynamic envelopes; the evaluation method follows IEC 80601-2-78 §5 essential-performance requirements with the per-axis position + velocity + force verification battery. The standard does not prescribe a particular control law — it constrains the safety envelope around whatever law the manufacturer adopts.

2.2 Control strategies

Impedance control (Hogan 1985) and admittance control are the two dominant paradigms. The choice is driven by patient acuity: stiff impedance early when the patient cannot generate force; soft admittance later when the patient drives the trajectory. Assist-as-needed (Reinkensmeyer + Akoner + Ferris 2002) modulates assistance trial-by-trial against patient performance, preserving the motor-learning signal. Error-augmentation (Patton + Mussa-Ivaldi 2004) deliberately magnifies trajectory error to recruit a stronger correction; it remains a research-grade modality outside specific clinical protocols.

2.3 Safety envelope

Per IEC 80601-2-78 §201.13, every robotic rehab device implements at least two independent emergency-stop channels and a watchdog-based motion supervisor. The patient-applied-part is type BF or type CF per IEC 60601-1; for FES-integrated robotics, the type-CF requirement applies to the stim channel. Range-of-motion and torque envelopes are configured per-patient against the per-joint anatomical limit and the per-protocol prescription, with hard mechanical end-stops as the ultimate safety boundary.

2.4 Clinical evidence

Pivotal trials follow ISO 14155:2020 + ICH E6(R3) + the per-jurisdiction IDE or equivalent. The primary outcome is typically the Fugl-Meyer Assessment (Fugl-Meyer 1975) for stroke upper-limb, ARAT (Lyle 1981), or the 10-meter walk for gait. The minimal clinically important difference is condition-specific and must be pre-specified in the protocol. Post-market clinical follow-up per EU MDR Annex XIV Part B keeps the evidence current across the device's service life.
