Grading & Rubrics

Fair Assessment and Transparent Evaluation

Grading Schemes

WIA-EDU-009 supports multiple grading schemes to accommodate diverse educational contexts and institutional requirements.

Points-Based Grading

  • Assignments worth specific point values (e.g., 100 points)
  • Total points accumulated over the term
  • Percentage calculated from points earned / points possible
  • Simple, transparent, widely understood

Weighted Categories

  • Category Weights: Assignments 40%, Quizzes 20%, Exams 30%, Participation 10%
  • Flexible weight distribution across categories
  • Automatic calculation of final grade
  • Option to drop lowest score(s) per category

Letter Grades

  • Traditional A-F scale
  • Plus/minus variations (A+, A, A-, B+, etc.)
  • Customizable grade ranges (e.g., A = 93-100 or A = 90-100)
  • International variations (UK, European grading systems)

Pass/Fail Grading

  • Binary outcome (passed or failed)
  • Threshold score for passing (e.g., 70%)
  • Reduced anxiety for exploratory courses
  • Compliance with credit/no-credit policies

Standards-Based Grading (SBG)

  • Competency Levels: Exceeds, Meets, Approaching, Below expectations
  • Focus on mastery of specific learning objectives
  • Multiple opportunities to demonstrate competency
  • Popular in K-12 and competency-based education

Gradebook Management

Gradebook Views

  • Student View: Individual row per student with all assignments
  • Assignment View: All student scores for single assignment
  • Learning Outcomes View: Mastery tracking by objective
  • Summary View: Current grade, missing assignments, trends

Gradebook Features

  • Manual Entry: Direct score input with validation
  • Import/Export: CSV upload/download for bulk operations
  • Grade Override: Manual adjustments with audit trail
  • Excuse Assignments: Remove from grade calculation
  • What-If Calculations: Students predict final grade with hypothetical scores

Grade Visibility Settings

  • Hide grades until instructor releases them
  • Automatic release upon grading completion
  • Scheduled release (e.g., every Friday at 5 PM)
  • Individual assignment visibility controls

Rubric Design

Why Use Rubrics?
  • Clarify expectations before students begin work
  • Provide consistent, objective grading criteria
  • Expedite grading process with structured framework
  • Offer detailed feedback aligned with criteria
  • Reduce grading bias and increase transparency

Rubric Types

  • Holistic Rubrics: Single overall score based on general impression
  • Analytic Rubrics: Separate scores for multiple criteria
  • Single-Point Rubrics: Describe proficiency level, note areas above/below
  • Developmental Rubrics: Track progress over time

Example Analytic Rubric

Criteria Excellent (4) Proficient (3) Developing (2) Beginning (1)
Content Exceptional depth and accuracy Good understanding with minor gaps Basic understanding, some errors Limited understanding, major errors
Organization Clear, logical flow throughout Generally well-organized Some organizational issues Confusing or illogical structure
Writing Polished, error-free Few minor errors Multiple errors, readability affected Numerous errors, difficult to read

Rubric Creation Process

  1. Identify Criteria: Key aspects to evaluate (content, organization, mechanics)
  2. Define Levels: Performance standards (4-5 levels typical)
  3. Write Descriptors: Clear description of each level for each criterion
  4. Assign Points: Determine point value for each level
  5. Test & Refine: Pilot with sample work, adjust descriptors

Grading Policies & Features

Late Work Handling

  • Automatic Deductions: 10% off per day late
  • Grace Periods: 24-hour buffer without penalty
  • Hard Deadlines: No credit after cutoff
  • Individual Extensions: Accommodations for specific students

Missing Assignment Policies

  • Zero Policy: Unsubmitted work receives 0
  • Placeholder Scores: Temporary mark (e.g., "M" for missing)
  • Weighted Impact: Exclude from grade calculation until submitted
  • Minimum Scores: No grade lower than 50% to allow recovery

Extra Credit

  • Optional assignments for bonus points
  • Attendance or participation bonuses
  • Grade replacement (better score replaces lower one)
  • Cap on total extra credit points

Dropped Assignments

  • Drop Lowest: Automatically exclude worst score(s)
  • Category-Specific: Drop 1 quiz, 1 homework, etc.
  • Conditional Drops: Only if final exam score exceeds threshold
  • Student Selection: Choose which assignment to drop

Grade Curves & Adjustments

Curve Types

  • Linear Curve: Add fixed points to all scores (e.g., +5 points)
  • Square Root Curve: sqrt(score) × 10 (helps lower scores more)
  • Bell Curve: Force normal distribution (e.g., 10% A's, 20% B's, 40% C's)
  • Custom Curve: Manual adjustments based on difficulty

When to Curve

  • Assessment was unexpectedly difficult
  • Technical issues affected performance
  • Teaching error or ambiguous question
  • Institutional grade distribution requirements
Curve Considerations: Curving can demotivate high-performing students and doesn't address underlying instructional issues. Use sparingly and transparently.

Grade Communication

Feedback Methods

  • Inline Comments: Annotations directly on submissions
  • Summary Comments: Overall feedback for entire assignment
  • Rubric Feedback: Criterion-specific comments
  • Audio/Video Feedback: Recorded explanations
  • Email Notifications: Alerts when grades are posted

Grade Transparency

  • Students can see current grade anytime
  • "What-if" tool for grade projections
  • Clear syllabus explanation of grading policies
  • Breakdown showing calculation method

Grade Disputes & Appeals

  • Formal process for contesting grades
  • Required waiting period before appeals (e.g., 24 hours)
  • Documentation of grading rationale
  • Department or administrator review procedures

Learning Outcomes & Competencies

Outcome-Based Assessment

  • Link assignments to specific learning objectives
  • Track mastery of each outcome across multiple assessments
  • Identify outcome areas needing improvement
  • Program-level accreditation reporting

Mastery Grading

  • Students must demonstrate proficiency to progress
  • Multiple attempts allowed until mastery achieved
  • Focus on learning rather than time-based completion
  • Self-paced advancement through material

Gradebook Analytics

Performance Insights

  • Grade Distribution: Histogram of class performance
  • Trends Over Time: Improving, stable, or declining
  • Assignment Difficulty: Average scores identify hard/easy assessments
  • Correlation Analysis: Which assignments predict final grade

Early Warning Indicators

  • Students below threshold (e.g., < 70%)
  • Sudden grade drops
  • Pattern of missing assignments
  • Intervention recommendations

Best Practices for Grading

Effective Grading Strategies:
  1. Use clear, detailed rubrics for consistency
  2. Provide timely feedback (within 1 week)
  3. Focus feedback on improvement, not just scores
  4. Be transparent about grading policies from day one
  5. Avoid grading bias through blind grading when possible
  6. Balance formative and summative assessments
  7. Allow for growth and second chances
  8. Use data to identify and address learning gaps
  9. Maintain accurate, up-to-date gradebook
  10. Regularly review grading policies for equity and effectiveness
