CHAPTER 8

☢️ Future of Waste Treatment

弘益人間 - Transforming challenges into opportunities

Chapter Overview: The future of radioactive waste management lies not in burial and forgetting, but in transformation and value extraction. This chapter explores cutting-edge technologies: transmutation that converts long-lived isotopes to shorter-lived ones, partitioning that separates waste for tailored treatment, advanced reactors that burn actinides for energy, and revolutionary concepts like deep borehole disposal. These innovations promise to reduce the waste burden for future generations dramatically.

8.1 Transmutation: Reducing the Hazard Duration

Transmutation transforms long-lived radioactive isotopes into shorter-lived or stable isotopes through nuclear reactions. This could reduce the required isolation period from hundreds of thousands of years to just centuries.

8.1.1 Target Isotopes

The most valuable transmutation targets are minor actinides (MA) and long-lived fission products (LLFP):

Isotope Half-life Transmutation Approach Challenge Benefit
Np-237 2.14 million years Fast reactor fission Multiple recycles needed Dominant long-term hazard eliminated
Am-241 432 years Fast reactor or ADS High heat generation Reduces repository thermal load
Cm-244 18 years Allow decay then recycle Intense heat/neutrons Enables Am recycling
Tc-99 211,000 years Neutron capture Low concentration, volatile Mobile in environment
I-129 15.7 million years Neutron capture Very volatile, low concentration Most mobile fission product

Transmuting minor actinides could reduce long-term radiotoxicity by a factor of 100-1,000, and repository isolation requirements from 100,000+ years to ~500 years when fission products dominate.

8.1.2 Fast Reactor Transmutation

Fast reactors with high-energy neutron spectra efficiently fission heavy actinides. Designs like Russia's BN-600 and BN-800 demonstrate the concept operationally. The fuel cycle requires:

  • Reprocessing LWR spent fuel to separate plutonium and minor actinides
  • Fabricating mixed actinide fuel (Pu + MA in oxide or nitride form)
  • Burning fuel in fast reactor (multiple recycles to destroy MA)
  • Partitioning between recycles to remove fission products

TerraPower's Natrium reactor (USA) combines sodium cooling with molten salt energy storage, planning to demonstrate waste-burning capabilities while providing grid flexibility.

8.1.3 Accelerator-Driven Systems (ADS)

ADS couple a proton accelerator, spallation target, and subcritical reactor core. The accelerator produces high-energy protons that strike a heavy metal target (lead), producing copious neutrons via spallation. These neutrons drive fission in surrounding fuel containing minor actinides.

Advantages include inherent safety (turn off the beam and reactions stop), ability to burn problematic isotopes without achieving criticality, and flexibility in neutron spectrum. Belgium's MYRRHA project aims to demonstrate ADS feasibility at 100 MWth scale.

Challenges include accelerator reliability (must operate >90% uptime for economic viability), beam window engineering (intense radiation and thermal stress), and high capital costs. ADS is likely best suited for dedicated waste burners rather than dual energy/waste missions.

8.2 Partitioning: Tailored Treatment for Each Element

Partitioning chemically separates spent fuel into elemental groups, enabling tailored waste management strategies. Traditional PUREX reprocessing separates only uranium and plutonium. Advanced partitioning recovers minor actinides and key fission products.

8.2.1 UREX+ Family

Uranium Extraction Plus (UREX+) processes use solvent extraction to separate spent fuel into multiple streams:

  • UREX+1: Separates U, Tc, Cs/Sr, and TRU (combined Pu+MA). TRU product suitable for fast reactor fuel.
  • UREX+2: Further separates Pu from MA, enabling different fuel fabrication approaches
  • UREX+3: Separates Am from Cm. Am has higher priority for transmutation; Cm may require aging storage before handling due to intense heat and neutron emission
  • UREX+4: Complete separation of all actinides and key fission products

Demonstrated at laboratory scale, UREX+ could enable closure of the nuclear fuel cycle by recycling all actinides while separating heat-generating Sr-90/Cs-137 for near-term interim storage.

8.2.2 Pyroprocessing

Electrochemical separation in molten salts offers an alternative to aqueous reprocessing. Demonstrated at Idaho National Laboratory on EBR-II metal fuel, pyroprocessing electrorefines metallic fuel in a LiCl-KCl eutectic at 500°C.

Advantages include compact footprint, radiation resistance (no organic solvents to degrade), and inherent proliferation resistance (TRU not separated from each other). However, lower decontamination factors than aqueous methods and generation of salt waste streams are challenges.

Future waste treatment technologies vary dramatically in maturity, cost, and waste reduction potential. Fast reactors offer the most near-term deployment potential with proven technology already operating in Russia, though requiring substantial capital investment. Accelerator-driven systems promise the highest waste reduction factors but remain experimental and costly. The choice between technologies depends on national priorities: countries with large existing waste inventories may prioritize rapid deployment of proven fast reactors, while those with smaller inventories and strong research capabilities might invest in breakthrough technologies like MSRs or ADS. Deep borehole disposal offers a parallel path—reducing repository costs and timelines while advanced treatment technologies mature.

8.3.1 Molten Salt Reactors (MSR)

Liquid-fueled reactors where fissile and fertile materials dissolve in molten fluoride salts (typically LiF-BeF₂-UF₄) promise inherent safety and minimal waste. Online fuel processing removes fission products continuously while adding fresh fuel, approaching a steady-state composition.

Waste advantages include: minimal actinide buildup (continuously recycled), fission products separated and vitrified (shorter hazard duration), potential to consume existing Pu/MA inventories, and reduced geological repository burden.

Companies like Terrestrial Energy (Canada), ThorCon (USA), and Copenhagen Atomics (Denmark) are developing MSR designs, though regulatory frameworks for liquid-fueled reactors remain undeveloped.

8.3.2 Traveling Wave Reactors

TerraPower's Natrium design (formerly TWR concept) aims to burn depleted uranium without enrichment or reprocessing. The reactor starts with enriched fuel in the core center, breeds Pu-239 from surrounding U-238, and the "wave" of fission gradually moves outward through the core over decades.

If successful, TWRs could utilize 700,000 tonnes of depleted uranium stockpiled in the U.S. alone, generating clean energy while consuming material currently considered waste. Burnup exceeds 90% of uranium (versus ~1% in current LWRs), dramatically reducing waste generation per energy produced.

8.4 Deep Borehole Disposal

An alternative to mined repositories, deep borehole disposal places waste 3-5 km underground in crystalline basement rock. Drilling technology from oil and gas industry enables 40-50 cm diameter boreholes to these depths.

8.4.1 Advantages

  • Greater depth: 3-5 km versus 0.3-1 km for mined repositories, providing enhanced isolation
  • Favorable geochemistry: Deep groundwater is ancient, saline (high density prevents upward flow), and reducing
  • Lower cost: Estimates suggest 50% cost reduction versus mined repositories
  • Modularity: Incremental deployment as waste volumes accumulate
  • Smaller footprint: Hundreds of boreholes versus km² mined facility

8.4.2 Challenges

  • Waste must fit in narrow boreholes (limits to Cs/Sr capsules, disassembled fuel rods, vitrified HLW)
  • Retrievability extremely difficult once emplaced
  • Regulatory framework absent (no licensing precedent)
  • High temperatures at depth (geothermal gradient ~25°C/km) may affect waste forms

Test boreholes in the U.S. and international collaboration through IAEA are advancing the concept. Commercial deployment possible by 2030s-2040s if regulatory frameworks develop.

8.5 Robotics and AI in Waste Management

8.5.1 Remote Handling Advances

Modern robotics enable safer, more efficient waste operations:

  • Robotic arms: Computer-controlled manipulators with 6+ degrees of freedom, programmable operations, and interchangeable end effectors handle drums, perform sampling, and decontaminate surfaces
  • Mobile robots: Boston Dynamics' Spot deployed at Chernobyl for radiation mapping, PackBot used at Fukushima, custom robots for facility-specific tasks
  • Aerial drones: Radiation surveys of large areas, tall structure inspections, thermal imaging of dry casks
  • Underwater ROVs: Spent fuel pool inspections, cask loading observations without human divers

8.5.2 Artificial Intelligence Applications

Machine learning and AI optimize operations and predict problems:

  • Predictive maintenance: Analyzing vibration, temperature, and current data to predict equipment failures before occurrence, reducing downtime 20-40%
  • Automated gamma spectroscopy: AI interprets spectra, identifies isotopes, calculates activities, and performs QA checks, reducing analysis time by 80%
  • Anomaly detection: Identifying unusual patterns in monitoring data for early warning of releases or equipment degradation
  • Optimization algorithms: Package placement to minimize dose, transport routing, processing schedules

8.6 Space Disposal (Long-term Vision)

Space disposal—launching waste to the Sun or deep space—periodically resurfaces as an idea. While theoretically eliminating Earthly concerns, practical reality renders it infeasible for foreseeable future:

8.6.1 Technical and Economic Barriers

  • Launch cost: Currently ~$1,500/kg to LEO, much more for solar or escape trajectories. Disposing of 100,000 tonnes HLW at $500/kg: $50 trillion (500x more than geological repositories)
  • Reliability: Historical launch success ~97%. Even 99.99% reliability means 1 catastrophic failure per 10,000 launches—unacceptable for dispersing radioactive material in atmosphere
  • Regulatory hurdles: Outer Space Treaty prohibits harmful contamination of space, requiring international agreement
  • Energy requirements: Enormous delta-V to reach Sun or escape solar system

8.6.2 When Might It Be Feasible?

Prerequisites for serious consideration:

  • Launch costs < $10/kg (100x reduction from current, requires fully reusable spacecraft like Starship at scale)
  • Launch reliability > 99.99% (essentially perfect)
  • Moon base as staging area (reducing Earth escape costs)
  • In-space manufacturing and assembly
  • International consensus and treaty modifications

Timeline: Not before 2075-2100. Even then, likely only for high-risk, small-volume materials (Pu, select long-lived isotopes) rather than bulk waste. Geological disposal remains the practical solution for centuries to come.

🎯 Key Takeaways

  • Transmutation can reduce isolation time 200-fold: Converting long-lived actinides to fission products shortens required isolation from 100,000+ years to ~500 years
  • Fast reactors enable fuel recycling: Demonstrated technology in Russia, advancing in U.S. and elsewhere, can burn plutonium and minor actinides while generating electricity
  • Partitioning is key enabler: Separating actinides from fission products allows tailored treatment: transmutation for MA, interim storage for heat-generators, optimized waste forms for disposal
  • Deep boreholes offer alternative disposal: Lower cost, modular deployment, and enhanced isolation make borehole disposal attractive for certain waste forms
  • AI and robotics reduce costs and doses: Automation minimizes worker exposure, improves efficiency, and enables predictive maintenance
  • Space disposal remains science fiction: Cost and reliability barriers make space disposal impractical for bulk waste for many decades, if ever
  • The future is waste minimization: Closing the fuel cycle, transmuting actinides, and extracting energy from waste transforms "waste" into resource
