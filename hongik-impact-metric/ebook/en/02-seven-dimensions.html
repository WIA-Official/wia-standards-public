Chapter 2: The Seven Dimensions of Impact

A comprehensive framework for measuring how we benefit all humanity

The Hongik Impact Metric measures impact across seven fundamental dimensions. Each dimension represents a critical aspect of human flourishing. Together, they provide a holistic view of whether an organization, project, or initiative truly serves 弘益人間—the broad benefit of all humanity.

This chapter explores each dimension in depth, examining the philosophy behind it, the key metrics used, and real-world examples of excellence.

❤️

Dimension 1: Social Good

Strengthening communities and fostering genuine human connection

The Philosophy

Humans are fundamentally social beings. We evolved in tight-knit groups, and our wellbeing remains deeply intertwined with the quality of our relationships and communities. Social Good measures the degree to which work strengthens these vital human connections.

This dimension goes beyond simple reach or engagement metrics. It asks: Are you creating genuine community? Are you fostering inclusion? Are you building bridges between divided groups? Are you strengthening social fabric in a way that makes people feel more connected, supported, and part of something larger than themselves?

Core Metrics

Community Strength Index (CSI)
Measures the depth and resilience of community connections formed. Evaluates factors like mutual support networks, collective action capability, conflict resolution mechanisms, and sustained engagement over time.

Scale: 0-100, weighted 30% of Social Good score
Inclusion Score
Assesses how well the work includes marginalized groups, bridges divides, and creates space for diverse voices and perspectives. Examines representation, participation equity, and power distribution.

Scale: 0-100, weighted 35% of Social Good score
Social Capital Generated
Quantifies new relationships formed, trust networks established, and collective resources mobilized. Includes both bonding capital (within groups) and bridging capital (between groups).

Scale: 0-100, weighted 35% of Social Good score

Excellence Example: Community Land Trusts

Community Land Trusts (CLTs) consistently score high on Social Good. By giving communities collective ownership of land, they create sustained engagement, build inclusive decision-making processes, and generate both bonding and bridging social capital. The Dudley Street Neighborhood Initiative in Boston achieved a Social Good score of 94/100 by transforming a divided, disinvested neighborhood into a thriving, self-governing community.

Dimension 2: Accessibility

Breaking barriers and creating universal access

The Philosophy

True benefit to humanity means ensuring that everyone can participate and benefit, regardless of disability, language, geography, economic status, or any other potential barrier. Accessibility is not an afterthought— it's a core requirement of 弘益人間.

This dimension evaluates multiple forms of accessibility: physical access for people with disabilities, linguistic access through multilingual support, economic access through affordable or free options, digital access across different technologies and internet speeds, and cognitive access through clear communication.

Core Metrics

Universal Design Score
Assesses compliance with accessibility standards (WCAG, ADA, etc.) and goes beyond compliance to evaluate truly inclusive design. Includes testing with diverse users and continuous improvement.

Scale: 0-100, weighted 40% of Accessibility score
Linguistic Accessibility
Measures number of languages supported, quality of translations, cultural appropriateness, and whether support includes both written and spoken forms. Bonus points for supporting indigenous and minority languages.

Scale: 0-100, weighted 30% of Accessibility score
Economic Accessibility
Evaluates pricing structures, free tiers, sliding scales, and financial accessibility for low-income users. Considers both direct costs and indirect costs (technology requirements, data usage, etc.).

Scale: 0-100, weighted 30% of Accessibility score

Excellence Example: Wikipedia

Wikipedia exemplifies accessibility with a score of 96/100. It's free, works on low-bandwidth connections, supports 300+ languages, has extensive screen reader compatibility, and requires no account or personal data. The Wikimedia Foundation continuously improves accessibility based on user feedback.

Dimension 3: Sustainability

Protecting our planet for future generations

The Philosophy

弘益人間 extends beyond people alive today to include future generations. We cannot claim to benefit all humanity if we're destroying the ecological systems that sustain human life. Sustainability measures environmental impact, resource efficiency, and long-term viability.

This dimension embraces not just harm reduction but regenerative practices that actively heal and restore ecosystems. It recognizes that human and planetary health are inextricably linked.

Core Metrics

Carbon Footprint Analysis
Comprehensive assessment of greenhouse gas emissions across the full lifecycle (Scopes 1, 2, and 3). Includes reduction targets, progress tracking, and offset strategies. Bonus for carbon-negative operations.

Scale: 0-100, weighted 35% of Sustainability score
Circular Economy Practices
Evaluates resource efficiency, waste reduction, recycling, upcycling, and product lifecycle design. Measures movement away from linear "take-make-waste" models toward circular systems.

Scale: 0-100, weighted 30% of Sustainability score
Ecosystem Impact
Assesses impact on biodiversity, water systems, soil health, and natural habitats. Includes both direct impacts and supply chain effects. Bonus for regenerative practices that improve ecosystem health.

Scale: 0-100, weighted 35% of Sustainability score

Excellence Example: Interface Carpets

Interface achieved a Sustainability score of 93/100 through their "Mission Zero" and "Climate Take Back" initiatives. They reduced carbon emissions by 96%, achieved carbon-negative carpets, implemented fully circular production, and actively work to reverse climate change. Their model proves profitability and sustainability aren't mutually exclusive.

Dimension 4: Health & Wellbeing

Nurturing physical, mental, and emotional wellness

The Philosophy

Human flourishing requires health in its fullest sense: physical vitality, mental clarity, emotional balance, and spiritual meaning. This dimension recognizes that true benefit to humanity must support holistic wellness, not just the absence of disease.

The Health & Wellbeing dimension evaluates both direct health services and indirect impacts on wellness— including stress levels, work-life balance, mental health support, and conditions that promote or undermine human thriving.

Core Metrics

Physical Health Impact
Measures improvements in physical health outcomes, healthcare access, disease prevention, nutrition, physical activity, and environmental health factors. Includes both direct services and systemic changes.

Scale: 0-100, weighted 35% of Health & Wellbeing score
Mental & Emotional Wellness
Assesses mental health support, stress reduction, emotional resilience, purpose and meaning, and psychological safety. Evaluates both interventions and prevention.

Scale: 0-100, weighted 35% of Health & Wellbeing score
Holistic Wellbeing
Evaluates work-life balance, social connections, spiritual fulfillment, autonomy, and overall life satisfaction. Considers quality of life, not just health metrics.

Scale: 0-100, weighted 30% of Health & Wellbeing score

Excellence Example: Aravind Eye Care System

Aravind achieved a Health & Wellbeing score of 97/100 by providing world-class eye care at minimal or no cost, restoring sight to millions, training healthcare workers, and creating a sustainable model that serves the poorest communities while remaining financially viable. They treat the whole person, not just medical conditions.

💰

Dimension 5: Economic Equity

Ensuring fair distribution of resources and opportunities

The Philosophy

Economic inequality is one of the defining challenges of our era. Economic Equity measures whether work contributes to fair resource distribution, creates genuine opportunity, and ensures economic systems serve humanity rather than concentrating wealth and power.

This dimension recognizes that 弘益人間 requires economic justice. We cannot claim to benefit all humanity while perpetuating systems that enrich a few at the expense of many.

Core Metrics

Wealth Distribution Impact
Measures wage ratios, profit sharing, employee ownership, and impact on wealth inequality. Evaluates both internal practices and broader economic effects.

Scale: 0-100, weighted 35% of Economic Equity score
Opportunity Creation
Assesses jobs created, skills developed, entrepreneurship enabled, and upward mobility facilitated. Special focus on opportunities for underserved communities.

Scale: 0-100, weighted 35% of Economic Equity score
Fair Practices
Evaluates supply chain equity, fair trade practices, living wages, benefits, and working conditions. Includes transparency and accountability mechanisms.

Scale: 0-100, weighted 30% of Economic Equity score

Excellence Example: Mondragon Corporation

This Spanish cooperative achieved an Economic Equity score of 95/100 through worker ownership, capped wage ratios (highest paid earns max 6x lowest), democratic governance, and profit sharing. With 80,000+ worker-owners, they prove large-scale economic democracy is viable.

Dimension 6: Education

Empowering people through knowledge and skills

The Philosophy

Education is perhaps the most powerful multiplier of human benefit. When you educate someone, you empower them to improve their own life, contribute to their community, and educate others. Education dimension measures both formal learning and all forms of knowledge sharing and skill development.

Core Metrics

Learning Quality
Assesses curriculum quality, pedagogical effectiveness, learning outcomes, and long-term knowledge retention. Evaluates both what is taught and how well it's taught.

Scale: 0-100, weighted 40% of Education score
Educational Reach
Measures number of learners, diversity of learners, and accessibility of educational resources. Bonus for reaching underserved populations.

Scale: 0-100, weighted 30% of Education score
Skill Transferability
Evaluates whether skills learned are practical, transferable, and lead to improved life outcomes. Includes employment, entrepreneurship, and community contribution.

Scale: 0-100, weighted 30% of Education score

Excellence Example: Khan Academy

Khan Academy scores 94/100 on Education through free, world-class education for anyone, anywhere. With content in 46 languages, personalized learning, and proven effectiveness, they've educated hundreds of millions while remaining completely free.

Dimension 7: Innovation

Creative solutions to human challenges

The Philosophy

Advancing humanity often requires thinking beyond conventional solutions. Innovation measures breakthrough thinking, creative problem-solving, and novel approaches that open new possibilities for human flourishing.

Core Metrics

Novelty & Originality
Assesses how novel the approach is, whether it represents genuine innovation or iteration, and its potential to shift paradigms.

Scale: 0-100, weighted 30% of Innovation score
Scalability & Replicability
Evaluates whether the innovation can scale to benefit more people and whether others can replicate and adapt it. Open source and knowledge sharing receive bonus points.

Scale: 0-100, weighted 35% of Innovation score
Problem-Solution Fit
Measures how well the innovation addresses real human needs and whether it creates more value than problems. Includes consideration of unintended consequences.

Scale: 0-100, weighted 35% of Innovation score

Excellence Example: CRISPR Gene Editing

CRISPR achieved an Innovation score of 98/100 as a paradigm-shifting technology with potential to cure genetic diseases, improve agriculture, and address countless human challenges. The creators made it open-source, maximizing benefit to humanity.

Integration Across Dimensions

While each dimension is measured independently, the most impactful work excels across multiple dimensions. A regenerative agriculture project, for example, might score high on Sustainability (ecological restoration), Economic Equity (fair pay for farmers), Health (nutritious food), Education (training farmers), and Innovation (new techniques).

The Hongik Impact Score integrates all seven dimensions to provide a holistic assessment of benefit to humanity. The next chapter explores exactly how this measurement works.
