Chapter 4: Implementation Guide

A step-by-step roadmap to measuring and improving your impact on humanity

You understand the philosophy of 弘益人間. You've learned about the seven dimensions of impact. You know how the methodology works. Now it's time to implement the Hongik Impact Metric in your organization.

This chapter provides a practical, step-by-step guide to preparing for assessment, collecting the necessary data, and using the results to enhance your impact on humanity.

Phase 1: Preparation (Weeks 1-2)

Step 1: Build Internal Alignment

Before beginning the assessment process, ensure organizational buy-in and clarity of purpose.

Key Activities:
  • Present 弘익人間 philosophy to leadership and key stakeholders
  • Discuss why measuring impact matters for your organization
  • Identify potential resistance and address concerns proactively
  • Form an impact assessment team (recommended 3-5 people)
  • Allocate necessary time and resources for the process
Frame the assessment as an opportunity for learning and improvement, not judgment. The goal is to enhance your benefit to humanity, not to achieve a particular score.

Step 2: Conduct Baseline Self-Assessment

Use the free WIA self-assessment tool to get a preliminary sense of where you stand across the seven dimensions.

Access the tool at: wia.org/hongik-self-assessment

The self-assessment takes approximately 2-3 hours and provides:

  • Preliminary HIS score estimate
  • Dimension-by-dimension breakdown
  • Identification of data gaps
  • Suggested areas for improvement
  • Comparison with similar organizations

Step 3: Determine Target Certification Level

Based on your self-assessment and organizational readiness, choose which certification level to pursue:

Level When to Pursue Typical Timeline
Bronze New to impact measurement; building foundational practices 1-2 months
Silver Some impact tracking in place; ready to formalize 2-3 months
Gold Strong impact practices; seeking external validation 3-4 months
Platinum Industry leader; comprehensive impact program 4-6 months
Diamond Best-in-class across all dimensions; years of proven impact 6-12 months
Important: It's better to achieve Bronze or Silver certification and improve over time than to stretch for Gold and fall short. You can always upgrade your certification level in future assessment cycles.

Phase 2: Data Collection (Weeks 3-8)

This is typically the most time-intensive phase. You'll gather quantitative data, qualitative evidence, and stakeholder feedback across all seven dimensions.

Dimension 1: Social Good Data

Quantitative Metrics to Collect:
  • Number of people directly served/engaged
  • Number of community connections facilitated
  • Participation rates in community activities
  • Demographic diversity of participants
  • Retention/sustained engagement rates
  • Community assets developed (spaces, resources, etc.)
Qualitative Evidence:
  • Stories of community transformation
  • Examples of collaborative initiatives that emerged
  • Testimonials about increased sense of belonging
  • Evidence of bridging across different groups
  • Documentation of conflict resolution or healing
Stakeholder Feedback:
  • Community member surveys on social cohesion
  • Interviews with diverse participants
  • Partner organization assessments

Dimension 2: Accessibility Data

Quantitative Metrics:
  • WCAG compliance test results (use automated tools + manual testing)
  • Number of languages supported
  • Percentage of users on free/low-cost tiers
  • Geographic distribution of access
  • Bandwidth requirements and low-connectivity accessibility
  • Device compatibility range
Qualitative Evidence:
  • User testing with people with disabilities
  • Feedback from non-native language speakers
  • Stories of barriers removed
  • Evidence of inclusive design process
For WCAG testing, use tools like WAVE, axe DevTools, or Lighthouse for automated testing, but always supplement with actual user testing with people who have disabilities.

Dimension 3: Sustainability Data

Quantitative Metrics:
  • Carbon footprint calculation (Scope 1, 2, and 3 if possible)
  • Energy consumption and renewable energy percentage
  • Waste generated and diversion rates
  • Water usage
  • Supply chain sustainability metrics
  • Product lifecycle and circularity metrics
Qualitative Evidence:
  • Sustainability policies and commitments
  • Innovation in sustainable practices
  • Ecosystem restoration or protection activities
  • Employee engagement in sustainability
Data Collection Example:

Organization: Community Solar Cooperative

They used the following approach for carbon footprint:
  • Scope 1: Direct emissions from owned vehicles (calculated using fuel consumption records)
  • Scope 2: Electricity for office (99% renewable, tracked via utility bills)
  • Scope 3: Supply chain emissions (estimated using industry averages), employee commuting (survey-based), business travel (expense records)
Total carbon footprint: 15 tons CO2e/year
Carbon offset: 2,500 tons CO2e/year (from solar installations)
Net impact: -2,485 tons CO2e/year (carbon negative)

Dimension 4: Health & Wellbeing Data

Quantitative Metrics:
  • Health outcomes improved (disease rates, access metrics, etc.)
  • Mental health support provided (sessions, resources, etc.)
  • Employee wellbeing metrics (if applicable)
  • Stress/burnout indicators
  • Work-life balance measures
  • Wellness program participation
Qualitative Evidence:
  • Health improvement stories
  • Mental health stigma reduction efforts
  • Holistic wellness culture indicators
  • Preventive health initiatives

Dimension 5: Economic Equity Data

Quantitative Metrics:
  • Wage ratios (highest to lowest compensation)
  • Living wage compliance
  • Benefits provided (health insurance, retirement, etc.)
  • Jobs created and their quality
  • Wealth distribution (equity ownership, profit sharing)
  • Supply chain labor practices
  • Support for underserved communities (hiring, services, investment)
Qualitative Evidence:
  • Economic opportunity creation stories
  • Fair trade or ethical sourcing practices
  • Worker voice and democratic governance
  • Economic mobility enabled
Calculate your wage ratio honestly. Many high-impact organizations maintain ratios of 5:1 to 10:1 (highest to lowest), compared to 300:1+ at many large corporations. This metric powerfully demonstrates commitment to economic equity.

Dimension 6: Education Data

Quantitative Metrics:
  • Number of people educated/trained
  • Learning outcomes and completion rates
  • Skill acquisition and certification
  • Knowledge retention measures
  • Educational resources created and accessed
  • Long-term educational impact (career changes, further education, etc.)
Qualitative Evidence:
  • Learning transformation stories
  • Curriculum quality and innovation
  • Pedagogical approaches
  • Accessibility of educational content

Dimension 7: Innovation Data

Quantitative Metrics:
  • Patents, publications, or open-source contributions
  • Novel approaches developed
  • Adoption/replication by others
  • Scalability metrics
  • R&D investment
Qualitative Evidence:
  • Innovation process and culture
  • Breakthrough achievements
  • Interdisciplinary collaboration
  • Knowledge sharing and technology transfer
  • Paradigm-shifting potential

Phase 3: Stakeholder Engagement (Weeks 6-10)

One of the most critical—and often overlooked—aspects of impact measurement is gathering systematic feedback from those your work affects.

Beneficiary Surveys

Design and distribute surveys to people you serve. Best practices:

  • Keep surveys reasonably short (10-15 minutes maximum)
  • Offer in multiple languages
  • Use accessible formats (large print, audio, etc. as needed)
  • Include both quantitative ratings and open-ended questions
  • Ensure anonymity to encourage honesty
  • Consider incentives for participation (gift cards, etc.)
  • Aim for representative sample, not necessarily 100% participation
Key questions to ask:
  • "How has [our work] affected your life?" (open-ended)
  • "On a scale of 1-10, how satisfied are you with...?"
  • "What could we do better?"
  • "Would you recommend us to others? Why or why not?"
  • "What unexpected impacts, positive or negative, have you experienced?"

In-Depth Interviews

Supplement surveys with detailed interviews with a subset of stakeholders:

  • Select diverse interview subjects (different demographics, experiences, etc.)
  • Use semi-structured interview protocol for consistency
  • Record and transcribe (with permission) for accurate analysis
  • Look for patterns and themes across interviews
  • Pay special attention to unexpected findings

Worker/Staff Surveys

Anonymous surveys of employees, volunteers, and contractors assess internal culture and practices:

  • Workplace equity and inclusion
  • Alignment between stated values and lived reality
  • Wellbeing and work-life balance
  • Voice and participation in decision-making
  • Confidence in organization's impact
  • Suggestions for improvement
Common Mistake: Organizations often skip stakeholder engagement or treat it superficially. This is a critical error. Stakeholder perspectives are weighted heavily in the assessment, and for good reason— they're the people who actually experience your impact.

Phase 4: Documentation & Submission (Weeks 11-12)

Compile Your Evidence

Organize all collected data and evidence into the WIA submission format:

  • Quantitative data spreadsheets (use provided templates)
  • Qualitative evidence summaries
  • Stakeholder feedback analysis
  • Supporting documentation (policies, reports, etc.)
  • Case studies and impact stories

Complete the Assessment Application

Submit through the WIA platform at wia.org/hongik-assessment

The application includes:

  • Organization profile and context
  • Dimension-by-dimension data submission
  • Narrative responses to qualitative questions
  • Document uploads
  • Stakeholder feedback summaries
  • Self-assessed preliminary HIS
Budget 15-20 hours for compiling and submitting your application. Start early—the deadline for submission is non-negotiable, and incomplete applications will be returned without assessment.

Phase 5: Assessment & Review (Weeks 13-16)

Once submitted, WIA conducts the formal assessment:

Week Activity Your Role
Week 13 Initial document review Respond to any clarification requests within 48 hours
Week 14 Quantitative data verification Provide additional verification if requested
Week 15 Qualitative assessment and stakeholder validation Facilitate any follow-up stakeholder contacts if needed
Week 16 Final scoring and report preparation Review draft findings, submit any corrections

For Gold, Platinum, and Diamond certifications, this phase may include:

  • Site visits to observe operations firsthand
  • Additional stakeholder interviews conducted by WIA
  • Expert panel review for technical domains
  • Comparative benchmarking against best-in-class organizations

Phase 6: Results & Continuous Improvement (Ongoing)

Receiving Your Results

You'll receive a comprehensive impact report including:

  • Your overall Hongik Impact Score (HIS)
  • Certification level achieved
  • Dimension-by-dimension scores and analysis
  • Strengths identified
  • Growth opportunities
  • Specific recommendations for improvement
  • Benchmark comparisons
  • Certification badge and materials

Using Your Certification

Once certified, you can:

  • Display WIA certification badge on website, marketing materials, and proposals
  • List certification in funding applications and reports
  • Share full impact report with stakeholders
  • Use dimension scores to guide strategic planning
  • Publicize certification to demonstrate commitment to 弘益人間
  • Join WIA community of practice and learning network

Continuous Improvement

The assessment isn't the end—it's the beginning of an ongoing journey:

  • Set improvement targets for each dimension
  • Develop action plans to address identified gaps
  • Track progress using the recommendations provided
  • Engage your team in enhancing impact
  • Prepare for reassessment (required annually for certification maintenance)
  • Consider upgrading to higher certification levels over time
"We didn't achieve Platinum certification in our first assessment—we earned Silver. But the process showed us exactly where to focus. Two years later, we achieved Platinum, and more importantly, we genuinely transformed our impact. The certification mattered less than the journey."

— Amira Hassan, Director, Global Health Collaborative

Common Implementation Challenges & Solutions

Challenge Solution
Limited historical data Start tracking now; use current snapshot plus 6 months forward; pursue Bronze/Silver first
Small team overwhelmed by data needs Focus on most impactful metrics; use existing reports; engage volunteers to help
Stakeholder survey fatigue Keep surveys brief; clearly explain value; offer incentives; integrate with existing feedback
Resistance from leadership Start with self-assessment; share results showing value; emphasize learning not judgment
Uncertainty about dimension scoring Use WIA calculators and examples; attend training workshops; consult with WIA advisors

Timeline Summary

Minimum Timeline: 12-16 weeks from start to certification
Recommended Timeline: 16-20 weeks for thorough, high-quality assessment
Ongoing: Continuous improvement and annual reassessment
