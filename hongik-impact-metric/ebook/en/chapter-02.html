弘益人間

Chapter 2: The Seven Dimensions Explained

Deep dive into the seven fundamental dimensions of human flourishing that comprise the Hongik Impact Metric.

2.1 Understanding the Dimensional Framework

The seven dimensions of the Hongik Impact Metric emerged from extensive research into what constitutes genuine human flourishing. Rather than being arbitrarily chosen, each dimension represents a fundamental aspect of wellbeing consistently identified across cultures, philosophical traditions, and empirical research.

The dimensions are not isolated silos but interconnected elements of a holistic system. Strong performance in one dimension often reinforces others—for example, improved health (Dimension 4) typically enhances educational outcomes (Dimension 6), while greater accessibility (Dimension 2) strengthens social cohesion (Dimension 1). Understanding these interconnections is crucial for maximizing overall impact.

Dimension 1: Social Good (20% Weight)

Definition: The contribution to community welfare, social cohesion, and the strength of human connections and relationships.

Why Social Good Matters

Humans are fundamentally social beings. Research consistently shows that social connection is one of the strongest predictors of happiness, health, and longevity—often more important than income, health behaviors, or other factors. The Harvard Study of Adult Development, which tracked individuals for over 80 years, found that the quality of relationships is the clearest predictor of life satisfaction and health outcomes.

Yet modern society faces a social connection crisis. Loneliness has reached epidemic levels in many countries. Social trust has declined. Community institutions have weakened. Digital technologies promise connection but often deliver isolation. The Social Good dimension asks whether initiatives genuinely strengthen human bonds or merely claim to do so.

Assessment Criteria

1. Community Engagement (25% of Social Good score)

  • Level of community participation in design and implementation
  • Stakeholder consultation and co-creation processes
  • Local ownership and governance structures
  • Community feedback integration mechanisms

2. Social Equity (30% of Social Good score)

  • Reduction of social disparities and discrimination
  • Inclusion of marginalized and underrepresented groups
  • Anti-discrimination measures and policies
  • Equitable distribution of benefits across demographics

3. Public Benefit (25% of Social Good score)

  • Balance between common good and private profit
  • Open access to knowledge and resources
  • Non-excludable benefits (can't exclude people from benefiting)
  • Contribution to collective wellbeing versus individual gain

4. Cultural Impact (20% of Social Good score)

  • Cultural preservation and respect for traditions
  • Celebration and support of diversity
  • Cross-cultural understanding and exchange
  • Protection of cultural heritage and indigenous knowledge
Social Good Score Calculation: SG = (CE × 0.25) + (SE × 0.30) + (PB × 0.25) + (CI × 0.20) Where each component is scored 0-1: CE = Community Engagement score SE = Social Equity score PB = Public Benefit score CI = Cultural Impact score Example: CE = 0.85 (strong community participation) SE = 0.90 (excellent inclusion practices) PB = 0.75 (good public benefit, some commercial aspects) CI = 0.80 (respects and celebrates diversity) SG = (0.85 × 0.25) + (0.90 × 0.30) + (0.75 × 0.25) + (0.80 × 0.20) = 0.2125 + 0.27 + 0.1875 + 0.16 = 0.83 or 83/100

Dimension 2: Accessibility (20% Weight)

Definition: The degree to which benefits are universally available and usable across all populations and circumstances.

Why Accessibility Matters

True benefit to humanity requires that everyone can participate and benefit regardless of their circumstances. Yet barriers—physical, digital, linguistic, economic, geographic—systematically exclude billions of people from opportunities and resources. An innovation that only serves the wealthy, educated, urban, able-bodied, and digitally connected fails the 弘益人間 principle of benefiting ALL humanity.

Accessibility is not charity or accommodation—it's fundamental justice and practical effectiveness. Accessible design often benefits everyone, not just those with disabilities (the "curb-cut effect"). Multi-language support opens opportunities for billions. Low-bandwidth optimization reaches rural communities. Economic accessibility ensures solutions can scale to those who need them most.

Assessment Criteria

1. Physical Accessibility (20%)

  • Wheelchair and mobility device access
  • Sensory accommodations (visual, auditory, tactile)
  • Cognitive accessibility (clarity, simplicity, intuitive design)
  • Adaptive technology compatibility

2. Digital Accessibility (25%)

  • WCAG 2.1 Level AA compliance minimum (AAA preferred)
  • Screen reader and assistive technology compatibility
  • Keyboard navigation and alternative input methods
  • Semantic HTML and accessible rich internet applications (ARIA)

3. Language & Cultural Accessibility (15%)

  • Number of languages supported (weighted by global speaker population)
  • Quality of localization (not just translation)
  • Right-to-left language support and non-Latin scripts
  • Cultural adaptation beyond language

4. Economic Accessibility (20%)

  • Affordability relative to median income in served regions
  • Free tier or subsidized access for low-income users
  • Sliding scale pricing models
  • Open source or freely available components

5. Geographic Accessibility (20%)

  • Rural and remote area availability
  • Offline functionality and data synchronization
  • Low-bandwidth and 2G/3G optimization
  • Minimal infrastructure requirements
Language Coverage Score Formula: LCS = Σ(Li × Pi) / Σ(Pi) Where: Li = 1 if language i is supported, 0 otherwise Pi = Global speaker population for language i Sum over all major languages (top 100 by speakers) Example: Languages supported: English, Spanish, Mandarin, Hindi, Arabic Combined speakers: ~3.5 billion people Global population: ~8 billion LCS = 3.5B / 8B = 0.4375 or 43.75% To achieve 80%+ language coverage (high score): Need ~10-15 languages covering major populations

Dimension 3: Sustainability (15% Weight)

Definition: Environmental impact, resource efficiency, and long-term ecological viability.

Why Sustainability Matters

弘益人間 extends to future generations and to the planetary systems that sustain all life. Solutions that benefit current humans while destroying the environment or depleting resources mortgage our children's future. Climate change, biodiversity loss, and ecosystem degradation threaten the foundation of human civilization.

Sustainability isn't just environmental protection—it's recognition that human wellbeing depends entirely on planetary health. We cannot have healthy humans on a sick planet. Every organization, regardless of sector or mission, has environmental impact that must be measured, minimized, and ultimately reversed.

Assessment Criteria

1. Carbon Footprint (30%)

  • Net carbon emissions (Scope 1, 2, and 3)
  • Renewable energy usage percentage
  • Carbon offset programs and their quality
  • Trajectory toward carbon neutrality or negativity

2. Resource Efficiency (25%)

  • Material usage and waste reduction
  • Circular economy integration (reuse, repair, recycling)
  • Energy efficiency and conservation
  • Water usage and conservation

3. Ecosystem Impact (20%)

  • Biodiversity effects (positive or negative)
  • Habitat preservation or restoration
  • Pollution levels (air, water, soil, noise, light)
  • Chemical and toxic substance management

4. Long-term Viability (15%)

  • Financial sustainability without continuous external funding
  • Organizational resilience and adaptability
  • Succession planning and institutional knowledge
  • Independence from non-renewable resources

5. Adaptability (10%)

  • Climate change resilience and adaptation
  • Technological adaptability and future-proofing
  • Scalability without proportional resource increase
  • Flexibility in changing circumstances
Carbon Footprint Scoring: CF_score = 1 - (actual_emissions / baseline_emissions) For carbon-negative initiatives: CF_score = 1.0 + |net_removal / baseline|, capped at 1.0 Example 1 - Carbon Reduction: Baseline emissions (industry average): 1000 tons CO2/year Actual emissions: 400 tons CO2/year CF_score = 1 - (400/1000) = 0.60 or 60/100 Example 2 - Carbon Negative: Baseline: 1000 tons Net removal: -200 tons (removes from atmosphere) CF_score = 1.0 + (200/1000) = 1.0 (capped)

Dimension 4: Health & Wellbeing (15% Weight)

Definition: Impact on physical health, mental wellness, and emotional balance.

Why Health & Wellbeing Matters

Health is foundational to all other aspects of human flourishing. Without health, opportunities in education, work, and social life become limited or impossible. Yet health must be understood holistically— not just absence of disease but presence of vitality, mental wellness, emotional balance, and the capacity to thrive.

Modern approaches often fragment health (physical vs. mental, treatment vs. prevention, individual vs. population) in ways that miss interconnections. The Health & Wellbeing dimension embraces comprehensive wellbeing while recognizing that different aspects may be more or less relevant depending on the organization's work.

Assessment Criteria

1. Disease Prevention (30%)

  • Morbidity reduction (decreased illness rates)
  • Mortality reduction (lives saved or extended)
  • Quality-Adjusted Life Years (QALYs) gained
  • Preventive care access and utilization

2. Mental Health (25%)

  • Stress reduction and management
  • Depression and anxiety mitigation
  • Social connection and support systems
  • Psychological safety and emotional wellbeing

3. Nutrition & Food Security (15%)

  • Nutritional quality improvement
  • Food access and availability
  • Malnutrition reduction (both under and over-nutrition)
  • Food system sustainability

4. Safety (15%)

  • Injury prevention and reduction
  • Violence reduction (interpersonal, structural, collective)
  • Occupational health and safety improvements
  • Environmental health hazard mitigation

5. Quality of Life (15%)

  • Functional status improvement
  • Pain reduction and management
  • Independence and autonomy enhancement
  • Dignity and self-determination
Quality-Adjusted Life Year (QALY) Calculation: QALY = Σ(Years_lived × Quality_of_life_weight) Quality of life weight ranges from 0 (death) to 1 (perfect health) Example: Intervention extends life by 10 years at 0.8 quality QALY gained = 10 × 0.8 = 8 QALYs Comparison: Program A: 5 years at 1.0 quality = 5 QALYs Program B: 10 years at 0.4 quality = 4 QALYs Program A provides more quality-adjusted benefit despite shorter duration

Dimension 5: Economic Equity (10% Weight)

Definition: Fair distribution of economic benefits, opportunities, and resources.

Why Economic Equity Matters

Economic systems are human creations meant to serve human needs. Yet extreme inequality—where a handful of individuals possess more wealth than billions of others—violates the 弘익人間 principle and creates social instability, health problems, and reduced overall wellbeing even for the wealthy.

Economic equity isn't about enforced equality or eliminating all differences. It's about ensuring economic systems serve humanity rather than concentrating resources in ways that diminish collective flourishing. It's about opportunity, fairness, and preventing poverty while allowing prosperity.

Assessment Criteria

1. Income Equality (35%)

  • Gini coefficient improvement within organization/community
  • Living wage provision (relative to local cost of living)
  • Income distribution across quintiles
  • Pay ratio between highest and lowest compensated

2. Wealth Distribution (25%)

  • Asset ownership dispersion
  • Wealth accumulation opportunities for lower-income groups
  • Intergenerational wealth mobility
  • Ownership structures (employee ownership, cooperatives, etc.)

3. Economic Opportunity (25%)

  • Quality jobs created (stability, benefits, growth potential)
  • Skill development and career mobility support
  • Entrepreneurship support and access to capital
  • Barriers to economic participation removed

4. Poverty Reduction (15%)

  • Extreme poverty ($2.15/day) reduction
  • Moderate poverty reduction relative to local standards
  • Economic vulnerability reduction
  • Social safety net strengthening

Dimension 6: Education & Growth (10% Weight)

Definition: Knowledge sharing, skill development, capacity building, and human potential development.

Assessment Criteria

1. Educational Access (30%)

  • Number of learners reached (weighted by need)
  • Underserved population access
  • Barriers to learning removed
  • Inclusivity across demographics

2. Learning Quality (30%)

  • Learning outcomes and knowledge retention
  • Skill acquisition and competency development
  • Alignment with learner needs and goals
  • Pedagogical effectiveness

3. Knowledge Sharing (20%)

  • Open educational resources
  • Peer learning and collaborative knowledge creation
  • Documentation quality and accessibility
  • Knowledge preservation and dissemination

4. Capacity Building (20%)

  • Teacher and trainer development
  • Institutional strengthening
  • Community of practice creation
  • Sustainable capacity beyond initial intervention

Dimension 7: Innovation & Progress (10% Weight)

Definition: Creative problem-solving, breakthrough thinking, and advancement of human capabilities.

Assessment Criteria

1. Technological Breakthrough (35%)

  • Novelty and originality of approach
  • Technical advancement over state-of-the-art
  • Patent significance and prior art analysis
  • Paradigm shift potential

2. Scientific Contribution (25%)

  • Research publications and citation impact
  • New knowledge creation and validation
  • Methodology advancement
  • Replication and validation by others

3. Creative Innovation (15%)

  • Cultural and artistic contribution
  • Design innovation and user experience
  • Novel approaches to human challenges
  • Aesthetic and experiential advancement

4. Problem-Solving (15%)

  • Complexity of problem addressed
  • Solution effectiveness and efficiency
  • Generalizability to other contexts
  • Elegance and simplicity

5. Future Potential (10%)

  • Scalability of innovation
  • Derivative innovation potential
  • Long-term impact trajectory
  • Enabling effect for future innovations

Key Takeaways

  • The seven dimensions are interconnected, not isolated—strong performance in one often reinforces others through systemic effects.
  • Social Good (20%) and Accessibility (20%) receive the highest weights, reflecting the centrality of human connection and universal benefit to the 弘익人間 philosophy.
  • Each dimension uses weighted sub-criteria to enable nuanced assessment while maintaining consistency across diverse organizations and contexts.
  • Sustainability (15%) and Health & Wellbeing (15%) recognize that planetary health and human health are foundational to all other forms of flourishing.
  • Economic Equity (10%) focuses on fair distribution and opportunity rather than wealth creation alone, addressing systemic inequality.
  • Education (10%) and Innovation (10%) represent multiplier effects—investments that compound over time and enable future benefits.
  • Mathematical formulas provide objective scoring frameworks, but qualitative assessment remains essential for capturing context and nuance.
