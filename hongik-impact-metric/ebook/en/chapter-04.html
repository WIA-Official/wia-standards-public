Chapter 4: Data Collection and Verification

From claims to evidence: Building credible impact measurement through rigorous data practices.

4.1 The Challenge of Impact Data

Impact measurement faces a fundamental credibility challenge. Organizations have strong incentives to overstate their positive impact and understate or ignore negative effects. Without robust data collection and verification processes, impact scores become marketing tools rather than meaningful assessments of genuine human benefit.

The WIA-CORE-005 standard addresses this through a multi-layered approach:

  • Clear data requirements for each dimension and certification level
  • Multiple data sources to cross-validate claims
  • Stakeholder verification involving those directly affected
  • Third-party audits for higher certification levels
  • Transparent methodology enabling external scrutiny
  • Confidence intervals acknowledging uncertainty
"Trust but verify. In impact measurement, we actually need to verify first, then trust. The burden of proof must be on organizations making impact claims, with verification proportional to the magnitude of claims."

— Dr. Marcus Chen, WIA Verification Standards Lead

4.2 Data Source Hierarchy

Not all data is equally reliable. The WIA standard establishes a hierarchy of evidence quality, with higher-quality sources given greater weight in assessment:

Tier Data Source Quality Weight Use Cases
1 Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs) 1.0 Causal impact assessment, program effectiveness
2 Quasi-experimental studies 0.85 Natural experiments, matched comparison groups
3 Longitudinal observational studies 0.75 Tracking change over time, trend analysis
4 Cross-sectional surveys 0.60 Snapshot data, perception measurement
5 Administrative data 0.70 Service delivery counts, resource allocation
6 Self-reported data (verified) 0.50 Organizational metrics, third-party confirmed
7 Self-reported data (unverified) 0.30 Bronze certification only, must be conservative

Organizations should use the highest-quality data sources feasible given their resources and operational context. Higher certification levels require higher-tier evidence.

4.3 Required Data by Dimension

Social Good Data Requirements

Quantitative Metrics:

  • Number of community members actively engaged in governance/decision-making
  • Diversity metrics across served population (age, gender, ethnicity, ability, income)
  • Retention rates (sustained engagement over time)
  • Social network analysis data (if applicable)
  • Participation rates in community activities

Qualitative Evidence:

  • Stakeholder testimonials (minimum 25 for Bronze, 100+ for Gold)
  • Case studies demonstrating community strengthening
  • Documentation of co-creation processes
  • Cultural impact assessment reports

Verification Methods:

  • Third-party interviews with community members
  • Review of governance meeting minutes and records
  • Analysis of demographic data against regional baselines
  • Community validation sessions

Accessibility Data Requirements

Physical Accessibility:

  • Accessibility audit results (professional assessment for Silver+)
  • Compliance checklist completion rates
  • User feedback from people with disabilities
  • Assistive technology compatibility testing results

Digital Accessibility:

  • WCAG 2.1 automated testing results (tools: axe, WAVE, Lighthouse)
  • Manual accessibility testing documentation
  • Screen reader compatibility verification
  • Keyboard navigation testing results
WCAG Compliance Verification Example: Automated Testing (30% of accessibility score): - axe DevTools: 0 critical issues, 2 moderate issues - WAVE: Accessibility score 95/100 - Lighthouse: Accessibility 92/100 Manual Testing (70% of accessibility score): - Screen reader navigation: Full compatibility (NVDA, JAWS) - Keyboard-only navigation: 100% functional - Color contrast: WCAG AAA compliance - Alternative text: 98% coverage, quality verified - Focus indicators: Clearly visible throughout Overall Digital Accessibility Score: 0.94 (94/100)

Language & Cultural Accessibility:

  • List of supported languages with native speaker verification
  • Translation quality assessment (professional evaluation for Gold+)
  • Cultural adaptation documentation beyond literal translation
  • User feedback from non-English speakers

Economic Accessibility:

  • Pricing structure documentation
  • Free/subsidized tier usage data
  • Cost relative to median income in served regions
  • Scholarship/assistance program data

Geographic Accessibility:

  • Geographic distribution of users/beneficiaries
  • Rural vs. urban penetration rates
  • Offline functionality testing results
  • Bandwidth usage analysis (testing on 2G/3G)

Sustainability Data Requirements

Carbon Footprint:

  • Scope 1 emissions: Direct emissions (required for all)
  • Scope 2 emissions: Purchased energy (required for all)
  • Scope 3 emissions: Value chain (required for Gold+)
  • Carbon accounting methodology documentation
  • Third-party verification (Silver+)

Resource Efficiency:

  • Waste generation and diversion rates
  • Energy consumption per unit of output
  • Water usage and conservation measures
  • Material circularity metrics (reuse, recycling, repair)

Ecosystem Impact:

  • Biodiversity assessments (for organizations with environmental footprint)
  • Pollution monitoring data (air, water, soil)
  • Habitat restoration activities and results
  • Chemical/toxin management records

Health & Wellbeing Data Requirements

Quantitative Health Outcomes:

  • Morbidity rates (illness incidence/prevalence)
  • Mortality data (age-adjusted, cause-specific)
  • QALY calculations with methodology documentation
  • Health service utilization rates

Mental Health Metrics:

  • Validated assessment tools (PHQ-9, GAD-7, WHO-5, etc.)
  • Stress level measurements (PSS, job strain models)
  • Social connection indices
  • Psychological safety surveys

Safety Data:

  • Injury rates and severity (OSHA-compliant reporting)
  • Violence/conflict incidence
  • Safety training completion rates
  • Environmental health hazard assessments

Economic Equity Data Requirements

Income and Wealth Data:

  • Wage ratios (highest to lowest paid, median to lowest)
  • Living wage analysis relative to local cost of living
  • Income distribution across quintiles/deciles
  • Wealth/ownership dispersion metrics

Opportunity Metrics:

  • Jobs created with quality indicators (full-time, benefits, stability)
  • Internal promotion rates
  • Training investment per employee
  • Entrepreneurship support outcomes

Education & Growth Data Requirements

Learning Outcomes:

  • Pre/post knowledge assessments
  • Skill acquisition validation
  • Competency-based evaluations
  • Long-term application of learning (6-12 month follow-up)

Access Metrics:

  • Number of learners by demographic categories
  • Comparison to underserved population baselines
  • Completion rates
  • Barriers removed documentation

Innovation & Progress Data Requirements

Novelty Assessment:

  • Prior art search and analysis
  • Patent landscaping
  • Literature review documenting state-of-the-art
  • Expert evaluation of advancement

Scientific Contribution:

  • Publication data with citation metrics
  • Peer review outcomes
  • Replication studies by independent researchers
  • Methodology validation documentation

4.4 Stakeholder Feedback Collection

Stakeholder feedback is essential—those affected by the work must have voice in assessing its impact. The standard establishes minimum requirements that increase with certification level:

Certification Level Minimum Responses OR Percentage Methods Required
Bronze 25 10% of users Surveys (accessible format)
Silver 100 20% of users Surveys + interviews (10+)
Gold 200 30% of users Surveys + interviews (20+) + focus groups
Platinum 500 40% of users Multi-method + WIA interviews (50+)
Diamond 1000+ 50% of users Comprehensive + WIA interviews (100+)

Survey Design Principles

  • Accessibility: Multiple languages, audio options, simple language
  • Brevity: 5-10 minutes maximum completion time
  • Neutrality: Avoid leading questions or biased framing
  • Anonymity: Optional anonymous responses to encourage honesty
  • Validation: Include attention checks and consistency validation
  • Open-ended: Include qualitative questions for context

4.5 Verification Processes by Certification Level

Bronze Certification Verification

  • Self-assessment with good faith attestation
  • Document review by single WIA assessor
  • Clarification questions as needed
  • Basic data quality checks
  • No site visit required
  • Timeline: 2-4 weeks

Silver Certification Verification

  • Third-party verification of key metrics (carbon, major outcomes)
  • Comprehensive document review by dual assessors
  • Stakeholder interviews (10+) conducted by WIA
  • Optional site visit if organization requests
  • Consensus review between assessors
  • Timeline: 4-8 weeks

Gold Certification Verification

  • Third-party verification of ALL quantitative metrics
  • Mandatory site visit (1-2 days)
  • Stakeholder interviews (20+) by WIA
  • Expert panel review for technical dimensions
  • Triple assessor review with consensus requirement
  • Comparative benchmarking analysis
  • Timeline: 16-20 weeks

Platinum Certification Verification

  • Independent third-party audit of all impact claims
  • Extended site visit (3-5 days) including operations observation
  • Extensive stakeholder interviews (50+)
  • Expert panels across all relevant domains
  • Supply chain assessment (if applicable)
  • Unintended consequences analysis
  • Senior assessor team review
  • Timeline: 20-24 weeks

Diamond Certification Verification

  • Continuous independent monitoring
  • Week-long comprehensive site visit
  • Global stakeholder engagement (100+ interviews)
  • Multi-disciplinary expert panels
  • Academic research review of impact claims
  • Comparative analysis with historical high-performers
  • WIA Board-level review and approval
  • External validation from field experts
  • Timeline: 24-48 weeks

⚠️ Common Data Quality Issues

  • Selection bias: Only surveying satisfied beneficiaries
  • Attribution errors: Claiming credit for changes caused by other factors
  • Measurement timing: Measuring immediately after intervention before effects manifest
  • Survivorship bias: Only tracking those who remain engaged, ignoring dropouts
  • Gaming metrics: Optimizing for measurement rather than genuine impact
  • Insufficient sample size: Drawing conclusions from too little data
  • Lack of comparison: No baseline or control group for context

Organizations must proactively address these issues through rigorous methodology.

Key Takeaways

  • Data quality varies significantly—RCTs provide highest-quality evidence (1.0 weight), while unverified self-reports receive lowest weight (0.3) and are only acceptable for Bronze certification.
  • Each dimension requires both quantitative metrics and qualitative evidence, with specific requirements increasing at higher certification levels.
  • Stakeholder feedback is mandatory, with minimum response thresholds ranging from 25 (Bronze) to 1000+ (Diamond) and methodological rigor increasing with certification level.
  • Carbon footprint measurement must follow GHG Protocol standards, with Scope 1&2 required for all and Scope 3 required for Gold+ certification.
  • Accessibility verification requires both automated testing tools and manual assessment, with professional audits required for Silver+ certification.
  • Verification processes scale with certification level, from simple document review (Bronze) to continuous monitoring with academic validation (Diamond).
  • Common data quality issues like selection bias, attribution errors, and gaming metrics must be proactively addressed through rigorous methodology and third-party verification.
