Chapter 3: Measurement Methodology

How we calculate the Hongik Impact Score with scientific rigor and practical application

Measuring impact on humanity is inherently challenging. How do you quantify social good? How do you compare healthcare access improvements with educational reach? How do you balance short-term metrics with long-term outcomes?

This chapter details the methodology WIA-CORE-005 uses to address these challenges. The approach balances scientific rigor with practical applicability, ensuring measurements are both meaningful and feasible for organizations of all sizes.

Core Principles of Measurement

The methodology is built on five foundational principles:

1. Multi-Method Triangulation
No single metric tells the whole story. We use quantitative data, qualitative assessments, stakeholder feedback, expert evaluation, and comparative benchmarking to triangulate toward truth.
2. Context Sensitivity
Impact must be assessed in context. A small community organization serving 500 people deeply may have more impact than a tech company reaching millions superficially. Context matters.
3. Long-Term Orientation
True impact reveals itself over time. We assess both immediate effects and sustained, long-term outcomes. Short-term gains that create long-term problems receive lower scores.
4. Stakeholder-Centric
Those most affected by the work—beneficiaries, workers, communities—have the most important perspective. Their voices are central to assessment.
5. Continuous Improvement
Measurement is not about judgment but improvement. The goal is to help organizations enhance their impact on humanity over time.

The Calculation Framework

Step 1: Dimension-Level Scoring

Each of the seven dimensions is scored on a 0-100 scale. The dimension score is calculated by weighting multiple metrics according to importance:

Dimension Score = Σ (Metric Score × Weight)

For example, the Social Good dimension combines:

Metric Weight Description
Community Strength Index 30% Depth and resilience of connections
Inclusion Score 35% Equity and representation
Social Capital Generated 35% Networks and trust created
Example Calculation:

Organization: Community Health Cooperative
Dimension: Social Good

Community Strength Index: 85/100 × 0.30 = 25.5
Inclusion Score: 78/100 × 0.35 = 27.3
Social Capital Generated: 92/100 × 0.35 = 32.2
Social Good Dimension Score: 85.0/100

Step 2: Calculating the Hongik Impact Score (HIS)

The overall HIS is calculated by combining all seven dimension scores with appropriate weighting and scaling. The base formula is:

HIS = (Σ Dimension Scores) × Scale Factor × Context Multiplier

Standard Dimension Weights:

Dimension Base Weight Rationale
Social Good 15% Foundation of human wellbeing
Accessibility 14% Critical for universal benefit
Sustainability 16% Essential for future generations
Health & Wellbeing 15% Core human need
Economic Equity 14% Enables all other dimensions
Education 14% Multiplier of impact
Innovation 12% Accelerates progress

The scale factor converts the 0-100 dimension scores to the 0-1000+ HIS scale. The standard scale factor is 1.43, though this can be adjusted based on organization type and context.

Complete HIS Calculation Example:

Organization: GreenTech Education Initiative

Social Good: 85 × 0.15 = 12.75
Accessibility: 78 × 0.14 = 10.92
Sustainability: 72 × 0.16 = 11.52
Health & Wellbeing: 81 × 0.15 = 12.15
Economic Equity: 76 × 0.14 = 10.64
Education: 92 × 0.14 = 12.88
Innovation: 83 × 0.12 = 9.96
Weighted Sum: 80.82
HIS (× 1.43 scale factor): 115.6 → 567/1000
Result: HIS of 567 = Gold Certification Level

Step 3: Context Multipliers

Context multipliers adjust scores based on operational context. These ensure fair comparison between different types of organizations:

Context Factor Multiplier Range Application
Resource Constraints 1.0 - 1.15 Organizations operating with limited resources get credit for efficiency
Geographic Challenge 1.0 - 1.12 Work in remote, conflict-affected, or otherwise challenging regions
Population Served 1.0 - 1.10 Serving especially marginalized or underserved populations
Systemic Change 1.0 - 1.20 Work that changes systems, not just symptoms
Important: Context multipliers are capped at a combined maximum of 1.35 to prevent gaming the system. They're applied after dimension scoring but before final HIS calculation.

Data Collection Methods

Quantitative Data

Organizations submit verifiable quantitative metrics through the WIA assessment platform:

  • Number of people served/reached
  • Resources distributed or mobilized
  • Carbon footprint data and reduction metrics
  • Accessibility compliance test results
  • Economic indicators (wages, wealth distribution, etc.)
  • Education outcomes (completion rates, skill acquisition, etc.)
  • Health outcomes and service delivery metrics

All quantitative data must be verifiable through third-party sources, audited financials, or independent verification. Self-reported data without verification receives lower weighting.

Qualitative Assessment

Trained assessors conduct qualitative evaluations through:

  • Document Review: Analysis of organizational materials, impact reports, case studies
  • Site Visits: Direct observation of operations and impact (for higher certification levels)
  • Interviews: Structured conversations with leadership, staff, and stakeholders
  • Process Evaluation: Assessment of systems, culture, and practices beyond measurable outputs

Stakeholder Feedback

Perhaps most important, we gather systematic feedback from those most affected:

Beneficiary Surveys
Structured surveys with people served, using culturally appropriate methods and languages. Focus on perceived impact, satisfaction, and suggested improvements.
Worker/Staff Input
Anonymous surveys and interviews with employees, volunteers, and contractors about organizational culture, equity, and alignment with stated values.
Community Consultation
Engagement with broader community members, including those not directly served, to assess community-level impacts and relationships.
Expert Review
Domain experts evaluate technical quality and best practice alignment within specific fields (healthcare, education, environmental science, etc.).

Temporal Considerations

Impact unfolds over time. The methodology accounts for this through:

Snapshot vs. Longitudinal

Initial certification uses a snapshot assessment—current state and recent history (past 2-3 years). Renewal and higher certification levels require longitudinal data showing sustained impact over time.

Certification Level Time Period Required Assessment Frequency
Bronze 1 year history Annual review
Silver 2 years history Annual review
Gold 3 years history Biannual review
Platinum 5 years history Annual in-depth review
Diamond 7+ years history Continuous monitoring

Leading vs. Lagging Indicators

The methodology balances:

  • Lagging Indicators: Concrete outcomes already achieved (60% weight)
  • Leading Indicators: Processes and systems likely to produce future impact (40% weight)

This prevents organizations from scoring well based solely on good intentions while also rewarding strong systems that haven't yet produced full outcomes.

Quality Assurance

Multiple mechanisms ensure measurement integrity:

Inter-Rater Reliability
Multiple assessors independently evaluate qualitative factors. High agreement required for final scores. Disagreements trigger additional review.
Calibration Sessions
Assessors regularly calibrate their evaluations using standard cases to ensure consistency across evaluators and over time.
Third-Party Verification
Organizations seeking Gold, Platinum, or Diamond certification must have key metrics verified by independent third parties.
Appeals Process
Organizations can appeal scores they believe are inaccurate, with appeals reviewed by senior assessors not involved in the original evaluation.
Continuous Methodology Review
The WIA Research Committee reviews methodology annually, incorporating learnings, stakeholder feedback, and evolving best practices.

Practical Considerations

Assessment Burden

The methodology is designed to be rigorous without being burdensome:

  • Self-Assessment Option: Organizations can start with a free self-assessment tool
  • Tiered Depth: Bronze certification requires less documentation than Diamond
  • Existing Data: Methodology accepts existing reports and metrics rather than requiring new data collection
  • Support Provided: WIA provides templates, calculators, and guidance throughout the process
