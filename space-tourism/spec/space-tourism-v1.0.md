# WIA-SPACE-014: Space Tourism Standards v1.0

**Standard ID:** WIA-SPACE-014
**Title:** Space Tourism Standards
**Version:** 1.0
**Status:** Published
**Date:** 2025-12-28
**Organization:** World Certification Industry Association (WIA)
**Philosophy:** 弘益人間 (홍익인간) - Benefit All Humanity

## Abstract

This standard establishes comprehensive guidelines, best practices, and technical specifications for commercial space tourism operations. It covers three primary categories of space tourism (suborbital, orbital, and lunar), addressing safety protocols, training requirements, medical standards, legal frameworks, business models, and future development pathways.

## 1. Scope

### 1.1 Applicability

This standard applies to:
- Commercial space tourism operators (Virgin Galactic, Blue Origin, SpaceX, Axiom Space, etc.)
- Spaceport operators and ground support services
- Training facilities and medical screening providers
- Regulatory bodies overseeing commercial spaceflight
- Insurance providers for space tourism
- Space tourism customers and participants
- Equipment manufacturers for tourism spacecraft

### 1.2 Categories Covered

1. **Suborbital Tourism:** Flights reaching 80-110 km altitude with brief weightlessness (Virgin Galactic, Blue Origin)
2. **Orbital Tourism:** Missions to Low Earth Orbit (LEO) lasting days to weeks (SpaceX, Axiom Space)
3. **Lunar Tourism:** Missions to the Moon including flybys, orbit, and surface landings (future)

### 1.3 Exclusions

This standard does not cover:
- Government astronaut programs
- Military space operations
- Scientific research missions without tourism component
- Cargo-only spaceflights
- Deep space missions beyond cislunar space (Mars missions addressed separately)

## 3. Terms and Definitions

### 3.1 Space Tourism Participant
A private individual who pays to travel to space for recreational, educational, or personal reasons, not as a professional astronaut or crew member.

### 3.2 Suborbital Flight
A spaceflight that reaches space (>80 km or >100 km depending on definition) but does not achieve orbital velocity, following a ballistic trajectory.

### 3.3 Orbital Flight
A spaceflight achieving sufficient velocity (~7.8 km/s) to orbit Earth, maintaining continuous freefall around the planet.

### 3.4 Karman Line
The internationally recognized boundary of space at 100 km (62 miles) altitude.

### 3.5 Microgravity
The condition of apparent weightlessness experienced in orbit or during freefall, typically <0.001 g.

### 3.6 Space Adaptation Syndrome (SAS)
Also called "space sickness," a temporary condition affecting 40-75% of first-time space travelers, characterized by nausea, disorientation, and headache.

### 3.7 Informed Consent
Documented acknowledgment by space tourism participants that they understand the risks of spaceflight, including that vehicles are not certified as safe by government authorities.

### 3.8 Maximum Probable Loss (MPL)
The worst-case financial loss scenario for a given space mission, used to calculate required insurance coverage.

### 3.9 Cross-Waiver of Liability
Agreement among parties (operator, participants, government, contractors) not to sue each other for injuries or damages arising from space operations.

### 3.10 Overview Effect
The profound cognitive shift in awareness reported by people who view Earth from space, characterized by increased environmental concern and sense of global unity.

## 4. Space Tourism Types and Specifications

### 4.1 Suborbital Tourism

**Altitude:** 80-110 km (50-68 miles)
**Duration:** 11-90 minutes total flight time
**Weightlessness:** 3-4 minutes
**G-Forces:** 3-6 G during ascent/descent
**Training Required:** 2-3 days
**Price Range (2025):** $200,000 - $500,000

**Primary Providers:**
- Virgin Galactic (SpaceShipTwo): Air-launched, 6 passengers + 2 pilots, ~86 km altitude
- Blue Origin (New Shepard): Vertical launch, 6 passengers (autonomous), ~107 km altitude

**Key Standards:**
- Pre-flight medical screening (basic cardiovascular health)
- Safety briefing and emergency procedures training
- G-force exposure limited to 6 G maximum
- Informed consent documentation required
- FAA launch license compliance

### 4.2 Orbital Tourism

**Altitude:** 400-600 km (250-375 miles)
**Duration:** 3-14 days typical
**Orbit Type:** Low Earth Orbit (LEO)
**G-Forces:** 3-5 G during launch/reentry
**Training Required:** 3-6 months
**Price Range (2025):** $50-60 million per seat

**Primary Providers:**
- SpaceX (Crew Dragon + Falcon 9): 4-7 passengers, autonomous with manual capability
- Axiom Space (using Crew Dragon): ISS visits, 8-14 day missions
- Space Adventures (Soyuz): Russian spacecraft, 3-person capacity

**Key Standards:**
- Comprehensive medical evaluation including:
  - Cardiovascular stress test
  - Bone density scan (DEXA)
  - Vestibular function assessment
  - Psychological evaluation
- Extended training program:
  - Spacecraft systems familiarization
  - Emergency procedures (abort, decompression, fire)
  - Zero-gravity adaptation (parabolic flights)
  - Centrifuge training for G-forces
  - Telemedicine protocols
- Radiation exposure monitoring (dosimetry)
- Post-flight medical follow-up

### 4.3 Lunar Tourism (Future)

**Distance:** 380,000+ km to Moon
**Duration:** 6-14 days typical
**Mission Types:** Flyby, orbit, surface landing
**Training Required:** 6-12 months
**Price Range (Projected):** $100 million+ initially, decreasing with infrastructure

**Development Status:** Dear Moon mission (SpaceX) planned for 2025+

**Key Standards (Anticipated):**
- Peak physical and psychological health requirements
- Extended radiation exposure protocols (10-20 mSv expected)
- Deep space communication standards
- Lunar-specific emergency procedures
- Environmental protection of celestial bodies

## 5. Safety Standards

### 5.1 Vehicle Requirements

All space tourism vehicles SHALL:
- Undergo rigorous testing including unmanned test flights
- Implement triple redundancy for critical systems (life support, propulsion, communications)
- Include abort capability from pad through ascent
- Provide emergency life support for 24+ hours beyond nominal mission duration
- Maintain detailed flight records and anomaly tracking
- Implement continuous improvement based on lessons learned

### 5.2 Medical Screening

**Suborbital Missions:**
- Blood pressure controlled below 140/90 mmHg
- Normal EKG and cardiovascular function
- Ability to climb stairs and tolerate 3-6 G forces
- Age 18+, no upper limit if healthy
- Medical clearance from approved physician

**Orbital Missions:**
- All suborbital requirements PLUS:
- Comprehensive cardiovascular evaluation (echocardiogram, stress test)
- Bone density scan (DEXA) for baseline
- Vestibular function testing
- Complete blood panel and organ function tests
- Psychological evaluation for extended isolation
- No pregnancy (confirmed before flight)

### 5.3 Radiation Protection

**Exposure Limits:**
- Suborbital: <0.002 mSv (negligible)
- LEO Orbital (3 days): 0.5-1.0 mSv
- ISS Visit (14 days): 2-5 mSv
- Lunar Mission (7 days): 10-20 mSv (estimated)

**Protection Measures:**
- Spacecraft shielding (aluminum, composite materials)
- Mission timing to avoid solar particle events
- Real-time space weather monitoring
- Personal dosimetry for all participants
- Post-flight dose verification

**Reference Limits:**
- Annual natural background: 2-3 mSv
- Single transcontinental flight: 0.03 mSv
- Occupational worker annual limit: 50 mSv
- Space tourism missions well within safe limits

### 5.4 Emergency Protocols

All space tourism operations SHALL maintain:
- Documented emergency response procedures
- 24/7 mission control with flight surgeon
- Abort capability throughout launch and ascent
- Emergency medical equipment appropriate to mission duration
- Telemedicine capability for orbital missions
- Search and rescue coordination
- Contingency landing sites

## 6. Training Standards

### 6.1 Suborbital Training (2-3 days)

**Required Components:**
- Spacecraft familiarization and safety briefing (4-8 hours)
- G-force preparation and coping techniques (2-4 hours)
- Emergency procedures practice (2-4 hours)
- Mission simulation in high-fidelity simulator (1-2 hours)
- Medical final check (1 hour)

**Optional Components:**
- Parabolic zero-G flights
- Centrifuge training
- Extended orientation

### 6.2 Orbital Training (3-6 months)

**Required Components:**
- Spacecraft systems training (40-80 hours)
- Emergency procedures and abort scenarios (40-60 hours)
- Zero-gravity adaptation:
  - Parabolic flights (3-5 sessions)
  - Neutral buoyancy (if EVA planned)
- Centrifuge training for launch/reentry (5-10 sessions)
- Space suit operations (20-40 hours)
- Science experiments and research protocols (if applicable)
- Russian language basics (for ISS missions)
- Psychological preparation and team building
- Final mission simulation (full duration)

### 6.3 Crew Qualification Standards

Crew members (pilots, commanders) SHALL meet higher standards:
- Professional astronaut training or equivalent
- Minimum flight hours in spacecraft type
- Emergency response certification
- Medical certification to higher standard
- Regular recertification (annual or per mission)

## 7. Legal and Regulatory Framework

### 7.1 Informed Consent Requirements

Operators SHALL provide written disclosure including:
- Government has not certified spacecraft as safe
- Risks include death or serious injury
- List of known hazards specific to the vehicle
- Safety record of vehicle type (test flights, incidents)
- No guarantee of mission success or completion
- Participant signature acknowledging understanding

### 7.2 Liability Framework

**Cross-Waiver Standard:**
- All participants SHALL sign cross-waiver of liability
- Waiver covers operator, government, contractors, other participants
- Exceptions: gross negligence, willful misconduct, criminal acts
- Applies to participant, heirs, estates, and family members

**Insurance Requirements:**
- Third-party liability insurance to Maximum Probable Loss (MPL)
- Typical MPL: $100 million - $500 million per mission
- Government indemnification above MPL (U.S.: $1.5 billion cap)
- Optional participant life insurance

### 7.3 Launch Licensing

Operators SHALL obtain:
- Launch license from national authority (FAA in U.S.)
- Environmental impact assessment approval
- Range safety approval
- Payload review (if applicable)
- Frequency allocation for communications
- International coordination for trajectory

### 7.4 Export Control Compliance

Operations SHALL comply with:
- ITAR (International Traffic in Arms Regulations) for U.S. technology
- Export Administration Regulations (EAR)
- National security requirements
- Background checks for participants (if required)

## 8. Environmental Standards

### 8.1 Launch Site Impact

Operators SHALL:
- Conduct environmental impact assessment
- Minimize noise impact on communities and wildlife
- Monitor air and water quality
- Protect endangered species and habitats
- Implement mitigation measures for identified impacts

### 8.2 Space Debris Mitigation

Orbital operations SHALL:
- Limit debris release during normal operations
- Plan end-of-life deorbit within 25 years (LEO)
- Minimize breakup potential
- Report orbital parameters to tracking authorities
- Coordinate to avoid collisions with existing objects

### 8.3 Emissions and Sustainability

Operators SHOULD:
- Use cleanest propellants practical (hydrogen/oxygen preferred)
- Implement carbon offset programs
- Research and develop green propulsion alternatives
- Monitor and report environmental impact metrics
- Participate in industry sustainability initiatives

## 9. Business and Economic Standards

### 9.1 Pricing Transparency

Operators SHALL:
- Clearly disclose total price and what is included
- Specify any additional fees (training, medical, insurance)
- Provide refund/cancellation policy in writing
- Honor pricing commitments (subject to force majeure)

### 9.2 Consumer Protection

Operators SHALL:
- Maintain financial reserves for refunds
- Provide clear timeline and schedule (subject to safety scrubs)
- Honor medical disqualification refunds (per policy)
- Maintain privacy of participant medical information (HIPAA compliance)
- Provide authentic safety record information

### 9.3 Revenue Diversification

Sustainable business models SHOULD include:
- Flight ticket sales (primary revenue)
- Research and microgravity services
- Media rights and sponsorships
- Training and educational programs
- Government contracts for complementary services
- Merchandise and ancillary services

## 10. Future Development Standards

### 10.1 Space Hotels and Habitats

Orbital accommodation facilities SHALL:
- Meet all orbital tourism standards
- Provide life support for rated capacity plus margin
- Include artificial gravity capability OR countermeasures for prolonged stays
- Implement fire suppression and emergency evacuation
- Provide medical facilities appropriate to capacity
- Plan for end-of-life deorbit

### 10.2 Lunar Tourism Operations

Lunar missions SHALL:
- Comply with Artemis Accords principles
- Protect historic Apollo landing sites
- Minimize environmental impact on lunar surface
- Implement planetary protection protocols
- Establish safety zones around operational areas
- Plan for long-term sustainability

### 10.3 Technology Development

Industry SHOULD invest in:
- Reusable vehicle technology (target 50-100 flights per vehicle)
- Green propulsion alternatives
- Improved radiation shielding
- Artificial gravity systems
- Advanced life support (closed-loop)
- Autonomous operations and AI assistance

## 11. Compliance and Certification

### 11.1 Self-Certification

During industry learning period, operators:
- Self-certify compliance with safety standards
- Document all deviations and justifications
- Implement continuous improvement programs
- Share lessons learned with industry

### 11.2 Future Certification (Post-Learning Period)

Expected regulatory evolution:
- Government certification of vehicle design
- Crew qualification standards
- Operational procedure approvals
- Regular safety audits
- Industry-wide best practices codification

### 11.3 International Harmonization

Industry SHALL work toward:
- Consistent safety standards across nations
- Mutual recognition of licenses and certifications
- Unified passenger rights framework
- Coordinated space traffic management
- Shared incident reporting and analysis

## 12. Philosophy and Principles

### 12.1 弘益人間 (Benefit All Humanity)

All space tourism activities SHOULD embody this philosophy by:
- Advancing technology that benefits all people
- Inspiring global cooperation and understanding
- Democratizing access to space over time
- Promoting environmental stewardship of Earth
- Expanding human civilization sustainably
- Supporting scientific research and education
- Creating economic opportunity and jobs
- Fostering the Overview Effect awareness

### 12.2 Core Principles

1. **Safety First:** No mission proceeds if safety is compromised
2. **Transparency:** Honest communication of risks and capabilities
3. **Accessibility:** Work toward decreasing costs to expand access
4. **Sustainability:** Minimize environmental impact, plan for long term
5. **Cooperation:** Share knowledge and lessons learned
6. **Innovation:** Continuously improve technology and processes
7. **Responsibility:** Protect participants, public, and environment
8. **Inspiration:** Catalyze human progress through space exploration
