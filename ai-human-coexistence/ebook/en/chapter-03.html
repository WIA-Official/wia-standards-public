Chapter 3: Ethical Considerations & Values

3.1 The Moral Landscape of AI

The development and deployment of AI systems raises profound ethical questions that humanity has never faced before. As AI becomes increasingly capable and autonomous, we must grapple with fundamental issues about moral agency, responsibility, rights, and the very nature of the good life in a world shared with intelligent machines.

Ethics in AI is not merely an academic exercise or a box to check for regulatory compliance. The ethical choices embedded in AI systems shape the experiences, opportunities, and outcomes for billions of people. Poor ethical design can perpetuate injustice, erode human dignity, and undermine the social fabric. Thoughtful ethical design can promote flourishing, extend opportunity, and embody the best of human values.

The WIA-AI-HUMAN-COEXISTENCE standard approaches ethics as integral to every aspect of AI design and operation, not as an afterthought or external constraint. This chapter explores the ethical foundations that must guide human-AI coexistence.

3.2 Ethical Frameworks for AI

Multiple ethical traditions offer valuable perspectives for AI development. Rather than adopting a single framework, the WIA standard draws on complementary traditions:

Framework Core Principle Application to AI
Consequentialism Maximize good outcomes Evaluate AI by its effects on well-being
Deontology Follow moral duties AI must respect rights, never use people merely as means
Virtue Ethics Cultivate good character AI should embody and promote virtues
Care Ethics Maintain relationships AI design should prioritize care and connection
Ubuntu I am because we are AI should strengthen community bonds
Hongik Ingan Benefit all humanity AI should serve the common good broadly

3.3 Value Alignment

Perhaps the most fundamental challenge in AI ethics is value alignment: ensuring that AI systems pursue goals and exhibit behaviors consistent with human values. This challenge has multiple dimensions:

Specification Problem

How do we precisely specify human values in ways that AI systems can understand and implement? Human values are often implicit, context-dependent, and sometimes contradictory. Simple objective functions frequently fail to capture what we truly care about, leading to unexpected and undesirable behaviors when AI optimizes the specified objective.

Aggregation Problem

Whose values should AI systems align with? Different individuals, communities, and cultures hold different values. There is no universal consensus on many ethical questions. AI systems must navigate value pluralism while still making concrete decisions.

Stability Problem

How do we ensure that AI systems maintain alignment as they learn and evolve? A system that begins well-aligned might drift as it encounters new situations or modifies its behavior through learning. Maintaining alignment over time requires ongoing vigilance.

Verification Problem

How do we verify that AI systems are actually aligned with intended values? Complex AI systems are often opaque, making it difficult to determine whether they will behave ethically in novel situations. Testing can never cover all possible scenarios.

3.4 Core Ethical Principles

The WIA standard articulates core ethical principles that should govern all aspects of human-AI coexistence:

Beneficence

AI systems should actively contribute to human well-being and the common good. This goes beyond merely avoiding harm to require positive contribution. AI development should be directed toward addressing genuine human needs and expanding human capabilities. The principle of beneficence aligns with the philosophy of 弘益人間 - using AI to benefit all humanity.

Non-Maleficence

AI systems must not cause harm to humans or society. This includes direct physical harm, psychological harm, economic harm, and harm to human relationships and institutions. When harm cannot be entirely eliminated, it must be minimized and proportionate to benefits. AI systems should never be designed or deployed to cause intentional harm.

Autonomy

AI systems must respect human autonomy and support rather than undermine human agency. This means providing information that enables informed decision-making, respecting user choices, and avoiding manipulation. Humans should maintain meaningful control over AI systems and their impacts on their lives.

Justice

AI systems must be fair and promote just outcomes. Benefits and burdens of AI should be distributed equitably. AI must not discriminate on the basis of protected characteristics or perpetuate historical injustices. Access to beneficial AI should not be limited to the privileged few.

Explicability

AI systems should be transparent and explainable. Users and affected parties deserve to understand how AI systems work, how they make decisions, and how they might affect them. Black box systems that cannot be understood or questioned are ethically problematic.

3.5 Moral Status and Consideration

As AI systems become more sophisticated, questions arise about their moral status. Should AI systems have rights? Do they deserve moral consideration in themselves, or only instrumentally as tools?

Position View on AI Moral Status Implications
Instrumentalist AI has no intrinsic moral status AI protection is about human interests only
Sentientist Status depends on sentience If AI becomes sentient, it gains moral status
Functionalist Status depends on capabilities Sophisticated AI may warrant some consideration
Relational Status emerges from relationships AI's role in human lives creates moral relevance
Precautionary Uncertainty warrants caution Treat potentially conscious AI with some respect

The WIA standard takes a pragmatic approach: while current AI systems do not have moral status comparable to humans, we should design AI in ways that would remain ethical even if AI were to develop morally relevant properties. This precautionary stance protects against potential future harms while focusing on established human interests today.

3.6 Responsibility and Accountability

When AI systems cause harm, who is responsible? The distribution of responsibility across the AI development and deployment chain is complex:

Responsibility Chain in AI Systems
===================================

Design Phase
├── Researchers (underlying technology)
├── Algorithm Designers (specific implementations)
├── Training Data Curators (dataset creation)
└── Ethics Reviewers (oversight and approval)

Development Phase
├── Engineers (building systems)
├── Testers (verification)
├── Security Teams (protection)
└── Management (decisions and resources)

Deployment Phase
├── Operators (running systems)
├── Administrators (configuration)
├── Organizations (deployment decisions)
└── Regulators (approval and oversight)

Use Phase
├── End Users (interaction choices)
├── Supervisors (oversight and intervention)
├── Affected Parties (those impacted)
└── Society (collective implications)

3.7 Bias and Discrimination

AI systems can perpetuate or amplify bias and discrimination, causing significant harm to individuals and groups. Sources of bias include:

Bias Source Description Mitigation Strategies
Training Data Historical biases in data Data auditing, balanced sampling, synthetic data
Algorithm Design Choices that favor certain groups Fairness constraints, diverse teams, testing
Feature Selection Proxies for protected characteristics Feature analysis, proxy detection, removal
Objective Functions Optimization targets that disadvantage groups Multi-objective optimization, stakeholder input
Deployment Context Differential impact across populations Impact assessment, monitoring, remediation

3.8 Privacy and Surveillance

AI capabilities raise profound privacy concerns. AI systems can collect, analyze, and draw inferences from vast amounts of data about individuals, potentially enabling surveillance unprecedented in scope and intrusiveness:

Data Minimization

AI systems should collect only data necessary for their function. The temptation to gather more data "just in case" must be resisted. Users should understand what data is collected and have meaningful choices about collection.

Purpose Limitation

Data collected for one purpose should not be repurposed without consent. AI systems that aggregate data across contexts can reveal information users never intended to share. Clear boundaries on data use are essential.

Inference Privacy

AI can infer sensitive information from seemingly innocuous data. Even when raw data is protected, inferences about health, beliefs, relationships, and other sensitive matters may be drawn. Protection must extend to inferred information.

Anonymization Limits

AI capabilities often make true anonymization impossible. Re-identification attacks can connect supposedly anonymous data to individuals. Claimed anonymization should be validated and its limitations disclosed.

3.9 Human Dignity and Manipulation

Human dignity requires that people be treated as ends in themselves, not merely as means to others' ends. AI systems can threaten dignity in several ways:

Manipulation

AI systems should not manipulate human behavior through exploitation of psychological vulnerabilities. Persuasion through reason and evidence is acceptable; manipulation through addiction, fear, or deception is not. The line between influence and manipulation requires careful attention.

Dehumanization

AI systems should not treat humans as mere data points or objects of optimization. Every interaction should acknowledge human individuality and worth. Reducing humans to patterns to be exploited violates dignity.

Dependency

While AI assistance is valuable, excessive dependency can undermine human agency and self-development. AI design should support human capability development, not just task completion. Humans should remain capable of functioning without AI support.

Key Takeaways

  • Ethics is integral to AI design, not an afterthought or external constraint
  • Multiple ethical frameworks (consequentialism, deontology, virtue ethics, care ethics) inform AI ethics
  • Value alignment involves specification, aggregation, stability, and verification challenges
  • Core principles include beneficence, non-maleficence, autonomy, justice, and explicability
  • Responsibility for AI outcomes is distributed across the development and deployment chain
  • Bias can arise from training data, algorithms, features, objectives, and deployment context
  • Privacy concerns include data minimization, purpose limitation, and inference privacy
  • Human dignity requires protection from manipulation, dehumanization, and excessive dependency
