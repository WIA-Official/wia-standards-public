Chapter 01: Introduction & The Multi-Modal Transit Problem

1.1 Why this book exists

WIA-UNI-008 is the open standard for multi-modal transportation network data exchange — bus, rail, ferry, micromobility, ride-share, autonomous shuttles. This book is the practitioner introduction.

1.2 The multi-modal transit problem

Urban transportation is multi-modal but per-mode silos remain. Public transit agencies publish GTFS for bus and rail; micromobility operators publish GBFS for bikes and scooters; ride-share platforms publish proprietary APIs; autonomous shuttle pilots publish vendor-specific telemetry. A traveller planning a trip across modes (bus to bike to scooter to walk) cannot get a single coherent view; trip-planning apps must integrate each silo separately.

1.3 What WIA-UNI-008 changes

The standard collapses mode-specific silos into one open wire format. A traveller-facing trip-planning app subscribes to one feed and receives bus, rail, micromobility, ride-share, and autonomous-shuttle availability through one wire format. Agency operators retain control of their data; the standard defines the wire shape, not the storage location.

1.4 The 4-Phase architecture

Phase 1 (Data Format) defines JSON envelopes for routes, schedules, vehicle positions, trip updates, service alerts, fare products, and rider trips. Phase 2 (API Interface) specifies the HTTP surface for publish, query, stream, and federation. Phase 3 (Federation Protocol) handles cross-agency data flow. Phase 4 (Integration) bridges to GTFS, GTFS-Realtime, NeTEx (CEN), SIRI (CEN), GBFS (NABSA), MDS (Open Mobility Foundation), and the WIA family.

1.5 Rider privacy as a baked-in primitive

Earlier transportation data standards have treated rider trip data as either fully public (privacy-violating) or fully private (impossible to study). WIA-UNI-008 treats privacy as a Phase 3 primitive: rider_trip envelopes carry per-trip consent for downstream use (aggregate statistics, planning research, marketing) and downstream consumers MUST honour the per-trip consent or refuse to ingest.

1.6 How this book is organised

Chapter 2 covers existing transit data standards. Chapter 3 introduces the standard at a glance. Chapters 4-7 walk the four phases. Chapter 8 closes with conformance and roadmap.

Implementer Notes

Three operational concerns recur in transportation network infrastructure and are baked into the standard. First, vehicle_position update frequency: most US transit agencies emit every 15-30 seconds; high-frequency rail systems emit every second. Hosts MUST provision sufficient throughput. Second, rider trip consent revocation: when a rider revokes consent, the data must stop flowing within minutes; the consent envelope MUST carry an explicit revocation timestamp. Third, GTFS feed updates: agencies typically publish weekly GTFS updates; the standard\x27s schedule envelope versioning lets downstream consumers detect new feeds without polling.

Multi-Modal Trip Planning Pattern

The standard's core use case is multi-modal trip planning: a traveller wants to go from point A to point B and the planner needs to combine bus, rail, micromobility, and walking. The standard makes this possible without per-vendor integration: the traveller's app subscribes to one WIA-UNI-008 host that aggregates feeds from all participating providers in the city. Trip search returns multi-modal options ranked by total time, total cost, or carbon footprint. The traveller selects an option and the app initiates rider_trip envelopes with explicit consent for the trip duration only.
