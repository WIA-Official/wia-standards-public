Chapter 02: GTFS, GTFS-Realtime, NeTEx, SIRI Heritage

2.1 GTFS — General Transit Feed Specification

GTFS is the de-facto static transit schedule format published by Google in 2006 and now maintained by MobilityData. It defines CSV files for routes, stops, trips, stop_times, calendar, fare attributes, and shapes. Most US, Canadian, and European agencies publish GTFS feeds. The standard\x27s Phase 4 GTFS bridge ingests GTFS feeds and emits WIA route/schedule/stop_time envelopes.

2.2 GTFS-Realtime

GTFS-Realtime extends GTFS with three real-time message types: trip updates (predicted arrivals + delays), vehicle positions (GPS), and service alerts (disruptions). Encoded as Protocol Buffers. The standard\x27s vehicle_position and trip_update envelopes are structurally compatible.

2.3 NeTEx — Network Timetable Exchange

NeTEx (CEN/TS 16614) is the European standardised network timetable exchange format. More expressive than GTFS — covers tariffs, accessibility, multimodal interchanges. Adopted by national transit agencies in EU member states. The standard\x27s Phase 4 NeTEx bridge supports bidirectional conversion.

2.4 SIRI — Service Interface for Real-Time Information

SIRI (CEN/TS 15531) is the European real-time transit data exchange standard. Covers vehicle monitoring, stop monitoring, situation exchange, and connection monitoring. The standard\x27s Phase 4 SIRI bridge enables EU public transport authorities to ingest WIA feeds without changing existing dispatcher infrastructure.

2.5 GBFS — General Bikeshare Feed Specification

GBFS is the NABSA-maintained standard for shared micromobility (bikes, scooters, mopeds). Defines free_bike_status, station_information, system_pricing_plans. The standard\x27s Phase 4 GBFS bridge maps WIA shared_vehicle envelopes to GBFS feeds.

2.6 MDS — Mobility Data Specification

MDS, maintained by the Open Mobility Foundation, is the data exchange standard for cities to manage shared micromobility. Covers Provider, Agency, and Policy APIs. The standard\x27s Phase 4 MDS bridge enables cities to ingest WIA shared_vehicle telemetry through their existing MDS pipelines.

Implementer Notes

Three operational concerns recur in transportation network infrastructure and are baked into the standard. First, vehicle_position update frequency: most US transit agencies emit every 15-30 seconds; high-frequency rail systems emit every second. Hosts MUST provision sufficient throughput. Second, rider trip consent revocation: when a rider revokes consent, the data must stop flowing within minutes; the consent envelope MUST carry an explicit revocation timestamp. Third, GTFS feed updates: agencies typically publish weekly GTFS updates; the standard\x27s schedule envelope versioning lets downstream consumers detect new feeds without polling.

Multi-Modal Trip Planning Pattern

The standard's core use case is multi-modal trip planning: a traveller wants to go from point A to point B and the planner needs to combine bus, rail, micromobility, and walking. The standard makes this possible without per-vendor integration: the traveller's app subscribes to one WIA-UNI-008 host that aggregates feeds from all participating providers in the city. Trip search returns multi-modal options ranked by total time, total cost, or carbon footprint. The traveller selects an option and the app initiates rider_trip envelopes with explicit consent for the trip duration only.
