Chapter 08: Conformance, Roadmap, Closing Notes

8.1 Conformance test suite

A black-box conformance test suite is published at https://github.com/WIA-Official/wia-transportation-network-conformance and walks discovery, route + schedule publish/read, vehicle position stream subscribe/reconnect, trip update prediction validation, service alert with severity ladder, fare product structures, shared vehicle availability stream, rider trip consent envelope enforcement, federation handshake, audience-control matrix, rate-limit headers and exhaustion behaviour. Hosts publishing bridge_profile=Full SHOULD additionally pass the suite extension tests for GTFS / GTFS-Realtime / NeTEx / SIRI / GBFS / MDS bridges.

8.2 Reference container

The wia/transportation-network-host:1.0.0 container image implements every Phase 2 endpoint with mock data including a synthetic urban transit network with 5 bus routes, 2 rail lines, and a micromobility zone.

8.3 Rider privacy considerations

The standard takes rider trip data privacy as a Phase 3 primitive. Practical consequences: agencies cannot share rider trip data with marketing partners, ride-share platforms, or research aggregators without the rider\x27s explicit signed consent. Aggregate statistics with k-anonymity ≥ 5 are public; per-trip detail requires per-trip consent.

8.5 Backwards-compatibility promise

Within the 1.x line every Phase 2 endpoint MUST remain reachable. Breaking changes ride a major version bump with 12-month deprecation window per IETF RFC 8594 and RFC 9745.

8.6 Closing note

Multi-modal urban mobility is the future of cities; per-mode silos are the present obstacle. WIA-UNI-008 exists to remove the silos with an open, federation-aware, rider-consent-respecting standard.

Implementer Notes

Three operational concerns recur in transportation network infrastructure and are baked into the standard. First, vehicle_position update frequency: most US transit agencies emit every 15-30 seconds; high-frequency rail systems emit every second. Hosts MUST provision sufficient throughput. Second, rider trip consent revocation: when a rider revokes consent, the data must stop flowing within minutes; the consent envelope MUST carry an explicit revocation timestamp. Third, GTFS feed updates: agencies typically publish weekly GTFS updates; the standard\x27s schedule envelope versioning lets downstream consumers detect new feeds without polling.

Multi-Modal Trip Planning Pattern

The standard's core use case is multi-modal trip planning: a traveller wants to go from point A to point B and the planner needs to combine bus, rail, micromobility, and walking. The standard makes this possible without per-vendor integration: the traveller's app subscribes to one WIA-UNI-008 host that aggregates feeds from all participating providers in the city. Trip search returns multi-modal options ranked by total time, total cost, or carbon footprint. The traveller selects an option and the app initiates rider_trip envelopes with explicit consent for the trip duration only.
