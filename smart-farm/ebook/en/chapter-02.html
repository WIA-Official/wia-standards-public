Chapter 02: Sensor Networks: Soil, Weather, Crop Vision

2.1 Soil sensors

Soil moisture sensors (capacitance, TDR/TDT, neutron probes), soil temperature sensors, soil EC (electrical conductivity), soil pH, and increasingly soil nutrient (NPK) sensors provide field-scale resolution. The standard\x27s sensor_reading envelope preserves the measurement_kind, value, unit, and sensor calibration metadata so cross-vendor sensor networks can be read by one analytics platform.

2.2 Weather stations

On-farm weather stations measure air temperature, humidity, wind, precipitation, solar radiation, and increasingly evapotranspiration directly via lysimeters or eddy covariance. The standard supports both single-station and mesonet (multi-station) deployments via per-sensor field_id linkage.

2.3 Crop vision and remote sensing

Aerial imagery (drones, satellites) and ground-based crop sensors (NDVI, multispectral) provide field-scale crop condition data. The standard preserves spectral band metadata so downstream analytics can compute vegetation indices (NDVI, EVI, SAVI) without proprietary preprocessing.

2.4 Equipment telemetry

Modern tractors emit ISOBUS telemetry: engine load, fuel consumption, GPS position, implement engagement status, and per-row planting/spraying data. The standard\x27s irrigation_event, fertilisation_event, and harvest_record envelopes carry equipment telemetry where relevant for record-keeping.

2.5 Yield monitoring

Combine harvesters equipped with yield monitors record yield at sub-meter resolution. The standard preserves yield map data via harvest_record envelopes with embedded geometry. This is the dominant input to variable-rate management decisions.

2.6 Sensor calibration and drift

Soil sensors drift over time; weather sensors require periodic calibration. The standard\x27s sensor_reading envelope carries calibration_chain_id linking to the most recent calibration record so downstream analytics can detect when a sensor is overdue.

Farmer-First Design

Every choice in this standard is filtered through: does this make farm data more useful to the farmer who collected it, or just more accessible to the vendor who sold the equipment? When the two conflict, farmer sovereignty wins. That is why the standard mandates explicit farmer consent for cross-vendor data sharing, refuses to allow vendor terms-of-service to bypass the consent envelope, and requires implementations to surface vendor data flows so farmers can verify what their data is doing.

Implementer Notes

Three operational concerns recur in smart-farm infrastructure and are baked into the standard. First, sensor data volume during peak season: a 100-hectare farm with 20 soil sensors per hectare emitting at 5-minute cadence produces ~500 000 sensor readings per day. Hosts MUST provision for this peak load and SHOULD downsample to hourly aggregates after 30 days. Second, farmer consent revocation: when a farmer revokes consent, the data must stop flowing within minutes; the consent envelope MUST carry an explicit revocation timestamp and downstream consumers MUST honour it. Third, ISOBUS Task XML round-trip: variable-rate prescription maps emitted as ISOBUS Task XML must round-trip through WIA envelopes without losing the per-cell prescription rates; the harvest_record envelope embeds GeoJSON yield maps that are convertible back to ISOBUS Task XML for the next planting cycle.
