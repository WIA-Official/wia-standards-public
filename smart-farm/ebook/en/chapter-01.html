Chapter 01: Introduction & The Precision Agriculture Promise

1.1 Why this book exists

WIA Smart Farm is the open standard for precision-agriculture data exchange. This book is the practitioner introduction.

1.2 The precision agriculture promise

Precision agriculture promises to reduce input use (water, fertiliser, pesticide), improve yield, and lower environmental impact through data-driven decisions. Sensors measure soil moisture, weather, plant condition, and crop vigour at field-scale resolution. Variable-rate equipment applies inputs only where needed. The promise has been understood for decades; vendor-locked silos have been the brake on widespread adoption.

1.3 What WIA Smart Farm changes

The standard collapses vendor sensor networks, equipment telemetry, and farm management software into one open wire format. A farmer running John Deere tractors, Trimble guidance, AgLeader yield monitors, and Ag Drone aerial imagery can subscribe to all four data streams through one pipe and apply analytics across them — without per-vendor data export negotiation.

1.4 The 4-Phase architecture

Phase 1 (Data Format) defines JSON envelopes for fields/parcels, sensor readings, irrigation events, fertilisation events, harvest records, and crop scouting observations. Phase 2 (API Interface) specifies the HTTP surface. Phase 3 (Federation Protocol) handles cross-vendor data flow with farmer-controlled audience controls. Phase 4 (Integration) bridges to ISO 19156 (Observations & Measurements), AgGateway ADAPT, ISOBUS (ISO 11783) tractor electronics, FAO AgroVoc crop concept vocabulary, USDA Soil Taxonomy, and the WIA family.

1.5 Farmer sovereignty as a baked-in primitive

Earlier farm-data standards have treated farmer ownership of their own field data as an industry assumption rather than a wire-level primitive. WIA Smart Farm treats it as a Phase 3 primitive: every cross-vendor data flow involving a farmer\x27s field MUST be gated on a farmer-signed consent envelope. Default is no consent; the farmer must opt in. Vendors cannot share farmer data with seed companies, financial institutions, or insurance providers without the farmer\x27s explicit signature.

1.6 How this book is organised

Chapter 2 covers sensor network fundamentals. Chapter 3 introduces the standard at a glance. Chapters 4-7 walk the four phases. Chapter 8 closes with conformance and roadmap.

Farmer-First Design

Every choice in this standard is filtered through: does this make farm data more useful to the farmer who collected it, or just more accessible to the vendor who sold the equipment? When the two conflict, farmer sovereignty wins. That is why the standard mandates explicit farmer consent for cross-vendor data sharing, refuses to allow vendor terms-of-service to bypass the consent envelope, and requires implementations to surface vendor data flows so farmers can verify what their data is doing.

Implementer Notes

Three operational concerns recur in smart-farm infrastructure and are baked into the standard. First, sensor data volume during peak season: a 100-hectare farm with 20 soil sensors per hectare emitting at 5-minute cadence produces ~500 000 sensor readings per day. Hosts MUST provision for this peak load and SHOULD downsample to hourly aggregates after 30 days. Second, farmer consent revocation: when a farmer revokes consent, the data must stop flowing within minutes; the consent envelope MUST carry an explicit revocation timestamp and downstream consumers MUST honour it. Third, ISOBUS Task XML round-trip: variable-rate prescription maps emitted as ISOBUS Task XML must round-trip through WIA envelopes without losing the per-cell prescription rates; the harvest_record envelope embeds GeoJSON yield maps that are convertible back to ISOBUS Task XML for the next planting cycle.
