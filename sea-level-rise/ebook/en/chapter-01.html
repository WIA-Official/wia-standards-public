Chapter 01

Introduction to Sea Level Rise

Understanding the fundamentals, historical context, and global significance of rising ocean levels

Overview

Sea level rise represents one of the most consequential manifestations of global climate change, affecting every coastline on Earth and threatening the lives and livelihoods of hundreds of millions of people. As our planet warms due to anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions, the oceans respond in two fundamental ways: thermal expansion as water absorbs heat, and mass addition from melting glaciers and ice sheets. Together, these processes are driving an acceleration in sea level rise that poses unprecedented challenges to coastal communities, infrastructure, ecosystems, and economies worldwide.

The WIA-SLR-001 standard has been developed to provide a comprehensive, internationally recognized framework for monitoring, assessing, and responding to sea level rise. This introductory chapter establishes the foundational knowledge necessary to understand the scope and significance of this global challenge, setting the stage for the detailed technical and policy discussions that follow in subsequent chapters.

Key Definition

Sea Level Rise (SLR) refers to the long-term increase in the average level of the world's oceans. This is distinct from short-term fluctuations caused by tides, storms, or seasonal variations. Global Mean Sea Level (GMSL) is the primary metric used to track this phenomenon, representing the average sea surface height across all of Earth's oceans.

Why Sea Level Rise Matters

The significance of sea level rise extends far beyond simple inundation of coastal areas. It represents a fundamental reshaping of the relationship between human civilization and the ocean environment that has remained relatively stable for millennia. Throughout recorded history, coastal zones have attracted human settlement due to their accessibility for trade, abundance of marine resources, and favorable climatic conditions. Today, approximately 40% of the world's population lives within 100 kilometers of the coast, and this proportion continues to grow.

Rising seas threaten not only permanent flooding of low-lying areas but also exacerbate the impacts of storm surge, saltwater intrusion into freshwater aquifers, coastal erosion, and damage to critical infrastructure including ports, airports, power plants, and wastewater treatment facilities. The economic implications are staggering: without adaptation measures, annual flood damages in coastal cities could exceed $1 trillion by 2050.

Historical Context

To understand the current sea level rise crisis, we must first examine the historical record. Over geological timescales, sea levels have fluctuated dramatically in response to natural climate cycles, particularly the advance and retreat of ice ages. During the Last Glacial Maximum approximately 20,000 years ago, sea levels were roughly 120 meters lower than today, with vast ice sheets covering much of North America and Europe. As the climate warmed naturally over thousands of years, these ice sheets melted, and sea levels rose to approximately their current position by about 6,000 years ago.

For the past several thousand years, sea levels remained remarkably stable, varying by only a few tens of centimeters due to natural climate variability. This stability was crucial for the development of coastal civilizations, allowing the establishment of permanent settlements, ports, and agricultural systems in coastal zones. However, beginning in the late 19th century, this period of stability came to an end as anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions began to warm the planet at an unprecedented rate.

The Modern Acceleration

The instrumental record of sea level change begins in earnest around 1880 with the establishment of systematic tide gauge networks. These early measurements reveal that global mean sea level rose by approximately 1.4 millimeters per year throughout much of the 20th century. However, satellite altimetry observations since 1993 show a significant acceleration, with the rate increasing to 3.1 mm/year between 1993 and 2010, and further accelerating to 3.7 mm/year between 2006 and 2018.

This acceleration is not merely a statistical artifact but reflects the intensifying response of the ocean system to continued warming. Both thermal expansion and ice mass loss have accelerated in recent decades, driven by rising atmospheric greenhouse gas concentrations that have reached levels not seen in at least 800,000 years. Carbon dioxide concentrations, the primary driver of anthropogenic climate change, have increased from pre-industrial levels of approximately 280 parts per million to over 420 ppm in 2024.

People at Risk in Low-Lying Coastal Zones

As of 2024, global mean sea level stands approximately 20 centimeters (8 inches) higher than it was at the beginning of the 20th century. While this may seem modest, the impacts are already being felt worldwide. Higher baseline sea levels mean that storm surges reach further inland, coastal flooding occurs more frequently even during minor storms, and chronic "sunny day" flooding has become a regular occurrence in many low-lying coastal cities.

Regional Variations

While global mean sea level provides a useful metric for tracking overall trends, sea level change is not uniform across the world's oceans. Several factors contribute to significant regional variations in the rate and magnitude of sea level rise. These include:

  • Ocean Dynamics: Changes in ocean currents and winds can cause water to pile up in some regions while drawing down in others. The Pacific Ocean, for example, experiences significant interannual variability related to the El Nino-Southern Oscillation.
  • Gravitational Effects: As ice sheets lose mass, their gravitational pull on ocean water decreases, causing sea levels near the ice sheets to actually fall while rising more rapidly in far-field regions.
  • Vertical Land Motion: Many coastal areas are subsiding due to natural geological processes, groundwater extraction, or sediment compaction, effectively amplifying the rate of relative sea level rise experienced locally.
  • Ocean Heat Distribution: Different ocean basins absorb and redistribute heat at different rates, leading to variations in thermal expansion across regions.

Global Distribution of Impacts

The impacts of sea level rise are distributed unevenly across the globe, creating a significant dimension of climate injustice. Many of the nations most vulnerable to sea level rise have contributed least to the greenhouse gas emissions causing the problem. Small island developing states (SIDS), low-lying deltaic nations, and least developed countries bear a disproportionate burden of the impacts while possessing the fewest resources for adaptation.

Most Vulnerable Regions

Small island nations such as Tuvalu, Kiribati, and the Maldives face existential threats from sea level rise, with some projections suggesting they could become uninhabitable by century's end. Bangladesh, with its low-lying deltaic geography and population of over 160 million, faces massive displacement. Major global cities including Miami, Jakarta, Shanghai, Mumbai, and Lagos are all highly vulnerable to increasing coastal flood risks.

Coastal Megacities at Risk

The concentration of population and economic activity in coastal megacities creates enormous exposure to sea level rise risks. According to the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD), by 2070 the number of people exposed to coastal flooding could triple to 150 million, while exposed assets could reach $35 trillion - roughly 9% of projected global GDP. The cities facing the greatest exposure include many of the world's most economically important urban centers:

  • Jakarta, Indonesia: Home to 10 million people, the city is sinking at rates up to 25 cm/year in some areas due to groundwater extraction, compounding sea level rise impacts.
  • Miami, USA: Built on porous limestone that allows seawater to percolate beneath protective barriers, making traditional flood defenses ineffective.
  • Shanghai, China: China's largest city with 24 million people, situated on a low-lying delta with extensive land subsidence issues.
  • Mumbai, India: Financial capital of India with over 20 million people, facing increasing monsoon flooding exacerbated by sea level rise.
  • Lagos, Nigeria: Africa's largest city, with rapid population growth and expansion into vulnerable coastal wetlands.

The Human Dimension

"For us, sea level rise is not a distant threat but a present reality. Every king tide, every storm surge reminds us that the ocean we have lived with for generations is becoming our adversary."

- Anote Tong, Former President of Kiribati

Behind the statistics and projections lie profound human stories of communities facing the loss of their homes, livelihoods, and cultural heritage. Sea level rise is already displacing people and destroying communities, a phenomenon that will intensify dramatically in coming decades. The term "climate refugees" has entered our vocabulary, though it lacks formal legal recognition under international law, leaving affected populations in a precarious position.

In the United States, the relocation of the Isle de Jean Charles community in Louisiana represents one of the first federally-funded "climate retreats," as rising seas and land loss made their traditional home uninhabitable. Similar stories are emerging from Alaska, where indigenous communities are facing the loss of villages to erosion and permafrost thaw, and from Pacific island nations where entire cultures face an uncertain future.

Cultural and Heritage Losses

Beyond the immediate physical impacts, sea level rise threatens irreplaceable cultural heritage. Coastal archaeological sites, historic settlements, and sacred spaces accumulated over millennia of human civilization face destruction. UNESCO has identified numerous World Heritage sites at risk from sea level rise, including Venice, the Statue of Liberty, the Tower of London, and historic districts in cities from Sydney to Saint Petersburg.

For indigenous peoples and island nations, the loss extends to the very foundations of cultural identity. Languages, traditional knowledge systems, spiritual connections to ancestral lands, and ways of life developed over countless generations face extinction as communities are displaced. This cultural dimension of sea level rise impacts is often overlooked in technical assessments but represents a profound loss to human heritage.

The Purpose of WIA-SLR-001

The WIA-SLR-001 standard has been developed in recognition of the urgent need for a comprehensive, internationally coordinated response to sea level rise. The standard addresses the full spectrum of activities related to sea level rise, from monitoring and measurement to impact assessment, adaptation planning, and policy implementation. By establishing common protocols, data standards, and best practices, WIA-SLR-001 aims to:

  • Facilitate international cooperation and data sharing for improved understanding of sea level rise dynamics
  • Establish consistent methodologies for vulnerability assessment and risk analysis
  • Provide guidance on effective adaptation strategies across different geographic and socioeconomic contexts
  • Support evidence-based policy development at local, national, and international levels
  • Promote equity and justice in the distribution of adaptation resources and assistance
  • Enable tracking of progress toward climate adaptation goals

WIA Philosophy: 弘益人間 (Hongik Ingan)

The WIA-SLR-001 standard is grounded in the philosophy of 弘益人間 - "Benefit All Humanity." In addressing sea level rise, this principle demands that our response prioritize those most vulnerable, ensure equitable access to adaptation resources, and recognize the shared responsibility of all nations to address this global challenge. The standard embodies the conviction that effective climate action must be rooted in justice and human dignity.

Summary

Sea level rise represents a fundamental transformation of Earth's coastal geography, driven by human-caused climate change and accelerating year by year. With approximately 680 million people living in low-elevation coastal zones and trillions of dollars in infrastructure at risk, the stakes could not be higher. Yet the impacts are distributed unjustly, falling heaviest on those who have contributed least to the problem.

The WIA-SLR-001 standard provides a framework for understanding, monitoring, and responding to this challenge in a manner consistent with the principles of equity, scientific rigor, and practical effectiveness. In the chapters that follow, we will explore the full depth of this framework, from the physics of rising seas to the policies needed to protect vulnerable communities. The challenge is immense, but so too is humanity's capacity for coordinated action when the stakes demand it.
