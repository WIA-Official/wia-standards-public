Coastal Protection Engineering

Engineering Solutions for Defending Against Rising Seas

5.1 The Challenge of Coastal Protection

Coastal protection engineering encompasses the design, construction, and maintenance of structures and systems intended to defend coastlines and coastal communities against flooding, erosion, and storm damage. As sea levels rise, existing coastal defenses face increasing stress and many prove inadequate against more frequent and severe flood events. Meeting this challenge requires both strengthening existing defenses and developing new approaches suited to a changing climate.

The engineering response to sea level rise must balance multiple objectives: protecting human life and property, maintaining economic activity, preserving ecosystem functions, and ensuring sustainability over the long term. Different approaches achieve these objectives to varying degrees. Hard engineering structures like seawalls provide immediate, reliable protection but may have negative consequences for beaches and ecosystems. Softer approaches using natural features can provide multiple benefits but may be less predictable in their protective effects.

The WIA-SLR-001 standard provides guidance for evaluating and selecting coastal protection approaches appropriate to specific contexts. This chapter explores the range of engineering options available, from traditional hard structures to innovative nature-based solutions and hybrid approaches that combine elements of both.

5.2 Hard Engineering Structures

Hard engineering refers to the use of rigid man-made structures to protect coastlines from flooding and erosion. These structures have been the traditional approach to coastal defense for centuries and remain important components of protection systems worldwide. Hard structures include seawalls, revetments, bulkheads, breakwaters, groins, and flood barriers. Each type serves specific purposes and is suited to particular coastal conditions.

The advantages of hard engineering include predictable performance, immediate protection, and suitability for densely developed areas where space is limited. However, hard structures also have significant drawbacks. They are expensive to build and maintain, may accelerate erosion in adjacent areas, disrupt natural sediment transport, reduce habitat quality, and can create a false sense of security that encourages further development in hazardous areas.

5.2.1 Seawalls and Revetments

Seawalls are vertical or near-vertical structures built parallel to the shoreline to protect upland areas from wave action and flooding. They range from simple wooden bulkheads protecting individual properties to massive concrete structures defending entire cities. Modern seawalls often incorporate curved or stepped faces designed to reduce wave reflection and overtopping while minimizing structural loading.

Revetments are sloping structures placed on the shoreline to absorb wave energy and prevent erosion. They typically consist of rock armor (riprap), concrete armor units, or other materials placed on a prepared slope. Revetments are generally less expensive than vertical seawalls and create less wave reflection, but require more space on the beach and may still accelerate erosion at their edges.

Modern Seawall Design Features

Contemporary seawall design incorporates numerous innovations to improve performance and reduce negative impacts. Recurved crest walls direct wave runup back toward the sea. Drainage systems prevent water from accumulating behind the wall. Toe protection prevents undermining at the base. Wave return walls reduce overtopping while maintaining lower overall height.

5.2.2 Storm Surge Barriers

Storm surge barriers are movable structures that can be closed during storm events to prevent surge from entering protected waterways and harbors. These complex engineering projects represent some of the largest and most expensive coastal protection investments ever undertaken. Notable examples include the Thames Barrier protecting London, the Maeslantkering in the Netherlands, and the MOSE system in Venice.

Storm surge barriers allow normal tidal exchange and navigation when open, minimizing environmental and economic impacts during non-storm conditions. However, they require sophisticated operating systems, regular maintenance, and careful management to ensure they close when needed. The frequency of barrier closures is increasing as sea levels rise, raising questions about the long-term sustainability of this approach in some locations.

Case Study: The Netherlands

The Netherlands provides perhaps the most comprehensive example of hard engineering coastal defense, with an integrated system of dikes, storm surge barriers, and pumping stations protecting low-lying areas. The Delta Works, completed after the devastating 1953 flood, includes the Maeslantkering, one of the largest movable structures in the world. As sea levels rise, the Dutch are planning further strengthening of their defense system while also exploring alternatives including managed retreat from some areas.

5.2.3 Dikes and Levees

Dikes (or levees) are earthen embankments built to prevent flooding of low-lying areas. They have been used for centuries to reclaim and protect land from the sea and from rivers. Modern dikes are engineered structures with carefully designed cross-sections, impermeable cores, drainage systems, and erosion-resistant surfacing. They can extend for hundreds of kilometers along coastlines and river banks.

The effectiveness of dikes depends on their height, the integrity of their construction, and their maintenance over time. Dike failure can occur through overtopping, erosion, internal seepage (piping), or slope instability. Climate change increases the frequency of conditions that challenge dike integrity, requiring ongoing investment in monitoring, maintenance, and upgrading. The WIA-SLR-001 standard includes specifications for dike design, construction, and maintenance standards appropriate for various risk levels.

5.3 Soft Engineering and Beach Nourishment

Soft engineering approaches work with natural processes rather than against them, using materials and techniques that mimic or enhance natural coastal features. Beach nourishment—the placement of sand on eroding beaches—is the most widely used soft engineering technique. Other approaches include dune restoration, sand bypassing at inlets, and strategic placement of sediment to nourish beaches through natural processes.

Soft engineering offers several advantages over hard structures. Maintained beaches provide recreation and tourism value. Sandy foreshores dissipate wave energy, reducing forces on any structures behind them. Soft approaches can be adapted incrementally as sea levels rise. However, soft engineering requires ongoing renourishment as sediment is lost to erosion and storms, creating recurring costs that may exceed those of hard structures over time.

5.3.1 Beach Nourishment

Beach nourishment involves dredging sand from offshore or other sources and placing it on eroding beaches to restore their width and elevation. The added sand buffer protects upland structures by dissipating wave energy across a wider zone. Nourishment projects must carefully match sediment characteristics to the native beach to ensure stability and minimize environmental impacts.

The lifespan of nourishment projects varies widely depending on wave climate, sediment characteristics, and storm frequency. Projects in high-energy environments may require renourishment every few years, while those in calmer settings may last a decade or more. Sea level rise accelerates erosion rates, shortening nourishment lifespans and increasing long-term costs. Despite these limitations, beach nourishment remains a preferred approach in many resort communities where beach width is valuable for recreation and tourism.

5.3.2 Dune Restoration

Coastal dunes provide natural protection against storm surge and wave runup. Dune restoration involves rebuilding degraded dunes through sand placement, vegetation planting, and installation of sand fencing to trap wind-blown sand. Well-vegetated dunes are more resistant to erosion and can naturally rebuild after storms if given sufficient time.

Dune restoration is particularly effective when combined with beach nourishment to ensure adequate sand supply. The combination of wide beaches and healthy dune systems provides a resilient coastal buffer that can adapt to changing conditions. However, dunes require space that may not be available on developed coastlines, and public access must be carefully managed to prevent trampling of vegetation.

5.4 Nature-Based Solutions

Nature-based solutions (NBS) use natural ecosystems and ecological processes to provide coastal protection while delivering additional benefits including habitat, water quality improvement, carbon sequestration, and aesthetic value. These approaches are receiving increasing attention as communities seek sustainable, adaptable alternatives to traditional engineering. However, nature-based solutions also have limitations and are not appropriate for all contexts.

The key coastal ecosystems providing protection include wetlands (marshes and mangroves), coral reefs, oyster reefs, seagrass beds, and maritime forests. Each ecosystem type provides protection through different mechanisms and is suited to particular environmental conditions. Effective nature-based protection often requires combinations of ecosystem types working together as a system.

5.4.1 Wetland and Mangrove Systems

Coastal wetlands reduce flood risk through multiple mechanisms. Dense vegetation creates friction that slows water movement and reduces wave heights. Wetland soils store water during high water events, reducing peak flood levels. The root systems of wetland plants stabilize shorelines and trap sediments. Studies suggest that wetlands can reduce wave heights by 50-90% across relatively short distances, making them valuable components of coastal protection systems.

Mangrove forests in tropical and subtropical regions provide particularly effective coastal protection. Their complex root systems dissipate wave energy, trap sediments, and prevent erosion. Mangroves also provide critical habitat for commercially important fish species, support biodiversity, and sequester carbon at rates exceeding most terrestrial forests. Restoration of mangrove systems can provide cost-effective coastal protection while generating multiple co-benefits.

Wetland Protection Effectiveness

Research has quantified the protective value of coastal wetlands against storm surge and waves. While effectiveness varies with wetland type, width, vegetation density, and storm characteristics, studies consistently demonstrate significant wave attenuation and surge reduction benefits.

5.4.2 Living Shorelines

Living shorelines are a hybrid approach that combines natural elements—such as marsh plantings, oyster reefs, and natural fiber materials—with limited structural components to provide erosion control and habitat benefits. Unlike hard structures that reflect wave energy, living shorelines absorb and dissipate energy while allowing some natural shoreline dynamics to continue.

The design of living shorelines must be tailored to local conditions including wave energy, tidal range, sediment characteristics, and ecological context. In low-energy environments, simple marsh plantings with coir fiber rolls for initial protection may suffice. In higher-energy settings, rock sills or breakwaters may be needed to protect establishing vegetation. The WIA-SLR-001 standard provides design guidance for living shorelines across a range of coastal conditions.

5.4.3 Coral and Oyster Reef Restoration

Reef systems provide coastal protection by reducing wave energy before it reaches the shoreline. Coral reefs can reduce wave energy by 97% on average, providing substantial protection to shorelines in tropical regions. Oyster reefs in temperate waters provide similar functions on a smaller scale while also filtering water and providing habitat for commercially valuable species.

Climate change poses significant challenges for reef-based protection. Coral reefs face threats from ocean warming (causing bleaching), acidification (reducing calcification), and sea level rise (potentially drowning slow-growing reefs). Despite these challenges, reef restoration remains a valuable approach in appropriate settings, and research into heat-tolerant coral varieties may improve long-term prospects. Oyster reefs are more resilient to climate change in many regions and are increasingly popular for coastal protection and habitat restoration.

5.5 Hybrid and Adaptive Approaches

Increasingly, coastal engineers recognize that the most effective protection often combines multiple approaches into integrated systems. Hybrid approaches pair traditional hard structures with natural features to capture the benefits of both. For example, a seawall might be fronted by a restored marsh that attenuates waves and provides habitat, reducing forces on the structure while enhancing ecological value.

Adaptive design recognizes that sea level rise is an ongoing process and that protection systems must evolve over time. Rather than designing for a fixed future condition, adaptive approaches incorporate flexibility that allows defenses to be upgraded as conditions change. This might include designing seawalls with foundations capable of supporting future height increases, preserving setbacks for future marsh migration, or using modular barrier systems that can be extended.

5.5.1 Adaptation Pathways

The adaptation pathways approach provides a framework for making sequential decisions about coastal protection under uncertainty. Rather than committing to a single long-term solution, this approach identifies a series of options that can be implemented as conditions evolve and new information becomes available. Decision points—triggers based on sea level or flood frequency—indicate when to shift from one option to another.

This approach explicitly acknowledges uncertainty about future sea level rise while ensuring that near-term actions do not foreclose valuable future options. For example, a community might initially implement beach nourishment and living shorelines, with plans to add a seawall if sea level rise exceeds certain thresholds, and ultimately consider managed retreat if rise continues beyond the capacity of structural protection.

Building with Nature

The Dutch "Building with Nature" approach exemplifies integrated coastal management that combines engineering and ecological principles. This philosophy uses natural processes as much as possible while deploying engineered structures where necessary. The Sand Motor project, which placed 21 million cubic meters of sand off the Dutch coast to feed beaches through natural processes, demonstrates this approach at scale.

5.6 Design Standards and Specifications

Coastal protection structures must be designed to withstand the forces they will encounter over their intended service life. Design standards specify the loads structures must resist, the materials and construction methods to be used, and the safety factors to be applied. As sea levels rise and storm patterns change, design standards must evolve to ensure structures remain adequate.

The WIA-SLR-001 standard addresses coastal protection design in several key areas. Sea level rise projections must be incorporated into design water levels using specified scenarios and time horizons. Wave and surge loads must be calculated using approved methodologies. Material specifications ensure durability in the marine environment. Inspection and maintenance requirements ensure ongoing structural integrity.

WIA-SLR-001 Design Requirements

  • Design Life: Minimum 50 years for major structures, with provisions for adaptive upgrading
  • Sea Level Rise Allowance: Must include projected rise to end of design life plus safety margin
  • Extreme Event Standard: Design for events with appropriate return period based on consequence class
  • Overtopping Allowance: Specified limits based on protection objectives and consequences
  • Material Durability: Specifications for concrete, steel, and other materials in marine environment
  • Environmental Integration: Requirements for minimizing ecological impacts and enhancing habitat where feasible

5.7 Cost-Benefit Analysis

Coastal protection investments require careful economic analysis to ensure resources are directed to the most effective uses. Cost-benefit analysis compares the costs of protection measures against the expected reduction in flood damages and other benefits. This analysis helps prioritize investments, select among alternative approaches, and determine appropriate protection levels.

Costs include construction, maintenance, and operation over the structure's life, as well as indirect costs such as environmental impacts and loss of beach access. Benefits include avoided flood damages, but may also include recreation value (for beaches), ecosystem services (for nature-based solutions), and avoided displacement and social disruption. Proper analysis must account for how both costs and benefits change over time as sea levels rise.

5.7.1 Economic Decision Criteria

Several economic metrics are used to evaluate coastal protection investments. Benefit-cost ratio compares total benefits to total costs; ratios above 1.0 indicate net positive value. Net present value discounts future benefits and costs to present value and calculates the difference. Internal rate of return indicates the equivalent interest rate earned on the investment. Each metric has advantages for different decision contexts.

Economic analysis for sea level rise adaptation must address the challenge of deep uncertainty about future conditions. Sensitivity analysis explores how results change under different assumptions. Scenario analysis examines performance across a range of plausible futures. Robust decision-making approaches identify options that perform reasonably well across many scenarios rather than optimizing for a single expected future.

5.8 Chapter Summary

This chapter has explored the engineering approaches available to protect coastlines from sea level rise. Key points include hard engineering structures providing reliable, immediate protection but potentially causing environmental impacts. Soft engineering and beach nourishment work with natural processes but require ongoing maintenance. Nature-based solutions offer multiple benefits including habitat and carbon sequestration alongside protection.

Hybrid approaches combining hard and soft elements often provide optimal outcomes. Adaptive design and pathways approaches address uncertainty about future conditions. The WIA-SLR-001 standard provides specifications for design, construction, and maintenance of protection systems. Cost-benefit analysis supports prioritization and selection among alternatives.
