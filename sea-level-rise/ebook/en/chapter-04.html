Impact Assessment Methodologies

Frameworks for Evaluating Sea Level Rise Consequences

4.1 Understanding Impact Assessment

Impact assessment is the systematic process of identifying, analyzing, and evaluating the potential consequences of sea level rise on human systems and natural environments. Effective impact assessment translates scientific projections of physical changes into meaningful information about risks and vulnerabilities that can inform planning and decision-making. The WIA-SLR-001 standard establishes comprehensive frameworks for conducting rigorous, comparable impact assessments across diverse coastal contexts.

The complexity of sea level rise impacts arises from the interaction between physical changes and the social, economic, and ecological systems exposed to those changes. A given amount of sea level rise may have dramatically different consequences depending on the characteristics of the exposed coastline, the sensitivity of the systems present, and the adaptive capacity of communities and ecosystems. Impact assessment must therefore consider not just the physical hazard but the full chain of effects through exposure, sensitivity, and adaptive capacity.

Impact assessments serve multiple purposes in sea level rise planning. They provide the foundation for prioritizing adaptation investments, inform land use and development decisions, support insurance and financial risk management, guide ecosystem conservation efforts, and enable communities to understand and communicate their risks. The WIA-SLR-001 standard recognizes these diverse applications and provides guidance appropriate for each context.

4.2 Vulnerability Mapping

Vulnerability mapping is a foundational component of sea level rise impact assessment that identifies and visualizes the spatial distribution of risk factors. By combining information about physical exposure with data on sensitivity and adaptive capacity, vulnerability maps provide a powerful tool for understanding which areas and populations face the greatest risks from rising seas. These maps enable targeted intervention and efficient allocation of adaptation resources.

The development of vulnerability maps requires integration of diverse data sources. Elevation data, typically from LiDAR surveys or satellite-derived digital elevation models, defines the areas potentially exposed to flooding at various sea level rise scenarios. Land use and land cover data identify what is present in exposed areas. Census and socioeconomic data characterize the populations and economic activities at risk. Infrastructure inventories locate critical facilities that may be vulnerable.

4.2.1 Elevation and Exposure Mapping

Accurate elevation data is the foundation of exposure mapping. High-resolution elevation models derived from airborne LiDAR can represent coastal topography with vertical accuracy of 10-15 centimeters and horizontal resolution of 1-2 meters. This precision is essential for accurately delineating flood zones in the low-gradient coastal environments where even small elevation differences determine flood exposure.

Exposure maps are created by comparing elevation data against projected sea levels and flood heights. Static inundation mapping identifies areas below specified water levels, while more sophisticated hydrodynamic modeling accounts for the actual pathways water would take during flooding events. The WIA-SLR-001 standard specifies requirements for elevation data quality and mapping methodologies appropriate for different applications.

The Coastal DEM Challenge

Global elevation datasets have historically had significant errors in coastal areas, sometimes misrepresenting elevation by meters. The SRTM mission, for example, measured vegetation canopy height rather than bare earth in forested areas. Recent improvements in coastal DEMs through ICESat-2, TanDEM-X, and improved processing have dramatically improved global coastal elevation data, but local LiDAR remains the gold standard for detailed vulnerability assessment.

4.2.2 Social Vulnerability Indicators

Physical exposure alone does not determine vulnerability—the characteristics of exposed populations strongly influence their ability to prepare for, respond to, and recover from flooding events. Social vulnerability indices combine demographic and socioeconomic indicators that correlate with reduced adaptive capacity: poverty, age (very young and elderly), disability status, limited English proficiency, rental housing tenure, lack of vehicle access, and others.

Communities with high social vulnerability may lack resources to elevate homes, purchase flood insurance, or relocate from hazardous areas. They may have difficulty evacuating during emergencies or recovering afterwards. Overlaying social vulnerability on physical exposure maps reveals areas where sea level rise impacts are likely to be most severe and where adaptation assistance is most needed.

4.3 Risk Assessment Frameworks

Risk assessment combines information about hazards (the physical threat), exposure (what is in harm's way), and vulnerability (susceptibility to damage) to estimate the likelihood and magnitude of adverse outcomes. The WIA-SLR-001 standard adopts the risk framework used by the IPCC, which defines risk as a function of hazard, exposure, and vulnerability interactions. This framework enables systematic comparison of risks across locations and time periods.

Sea level rise risk assessment must account for the full range of hazards associated with rising seas. While permanent inundation—the loss of land to the sea—receives much attention, more frequent flooding from tides and storms often poses the more immediate threat. Saltwater intrusion into groundwater, coastal erosion, and ecosystem impacts each require specific assessment approaches. Comprehensive risk assessment addresses all relevant hazards.

4.3.1 Probabilistic Risk Assessment

Probabilistic risk assessment explicitly accounts for uncertainty in hazard characteristics, exposure, and vulnerability. Rather than calculating risk for a single scenario, probabilistic approaches consider the full range of possible outcomes and their associated probabilities. This enables calculation of expected annual damages, exceedance probability curves, and other risk metrics valuable for planning and financial analysis.

For sea level rise, probabilistic assessment must address uncertainty in future sea level projections, which depend on both climate scenarios and scientific understanding of ice sheet behavior. Flood hazard modeling introduces additional uncertainties related to storm characteristics and coastal dynamics. Damage functions that translate flood depths to economic losses carry their own uncertainties. Rigorous uncertainty quantification is essential for defensible risk estimates.

4.3.2 Scenario-Based Assessment

While probabilistic assessment provides the most complete picture of risk, scenario-based assessment offers a simpler approach appropriate for many planning applications. Scenarios define specific combinations of sea level rise, storm conditions, and other factors that can be used to evaluate impacts under clearly defined conditions. Common scenarios include high tide flooding, 10-year and 100-year storm events, and various sea level rise increments.

The choice of scenarios is critical for effective planning. Scenarios that are too conservative may underestimate risks and lead to inadequate preparation. Scenarios that are too extreme may seem implausible to stakeholders and fail to motivate action. The WIA-SLR-001 standard recommends using multiple scenarios spanning the range of plausible outcomes, enabling decision-makers to understand how risks change across scenarios and plan for a range of possible futures.

4.4 Economic Impact Analysis

Economic impact analysis quantifies the costs of sea level rise in monetary terms, enabling comparison with adaptation costs and supporting benefit-cost analysis of adaptation options. Economic impacts include direct damages to property and infrastructure, indirect effects on economic activity, and broader macroeconomic consequences. Comprehensive analysis must capture both market impacts (those reflected in market prices) and non-market impacts (such as ecosystem services and quality of life).

The economic consequences of sea level rise are staggering in scale. Global estimates suggest that without adaptation, annual coastal flood damages could reach $14 trillion by 2100 under high-emission scenarios. Property values in flood-prone areas are beginning to decline as risks become apparent, with implications for household wealth, local tax bases, and mortgage markets. Some analyses suggest that sea level rise could trigger significant macroeconomic disruptions through these financial channels.

4.4.1 Damage Functions

Damage functions relate flood characteristics (typically depth and sometimes duration and velocity) to the percentage of value lost for different structure types. Developed empirically from historical flood damage data, these functions enable estimation of flood losses given information about flood depths and the structures exposed. Separate functions are developed for residential, commercial, and industrial structures, as well as contents, infrastructure, and other asset categories.

Application of damage functions requires detailed inventories of structures and their characteristics, including building type, foundation type, first-floor elevation, and value. Geographic information systems combine this structure inventory with flood modeling results to calculate damages for each flood scenario. Aggregation across scenarios, weighted by probability, yields expected annual damage estimates.

Economic Impact Categories

  • Direct Physical Damages: Structural damage, contents loss, infrastructure damage, vehicle damage, crop losses
  • Business Interruption: Lost revenue during closure, supply chain disruptions, lost productivity
  • Emergency Response Costs: Evacuation, rescue operations, emergency shelter, debris removal
  • Recovery Costs: Repair and reconstruction, temporary housing, mental health services
  • Indirect Economic Impacts: Regional economic multiplier effects, property value changes, increased insurance costs
  • Non-Market Impacts: Ecosystem service losses, cultural heritage damage, psychological stress, mortality risk

4.4.2 Asset and Infrastructure Valuation

Accurate valuation of exposed assets is essential for economic impact assessment. Real estate valuation draws on tax assessor records, real estate transaction data, and replacement cost estimates. Infrastructure valuation is more challenging, as many public assets are not traded in markets; replacement cost approaches are typically used, drawing on engineering estimates and construction cost indices.

Critical infrastructure often has value beyond its replacement cost due to its role in supporting broader economic activity and public safety. The failure of a wastewater treatment plant, for example, has consequences extending far beyond the cost of repairing the facility. Economic impact analysis should capture these extended impacts, though doing so requires detailed understanding of infrastructure interdependencies and economic linkages.

4.5 Ecosystem Impact Assessment

Coastal ecosystems face profound threats from sea level rise, with implications for biodiversity conservation, ecosystem services, and human well-being. Impact assessment for ecosystems requires understanding how physical changes affect habitat extent and quality, species populations, and ecological functions. Unlike built assets with defined values, ecosystem impacts involve complex ecological dynamics and values that are difficult to quantify but essential to consider.

Coastal wetlands are among the ecosystems most threatened by sea level rise. These highly productive ecosystems provide critical habitat for fish and wildlife, filter pollutants, store carbon, and protect coastlines from storms and erosion. As sea levels rise, wetlands can potentially migrate landward, but this migration requires available upland habitat and sufficient time for soil development. Where development or topography prevents migration, wetlands may be drowned or squeezed out.

4.5.1 Habitat Migration Modeling

Habitat migration models predict how ecosystem distributions will shift in response to sea level rise. These models combine elevation data, habitat maps, and sea level rise scenarios to project where different habitat types will be able to persist or migrate. The Sea Level Affecting Marshes Model (SLAMM) is widely used for wetland migration analysis and has been applied to coastlines worldwide.

Model results reveal that habitat migration potential varies dramatically depending on coastal topography and development patterns. In areas with gentle slopes and undeveloped uplands, wetlands may be able to keep pace with moderate sea level rise by migrating inland. In areas with steep slopes, developed uplands, or coastal defenses that block migration, habitat losses may be severe. These results inform conservation planning and highlight the importance of protecting migration corridors.

Ecosystem Services at Risk

Coastal ecosystems provide services valued at billions of dollars annually: storm protection from wetlands and coral reefs, fisheries production, water filtration, carbon sequestration, recreation, and aesthetic values. Sea level rise threatens these services directly through habitat loss and indirectly through ecosystem degradation. Economic valuation of ecosystem service losses, while challenging, is increasingly incorporated in comprehensive impact assessments.

4.5.2 Species Vulnerability Assessment

Individual species respond to sea level rise based on their ecological requirements, dispersal abilities, and evolutionary potential. Species that depend on coastal habitats face threats from habitat loss and degradation, while those with limited ranges or dispersal abilities may be unable to track shifting conditions. Climate change vulnerability assessments for species combine information on exposure, sensitivity, and adaptive capacity to identify conservation priorities.

Sea level rise poses particular threats to species already of conservation concern. Sea turtles nest on beaches threatened by erosion and inundation. Shorebirds depend on tidal flats that may be lost to rising seas. Endemic species on low-lying islands face potential extinction if their entire habitat is submerged. Impact assessment should identify such species and inform conservation strategies to reduce risks.

4.6 Infrastructure Systems Analysis

Critical infrastructure systems—including transportation, energy, water, wastewater, and communications—face significant risks from sea level rise that extend beyond direct physical damage. These interconnected systems are vulnerable to cascading failures where disruption to one system affects others. Impact assessment for infrastructure must consider these interdependencies and the potential for system-wide consequences.

Transportation infrastructure is often concentrated in coastal areas and vulnerable to flooding. Roads, bridges, rail lines, ports, and airports all face risks from rising seas and intensifying storms. Transportation disruptions affect access to employment, healthcare, and emergency services; disrupt supply chains; and impede evacuation. Assessment of transportation impacts should consider both direct infrastructure damage and the broader consequences of disrupted mobility.

4.6.1 Critical Facility Identification

Impact assessment begins with identification of critical facilities and their exposure to sea level rise hazards. Critical facilities include hospitals and healthcare facilities, emergency response facilities, schools and community centers, power plants and substations, water treatment plants, wastewater facilities, telecommunications hubs, and transportation nodes. Mapping these facilities against flood hazard zones reveals which are at risk and enables prioritization of protective measures.

The criticality of facilities depends not only on their function but on their role in system networks. A seemingly minor facility may be a crucial node in infrastructure networks; its failure could have disproportionate system-wide consequences. Network analysis methods can identify such critical nodes and reveal system vulnerabilities that simple facility-by-facility assessment might miss.

4.7 Compound and Cascading Hazards

Sea level rise impacts rarely occur in isolation. Flooding events often involve the combination of elevated sea levels, storm surge, waves, and rainfall. The compounding of multiple hazards can produce impacts far exceeding what any single hazard would cause alone. Advanced impact assessment must account for these compound events and their potential for cascading consequences through interconnected systems.

Compound flooding occurs when multiple flood mechanisms coincide. Coastal flooding from storm surge may combine with river flooding from heavy rainfall in estuarine areas. High tides combined with rainfall can overwhelm stormwater systems designed assuming lower sea levels. As sea levels rise, the probability of compound events increases because elevated baseline water levels make it easier for additional factors to push total water levels above critical thresholds.

4.7.1 Multi-Hazard Assessment

Multi-hazard assessment considers the full range of hazards that may affect a coastal area, including not only flooding but also erosion, saltwater intrusion, and ecological impacts. Each hazard may affect different assets and populations, and their combined effects may be greater than the sum of individual impacts. Comprehensive assessment maps multiple hazards and identifies areas where they overlap.

The interaction between sea level rise and other climate change impacts—including more intense precipitation, stronger hurricanes, and drought—adds further complexity. While sea level rise slowly raises flood risk, changes in storm characteristics may cause more rapid increases in extreme water levels. Assessment frameworks must be flexible enough to incorporate these evolving understandings of climate risks.

4.8 WIA-SLR-001 Assessment Standards

The WIA-SLR-001 standard establishes requirements and best practices for sea level rise impact assessment that ensure consistency, comparability, and scientific rigor. These standards address data requirements, methodological approaches, uncertainty characterization, and reporting formats. Adherence to these standards enables meaningful comparison of assessments across jurisdictions and supports coordinated regional and global responses.

Key elements of the WIA-SLR-001 assessment standards include requirements for using the latest sea level rise projections from IPCC or equivalent authoritative sources, minimum data quality standards for elevation and exposure datasets, standard approaches for vulnerability and risk quantification, required uncertainty characterization for all quantitative results, and standard reporting formats enabling data aggregation and comparison.

WIA-SLR-001 Assessment Requirements

  • Sea Level Scenarios: Assessments must consider at least three scenarios spanning low, intermediate, and high projections
  • Time Horizons: Assessments must address near-term (2050), mid-century (2070), and end-of-century (2100) conditions
  • Hazard Coverage: Assessments must address permanent inundation, storm flooding, erosion, and saltwater intrusion as relevant
  • Exposure Analysis: Population, economic assets, and critical infrastructure must be characterized
  • Uncertainty Quantification: All quantitative results must include uncertainty estimates or ranges
  • Documentation: Data sources, methods, and assumptions must be fully documented

4.9 Chapter Summary

This chapter has explored the methodologies used to assess sea level rise impacts on communities, ecosystems, and infrastructure. Key points include vulnerability mapping combining physical exposure with social and economic factors to identify high-risk areas. Risk assessment frameworks enable systematic evaluation of hazard, exposure, and vulnerability interactions. Economic impact analysis quantifies damages in monetary terms, supporting cost-benefit analysis of adaptation options.

Ecosystem impact assessment addresses threats to coastal habitats and species that provide critical services. Infrastructure systems analysis considers interdependencies and potential for cascading failures. Compound and cascading hazards require multi-hazard assessment approaches. The WIA-SLR-001 standard establishes requirements ensuring assessment quality and comparability.

From Assessment to Action

Impact assessment provides the foundation for adaptation planning by revealing who and what is at risk, how risks may evolve over time, and where intervention can be most effective.
