Chapter 7: Use Cases

This chapter walks through the standard's application across the major species WIA Smart Breeding targets, with concrete trait sets, recording protocols, and federation patterns.

7.1 Dairy Cattle

Dairy is the most-evaluated species in genomic selection; the standard's vocabulary and federation patterns reflect that maturity.

7.2 Beef Cattle

Beef differs from dairy in that traits cluster around growth and carcass attributes, many of which are measured at slaughter rather than over a lactation. Recording protocols include BIF (Beef Improvement Federation) carcass grading and BCS (body condition score) field recording.

7.4 Poultry

Poultry breeding is dominated by integrated breeding companies; the standard's audience controls and IP-protection envelopes are particularly important here. Many companies share aggregate traits with the public while keeping per-individual data inside their commercial pipelines.

7.5 Crops

Plant breeding maps onto WIA Smart Breeding with one main difference: the individual family represents a line, variety, or breeding population rather than a single plant. Phenotype recording uses field trial designs (RCBD, alpha-lattice). Genotype data typically rides as imputed SNP panels from genotyping-by-sequencing.

7.6 Cross-Species Lessons

Across the five domains above, three patterns recur and are baked into the standard:

  1. The contemporary_group_id field on phenotype records is non-optional in practice; without it, federated GEBV evaluation drowns in herd / field / batch effects.
  2. Owner consent is the right control plane. Animal owners (dairy farmers, pig producers) and plant breeders both have strong intuitions about what they will and will not share, and the standard's per-scope consent envelope matches those intuitions.
  3. Audit logging is mandatory not optional. The species above all sit inside regulated trade and food-safety regimes; auditors and regulators require a defensible record of what data moved between which organisations.
