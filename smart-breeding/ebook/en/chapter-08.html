Chapter 8: Conformance and Roadmap

8.1 Conformance Test Suite

A black-box conformance suite is published at https://github.com/WIA-Official/wia-smart-breeding-conformance and walks through:

  1. Discovery document round-trip.
  2. Individual publish, read, withdraw.
  3. Genotype submit (inline) + integrity-hash check (external reference).
  4. Phenotype submit + correction-by-append flow.
  5. GEBV computation cycle (mock evaluation centre).
  6. Pedigree depth traversal up to 6 generations.
  7. Federation handshake against a mock cooperative peer.
  8. Owner-consent enforcement on cross-organisation reads.
  9. Audit log presence and append-only invariant.
  10. Rate-limit headers and exhaustion behaviour.

Hosts publishing bridge_profile=Full SHOULD additionally pass the suite's joint-evaluation tests against at least one mock peer pair.

8.2 Reference Implementation

A reference container image is published as wia/smart-breeding-host:1.0.0 on the WIA registry. The container ships with:

  • HTTP surface implementing all Phase 2 endpoints.
  • SQLite-backed record store suitable for cooperative-scale (under 1 M individuals).
  • VCF inline + external-reference genotype handling.
  • Built-in conformance test runner.

Production deployments typically swap SQLite for PostgreSQL with the WIA-supplied schema and add a dedicated object store (S3-compatible) for genotype payloads larger than 10 MiB.

8.4 Backwards-Compatibility Promise

Within the 1.x line every Phase 2 endpoint MUST remain reachable and MUST continue to honour the documented status codes and content shapes. Hosts MAY add optional query parameters, response fields, new endpoints, or media types. Hosts MUST NOT remove or repurpose existing ones. Breaking changes ride a major version bump and MUST be preceded by a 12-month deprecation window per IETF RFC 8594 and RFC 9745.

8.5 Governance

The standard is maintained by the WIA Standards Committee. Change proposals follow the WIA RFC process: anyone may submit a proposal; the Committee reviews quarterly; accepted proposals enter an open comment period before being merged into a minor-version release. Breaking changes require a two-thirds Committee vote.

8.6 Closing Note

Smart breeding is at heart a long-term decision-making tool: a sire selected today shapes a herd or flock that exists ten or twenty years from now. The WIA Smart Breeding standard exists because that long-term decision-making is poorly served by short-term vendor lock-in and by data silos that prevent fair joint genetic evaluations across cooperatives. The hope is that an open, federation-aware, audit-logged standard lets the field's collective decision-making improve over time without forcing breeders to choose between data sovereignty and access to global evaluations.
