💧 Wetland and Aquatic Restoration

Chapter Focus: Methods for restoring wetlands, rivers, lakes, and coastal ecosystems including hydrological restoration, aquatic habitat enhancement, and managing unique challenges of water-dependent ecosystems.

5.1 Wetland Restoration Fundamentals

Wetlands are among Earth's most productive ecosystems, providing critical services including water filtration, flood control, carbon storage, and biodiversity support. Restoration addresses hydrology, soils, vegetation, and wildlife habitat.

5.1.1 Wetland Types and Restoration Approaches

5.2 Hydrological Restoration

5.2.1 Restoring Natural Hydrology

Hydrology is the foundation of wetland function. Restoration must recreate appropriate water levels, duration, and frequency:

  • Ditch Plugging: Fill or block drainage ditches to raise water tables and restore inundation patterns
  • Levee Removal/Setback: Remove or relocate levees to reconnect floodplains and restore overbank flooding
  • Water Control Structures: Install gates, weirs, or flashboard risers to manage water levels actively
  • Microtopography Creation: Regrade sites to create natural elevation variation supporting diverse hydroperiods

5.2.2 Calculating Hydrological Requirements

HYDROPERIOD REQUIREMENTS:

Target Water Depths by Zone:
  Deep Marsh:    30-100 cm water depth year-round
  Shallow Marsh: 10-50 cm seasonal inundation
  Wet Meadow:    0-20 cm saturated to surface
  Upland Buffer: Above high water level

Inundation Duration:
  Permanent:   >275 days/year
  Seasonal:    90-275 days/year  
  Temporary:   30-90 days/year
  Ephemeral:   <30 days/year

Design Considerations:
  • Match reference wetland hydroperiods
  • Account for evapotranspiration rates
  • Plan for climate variability (drought/flood)
  • Ensure gradual transitions between zones

5.3 Stream and River Restoration

5.3.1 Channel Restoration Techniques

Stream restoration addresses channel form, function, and connectivity:

  • Meander Reconstruction: Re-establish natural sinuosity in channelized streams to reduce velocity and increase habitat complexity
  • Riffle-Pool Sequences: Create alternating shallow/fast and deep/slow water habitats
  • Bank Stabilization: Use bioengineering techniques (live stakes, fascines, brush mattresses) to stabilize eroding banks
  • In-Stream Structures: Install log jams, boulder clusters, or cross-vanes to create habitat and manage flow

5.3.2 Dam Removal

Dams fragment river systems and alter flow, sediment, and temperature regimes. Dam removal can restore connectivity but requires careful planning:

5.4 Riparian Zone Restoration

5.4.1 Functions of Riparian Zones

Riparian zones interface between aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems, providing critical functions:

  • Water Quality: Filter nutrients, sediment, and pollutants before reaching streams
  • Bank Stability: Deep root systems stabilize banks and reduce erosion
  • Shade: Canopy cover moderates water temperature critical for aquatic life
  • Organic Matter: Leaf litter and woody debris provide energy base for aquatic food webs
  • Wildlife Habitat: High biodiversity supported by water access and vegetation structure

5.4.2 Riparian Planting Strategies

Successful riparian restoration employs zone-based planting reflecting moisture gradients:

RIPARIAN PLANTING ZONES:

Zone 1: TOE (0-2m from bankfull)
  • Permanently saturated
  • Fast-growing willows, alders
  • Live stakes, whips, pole cuttings
  • High density (1-2m spacing)

Zone 2: BANK (2-10m from bankfull)
  • Seasonally saturated
  • Shrubs and small trees
  • Container plants or cuttings  
  • Medium density (2-3m spacing)

Zone 3: TERRACE (10-30m from bankfull)
  • Moist but not saturated
  • Large trees, diverse shrubs
  • Container plants, direct seeding
  • Lower density (3-5m spacing)

Zone 4: UPLAND TRANSITION (30-50m)
  • Occasionally flooded
  • Upland-riparian transition species
  • Native prairie, savanna, or forest
  • Natural density patterns

5.5 Coastal and Marine Restoration

5.5.1 Mangrove Restoration

Mangroves protect coastlines, sequester carbon, and support fisheries. Restoration approaches include:

  • Hydrological Restoration: Reestablish tidal flow through culvert installation or berm removal before planting
  • Propagule Planting: Collect and plant mangrove propagules during appropriate tidal and seasonal windows
  • Natural Regeneration: Often successful when hydrology is restored; more cost-effective than planting
  • Community Engagement: Critical for long-term protection and sustainable use

5.5.2 Seagrass Restoration

Seagrass meadows support fisheries, stabilize sediments, and store carbon. Restoration faces challenges:

  • Water Quality: Requires clear water for photosynthesis; address nutrient pollution first
  • Planting Methods: Transplant plugs, shoots with anchors, or seed broadcasting depending on species
  • Protection: Exclude boat traffic, dredging, and physical disturbance during establishment
  • Monitoring: Track survival, growth, and expansion using underwater surveys or remote sensing

⚠️ Climate Change Considerations

Coastal ecosystems face sea-level rise, increased storm intensity, and ocean acidification. Design for resilience: provide inland migration space for marshes, select climate-adapted species and provenances, create elevation gradients accommodating rising seas, and establish monitoring to detect and respond to climate impacts early.

5.7 Monitoring Wetland Restoration

5.7.1 Hydrological Monitoring

  • Water Level Loggers: Automated sensors recording water depth at regular intervals (hourly/daily)
  • Staff Gauges: Permanent markers for manual water level readings
  • Piezometers: Measure groundwater levels at various depths
  • Photo Documentation: Repeat photography from fixed points showing inundation patterns

💡 Wetland Success Criteria

The US Army Corps of Engineers defines wetland restoration success using three criteria: (1) Hydrology - wetland hydrology indicators present, (2) Soils - hydric soil development evident, (3) Vegetation - dominance by wetland indicator species (>50% obligate/facultative wetland plants). While developed for regulatory purposes, these criteria provide useful benchmarks for restoration projects globally.

Key Takeaways

  • Hydrology is the master variable in wetland restoration; getting water right is essential for all other functions
  • Stream restoration addresses channel form, riparian vegetation, and connectivity through diverse techniques
  • Dam removal can restore river connectivity but requires careful planning for sediment, fish, and stakeholders
  • Riparian zones provide critical functions; restoration employs zone-based approaches reflecting moisture gradients
  • Coastal restoration faces unique challenges from tides, waves, and accelerating climate change impacts
  • Monitoring must track hydrological and biological indicators to assess progress toward restoration goals
  • Patience is essential; wetland and aquatic systems may require 5-10+ years to develop mature characteristics
