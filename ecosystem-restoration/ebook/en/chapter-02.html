CHAPTER 2

Ecosystem Assessment and Diagnosis

Learning Objectives:

  • Master comprehensive ecosystem assessment methodologies
  • Identify and analyze degradation factors and root causes
  • Establish robust baseline conditions for restoration planning
  • Set measurable, achievable restoration goals
  • Select appropriate reference ecosystems for restoration targets

2.1 The Assessment Process

Ecosystem assessment is the systematic evaluation of ecological conditions, degradation factors, and restoration potential. A thorough assessment forms the foundation for effective restoration planning and implementation. The process involves multiple scales of analysis, from landscape-level patterns to site-specific conditions.

Effective assessment operates at multiple spatial and temporal scales:

Scale Scope Key Questions Methods

Watershed 10s-100s km² Hydrological integrity, upstream influences, sediment Stream surveys, water quality monitoring, flow analysis

2.2 Physical Environment Assessment

2.2.1 Soil Assessment

Soil is fundamental to ecosystem function and recovery. Comprehensive soil assessment includes:

Physical Properties

  • Texture: Proportions of sand, silt, and clay affecting water retention and drainage
  • Structure: Arrangement of soil particles influencing root penetration and water movement
  • Bulk Density: Soil compaction levels affecting root growth and water infiltration
  • Depth: Soil profile depth and horizons indicating development or truncation
  • Erosion Status: Signs of active erosion, historical loss, sedimentation patterns

Chemical Properties

  • pH: Soil acidity/alkalinity affecting nutrient availability and plant growth
  • Organic Matter: Carbon content indicating soil health and fertility
  • Nutrients: Nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, and micronutrient levels
  • Contaminants: Heavy metals, salts, or pollutants requiring remediation
  • Cation Exchange Capacity: Soil's ability to hold and exchange nutrients

Biological Properties

  • Microbial Activity: Soil respiration rates, enzyme activity
  • Organic Matter Decomposition: Litter breakdown rates
  • Mycorrhizal Fungi: Presence and diversity of beneficial fungi
  • Soil Invertebrates: Earthworms, arthropods indicating soil health

💡 Rapid Soil Assessment Protocol

For initial site evaluation, use a simplified rapid soil assessment: (1) Dig soil pits to 30-50cm depth, (2) Photograph soil profiles, (3) Assess texture by hand (feel test), (4) Note color, odor, and mottling, (5) Check for hardpans or compaction, (6) Observe root presence and depth, (7) Collect samples for lab analysis if needed. This quick assessment identifies major soil issues requiring attention.

2.2.2 Hydrological Assessment

Water is critical to ecosystem function. Hydrological assessment addresses:

Surface Water

Component Assessment Methods Key Indicators
Flow Regime Stream gauges, flow measurements, historical records Baseflow, peak flows, seasonal patterns, flow duration
Water Quality Chemical analysis, biological monitoring Temperature, dissolved oxygen, nutrients, pollutants, pH
Channel Morphology Cross-section surveys, longitudinal profiles Width, depth, gradient, sinuosity, substrate composition
Connectivity Barrier surveys, floodplain mapping Dams, culverts, floodplain access, wetland connections

Groundwater

  • Water Table Depth: Monitoring wells to track seasonal fluctuations
  • Aquifer Characteristics: Recharge rates, discharge zones, springs
  • Groundwater Quality: Chemical composition, contamination
  • Surface-Groundwater Interaction: Gaining vs losing streams, hyporheic zones

Wetland Hydrology

  • Hydroperiod: Duration, frequency, and depth of inundation
  • Water Sources: Rainfall, runoff, groundwater, tidal influences
  • Drainage Patterns: Natural vs altered drainage systems
  • Hydrological Modifications: Ditches, tiles, levees affecting water movement

2.2.3 Topography and Geomorphology

Landform characteristics influence ecosystem processes and restoration approaches:

  • Elevation and Slope: Affecting microclimate, drainage, erosion potential
  • Aspect: Cardinal direction influencing solar exposure and moisture
  • Landform Position: Ridgetop, slope, valley bottom affecting soil and water
  • Geomorphic Processes: Active erosion, deposition, mass wasting
  • Microtopography: Small-scale variation creating habitat diversity

2.3 Biological Assessment

2.3.1 Vegetation Assessment

Vegetation provides the structure and primary productivity of most ecosystems. Comprehensive vegetation assessment includes:

Community Composition

VEGETATION SAMPLING METHODS:

1. QUADRAT SAMPLING (Herbaceous vegetation)
   ┌────────────────┐
   │  1m x 1m plot  │  • Random or systematic placement
   │  • % cover     │  • Species identification
   │  • Density     │  • Height measurements
   │  • Frequency   │  • Minimum 20-30 plots per site
   └────────────────┘

2. TRANSECT SAMPLING (Shrubs)
   ─────────────────────────  • Line intercept method
   ├─┼──┼───┼────┼──┤         • Point-quarter method
   0  5  10  15  20m          • Belt transects 2-5m wide

3. PLOT SAMPLING (Trees)
   • Fixed-area plots (10m radius for large trees)
   • Variable-radius plots (point-centered quarter)
   • Measure DBH (diameter at breast height)
   • Record species, height, canopy position
   • Note dead wood, snags, coarse woody debris

4. RAPID ASSESSMENT
   • Relevé method: ranked abundance in homogeneous stands
   • Photo-point monitoring: repeat photography
   • Walk-through surveys: comprehensive species lists

Structural Attributes

  • Canopy Layers: Tree canopy, subcanopy, shrub, herbaceous, ground layers
  • Cover: Percentage of ground covered by vegetation in each layer
  • Height: Average and maximum heights by layer
  • Density: Number of individuals per unit area by size class
  • Basal Area: Cross-sectional area of tree stems (forest metric)
  • Age Structure: Size/age distribution indicating recruitment patterns

Species Composition Metrics

Metric Definition Restoration Significance
Species Richness Total number of species present Indicates biodiversity recovery potential
Species Diversity Richness + evenness (Shannon, Simpson indices) Measures community complexity and health
Native/Exotic Ratio Proportion of native vs non-native species Indicates invasion severity, restoration need
Coefficient of Conservatism Species fidelity to intact habitats (C-values) Assesses site quality and degradation level
Floristic Quality Index Mean C-value × √richness Overall site quality metric for comparisons

2.3.2 Fauna Assessment

Animal communities are indicators of ecosystem health and function. Assessment methods vary by taxonomic group:

Birds

  • Point Counts: Fixed-location surveys recording all birds seen/heard in time period
  • Transect Surveys: Walking surveys with distance estimation for density calculations
  • Mist Netting: Capture for detailed examination, banding, age/sex determination
  • Breeding Bird Surveys: Standardized protocols for breeding season monitoring
  • Focal Species: Target indicator species or species of conservation concern

Mammals

  • Camera Traps: Motion-activated cameras for presence, activity patterns, populations
  • Track/Sign Surveys: Footprints, scat, browse signs, burrows, dens
  • Live Trapping: Small mammal trapping for species identification, population estimates
  • Acoustic Monitoring: Bat detectors for ultrasonic echolocation calls
  • Hair Snares: Collection of hair for genetic analysis, species confirmation

Reptiles and Amphibians

  • Visual Encounter Surveys: Time-constrained searches in suitable habitats
  • Cover Object Surveys: Checking under logs, rocks, debris
  • Drift Fence/Pitfall Traps: Arrays for capturing terrestrial species
  • Aquatic Surveys: Dip netting, seining for larvae and aquatic adults
  • Call Surveys: Nighttime surveys for breeding frog calls

Aquatic Invertebrates

  • Benthic Macroinvertebrates: Kick-net sampling, taxonomic identification
  • Diversity Indices: EPT Index (Ephemeroptera, Plecoptera, Trichoptera richness)
  • Biotic Indices: Tolerance values indicating water quality (e.g., Hilsenhoff Index)
  • Functional Feeding Groups: Shredders, collectors, scrapers, predators

⚠️ Survey Timing Considerations

Wildlife surveys require appropriate timing: breeding bird surveys during spring migration and nesting; amphibian surveys during breeding seasons (often early spring); butterfly surveys during flight seasons; bat surveys during active seasons (avoid hibernation). Multiple survey periods across seasons provide comprehensive species inventories. Consult regional protocols for optimal survey windows.

2.5 Reference Ecosystems

2.5.1 Selecting Reference Sites

Reference ecosystems guide restoration targets. Ideal reference sites:

  • Geographic Proximity: Similar climate, geology, soils to restoration site
  • Minimal Degradation: Representative of desired conditions
  • Similar Landscape Context: Comparable topography, hydrology, disturbance history
  • Accessibility: Sufficient for thorough assessment and monitoring
  • Multiple Examples: Several sites to capture natural variability

2.5.2 Types of Reference Ecosystems

Reference Type Application Considerations
Contemporary Intact Nearby undegraded ecosystems Best option when available; accounts for current conditions
Historical Pre-degradation conditions from records Useful for species lists; may not be achievable under current climate
Composite Combine multiple partial references When no single intact example exists; synthesize desired attributes
Best Available Least degraded examples When intact references don't exist; set stepwise improvement goals
Future-Oriented Project future climate conditions For climate-adaptive restoration; use climate analogs from warmer regions

💡 Climate Analog Method

For climate-adaptive restoration, identify "climate analog" reference sites: locations that currently experience the climate conditions projected for your restoration site in 20-50 years. Examine species composition and ecosystem characteristics at analog sites to inform species selection and restoration targets that will be adapted to future conditions.

Key Takeaways

  • Comprehensive ecosystem assessment combines physical, biological, and social dimensions across multiple scales
  • Soil and hydrological assessments are fundamental, as these factors often limit restoration success
  • Vegetation and fauna assessments establish baseline conditions and track recovery trajectories
  • Degradation analysis must identify root causes, not just symptoms, to design effective interventions
  • Reference ecosystems provide restoration targets but must be adapted to current and future conditions
  • Restoration goals should follow a hierarchy from broad vision to specific, measurable objectives with clear success criteria
  • Climate change requires consideration of future conditions, not just historical baselines
