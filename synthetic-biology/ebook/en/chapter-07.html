Chapter 7

Ethical Considerations and Governance

Synthetic biology's power to redesign life raises profound ethical questions that extend far beyond technical biosafety concerns. As we gain the ability to edit human embryos, create synthetic organisms, and potentially alter ecosystems, society must grapple with questions of moral permissibility, equity, consent, and the appropriate limits of human intervention in biological systems. This chapter examines key ethical challenges—from germline editing to environmental release—and explores governance frameworks that balance innovation with responsibility, ensuring synthetic biology benefits all humanity.

Fundamental Ethical Principles

Beneficence and Non-Maleficence

The medical ethics principles of beneficence (obligation to benefit others) and non-maleficence (obligation to avoid harm) apply directly to synthetic biology:

  • Beneficence: Synthetic biology should aim to improve human health, food security, environmental sustainability, and overall well-being
  • Non-Maleficence: Researchers must minimize risks of accidental harm (biosafety) and prevent malicious misuse (biosecurity)

Tension arises when benefits and risks are uncertain or unevenly distributed. CASGEVY provides dramatic health benefits to sickle cell patients but costs $2.2 million—raising questions about who benefits and whether high costs constitute a form of harm through exclusion.

Autonomy and Informed Consent

Respect for autonomy requires individuals make informed decisions about their participation in synthetic biology applications:

  • Patient Consent: Gene therapy patients must understand risks, benefits, alternatives, and uncertainties
  • Community Consent: Environmental release affecting communities requires community engagement and consent
  • Future Generations: Germline editing affects descendants who cannot consent—how do we respect their autonomy?

Justice and Equity

Justice requires fair distribution of benefits and burdens:

  • Access to Benefits: Should life-saving therapies be available only to wealthy patients/nations?
  • Distribution of Risks: Are risks (environmental, health) disproportionately borne by disadvantaged communities?
  • Global Equity: How do we ensure synthetic biology innovations benefit low-resource countries, not just developed nations?
弘益人間 (홍익인간)
Benefit All Humanity

The WIA-SYNTHETIC-BIOLOGY standard is grounded in the principle of 弘益人間 (Hongik Ingan)—"Benefit All Humanity." Synthetic biology must prioritize equitable access, environmental stewardship, and inclusive governance, ensuring innovations serve the common good rather than exacerbating inequality. Technologies that benefit only the wealthy or powerful violate this foundational principle.

Human Germline Editing

Technical Capabilities and Current Status

CRISPR enables precise editing of human embryos, potentially eliminating genetic diseases before birth. Current technical status:

  • Somatic Editing: Editing adult cells (CASGEVY, VERVE-101) affects only the treated individual; widely accepted ethically
  • Germline Editing: Editing embryos creates heritable changes affecting all descendants; highly controversial
  • Technical Feasibility: Demonstrated in research settings (2018 He Jiankui controversy) but forbidden clinically

The He Jiankui Case (2018)

In November 2018, Chinese researcher He Jiankui announced the birth of twin girls whose embryos he had edited with CRISPR to confer HIV resistance. The announcement shocked the scientific community:

Why He Jiankui's Experiment Was Condemned

  • Lack of Medical Necessity: HIV can be prevented through less invasive means; germline editing unnecessary
  • Inadequate Safety Assessment: Off-target effects and mosaicism risks inadequately evaluated
  • Ethical Violations: Parents inadequately informed; consent process flawed
  • Regulatory Violations: Conducted without proper approvals; circumvented oversight
  • Premature Application: Scientific consensus opposed clinical germline editing; He violated international norms

Consequences: He Jiankui sentenced to 3 years imprisonment; global calls for stricter oversight of germline editing research.

Arguments For and Against Germline Editing

Arguments Supporting Germline Editing (Under Strict Conditions):

  • Prevention of Genetic Diseases: Could eliminate devastating inherited conditions (Huntington's disease, cystic fibrosis, sickle cell anemia)
  • Reproductive Liberty: Parents should have freedom to make reproductive choices including genetic health of children
  • Public Health Benefits: Eliminating disease genes from population reduces healthcare burdens
  • Consistency with Somatic Editing: If editing adult cells is acceptable, editing embryos to achieve same goals should be acceptable

Arguments Against Germline Editing:

  • Safety Concerns: Long-term effects on edited individuals and descendants unknown; irreversible harm possible
  • Consent Issues: Edited individuals and their descendants cannot consent to modifications affecting them
  • Slippery Slope: Medical necessity might expand to enhancement (intelligence, appearance), creating genetic inequality
  • Social Justice: Risk of creating genetic "haves" and "have-nots"; exacerbating inequality
  • Playing God: Humans lack wisdom to responsibly redesign human germline; should show humility
  • Alternative Approaches: Preimplantation genetic diagnosis (PGD) achieves similar goals without editing

Current International Consensus

WHO Expert Advisory Committee (2021) recommendations:

  1. Moratorium on Clinical Germline Editing: No clinical applications until safety, efficacy, and ethical concerns adequately addressed
  2. Continued Research: Basic research on embryo editing may continue under strict oversight
  3. International Registry: Establish global registry of germline editing research to ensure transparency
  4. Governance Framework: Develop international governance framework before any clinical applications

Environmental Release and Ecosystem Alteration

Gene Drives: Rewriting Populations

Gene drives use CRISPR to spread engineered traits through wild populations rapidly, overriding Mendelian inheritance. Proposed applications include:

  • Malaria Control: Driving genes through mosquito populations making them unable to transmit malaria parasites
  • Invasive Species Control: Driving sterility genes to eliminate invasive rodents on islands
  • Agricultural Pests: Controlling crop pests without pesticides

Ethical Concerns with Gene Drives

Target Malaria: Ethical Considerations

Target Malaria consortium proposes releasing gene-drive mosquitoes in Africa to combat malaria (responsible for 400,000+ annual deaths, mostly children). Ethical considerations include:

Potential Benefits:

  • Dramatic reduction in malaria deaths and illness
  • Reduced pesticide use (environmental benefit)
  • Economic benefits from reduced disease burden

Ethical Concerns:

  • Consent: How does one obtain consent from entire populations potentially affected?
  • Transboundary Effects: Gene drives don't respect national borders; release affects neighboring countries that may not consent
  • Irreversibility: Once released, gene drives difficult or impossible to recall
  • Ecosystem Effects: Eliminating mosquito species might affect predators, pollinators, nutrient cycling
  • Colonialism Concerns: Is it ethical for Western scientists to release gene drives in African nations? Risk of repeating colonial exploitation patterns?
  • Alternative Approaches: Are there less risky approaches (improved vaccines, bed nets, environmental management)?

Governance Frameworks for Environmental Release

Multiple frameworks guide environmental release decisions:

Framework Key Principles Limitations
Precautionary Principle When activity threatens serious/irreversible harm, lack of full scientific certainty should not prevent precautionary measures Can be interpreted to block nearly all innovation; where to set bar for "sufficient" evidence?
Proactionary Principle People should be free to innovate; regulate based on demonstrated risks rather than hypothetical fears May inadequately account for irreversible harms; favors technological optimism
Responsible Innovation Balance benefits and risks; engage stakeholders; maintain flexibility; ensure accountability Requires resources for engagement; "stakeholder" definition unclear; process-heavy
Adaptive Management Proceed incrementally; monitor impacts; adjust based on observed outcomes; maintain reversibility where possible Some impacts (gene drives, extinction) inherently irreversible

Access, Equity, and Global Justice

The $2.2 Million Therapy Problem

CASGEVY's success treating sickle cell disease demonstrates CRISPR's transformative potential, but its $2.2 million price tag raises profound equity concerns:

  • Limited Access: Only patients in wealthy countries with comprehensive insurance can afford treatment
  • Global Distribution of Disease: Sickle cell disease disproportionately affects populations in sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, Middle East—regions with limited access to $2M therapies
  • Cost-Effectiveness vs. Justice: Is expensive one-time cure more just than affordable lifelong management, even if cure is technically superior?
  • R&D Incentives: Market incentives favor "luxury" therapies for wealthy markets over diseases primarily affecting poor countries

Strategies for Equitable Access

Promoting Equity in Synthetic Biology

Tiered Pricing: Charge wealthy patients/countries higher prices; provide discounted/free treatment to low-resource settings (pharmaceutical tiered pricing model)

Technology Transfer: Enable local production in developing countries through licensing agreements, training programs, facility establishment

Global Funds: Create international financing mechanisms for life-saving therapies (analogous to Global Fund for HIV/TB/Malaria)

Open-Source Approaches: Develop therapies through open-source research models minimizing IP restrictions

Neglected Disease Focus: Direct funding toward diseases primarily affecting developing countries (malaria, tuberculosis, dengue)

Capacity Building: Invest in research infrastructure, training, and expertise in low-resource countries

Italy's Space Law as Model: Benefit-Sharing Requirements

Italy's 2025 Space Law includes provisions requiring space resource activities to "ensure sustainability and share benefits with developing nations." This approach could inspire synthetic biology governance:

  • Require benefit-sharing agreements for therapies targeting diseases prevalent in developing countries
  • Mandate technology transfer as condition for regulatory approval
  • Establish international funds supported by synthetic biology revenue

Patenting Life and Intellectual Property

Current IP Landscape

Synthetic biology innovations are heavily patented:

  • CRISPR Patents: Bitter disputes between Broad Institute (Feng Zhang) and UC Berkeley (Jennifer Doudna) over CRISPR IP; billions of dollars at stake
  • BioBrick Parts: BioBricks Foundation promotes IP-free sharing, but not all parts in Registry are truly patent-free
  • Therapeutic Applications: CAR-T and gene therapy patents licensed exclusively to companies, limiting competition

Ethical Arguments About Patenting

Supporting Patents:

  • Incentivizes R&D investment (billions required to develop therapies)
  • Enables recouping development costs
  • Promotes disclosure (patent applications reveal technical details)

Opposing Patents on Living Organisms:

  • Life is sacred; should not be owned as property
  • Patents restrict research and slow innovation
  • Limits access to life-saving therapies
  • Indigenous knowledge appropriation concerns (biopiracy)

Synthetic Organisms and "Playing God"

Creating Synthetic Life

Craig Venter Institute created first organism with fully synthetic genome (2010). Future possibilities include:

  • Organisms with expanded genetic code (non-natural amino acids)
  • Minimal genomes with only essential functions
  • Xenobiology: organisms using different biochemistry than natural life

Religious and Philosophical Perspectives

Creating synthetic organisms raises questions about humanity's role in nature:

Diverse Perspectives on Creating Synthetic Life

Secular Concerns:

  • Hubris: Humans lack wisdom to responsibly create new life forms; should exercise humility
  • Unintended Consequences: Synthetic organisms might have unforeseen ecological or health impacts
  • Instrumentalization: Treating life as mere engineering material degrades our respect for living things

Religious Perspectives (Selected):

  • Some Christian Views: Humans created in God's image, given dominion over creation; synthetic biology can be act of faithful stewardship if done responsibly
  • Some Jewish Views: Human creativity reflects divine creativity; modifying nature permitted if improves human welfare and respects creation
  • Some Islamic Views: Humans are khalifa (stewards) of Earth; synthetic biology acceptable if serves human welfare, doesn't cause harm, and respects natural order
  • Buddhist Perspectives: Focus on compassion and reducing suffering; synthetic biology acceptable if motivated by compassion, minimizes harm to sentient beings

Note: Religious traditions are diverse; these represent some views within each tradition, not universal doctrine.

Governance Models

Self-Governance: The Asilomar Model

The 1975 Asilomar Conference on recombinant DNA established a model for scientific self-governance:

  • Scientists voluntarily imposed moratorium on certain experiments pending safety assessment
  • Developed safety guidelines (precursor to NIH Guidelines)
  • Demonstrated scientific community's capacity for responsible self-regulation

Limitations: Only works when scientists agree; cannot bind non-participants; no enforcement mechanism.

Regulatory Oversight

Government agencies regulate synthetic biology through existing frameworks (FDA, USDA, EPA in USA; EMA, EFSA in EU). Challenges:

  • Regulations designed for traditional GMOs may not fit novel synthetic biology applications
  • Regulatory fragmentation across agencies creates gaps and overlaps
  • International regulatory variation creates incentives for "regulatory arbitrage" (conduct research in permissive jurisdictions)

Public Engagement and Deliberation

Involving publics in governance decisions:

  • Citizen Juries: Representative groups deliberate on specific questions (e.g., "Should gene drives be released for malaria control?")
  • Stakeholder Workshops: Bring together researchers, ethicists, patient advocates, indigenous communities, policymakers
  • Participatory Technology Assessment: Ongoing public involvement in technology assessment and governance design

Benefits: Incorporates diverse values; builds legitimacy; identifies concerns scientists might miss.

Challenges: Time and resource intensive; who decides which publics to involve?; risk of symbolic consultation without real influence.

Future Ethical Challenges

Emerging capabilities will raise new ethical questions:

  • Enhancement: Using synthetic biology for cognitive, physical, or lifespan enhancement beyond treating disease
  • De-Extinction: Resurrecting extinct species (woolly mammoth, passenger pigeon); ecological and ethical implications
  • Synthetic Consciousness: If we engineer increasingly complex neural systems, might they develop morally relevant experiences?
  • Ecosystem Engineering: Using synthetic biology to address climate change (enhanced photosynthesis, carbon capture); unintended consequences?

Conclusion

Synthetic biology's ethical challenges extend far beyond technical biosafety, encompassing questions of equity (who benefits?), consent (who decides?), justice (fair distribution of benefits and burdens?), and humanity's proper relationship with nature. The WIA-SYNTHETIC-BIOLOGY standard grounds all synthetic biology research and application in the principle of 弘益人間 (Hongik Ingan)—"Benefit All Humanity"—requiring that innovations serve the common good, promote equitable access, respect environmental limits, and include diverse voices in governance.

No single governance model suffices. Effective oversight requires combining scientific self-governance, regulatory oversight, public engagement, international cooperation, and ongoing ethical reflection. As synthetic biology capabilities advance, society must continuously revisit ethical boundaries, adapting governance frameworks to balance innovation with responsibility, ensuring this powerful technology enhances human flourishing while respecting the integrity of life and the planet we share.
