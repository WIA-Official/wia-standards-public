Chapter 7: Conformance Levels & Certification

A standard earns trust only when conformance is precise and verifiable. The WIA Perception Clarity Standard defines three cumulative conformance levels — L-A, L-B, and L-C — each adding a layer of capability over the one below. This chapter specifies what each level requires, how a product declares its conformance through the ConformanceDeclaration schema, and how products are judged. The levels let an integrator know exactly what a sensor or platform can do: merely measure clarity, also report it, or fully close the safety loop.

7.1 The Three Levels

enum ConformanceLevel {
  L_A = "L-A"   // computes PCI + exposes clarity state enum
  L_B = "L-B"   // L-A + emits the standard reporting message
  L_C = "L-C"   // L-B + dwell-time SLA + safe-action trigger integration
}

The levels are strictly cumulative: every L-B product is also a conformant L-A product, and every L-C product satisfies L-B and L-A. This nesting means an integrator can specify a minimum level and be assured of all the capabilities below it.

LevelAddsCapabilityTypical fit
L-AMeasurementComputes per-sensor PCI and exposes the four-value clarity stateSensor modules, components
L-BReportingEmits the standard SensorClarityReport JSON over a transportNetworked sensors, sub-systems
L-CSafe actionDwell-time SLA plus integrated safe-action triggersComplete vehicles, robots, AMRs

7.2 Level L-A — Measurement

L-A is the foundation: the product computes a Perception Clarity Index for each sensor according to the PCI definition and class weights of Chapter 3, and exposes the corresponding clarity state. To be judged conformant at L-A, a product must:

  • Compute PCI as a 0-100 integer per sensor using the standard formula and class-appropriate weights summing to 1.00.
  • Derive the clarity state from PCI using the band thresholds, or declare any tuned thresholds explicitly.
  • Demonstrate reproducibility: under a controlled contamination protocol (Chapter 4), repeated measurements of the same condition yield PCI values within the standard's tolerance.
  • Expose PCI and state through some queryable interface (need not be the standard message — that is L-B).

L-A is appropriate for a sensor module or component that quantifies its own clarity but is not itself responsible for fleet communication.

7.3 Level L-B — Reporting

L-B adds standardized communication. Beyond L-A, the product must emit the SensorClarityReport message (Chapter 5) with all required header and per-sensor fields, valid against the published JSON Schema, over a real transport (for example MQTT, DDS, or an O-RAN-style channel). To be judged conformant at L-B, a product must:

  • Satisfy all L-A requirements.
  • Produce reports that validate against the standard schema, including header.version and all required fields.
  • Maintain consistency rules — state matching PCI, dwell > 0 implying non-CLEAR, weights summing to 1.00 — across emitted messages.
  • Report dwellSeconds and confidence for each sensor.

L-B is the level at which heterogeneous sensors and sub-systems become interoperable: a fleet can ingest and compare clarity from multiple vendors in one common format.

7.4 Level L-C — Safe Action

L-C is the full closed loop. Beyond L-B, the product must implement a dwell-time SLA and integrate the safe-action triggers (Chapter 6). To be judged conformant at L-C, a product must:

  • Satisfy all L-B requirements.
  • Declare dwell-time SLAs (dwellSlaSeconds) appropriate to its agent type and operating domain.
  • Support and wire at least the safe-action triggers relevant to its platform from the fixed set: slow_down, reroute, trigger_cleaning, safe_state, disable_sensor.
  • Demonstrate that an SLA breach actually fires the mandated response within the declared window, and that the response is observable for audit.

L-C is the appropriate target for a complete autonomous platform — a vehicle, service robot, drone, or AMR — that must not merely know and report its clarity but act safely on it.

7.5 The Conformance Declaration

A product declares its conformance in a machine-readable document. The declaration enumerates each sensor class present, how many of each, and the capabilities supported.

{
  "declaration_id": "uuid",
  "version": "1.0.0",
  "vendor": "string",
  "product": "string",
  "agentType": "vehicle | robot | drone | amr | other",
  "conformanceLevel": "L-A | L-B | L-C",
  "sensors": [
    {
      "sensorClass": "rgb_camera | ir_thermal | lidar_window | radar_radome | ultrasonic",
      "count": number,
      "pciSupported": true,
      "reportingSupported": true,
      "dwellSlaSeconds": number | null,
      "safeActionTriggers": ["slow_down","reroute","trigger_cleaning","safe_state","disable_sensor"]
    }
  ]
}

Required fields are declaration_id, version, vendor, conformanceLevel, and the sensors[] array. The per-sensor flags must be consistent with the declared level: an L-A declaration leaves reportingSupported false and dwellSlaSeconds null; an L-C declaration populates the SLA and trigger fields. A declaration that claims a level but omits its required capabilities is non-conformant.

7.6 How Products Are Judged

Certification follows the model used across the WIA standards family: documentation review, controlled testing, and operational validation.

  • Documentation review. The conformance declaration is checked for internal consistency and for alignment between the claimed level and the populated capability fields, and validated against the declaration schema.
  • Controlled testing. Under the reproducible contamination protocols of Chapter 4, the product's PCI computation is verified for accuracy and repeatability; emitted reports (L-B+) are validated against the message schema; SLA-and-trigger behavior (L-C) is exercised by inducing breaches and confirming the mandated response within the declared window.
  • Operational validation. The product is observed in representative deployment to confirm that clarity reporting and safe actions behave as declared under real, mixed contamination.

A product is certified at the highest level for which it passes all three stages across every declared sensor class. Where a product mixes levels by sensor — say, L-C cameras but L-A ultrasonics — the declaration records the per-sensor capability and the platform's overall level is bounded by its weakest safety-relevant path.

7.7 Versioning and Re-certification

Every declaration and message carries header.version. Under the standard's semantic versioning, a major change (incompatible) requires re-certification; a minor change (backward-compatible additions, such as a new contaminant type) does not invalidate an existing certification but a vendor may re-declare to claim new capability; a patch change is a bug fix. Pinning the version in both the declaration and every emitted report keeps conformance claims unambiguous over time.

Chapter Summary

Three cumulative levels grade conformance: L-A computes PCI and exposes the clarity state; L-B adds emission of the standard SensorClarityReport validated against schema; L-C adds dwell-time SLAs and integrated safe-action triggers, closing the safety loop. Products declare their level and per-sensor capabilities in the machine-readable ConformanceDeclaration, whose capability fields must match the claimed level. Certification proceeds through documentation review, controlled contamination testing, and operational validation, with semantic versioning governing when re-certification is required.
