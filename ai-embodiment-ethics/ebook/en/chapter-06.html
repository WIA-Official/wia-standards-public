Chapter 6: Transparency & Explainability

6.1 The Imperative of Transparency

Transparency is not merely a technical feature but a fundamental ethical requirement for embodied AI systems. When AI systems make decisions that affect human lives—determining medical treatments, controlling vehicles, managing critical infrastructure—those affected have a right to understand what is happening and why. This right to transparency is grounded in principles of human dignity, democratic governance, and the practical requirements of trust, accountability, and improvement.

For embodied AI systems, transparency takes on special significance. A digital AI that recommends a product leaves time for reflection and alternative consultation. An embodied AI that controls a vehicle, administers medication, or performs surgery acts immediately in ways that may not be reversible. The stakes of opacity are correspondingly higher.

This chapter explores the ethical foundations of transparency, distinguishes different types and levels of transparency, examines the technical challenges of explainability, and develops practical frameworks for implementing transparency in embodied AI systems.

6.2 Dimensions of Transparency

Transparency is not a single property but a multi-dimensional concept that can be analyzed across several axes:

Transparency Dimension Description Example Questions
System Transparency What the system is and what it can do Is this an AI? What are its capabilities and limitations?
Process Transparency How the system reaches decisions What factors were considered? What algorithm was used?
Outcome Transparency What decisions were made and their effects What did the system decide? What happened as a result?
Data Transparency What data the system uses and collects What information is being gathered? How is it used?
Purpose Transparency Why the system exists and whose interests it serves Who created this system? What are their goals?

6.3 Stakeholder-Specific Transparency Requirements

Different stakeholders have different transparency needs. What satisfies an AI researcher may be meaningless to an end user; what helps a regulator may be irrelevant to a victim seeking redress:

End Users

  • Clear indication that they are interacting with AI
  • Understandable explanation of what the system does
  • Guidance on how to interact effectively and safely
  • Information about system limitations and failure modes
  • Plain-language explanations of decisions affecting them

Operators and Deployers

  • Detailed technical specifications
  • Safety case documentation
  • Configuration options and their implications
  • Maintenance and monitoring requirements
  • Incident response protocols

Regulators and Auditors

  • Complete technical documentation
  • Training data provenance and characteristics
  • Testing methodology and results
  • Compliance evidence
  • Audit trail access

Affected Third Parties

  • Knowledge of AI presence in their environment
  • Information about data collection and use
  • Understanding of how they might be affected
  • Recourse mechanisms if harmed

6.4 The Technical Challenge of Explainability

Modern AI systems, particularly those using deep learning, are often described as "black boxes"—their internal workings are opaque even to their creators. This presents significant challenges for transparency:

Sources of Opacity

  • Complexity: Neural networks may have millions or billions of parameters; no human can comprehend them all
  • Emergence: Behavior emerges from training in ways not explicitly programmed
  • Non-linearity: Input-output relationships may be highly non-linear and context-dependent
  • Trade-offs: More interpretable models may sacrifice accuracy

Explainability Approaches

Explainability Techniques:

1. Inherently Interpretable Models
   ├── Decision Trees: Explicit decision logic
   ├── Rule-Based Systems: If-then reasoning
   ├── Linear Models: Clear feature weights
   └── Trade-off: May sacrifice predictive accuracy

2. Post-Hoc Explanation Methods
   ├── LIME: Local surrogate models
   ├── SHAP: Shapley value attributions
   ├── Attention Visualization: What the model "focuses" on
   ├── Counterfactual Explanations: "What would need to change?"
   └── Trade-off: May not accurately reflect true reasoning

3. Concept-Based Explanations
   ├── Concept Bottleneck Models: Intermediate concepts
   ├── Prototype-Based: Similarity to exemplars
   └── Trade-off: Requires careful concept design

4. Natural Language Explanations
   ├── Generated rationales for decisions
   ├── Dialogue-based explanation systems
   └── Trade-off: May be post-hoc rationalization

6.5 WIA Transparency Requirements

The WIA standard establishes mandatory transparency requirements for embodied AI systems:

WIA Transparency Requirements:

Level 1: Presence Disclosure (Mandatory for All)
├── Clear notification that AI is present
├── Basic description of AI function
├── Contact information for responsible party
└── Method to request human intervention

Level 2: Operational Transparency (Mandatory)
├── Description of AI capabilities and limitations
├── Data collection and use policies
├── Safety information and precautions
├── Maintenance and update information
└── Incident reporting procedures

Level 3: Decision Transparency (Risk-Dependent)
├── Explanation capability for significant decisions
├── Factors that influenced the decision
├── Confidence level of the system
├── Alternative options considered (where applicable)
└── Override and appeal mechanisms

Level 4: Technical Transparency (Auditors/Regulators)
├── Complete technical documentation
├── Training data documentation
├── Testing and validation results
├── Algorithm and architecture details
├── Security assessment results

Level 5: Source Transparency (Upon Request/Required)
├── Source code access (where appropriate)
├── Training procedures
├── Ethical review documentation
└── Change logs and version history

6.6 Explaining Physical Actions

Embodied AI systems must be able to explain not just abstract decisions but physical actions—why the robot moved this way, why the vehicle turned, why the surgical system made that incision:

Action Type Explanation Requirements Communication Method
Safety-Critical Immediate, complete explanation Audio alert + detailed log + post-hoc report
Routine Operations Available on request Query interface + log access
User-Affecting Proactive explanation Real-time narration or display
Environmental Impact Observable indication Visual/audio signals + activity logs

Case Study: The Surgical Robot's Explanation

During a robotic-assisted surgery, the AI system unexpectedly adjusts the planned incision path. The surgical team needs to understand why immediately:

Real-Time Explanation Provided:

"Incision path adjusted 3.2mm lateral. Detected: unexpected vessel variation not visible in pre-operative imaging. Confidence: 94%. Alternative: original path risk score 7.2/10 (vessel damage probable). Current path risk score: 2.1/10. Surgeon override available."

Post-Operative Detailed Report:

  • Timestamp: 14:23:47.332
  • Sensor data: Real-time perfusion imaging showed vessel branch at coordinates [x, y, z]
  • Reference: Anatomical variance database match #47293
  • Decision logic: Risk minimization protocol triggered per surgical parameters
  • Alternative paths evaluated: 4 (with risk scores)
  • Selected path rationale: Lowest combined risk for vessel damage and tissue exposure
  • Approval: Autonomous adjustment within delegated authority; override not invoked

WIA Compliance: This explanation meets WIA requirements by providing immediate justification, quantified risk assessment, reference to decision criteria, alternatives considered, and explicit acknowledgment of human override authority.

6.7 Transparency vs. Other Values

Transparency can conflict with other important values, requiring careful balancing:

Transparency vs. Privacy

Explaining AI decisions may require revealing information about individuals used in training or decision-making. The WIA standard requires:

  • Anonymization of personal data in explanations
  • Aggregate rather than individual data where possible
  • Clear policies on what data is used and how

Transparency vs. Security

Full transparency about AI systems may reveal vulnerabilities that could be exploited. The standard permits:

  • Restricted access to security-sensitive details
  • Tiered transparency based on need-to-know
  • Secure channels for auditor access

Transparency vs. Intellectual Property

Companies may resist transparency that reveals trade secrets. The standard balances:

  • Functional transparency without source code disclosure
  • Third-party auditor access under NDA
  • Regulatory access supersedes IP concerns for safety-critical systems

Transparency vs. Usability

Too much information can overwhelm users. The standard requires:

  • Layered transparency (simple summaries with drill-down)
  • User-appropriate language and format
  • Clear prioritization of most important information

6.8 Real-Time Communication of AI State

Embodied AI systems should communicate their operational state clearly and continuously:

AI State Communication Protocol:

Operational Status
├── Active: Normal operation (green indicator)
├── Degraded: Reduced capability (yellow indicator)
├── Fault: Malfunction detected (red indicator)
├── Learning: Adapting behavior (blue indicator)
└── Override: Human control active (white indicator)

Intent Communication
├── Movement intention (direction, speed, path)
├── Interaction intention (approaching, waiting, departing)
├── Task status (initiating, in-progress, completing, aborting)
└── Attention focus (what the AI is "looking at")

Uncertainty Communication
├── High confidence: Clear, decisive action
├── Moderate confidence: Cautious action, ready for input
├── Low confidence: Seeking confirmation or assistance
└── Unable to determine: Stopping and requesting help

Environmental Awareness
├── Detected entities and classifications
├── Known obstacles and constraints
├── Environmental conditions affecting operation
└── Potential hazards identified

6.9 Building Trust Through Transparency

Transparency is not just about compliance—it is fundamental to building appropriate trust in AI systems. Research shows that:

  • Users who understand AI limitations use systems more appropriately
  • Explanation provision increases acceptance of AI decisions
  • Transparency about errors builds rather than erodes trust (when combined with improvement)
  • Inappropriate trust (too high or too low) both lead to worse outcomes

The goal is "calibrated trust"—users should trust AI systems precisely to the degree that trust is warranted by the system's actual reliability and capability. Transparency enables this calibration.

6.10 The Ongoing Commitment to Transparency

Transparency is not a one-time achievement but an ongoing commitment. The WIA standard requires:

  • Continuous Improvement: Explanation systems should improve over time based on user feedback
  • Update Transparency: Changes to AI systems must be communicated to affected parties
  • Incident Transparency: Failures and near-misses should be disclosed and analyzed publicly
  • Research Transparency: Relevant research findings should inform practice
  • Dialogue: Ongoing engagement with stakeholders about transparency needs

In the spirit of 弘益人間, transparency serves the fundamental purpose of ensuring that AI systems truly benefit humanity. Without transparency, we cannot know whether this purpose is being achieved. Without transparency, we cannot identify and correct failures. Without transparency, trust becomes blind faith rather than informed confidence. Transparency is thus not merely one ethical requirement among many—it is the condition for achieving all the others.

Key Takeaways

  • Transparency is a multi-dimensional concept including system, process, outcome, data, and purpose transparency
  • Different stakeholders (users, operators, regulators, third parties) have different transparency needs
  • Technical explainability remains challenging for complex AI systems, requiring multiple approaches
  • WIA transparency requirements are tiered from basic presence disclosure to full source transparency
  • Embodied AI must explain physical actions with appropriate detail and timing
  • Transparency must be balanced against privacy, security, intellectual property, and usability
  • Real-time state communication enables appropriate human response to AI systems
  • Transparency enables calibrated trust—trusting AI precisely to the degree warranted
