Chapter 3: Moral Responsibility & Agency

3.1 The Problem of Responsibility Attribution

When an autonomous vehicle causes an accident, a surgical robot makes an error, or an industrial robot injures a worker, the question immediately arises: who is responsible? Traditional concepts of moral and legal responsibility were developed for human agents—beings with intentions, knowledge, free will, and moral understanding. Embodied AI systems challenge these concepts at their foundations.

The problem is not merely practical—though practical it certainly is. It cuts to the heart of what it means to be a responsible agent. If an embodied AI system makes a decision that causes harm, is it meaningful to hold the system itself responsible? Or must responsibility always flow to the humans involved in its creation, deployment, and operation?

This chapter explores the concept of moral responsibility as it applies to embodied AI, examining the nature of agency, the conditions for responsibility attribution, and frameworks for distributing responsibility among the many parties involved in an AI system's actions.

3.2 Traditional Concepts of Moral Agency

Philosophical tradition has identified several conditions typically required for moral agency and the attribution of responsibility:

Condition Traditional Understanding Application to Embodied AI
Intentionality Actions flow from mental states (beliefs, desires) AI has functional analogs but no genuine mental states
Knowledge Awareness of action and consequences AI processes information but may not "know" in relevant sense
Freedom Ability to do otherwise; not determined AI is deterministic but decisions may be unpredictable
Moral Understanding Grasp of right/wrong, ethical principles AI can apply rules but may not "understand" morality
Responsiveness Can respond to moral reasons AI can be programmed to weight ethical factors

3.3 The Agency Spectrum for Embodied AI

Rather than treating agency as binary—present or absent—the WIA standard recognizes a spectrum of agency in embodied AI systems:

Agency Spectrum for Embodied AI:

Level 1: Reactive Agency
├── Description: Direct sensor-response without deliberation
├── Examples: Obstacle avoidance, emergency stops
├── Decision Complexity: Minimal
└── Responsibility: Primarily on designers/operators

Level 2: Deliberative Agency
├── Description: Planning and goal-pursuit within defined parameters
├── Examples: Path planning, task sequencing
├── Decision Complexity: Moderate
└── Responsibility: Shared designer/operator/system

Level 3: Learning Agency
├── Description: Adapts behavior based on experience
├── Examples: Reinforcement-learned behaviors
├── Decision Complexity: High, partially emergent
└── Responsibility: Complex distribution

Level 4: Reflective Agency
├── Description: Self-monitoring and meta-cognitive processes
├── Examples: Self-diagnosis, strategy adjustment
├── Decision Complexity: Very high
└── Responsibility: May involve limited system accountability

Level 5: Autonomous Moral Agency (Theoretical)
├── Description: Genuine moral reasoning and self-governance
├── Examples: Not yet achieved
├── Decision Complexity: Moral complexity
└── Responsibility: Would require new frameworks

3.4 The Responsibility Gap Problem

Legal scholar Andreas Matthias identified the "responsibility gap"—a situation where no human agent can be appropriately held responsible for the actions of an autonomous system. This gap emerges from several features of embodied AI:

Learning and Emergence

When AI systems learn from data and experience, their behavior may diverge from what designers anticipated. The system may develop responses that no human explicitly programmed or approved. Who is responsible for emergent behavior?

Complexity and Opacity

Modern AI systems, particularly those using deep learning, can be opaque even to their creators. When no one fully understands why a system made a particular decision, the traditional requirement of knowledge for responsibility becomes problematic.

Distributed Development

Embodied AI systems are rarely created by a single individual or even a single organization. Hardware, software, training data, deployment configuration, and operational oversight may involve dozens of parties. Responsibility becomes diffused across this complex network.

"In traditional responsibility ascription, we seek an agent with sufficient knowledge, control, and intention to be held accountable. With autonomous embodied AI, these conditions may be satisfied by no single party or distributed across many parties in ways that defy simple attribution."

3.5 Frameworks for Responsibility Distribution

Given the challenges of responsibility attribution for embodied AI, several frameworks have been proposed for distributing responsibility among stakeholders:

The Chain of Responsibility Model

This model traces responsibility along the chain of causation, from designers through manufacturers, operators, and maintainers. Each party bears responsibility proportional to their causal contribution and the foreseeability of outcomes.

The Shared Responsibility Model

All parties involved in an AI system's development and operation share collective responsibility for its actions. This model emphasizes that creating autonomous systems is a collaborative endeavor requiring collective accountability.

The Structural Responsibility Model

Rather than focusing on individual actors, this model emphasizes the responsibility of institutions, regulations, and social structures to ensure safe and ethical AI development. Responsibility lies in creating appropriate governance frameworks.

Framework Advantages Limitations
Chain of Responsibility Clear attribution, legally tractable May miss systemic factors, complex to trace
Shared Responsibility Acknowledges collaborative nature Risk of diffused accountability, free-rider problems
Structural Responsibility Addresses systemic issues May obscure individual accountability

3.6 WIA Responsibility Attribution Framework

The WIA standard proposes a comprehensive responsibility attribution framework that combines elements of multiple approaches:

WIA Responsibility Attribution Framework:

1. Design-Phase Responsibility
   ├── Architects: Overall system design, safety architecture
   ├── Algorithm Developers: Decision logic, learning systems
   ├── Data Scientists: Training data quality, bias assessment
   └── Ethicists: Value alignment, ethical review

2. Production-Phase Responsibility
   ├── Manufacturers: Hardware quality, safety testing
   ├── Quality Assurance: Validation, verification
   ├── Certifiers: Compliance, standards adherence
   └── Supply Chain: Component integrity

3. Deployment-Phase Responsibility
   ├── Deployers: Context appropriateness, configuration
   ├── Integrators: System interaction, environment fit
   ├── Trainers: Operational instruction, user preparation
   └── Procurers: Due diligence, fitness assessment

4. Operational-Phase Responsibility
   ├── Operators: Proper use, monitoring, intervention
   ├── Maintainers: Updates, repairs, calibration
   ├── Supervisors: Oversight, anomaly response
   └── Users: Appropriate interaction, reporting

5. Systemic Responsibility
   ├── Regulators: Standards, enforcement
   ├── Insurers: Risk assessment, coverage requirements
   ├── Industry Bodies: Best practices, self-regulation
   └── Society: Democratic oversight, values expression

3.7 The Question of AI Moral Agency

Can embodied AI systems themselves be moral agents? This question has profound implications for how we design, treat, and hold accountable these systems:

Arguments Against AI Moral Agency

  • No Genuine Understanding: AI systems manipulate symbols without semantic understanding; they follow rules without grasping their meaning
  • No Free Will: AI systems are deterministic (or at best stochastic); they cannot genuinely choose otherwise
  • No Moral Emotions: Guilt, shame, empathy, and moral outrage are central to moral agency; AI lacks these
  • No Stakes: AI systems have nothing genuinely at stake in their decisions; they cannot suffer consequences in morally relevant ways

Arguments For AI Moral Agency (or Proto-Agency)

  • Functional Equivalence: If AI systems make decisions that function like moral decisions, the underlying substrate may be irrelevant
  • Expanding Concepts: Our concepts of agency evolved for humans; they may require expansion for new entities
  • Emergent Properties: Moral agency may emerge from sufficient complexity even without biological substrates
  • Practical Necessity: Treating advanced AI as moral agents may be necessary for effective governance

Case Study: The Autonomous Vehicle Trolley Problem

An autonomous vehicle encounters a sudden obstacle—a child running into the street. The vehicle's AI must choose between: (A) continuing straight, which will likely kill the child, or (B) swerving, which will likely kill the passenger. The vehicle has 200 milliseconds to decide.

Responsibility Analysis:

  • The AI System: Made the split-second decision based on its programming. It had no time for human consultation. Does it bear responsibility?
  • The Algorithm Designers: Programmed the decision logic. Did they make the right ethical choices in the code?
  • The Manufacturer: Sold the vehicle with specific ethical configurations. Are they responsible for these choices?
  • The Owner/Passenger: Purchased and used the vehicle. Did they accept responsibility through terms of use?
  • The Regulator: Approved the vehicle for road use. Did they adequately assess the ethical programming?
  • Society: Permitted autonomous vehicles on roads. Is there collective responsibility?

WIA Framework Application: Responsibility is distributed across all parties, with proportional weight based on decision authority, knowledge, and causal contribution. The AI system itself may bear "role responsibility" as the proximate decision-maker, even if it lacks full moral agency.

3.8 Role Responsibility and Moral Surrogacy

The WIA standard introduces the concept of "role responsibility" for embodied AI—a form of responsibility that does not require full moral agency but acknowledges the system's functional role in decision-making:

Role Responsibility Defined

An embodied AI system bears role responsibility when it occupies a decision-making position that was traditionally occupied by human agents. The system is responsible in the sense that it is the locus of decision, the entity whose behavior must be evaluated and potentially modified.

Moral Surrogacy

Embodied AI systems can be understood as moral surrogates—acting on behalf of human principals who have delegated decision-making authority. Like human surrogates (agents, attorneys, proxies), AI systems bear responsibility for faithful execution of their principals' moral commitments.

3.9 Practical Implications for System Design

The analysis of moral responsibility and agency has concrete implications for how embodied AI systems should be designed:

Design Principle Responsibility Justification Implementation
Responsibility Traceability Enable attribution of decisions Complete decision logs, audit trails
Meaningful Human Control Preserve human decision authority Override capabilities, supervision modes
Ethical Transparency Clarify value commitments Documented ethical parameters
Contestability Allow challenge to AI decisions Appeal mechanisms, decision review
Graceful Degradation Clear responsibility during failures Defined failure modes, handoff protocols

3.10 Toward a New Ethics of Distributed Agency

Embodied AI systems call for a new ethics that acknowledges distributed agency—decision-making spread across human and artificial actors, across time (designers, operators, users), and across space (networked systems, fleet behaviors).

This new ethics must balance several imperatives:

  • Ensuring that no harmful decision goes unattributed and unaddressed
  • Distributing responsibility fairly among those who contributed to outcomes
  • Creating incentives for responsible behavior at all stages of the AI lifecycle
  • Preserving meaningful human agency in a world of autonomous systems
  • Preparing for the possibility of systems with genuine moral agency

In alignment with 弘益人間, responsibility frameworks must ultimately serve human welfare. When harm occurs, the goal is not merely punishment but learning, improvement, and prevention of future harm. The responsible development of embodied AI is a collective human endeavor, requiring responsibility structures that enable rather than impede the beneficial potential of these transformative technologies.

Key Takeaways

  • Traditional concepts of moral responsibility (intentionality, knowledge, freedom, moral understanding) apply problematically to embodied AI
  • Agency in embodied AI exists on a spectrum from reactive to potentially autonomous moral agency
  • The "responsibility gap" emerges when no human agent can be appropriately held accountable for AI actions
  • Responsibility distribution frameworks include chain of responsibility, shared responsibility, and structural responsibility models
  • The WIA framework distributes responsibility across design, production, deployment, operational, and systemic phases
  • Arguments both for and against AI moral agency have philosophical merit; the question remains open
  • "Role responsibility" and "moral surrogacy" offer frameworks for AI accountability without requiring full moral agency
  • System design should incorporate responsibility traceability, meaningful human control, and contestability
