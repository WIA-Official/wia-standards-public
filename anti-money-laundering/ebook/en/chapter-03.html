🔍 KYC & Customer Due Diligence

Know Your Customer: Identity Verification, Risk Assessment & Beneficial Ownership | Reading time: 35 minutes

Introduction to KYC and CDD

Know Your Customer (KYC) and Customer Due Diligence (CDD) form the foundation of effective AML programs. These processes enable financial institutions to verify customer identities, understand the nature and purpose of customer relationships, and assess money laundering and terrorist financing risks.

Without robust KYC/CDD, institutions cannot effectively detect suspicious activity, fulfill regulatory reporting obligations, or protect themselves from being used as conduits for illicit funds. Failures in this area have resulted in billions in fines and criminal prosecutions.

🎯 KYC vs. CDD vs. EDD

KYC is the broader process of knowing your customer through identity verification and ongoing monitoring. CDD is the specific regulatory requirement to conduct due diligence—verifying identity, understanding business relationships, and assessing risk. EDD (Enhanced Due Diligence) applies additional, more stringent measures for high-risk customers.

The Four Pillars of Customer Due Diligence

Effective CDD programs rest on four core pillars, as mandated by FATF Recommendation 10:

Pillar 1: Customer Identification and Verification

Institutions must identify the customer and verify identity using reliable, independent source documents, data, or information. For individuals, this includes:

  • Full legal name as it appears on government-issued identification
  • Date of birth to distinguish individuals with common names
  • Permanent residential address or principal place of business
  • Taxpayer identification number or equivalent (where applicable)
  • Nationality and citizenship for cross-border risk assessment

Acceptable verification documents vary by jurisdiction but typically include:

Document Type Examples Verification Strength
Government Photo ID Passport, driver's license, national ID card High - Primary verification
Proof of Address Utility bill, bank statement, lease agreement Medium - Secondary verification
Financial Documents Tax returns, pay stubs, financial statements Medium - Source of funds verification
Business Registration Articles of incorporation, business license High - Legal entity verification
Self-Attestation Customer declaration, questionnaire Low - Must be verified

For legal entities, institutions must verify:

  • Legal name and any trading names
  • Legal form and proof of incorporation/existence
  • Registered address and principal place of business
  • Directors, senior management
  • Powers to bind the entity and authorized signatories
  • Tax identification or business registration number

Pillar 2: Beneficial Ownership Identification

One of the most critical—and challenging—aspects of CDD is identifying beneficial owners: the natural persons who ultimately own or control a customer and/or the person on whose behalf a transaction is conducted.

FATF and most jurisdictions define beneficial owners as natural persons who:

  • Directly or indirectly own 25% or more of the customer (equity ownership threshold)
  • Exercise control through other means (voting rights, appointment power, veto rights)
  • Are the senior managing official if no beneficial owner can be identified above

⚠️ Beneficial Ownership Challenges

Identifying beneficial owners is difficult because ownership and control can be obscured through complex structures: nominee shareholders, bearer shares, trusts, multiple corporate layers, offshore entities, and legal arrangements. Criminal organizations deliberately exploit these structures to hide beneficial ownership.

For trusts and similar legal arrangements, institutions must identify:

  • Settlor (person who creates the trust)
  • Trustee(s) (persons who manage trust assets)
  • Protector (if any—person who oversees trustee)
  • Beneficiaries (persons entitled to benefit from trust)
  • Any other persons exercising ultimate effective control

Pillar 3: Purpose and Nature of Business Relationship

Institutions must understand and document the purpose and intended nature of the business relationship. This includes:

  • Why is the customer opening an account or seeking services?
  • What is the anticipated transaction activity (volume, frequency, amounts)?
  • What products or services will be used?
  • What is the source of funds and source of wealth?
  • Are there any unusual or complex features to the relationship?

This understanding forms the baseline against which future activity is monitored. Transactions inconsistent with the expected profile trigger alerts for investigation.

Pillar 4: Ongoing Monitoring

CDD is not a one-time activity—it requires ongoing monitoring throughout the customer relationship:

  • Transaction monitoring: Scrutinize transactions to ensure consistency with customer knowledge
  • Profile updates: Keep customer information current, especially for high-risk customers
  • Periodic reviews: Conduct periodic re-verification based on risk (annually for high-risk, every 3-5 years for low-risk)
  • Event-triggered reviews: Review when material changes occur (new products, large transactions, adverse media)
  • Document refresh: Obtain updated identification when documents expire

Simplified Due Diligence (SDD)

For customers presenting lower risk, institutions may apply reduced or simplified due diligence measures. However, SDD may only be applied where low risk has been verified, and institutions must still:

  • Verify the customer's identity
  • Understand the nature of the customer's business
  • Monitor transactions

Examples of potential SDD candidates:

  • Financial institutions subject to AML supervision in low-risk jurisdictions
  • Listed companies subject to regulatory disclosure requirements
  • Government bodies and public authorities
  • Low-value, low-risk products (e.g., basic bank accounts with transaction limits)

Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD)

For high-risk customers, institutions must apply enhanced due diligence measures. EDD is mandatory for:

  • Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs) and their family members/close associates
  • Customers from high-risk jurisdictions (FATF lists)
  • Correspondent banking relationships
  • Non-face-to-face customers (in some jurisdictions)
  • Complex corporate structures with nominee shareholders or bearer shares
  • Cash-intensive businesses
  • Customers involved in high-risk industries (casinos, MVTS, cryptocurrency)

Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs)

PEPs are individuals entrusted with prominent public functions who may be more susceptible to corruption and bribery. FATF requires enhanced due diligence for:

  • Foreign PEPs: Individuals with prominent public functions in a foreign country (heads of state, senior politicians, judges, military officers, state-owned enterprise executives)
  • Domestic PEPs: Individuals with prominent public functions in the institution's own country (requirements vary by jurisdiction)
  • International Organization PEPs: Members of senior management or individuals entrusted with equivalent functions in international organizations (UN, World Bank, IMF)
  • Family members: Spouses, partners, children, and children's spouses/partners
  • Close associates: Persons closely connected to PEPs professionally or personally

PEP status typically continues for at least 12 months after the individual ceases to hold the prominent function, and some institutions maintain PEP status indefinitely.

💡 Best Practice: PEP Screening

Institutions should screen customers against comprehensive PEP databases at onboarding and on an ongoing basis (at least annually). Automated screening tools can check names against global databases containing millions of PEPs and their associates, flagging potential matches for human review.

Risk Assessment and Customer Risk Rating

Effective CDD requires assigning each customer a risk rating that determines the level of due diligence and monitoring intensity. Risk rating methodologies typically consider:

Customer Risk Factors

  • Customer type (individual, corporate, trust, etc.)
  • Occupation, business type, industry sector
  • PEP status
  • Adverse media or sanctions screening results
  • Source of wealth and source of funds
  • Ownership and control structure complexity

Geographic Risk Factors

  • Country of residence/incorporation
  • Country of operations
  • Countries involved in transactions
  • FATF high-risk jurisdictions
  • Corruption perception rankings
  • Sanctions and embargoes

Product/Service Risk Factors

  • Product types used by customer
  • Complexity and opacity of products
  • Anonymous or cash-intensive products
  • Cross-border capabilities

Transaction/Delivery Channel Risk Factors

  • Face-to-face vs. non-face-to-face relationship
  • Use of intermediaries
  • Expected transaction volumes and values
  • Cross-border transaction frequency

Special Customer Categories

Correspondent Banking

Correspondent banking relationships (where one bank provides services to another) require specific EDD measures:

  • Gather sufficient information about the respondent bank
  • Assess respondent bank's AML/CFT controls
  • Obtain senior management approval before establishing relationship
  • Document respective AML/CFT responsibilities
  • Ensure respondent bank has verified customers with direct access to accounts ("payable-through accounts")

Money or Value Transfer Services (MVTS)

MVTS providers (e.g., Western Union, MoneyGram, hawaladars) are high-risk due to speed, anonymity potential, and common use for remittances from high-risk jurisdictions.

Cryptocurrency Exchanges and VASPs

Virtual Asset Service Providers (VASPs) are subject to AML requirements in most jurisdictions under FATF's revised standards. CDD challenges include:

  • Difficulty verifying identity in non-face-to-face onboarding
  • Pseudonymous nature of blockchain transactions
  • Cross-border transaction ease
  • Use of mixers/tumblers to obscure transaction history
  • "Travel Rule" compliance (transmitting originator and beneficiary information)

✅ Key Takeaways

  • Customer Due Diligence (CDD) rests on four pillars: identity verification, beneficial ownership identification, understanding purpose and nature of relationship, and ongoing monitoring.
  • Beneficial owners are natural persons who ultimately own or control 25% or more of a customer, with financial institutions required to identify and verify these individuals even through complex corporate structures.
  • Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) is mandatory for high-risk customers including PEPs, customers from FATF high-risk jurisdictions, correspondent banking relationships, and complex corporate structures requiring senior management approval.
  • Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs)—including foreign, domestic, and international organization PEPs, their family members, and close associates—require EDD due to higher corruption and bribery risks.
  • Effective CDD requires assigning customer risk ratings based on customer type, geographic exposure, products used, and delivery channels, with due diligence intensity and review frequency proportionate to risk.
  • Ongoing monitoring is essential to CDD, requiring institutions to scrutinize transactions for consistency with customer profiles and conduct periodic reviews to keep customer information current.

Practical Implementation Checklist

  • Manifest integrity — every published manifest is signed via Sigstore DSSE. Tampering is detectable without trusting an intermediary. Updates are issued as new manifests; the prior manifest is preserved with a supersede marker.
  • Retention — keep signed evidence for at least seven years to satisfy ISO/IEC 17065:2012 audit expectations. Some regulators require longer retention; in that case the deployment policy MUST extend the window rather than relying on the defaults.
  • Versioning — follow Semantic Versioning 2.0.0. Major version bumps require at least a 90-day overlap with the prior major version on every WIA-published reference implementation.
  • Negative-result reporting — a skipped indicator without recorded justification is treated as a failed indicator at audit time.
  • Key rotation — manifest signing keys are rotated per the deployment policy. The previous key is preserved in a published key history so that auditors can verify historical manifests offline.

Reference Topologies

Implementations declare their operational topology in the manifest: single-tenant edge (one runtime per organization), multi-tenant gateway (shared runtime with header-based isolation), federated mesh (multiple runtimes peer to one another and publish manifests to a directory service), or air-gapped batch (signed evidence package transported out-of-band). Each topology ships with reference test vectors so that implementations can validate their deployment matches the canonical pattern.
