Introduction

This chapter covers essential aspects of Suspicious Activity Reporting in modern AML compliance programs. Understanding these concepts is critical for building effective systems that detect, prevent, and report financial crimes while meeting regulatory requirements.

Key Concepts

SAR/STR Filing, Investigation & Documentation represents a critical component of comprehensive AML compliance, enabling financial institutions to fulfill their regulatory obligations and protect the integrity of the financial system.

The SAR Filing Process

Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs) in the United States, or Suspicious Transaction Reports (STRs) in many other jurisdictions, are confidential filings made by financial institutions to report suspected money laundering, fraud, terrorist financing, or other financial crimes to regulatory authorities.

When to File a SAR

U.S. financial institutions must file a SAR when they detect:

  • Transactions of $5,000+ involving potential money laundering or BSA violations
  • Transactions of $5,000+ involving potential fraud against the institution
  • Transactions of $5,000+ conducted to evade BSA reporting requirements
  • Transactions of $2,000+ (for money services businesses) that appear suspicious
  • Any transaction by, at, or through the institution that involves or aggregates at least $5,000 in funds or other assets, and the institution knows, suspects, or has reason to suspect the transaction meets SAR filing criteria

SAR Filing Timeline

Scenario Filing Deadline Notes
Suspect Identified 30 calendar days from initial detection Standard timeline when subject known
No Suspect Identified 60 calendar days from initial detection Extended timeline to identify subject
Ongoing/Continuing Activity 120 days after prior SAR File continuing activity SAR referencing prior filing

SAR Narrative Best Practices

The narrative is the most critical part of a SAR, explaining the "5 W's":

  • WHO: Who conducted the activity? Who benefited? Who are related parties?
  • WHAT: What suspicious activity occurred? What transactions/instruments involved?
  • WHEN: When did activity occur? Over what time period?
  • WHERE: Where did transactions occur? What locations/countries involved?
  • WHY: Why is this activity suspicious? What red flags present?

SAR Confidentiality

CRITICAL: SAR filings are strictly confidential:

  • Financial institutions are prohibited from disclosing SAR filing to the subject or any other party
  • No notice of any kind may be provided to persons named in SAR
  • Violation of confidentiality can result in civil and criminal penalties
  • Limited sharing permitted with law enforcement, regulators, and within financial group for AML purposes (under 314(b))
  • Subpoenas for SARs should be referred to legal counsel—SARs generally have privilege protections

✅ Key Takeaways

  • This chapter covered critical aspects of modern AML compliance including suspicious activity reporting, sanctions screening, and emerging technologies.
  • SAR/STR filings are strictly confidential and must be submitted within regulatory timelines (typically 30-60 days), with comprehensive narratives explaining the suspicious activity.
  • Fuzzy matching algorithms like Levenshtein distance, Jaro-Winkler similarity, and phonetic matching (Soundex) are essential for accurate sanctions and PEP screening despite name variations.

  • The future of AML includes explainable AI for regulatory acceptance, privacy-preserving collaboration technologies, real-time reporting, and comprehensive cryptocurrency regulations across jurisdictions.

Practical Implementation Checklist

  • Manifest integrity — every published manifest is signed via Sigstore DSSE. Tampering is detectable without trusting an intermediary. Updates are issued as new manifests; the prior manifest is preserved with a supersede marker.
  • Retention — keep signed evidence for at least seven years to satisfy ISO/IEC 17065:2012 audit expectations. Some regulators require longer retention; in that case the deployment policy MUST extend the window rather than relying on the defaults.
  • Versioning — follow Semantic Versioning 2.0.0. Major version bumps require at least a 90-day overlap with the prior major version on every WIA-published reference implementation.
  • Negative-result reporting — a skipped indicator without recorded justification is treated as a failed indicator at audit time.
  • Key rotation — manifest signing keys are rotated per the deployment policy. The previous key is preserved in a published key history so that auditors can verify historical manifests offline.

Reference Topologies

Implementations declare their operational topology in the manifest: single-tenant edge (one runtime per organization), multi-tenant gateway (shared runtime with header-based isolation), federated mesh (multiple runtimes peer to one another and publish manifests to a directory service), or air-gapped batch (signed evidence package transported out-of-band). Each topology ships with reference test vectors so that implementations can validate their deployment matches the canonical pattern.
