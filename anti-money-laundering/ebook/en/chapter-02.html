Global AML Regulations: FATF, FinCEN, EU Directives & Risk-Based Approach | Reading time: 35 minutes

Introduction to AML Regulation

The global anti-money laundering regulatory framework represents one of the most comprehensive international compliance regimes, with over 200 countries and jurisdictions implementing AML/CFT (Counter-Financing of Terrorism) laws. This framework is driven by international standard-setting bodies, national regulators, and an evolving understanding of financial crime risks.

Understanding this regulatory landscape is essential for any organization operating in financial services, as non-compliance can result in criminal prosecution, multi-billion dollar fines, loss of banking licenses, and irreparable reputational damage.

FATF: The Global Standard Setter

The Financial Action Task Force (FATF), established in 1989 by the G7, is the premier international body setting global standards for combating money laundering and terrorist financing. FATF's recommendations form the foundation of AML/CFT regimes worldwide.

The FATF 40 Recommendations

FATF's 40 Recommendations (last updated 2023) provide a comprehensive framework covering:

  • Risk Assessment (R1): Countries and institutions must assess and understand ML/TF risks
  • National Coordination (R2): Establish domestic cooperation and policy coordination
  • Money Laundering Offense (R3): Criminalize money laundering based on Vienna and Palermo Conventions
  • Confiscation (R4): Enable authorities to confiscate criminal proceeds and instrumentalities
  • Terrorist Financing Offense (R5-8): Criminalize TF and implement targeted financial sanctions
  • Financial Institution Measures (R9-23): CDD, record-keeping, reporting, internal controls
  • Transparency (R24-25): Beneficial ownership of legal persons and arrangements
  • Powers and Responsibilities (R26-35): Supervisory and law enforcement authorities
  • International Cooperation (R36-40): Mutual legal assistance, extradition, information exchange

🎯 FATF Mutual Evaluations

FATF conducts peer reviews called "mutual evaluations" to assess member countries' compliance with the 40 Recommendations. Countries are rated on technical compliance (are the right laws in place?) and effectiveness (are the laws working?). Non-compliant jurisdictions may be placed on the "grey list" (increased monitoring) or "black list" (high-risk jurisdictions subject to countermeasures).

High-Risk and Non-Cooperative Jurisdictions

FATF maintains two public documents identifying jurisdictions with strategic AML/CFT deficiencies:

  • Grey List (Increased Monitoring): Jurisdictions actively working to address deficiencies (e.g., Panama, Albania, Barbados as of 2024)
  • Black List (Call for Action): High-risk jurisdictions with significant deficiencies (e.g., Democratic People's Republic of Korea, Iran, Myanmar as of 2024)

Financial institutions must apply Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) measures for transactions involving these jurisdictions, including additional information gathering, senior management approval, and enhanced ongoing monitoring.

United States: FinCEN and the Bank Secrecy Act

In the United States, AML compliance is primarily governed by the Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) of 1970 and subsequent amendments, enforced by the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN), a bureau of the U.S. Department of the Treasury.

Key BSA/AML Requirements

  • Currency Transaction Reports (CTR): Report cash transactions exceeding $10,000 in a single day
  • Suspicious Activity Reports (SAR): Report suspicious transactions of $5,000+ (insider transactions) or $2,000+ (other)
  • Customer Identification Program (CIP): Verify customer identity before account opening
  • Customer Due Diligence (CDD): Understand customer relationships and identify beneficial owners
  • AML Program: Maintain written policies, designate compliance officer, conduct training, independent testing
  • Record Retention: Maintain records for 5 years
  • Travel Rule: Transmit certain information with funds transfers of $3,000+

USA PATRIOT Act (2001)

Following 9/11, the USA PATRIOT Act significantly expanded BSA requirements:

  • Section 311: Special measures against foreign jurisdictions, institutions, or transactions of primary money laundering concern
  • Section 312: Enhanced due diligence for correspondent and private banking accounts
  • Section 313: Prohibition on correspondent accounts for foreign shell banks
  • Section 314(a): Information sharing between law enforcement and financial institutions
  • Section 314(b): Voluntary information sharing among financial institutions
  • Section 326: Customer Identification Program requirements

Anti-Money Laundering Act of 2020 (AMLA)

The AML Act of 2020, the most significant BSA update in decades, includes:

  • National Priorities: FinCEN must establish and update AML/CFT national priorities
  • Beneficial Ownership Registry: Creation of a non-public beneficial ownership database accessible to law enforcement and financial institutions (with consent)
  • Whistleblower Program: Incentivizes reporting of BSA violations
  • Subpoena Authority: Enhanced FinCEN investigatory powers
  • Innovation Focus: Pilot programs for AML technology and regulatory innovation

European Union: Anti-Money Laundering Directives

The European Union has implemented a series of Anti-Money Laundering Directives (AMLD) that EU member states must transpose into national law.

Evolution of EU AML Directives

Directive Year Key Provisions
1st AMLD 1991 Initial EU-wide framework, drug trafficking focus
2nd AMLD 2001 Expanded predicate offenses, included FT provisions
3rd AMLD 2005 Risk-based approach, PEP screening, beneficial ownership
4th AMLD 2015 Aligned with FATF standards, central BO registers
5th AMLD 2018 Crypto regulations, prepaid cards, public BO access
6th AMLD 2020 Harmonized ML offense, corporate criminal liability

6th Anti-Money Laundering Directive (6AMLD)

The 6th AMLD, effective across the EU since December 2020, introduces significant changes:

  • Harmonized Definition: Standard definition of money laundering across all member states
  • 22 Predicate Offenses: Expanded list of crimes that can generate laundered proceeds
  • Corporate Criminal Liability: Legal persons can be held criminally liable for ML
  • "Aiding and Abetting": Criminalization of assisting or facilitating ML
  • Extended Jurisdiction: Member states can prosecute ML committed abroad
  • Minimum Penalties: At least 4 years maximum imprisonment for ML offenses
  • Double Criminality Removed: Predicate offense need not be crime in both jurisdictions

Risk-Based Approach (RBA)

The Risk-Based Approach is a fundamental principle in modern AML compliance, endorsed by FATF and implemented globally. Rather than applying uniform procedures to all customers and transactions, institutions must:

  • Identify and assess money laundering and terrorist financing risks
  • Design and implement controls proportionate to those risks
  • Apply enhanced measures for higher-risk situations
  • Apply simplified measures where lower risks are identified and verified
  • Continuously monitor and update risk assessments

Risk Assessment Factors

Organizations must consider risk across multiple dimensions:

Customer Risk Factors

  • Customer type (individual, corporate, trust, MVTS, casino, etc.)
  • Occupation or business activity
  • Source of funds and source of wealth
  • Anticipated account activity and transaction patterns
  • Residency and citizenship
  • PEP status or association with PEPs
  • Adverse media or sanctions screening results

Geographic Risk Factors

  • FATF high-risk jurisdictions (grey and black lists)
  • Countries with high levels of corruption (CPI rankings)
  • Countries with significant drug production/trafficking
  • Countries subject to sanctions or embargoes
  • Tax havens and secrecy jurisdictions
  • Countries with weak AML/CFT frameworks

Product/Service Risk Factors

  • Private banking and wealth management
  • Correspondent banking relationships
  • Cash-intensive products
  • Anonymous products (e.g., prepaid cards, cryptocurrencies)
  • Cross-border transactions and trade finance
  • Complex or opaque structures

Transaction/Channel Risk Factors

  • Non-face-to-face transactions
  • Third-party payments
  • Use of intermediaries or nominees
  • Unusual transaction patterns or volumes
  • Transactions inconsistent with customer profile

Other Major AML Regimes

United Kingdom

UK AML regulations are governed by the Proceeds of Crime Act 2002 (POCA) and the Money Laundering, Terrorist Financing and Transfer of Funds Regulations 2017 (MLRs), enforced by the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA), Her Majesty's Revenue and Customs (HMRC), and other supervisors.

Canada

Canada's AML regime is based on the Proceeds of Crime (Money Laundering) and Terrorist Financing Act (PCMLTFA), administered by the Financial Transactions and Reports Analysis Centre of Canada (FINTRAC).

Australia

Australia implements AML/CFT requirements through the Anti-Money Laundering and Counter-Terrorism Financing Act 2006, regulated by the Australian Transaction Reports and Analysis Centre (AUSTRAC).

Singapore

Singapore's framework includes the Corruption, Drug Trafficking and Other Serious Crimes (Confiscation of Benefits) Act and the Terrorism (Suppression of Financing) Act, supervised by the Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS).

✅ Key Takeaways

  • FATF's 40 Recommendations form the global standard for AML/CFT, with over 200 countries implementing these standards into national law.
  • The U.S. Bank Secrecy Act requires financial institutions to file CTRs for cash transactions exceeding $10,000 and SARs for suspicious activity, with the 2020 AML Act modernizing requirements for the digital age.
  • The EU has progressively strengthened AML requirements through six directives, with 6AMLD harmonizing money laundering offenses and establishing corporate criminal liability across member states.
  • The Risk-Based Approach requires institutions to identify, assess, and mitigate ML/TF risks through proportionate controls, applying enhanced measures for high-risk situations and simplified measures for low-risk cases.
  • Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) is mandatory for high-risk customers including PEPs, correspondent banking relationships, and customers from FATF high-risk jurisdictions, requiring senior management approval and enhanced monitoring.
  • Non-compliance with AML regulations can result in criminal prosecution, multi-billion dollar penalties, loss of banking licenses, and severe reputational damage, as demonstrated by major enforcement actions against global banks.

Practical Implementation Checklist

  • Manifest integrity — every published manifest is signed via Sigstore DSSE. Tampering is detectable without trusting an intermediary. Updates are issued as new manifests; the prior manifest is preserved with a supersede marker.
  • Retention — keep signed evidence for at least seven years to satisfy ISO/IEC 17065:2012 audit expectations. Some regulators require longer retention; in that case the deployment policy MUST extend the window rather than relying on the defaults.
  • Versioning — follow Semantic Versioning 2.0.0. Major version bumps require at least a 90-day overlap with the prior major version on every WIA-published reference implementation.
  • Negative-result reporting — a skipped indicator without recorded justification is treated as a failed indicator at audit time.
  • Key rotation — manifest signing keys are rotated per the deployment policy. The previous key is preserved in a published key history so that auditors can verify historical manifests offline.

Reference Topologies

Implementations declare their operational topology in the manifest: single-tenant edge (one runtime per organization), multi-tenant gateway (shared runtime with header-based isolation), federated mesh (multiple runtimes peer to one another and publish manifests to a directory service), or air-gapped batch (signed evidence package transported out-of-band). Each topology ships with reference test vectors so that implementations can validate their deployment matches the canonical pattern.
