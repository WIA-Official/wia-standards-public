Chapter 06

Safety and Human Interaction

📖 15 min read 🏷️ Safety 📅 Updated Jan 2025

Safety-First Design Philosophy

Security robots operate in environments shared with people. While their purpose is to enhance safety, robots themselves must be designed and operated to ensure they never become hazards. This chapter explores the safety principles, standards, and practices essential for responsible security robot deployment.

The fundamental principle guiding security robot safety is that a robot must never cause harm to humans, either through action or inaction. This extends beyond physical safety to include privacy protection, psychological comfort, and social acceptance.

Safety is Non-Negotiable

Unlike features that can be prioritized or deferred, safety must be absolute. A security robot that injures someone—even once—can destroy trust in the technology, expose organizations to liability, and set back the entire industry. Safety considerations must be embedded in every aspect of design, deployment, and operation.

Physical Safety Systems

Security robots incorporate multiple layers of safety systems to prevent collisions and protect people in their environment.

Collision Avoidance

🛡️ LiDAR Safety Zone

Continuous 360° monitoring detects obstacles and triggers avoidance.

  • 20+ Hz scan rate
  • Multi-layer detection zones
  • Automatic speed reduction
📷 Camera Obstacle Detection

Visual detection complements LiDAR for comprehensive awareness.

  • Deep learning detection
  • Transparent object detection
  • Motion prediction
🔊 Ultrasonic Sensors

Close-range detection for low obstacles and edges.

  • Ground-level detection
  • Edge detection
  • Complement to LiDAR
🤚 Bump Sensors

Physical contact detection as last-resort safety layer.

  • Immediate stop on contact
  • Pressure-sensitive
  • Full perimeter coverage

Safety Zone Configuration

Security robots define multiple safety zones with different responses:

Zone Distance Response Purpose
Awareness Zone 5-10 m Track, plan alternative path Proactive avoidance
Caution Zone 2-5 m Reduce speed, prepare to stop Controlled approach
Warning Zone 0.5-2 m Slow to minimum, alert Close proximity handling
Stop Zone < 0.5 m Immediate stop Collision prevention

Emergency Stop Systems

Every security robot must implement reliable emergency stop (E-stop) capability:

  • Physical E-stop Button: Prominent, easily accessible button that immediately halts all motion
  • Wireless E-stop: Remote stop capability from operator console
  • Automatic E-stop: Triggered by detected safety violations
  • Watchdog Timer: Stops robot if software becomes unresponsive

E-stop Requirements

WIA-SEC-ROBOT-001 requires E-stop systems to bring the robot to a complete stop within specified distances based on speed. At maximum patrol speed, stop distance must not exceed 0.5 meters. E-stop must function even if primary computing systems fail.

Mechanical Safety

Robot physical design must minimize injury risk even in the event of contact.

Design Principles

  • Rounded Edges: No sharp corners or protrusions that could cause injury
  • Compliant Surfaces: Soft exterior materials absorb impact energy
  • Low Center of Gravity: Resist tipping that could trap or injure
  • Appropriate Weight: Manageable mass limits collision force
  • Pinch Point Protection: Guards prevent fingers getting caught in mechanisms

Speed Limits

Environment Maximum Speed Rationale
Crowded indoor 0.5 m/s (1.8 km/h) Walking pace, easy to avoid
Normal indoor 1.0 m/s (3.6 km/h) Brisk walk, adequate reaction time
Low-traffic indoor 1.5 m/s (5.4 km/h) Fast walk, reduced risk environment
Outdoor patrol 2.0 m/s (7.2 km/h) Jogging pace, open environment

Human-Robot Interaction

Positive human-robot interaction is essential for acceptance and effectiveness. Robots must communicate their intentions and respond appropriately to human behavior.

Signaling and Communication

💡 Visual Indicators

LED arrays communicate robot state and intentions.

  • Green: Normal operation
  • Yellow: Caution/Attention
  • Red: Alert/Emergency
  • Blue: Recording/Surveillance
🔊 Audio Cues

Sound signals warn of robot presence and actions.

  • Approach warning tones
  • Direction change indicators
  • Alert sounds
  • Voice announcements
📺 Display Screens

Visual displays provide status and enable interaction.

  • Status information
  • Wayfinding assistance
  • Two-way communication
  • Emergency information
🤝 Social Behaviors

Human-friendly behaviors build trust and acceptance.

  • Yielding to pedestrians
  • Predictable movements
  • Appropriate distancing
  • Non-threatening posture

Yielding Behavior

Security robots must defer to humans in shared spaces. This includes:

  • Stopping and waiting when humans are in the path
  • Taking wider detours rather than passing close to people
  • Slowing down when approaching from behind
  • Not "herding" or pressuring people to move
  • Waiting at narrow passages rather than forcing passage

Vulnerable Population Considerations

Security robots may encounter individuals who require special consideration:

Population Consideration Robot Behavior
Children Unpredictable movement, attraction to robot Extra clearance, slower speeds, engage staff
Elderly Slower reaction time, fall risk Extended warning time, wide clearance
Mobility impaired Wheelchair, walker, cane users Never obstruct accessible paths
Visually impaired May not see visual signals Audio announcements, predictable behavior
Hearing impaired Cannot hear audio warnings Visual signals, approach from front

Privacy Protection

Security robots collect significant personal data through their sensors. Privacy protection is both an ethical obligation and legal requirement.

Data Minimization

  • Purpose Limitation: Collect only data necessary for security functions
  • Retention Limits: Delete data when no longer needed
  • Anonymization: Remove identifying information where possible
  • Access Controls: Restrict who can view collected data

Facial Recognition Governance

If facial recognition is used, strict governance is essential:

  • Legal basis established for facial recognition use
  • Data protection impact assessment completed
  • Notice provided to individuals in monitored areas
  • Opt-out mechanisms available where required
  • Enrollment database limited to authorized subjects
  • Regular audits of recognition accuracy across demographics
  • Defined retention and deletion procedures

Regulatory Compliance

Key Privacy Regulations

  • GDPR (EU): Consent, data subject rights, DPIAs
  • CCPA (California): Consumer rights, disclosure requirements
  • BIPA (Illinois): Biometric data consent requirements
  • PIPA (Korea): Personal information protection
  • PDPA (Singapore): Data protection obligations

Cybersecurity Safety

A compromised security robot becomes a security threat. Cybersecurity is therefore a safety concern.

Threat Landscape

  • Unauthorized Control: Attacker takes control of robot movement
  • Data Exfiltration: Captured video/audio stolen
  • Denial of Service: Robot rendered inoperable
  • Surveillance Hijacking: Attacker uses robot cameras for spying
  • Physical Attack Vector: Robot used to gain physical access

Security Controls

Control Category Measures
Authentication Certificate-based authentication, MFA for operators
Encryption TLS 1.3, encrypted storage, secure boot
Network Security Segmentation, firewall, intrusion detection
Software Security Signed updates, vulnerability management
Physical Security Tamper detection, secure enclosures

Emergency Protocols

Security robots must respond appropriately during emergencies without interfering with human safety efforts.

Emergency Behaviors

  • Fire Alarm: Move to designated safe position, clear evacuation routes
  • Active Threat: Continue surveillance if safe, avoid interfering with response
  • Medical Emergency: Maintain area for responders, provide location guidance
  • Power Failure: Safe shutdown, avoid blocking pathways
  • Evacuation: Guide evacuees if programmed, otherwise clear routes

Supporting First Responders

Security robots can provide valuable support during emergencies: real-time video to incident commanders, thermal imaging for search operations, and communication relay in areas with damaged infrastructure. However, this support must never impede human responders.

Key Takeaways from Chapter 6

  • Safety is non-negotiable and must be embedded in all aspects of design and operation
  • Multiple collision avoidance layers provide redundant protection
  • Emergency stop must work even if primary systems fail
  • Mechanical design minimizes injury risk in contact scenarios
  • Clear signaling and yielding behavior build human acceptance
  • Privacy protection requires data minimization and regulatory compliance
  • Cybersecurity failures can create physical safety risks
  • Emergency protocols ensure robots support rather than impede crisis response
