Chapter 3

Material Composition Tracking

Chapter Overview: This chapter explores the comprehensive tracking of battery materials from source to end-of-life. Understanding material composition is essential for regulatory compliance, recycling optimization, and supply chain risk management.

Critical Battery Materials

Modern lithium-ion batteries contain a complex mix of materials, many of which are classified as critical raw materials due to supply constraints, geopolitical risks, or environmental concerns. The WIA-BATTERY_PASSPORT standard provides detailed tracking for all significant materials in the battery value chain.

Material tracking serves multiple purposes beyond regulatory compliance. It enables recyclers to optimize recovery processes, helps manufacturers identify supply chain vulnerabilities, and provides consumers with transparency about the environmental and social impact of their products.

Cathode Active Materials

The cathode represents the largest share of battery value and contains the most critical materials. Different cathode chemistries have distinct material profiles that must be accurately documented.

Li

Lithium

The essential element for all lithium-ion batteries. Sourced primarily from brine operations in South America and hard rock mining in Australia. Tracking includes extraction method, source location, and processing pathway.

Co

Cobalt

Critical for cathode stability in many chemistries. Due diligence requirements are particularly stringent given concentration of supply in the Democratic Republic of Congo. Artisanal mining risks must be documented and mitigated.

Ni

Nickel

Key element for high energy density batteries. Class 1 nickel required for battery applications. Sources include Indonesia, Philippines, Russia, and Canada. Processing pathway affects carbon footprint significantly.

Hazardous Substance Tracking

Battery passports must clearly document any hazardous substances present, in compliance with REACH regulations and other applicable frameworks. This information is critical for safe handling, transport, and end-of-life processing.

Substance Category Examples Documentation Required
SVHC (REACH) Cobalt compounds, certain electrolyte solvents Presence, concentration, SDS reference
Restricted Substances Mercury, cadmium, lead (above thresholds) Confirmed absence or exception documentation
Conflict Minerals Tantalum, tin, tungsten, gold, cobalt Source verification, due diligence reports
Flammable Materials Electrolyte solvents Transport classification, safety data

Supply Chain Due Diligence

Responsible sourcing requires comprehensive due diligence throughout the supply chain. The WIA standard integrates with established due diligence frameworks including OECD Guidelines and RMI programs.

Due Diligence Requirements: Economic operators must establish management systems, identify and assess risks, implement strategies to respond to identified risks, carry out independent third-party audits, and report annually on their supply chain due diligence.

Material Data Exchange

Efficient material tracking requires standardized data exchange between supply chain participants. The WIA standard supports multiple integration patterns for material data sharing.

  • Push Model: Suppliers proactively share material data with downstream customers
  • Pull Model: Customers request material data from suppliers as needed
  • Registry Model: Central registry stores material certificates accessible to authorized parties
  • Blockchain Model: Immutable distributed ledger for material provenance tracking

The choice of integration pattern depends on supply chain relationships, data sensitivity, and existing IT infrastructure. Most implementations use a hybrid approach combining multiple patterns.
