Chapter 2

EU Digital Product Passport Regulations

Chapter Overview: This chapter provides a comprehensive analysis of the European Union Battery Regulation (EU 2023/1542) and its digital product passport requirements. Understanding these regulations is essential for manufacturers, importers, and distributors operating in the European market.

The EU Battery Regulation Framework

The European Union Battery Regulation, officially published as Regulation (EU) 2023/1542, represents a landmark shift in how batteries are regulated throughout their lifecycle. Replacing the previous Battery Directive (2006/66/EC), this regulation introduces binding requirements for sustainability, safety, labeling, and digital transparency.

The regulation applies to all batteries placed on the EU market, regardless of their origin. This includes portable batteries, automotive batteries, electric vehicle batteries, light means of transport (LMT) batteries, and industrial batteries. Each category has specific requirements based on size, application, and environmental impact.

Digital Product Passport Requirements

Article 77 of the EU Battery Regulation mandates the creation of a digital battery passport for certain battery categories. This electronic record must be accessible via a QR code physically affixed to the battery and linked to a unique identifier in the EU Digital Product Passport registry.

Battery Categories Requiring Passports

Battery Category Capacity Threshold Passport Required From
Electric Vehicle Batteries All EV batteries February 2027
Industrial Batteries > 2 kWh capacity February 2027
LMT Batteries All LMT batteries February 2027
Stationary Storage > 2 kWh capacity February 2027

Regulatory Timeline and Milestones

The EU Battery Regulation introduces requirements in phases, giving industry time to adapt while progressively raising standards. Manufacturers must plan their compliance strategies according to this timeline.

August 2024

General provisions enter into force. Sustainability and safety requirements begin. Due diligence obligations for economic operators commence.

February 2025

Carbon footprint declaration becomes mandatory for EV batteries and industrial batteries above 2 kWh. Recycled content declaration required.

August 2025

QR code and data carrier requirements apply. Battery management system data access provisions take effect.

February 2027

Digital battery passport mandatory for all covered battery categories. Carbon footprint performance classes must be displayed. Maximum carbon footprint thresholds apply.

2031

Minimum recycled content requirements become mandatory: 16% cobalt, 6% lithium, 6% nickel.

Compliance Warning

Failure to comply with EU Battery Regulation requirements can result in significant penalties including market access restrictions, product recalls, and financial sanctions. Economic operators should begin compliance preparations immediately to meet the 2027 digital passport deadline.

Access Rights and Data Sharing

The regulation establishes different access levels for various stakeholders. The WIA standard implements these access controls through a comprehensive role-based permission system.

Stakeholder Access Level Data Accessible
General Public Public Basic product info, carbon footprint class, recycled content
End Users Standard Performance data, warranty info, maintenance records
Repairers/Servicers Professional Technical specifications, diagnostic data, repair history
Recyclers Professional Material composition, hazardous substance content, disassembly instructions
Regulators Full Complete passport data, supply chain details, audit logs

Global Compatibility: While designed for EU compliance, the WIA standard architecture supports adaptation to emerging battery passport requirements in other jurisdictions including the United States, China, and Japan. Regional compliance modules can be enabled without modifying core data structures.

Preparing for Compliance

Organizations should begin preparing for digital battery passport requirements immediately. Key steps include assessing current data collection capabilities, identifying gaps in supply chain traceability, and implementing systems that can capture and store required information.

  • Data Audit: Review existing data collection processes against EU requirements
  • System Integration: Prepare IT infrastructure for passport data management
  • Supply Chain Engagement: Ensure suppliers can provide required traceability data
  • Staff Training: Educate teams on new compliance obligations
  • Pilot Programs: Test passport generation before mandatory deadline
