Chapter 6

Recycling and Second-Life Applications

Chapter Overview: This chapter explores the end-of-life pathways for batteries, including recycling processes and second-life applications. The battery passport plays a crucial role in enabling efficient material recovery and extending battery value through repurposing.

Circular Economy for Batteries

The transition to a circular economy for batteries requires comprehensive lifecycle management that maximizes resource efficiency and minimizes waste. Batteries reaching the end of their useful life in one application often retain significant capacity for less demanding uses, and when truly spent, contain valuable materials that can be recovered and reused.

The EU Battery Regulation establishes ambitious targets for recycling efficiency and recycled content, driving the development of advanced recycling infrastructure and second-life business models. The battery passport enables these circular economy flows by providing essential information to recyclers and repurposers.

Second-Life Assessment

Before recycling, batteries should be evaluated for potential second-life applications. EV batteries typically reach end-of-life when capacity falls below 70-80% of original, but they may still be suitable for less demanding stationary storage applications.

Recycling Processes

When second-life is not viable, batteries proceed to recycling. Modern recycling processes can recover over 95% of critical materials including cobalt, nickel, and copper, with lithium recovery rates improving rapidly.

Collection → Discharge → Disassembly → Shredding → Separation → Refining

Recycling Technologies

Process Type Description Recovery Rates
Pyrometallurgical High-temperature smelting process Co, Ni, Cu: greater than 95%; Li: 30-50%
Hydrometallurgical Chemical leaching and precipitation Co, Ni, Li: greater than 90%; Graphite: 70%
Direct Recycling Cathode material regeneration Cathode material: greater than 95%
Hybrid Combined mechanical/chemical All materials: greater than 90%

Extended Producer Responsibility

The EU Battery Regulation establishes Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) obligations requiring producers to finance the collection and recycling of batteries they place on the market. The battery passport supports EPR compliance by tracking ownership and enabling proper allocation of end-of-life costs.

EPR Obligations: Producers must register with EPR schemes in each member state, report batteries placed on market, achieve collection targets (51% by 2028, 61% by 2031), and ensure batteries are processed by authorized recyclers achieving minimum efficiency levels.

Traceability Through End-of-Life

The battery passport maintains its record through the end-of-life phase, documenting the final disposition of the battery and the fate of recovered materials. This enables complete lifecycle accountability and supports verification of recycled content claims for new batteries.

  • Collection Event: Records when and where battery entered collection system
  • Second-Life Transition: Documents repurposing if applicable, with new operator details
  • Recycling Receipt: Confirms handover to authorized recycler
  • Material Recovery Report: Documents actual recovered material quantities
  • Certificate of Recycling: Final closure of passport record
