Current Treatment Landscape

Examining existing therapies, emerging approaches, and their limitations

2.1 The Evolution of Alzheimer's Pharmacotherapy

The journey to find effective Alzheimer's disease treatments has been marked by more failures than successes. Over the past three decades, hundreds of clinical trials have been conducted, with the vast majority failing to demonstrate meaningful clinical benefit. Understanding this history is essential for appreciating both the current treatment options and the revolutionary potential of NAD+ homeostasis approaches.

1993 - Tacrine (Cognex)

First FDA-approved AD medication. Cholinesterase inhibitor withdrawn due to hepatotoxicity. Modest symptomatic benefits only.

1996 - Donepezil (Aricept)

Still widely used cholinesterase inhibitor. Better safety profile than tacrine but limited efficacy.

2000 - Rivastigmine (Exelon)

Second-generation cholinesterase inhibitor with similar modest benefits.

2001 - Galantamine (Razadyne)

Third cholinesterase inhibitor with additional nicotinic receptor modulation.

2003 - Memantine (Namenda)

NMDA receptor antagonist, first non-cholinergic treatment. Approved for moderate to severe AD.

2021 - Aducanumab (Aduhelm)

Controversial anti-amyloid antibody. Accelerated approval later withdrawn. Highlighted desperate need for new approaches.

2023 - Lecanemab (Leqembi)

Anti-amyloid antibody showing modest clinical benefit. First disease-modifying therapy with convincing evidence.

2024 - Donanemab (Kisunla)

Second-generation anti-amyloid therapy. Demonstrates plaque clearing with modest cognitive benefits.

2.2 Cholinesterase Inhibitors: Symptomatic Treatment

The cholinesterase inhibitors remain the most commonly prescribed AD medications despite their limited efficacy. These drugs work by preventing the breakdown of acetylcholine, a neurotransmitter that is depleted in Alzheimer's disease. However, they address only the symptoms of the disease, not the underlying pathology.

Mechanism of Action

Cholinesterase inhibitors block the enzyme acetylcholinesterase, which normally breaks down acetylcholine at synaptic junctions. By inhibiting this enzyme, acetylcholine levels increase in the synaptic cleft, potentially improving neurotransmission in cholinergic pathways that are compromised in AD.

Clinical Efficacy

Meta-analyses of cholinesterase inhibitor trials reveal modest benefits at best:

  • Cognitive improvement: 2-3 point improvement on ADAS-Cog scale (out of 70 points)
  • Functional benefit: Slight delay in decline of activities of daily living
  • Duration: Benefits typically last 6-12 months before disease progression overwhelms the effect
  • Response rate: Only 30-40% of patients show measurable improvement
  • Disease modification: Zero - these drugs do not alter disease progression

The Symptomatic Treatment Paradox

While cholinesterase inhibitors may provide temporary symptomatic relief for some patients, they do nothing to address the underlying neurodegenerative process. Brain pathology continues to worsen even as symptoms are temporarily masked. This is akin to treating lung cancer with cough suppressants - the symptom may improve while the disease progresses unchecked.

2.3 Memantine: NMDA Receptor Antagonist

Memantine represents a different pharmacological approach to AD treatment. As an NMDA receptor antagonist, it modulates glutamatergic neurotransmission and may protect against excitotoxicity.

Mechanism and Clinical Use

Memantine is a low-to-moderate affinity, voltage-dependent, uncompetitive NMDA receptor antagonist. In AD, chronic low-level activation of NMDA receptors by glutamate may contribute to neuronal damage. Memantine blocks excessive NMDA receptor activation while allowing normal physiological activation to occur.

  • Approved indication: Moderate to severe AD
  • Dosing: 10 mg twice daily (or 28 mg extended-release daily)
  • Combination therapy: Often used with cholinesterase inhibitors
  • Side effects: Generally well-tolerated; dizziness, confusion, headache

2.4 Anti-Amyloid Therapies: The New Era

After decades of failure, anti-amyloid monoclonal antibodies have finally demonstrated modest clinical benefits. However, these successes come with significant caveats, costs, and risks.

Lecanemab (Leqembi)

Lecanemab, approved by the FDA in January 2023, represents a landmark in AD therapeutics as the first treatment with convincing evidence of disease modification.

Clarity AD Trial Results (2024 Long-term Follow-up)

Study design: 1,795 participants with early AD, 18-month double-blind phase

Primary outcome: 27% slowing of cognitive decline on CDR-SB scale

Amyloid reduction: 68% reduction in brain amyloid on PET imaging

Clinical significance: Approximately 5-month delay in disease progression

Administration and Monitoring

  • Route: Intravenous infusion every 2 weeks
  • Duration: Each infusion takes 60 minutes
  • Monitoring: Baseline and periodic MRI scans to detect ARIA
  • Setting: Specialized infusion centers only
  • Annual cost: $26,500 (drug only, not including monitoring and administration)

Donanemab (Kisunla)

Approved in July 2024, donanemab targets a pyroglutamate form of amyloid-beta found specifically in plaques.

2.5 ARIA: A Serious Safety Concern

Amyloid-related imaging abnormalities (ARIA) represent a significant and potentially life-threatening complication of anti-amyloid antibody therapies. ARIA encompasses both edema (ARIA-E) and microhemorrhages/hemosiderosis (ARIA-H).

Pathophysiology of ARIA

The exact mechanisms underlying ARIA remain incompletely understood, but current theories suggest:

  • Vascular amyloid clearance: Removal of amyloid from blood vessel walls may temporarily weaken vessel integrity
  • Inflammatory response: Antibody-mediated immune activation around amyloid deposits triggers local inflammation
  • Blood-brain barrier disruption: Inflammation and amyloid removal compromise BBB function
  • Fluid accumulation: BBB disruption leads to vasogenic edema

Clinical Presentation and Management

ARIA Warning Signs

Symptomatic ARIA (25-30% of cases):

  • Headache
  • Confusion or altered mental status
  • Visual disturbances
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Gait difficulty
  • Seizures (rare but serious)

Management: Immediate discontinuation of treatment, supportive care, and close monitoring until resolution.

Quality-Adjusted Life Years (QALY)

The Institute for Clinical and Economic Review (ICER) conducted comprehensive cost-effectiveness analyses of anti-amyloid therapies in 2024:

  • Lecanemab cost per QALY: $367,000 to $617,000 (depending on assumptions)
  • Standard cost-effectiveness threshold: $100,000-150,000 per QALY
  • Conclusion: Not cost-effective at current pricing
  • Break-even price: $8,900-$15,700 annually for cost-effectiveness

Access and Equity Concerns

The high cost and intensive monitoring requirements of anti-amyloid therapies create significant barriers to access. An estimated 95% of potentially eligible AD patients worldwide cannot access these treatments due to cost, lack of infusion infrastructure, or absence of specialized monitoring capabilities. This raises profound questions about health equity and the value of treatments that benefit only a privileged minority.

2.7 Pipeline Therapies and Alternative Approaches

Beyond anti-amyloid antibodies, numerous other therapeutic strategies are being investigated in 2024-2025 clinical trials.

Anti-Tau Therapies

Following the limited success of anti-amyloid approaches, tau-targeting therapies have garnered increased attention:

  • Semorinemab: Phase 2 trial in mild-moderate AD (ongoing 2025)
  • Tilavonemab: Phase 2 early AD trial results expected Q2 2025
  • E2814: Anti-tau antibody targeting extracellular tau, Phase 2
  • Challenge: Tau is primarily intracellular, making antibody access difficult

2.8 Limitations of Current Approaches

Despite recent progress, fundamental limitations constrain the effectiveness of current AD treatments:

Biological Limitations

  • Late intervention: Most therapies are tested in patients who already have significant irreversible brain damage
  • Single-target focus: AD is multifactorial; targeting one pathway leaves others unchecked
  • Blood-brain barrier: Many promising compounds cannot effectively penetrate into the brain
  • Heterogeneity: "Alzheimer's disease" likely represents multiple distinct pathologies requiring different treatments
  • Downstream intervention: Current therapies target consequences rather than root causes

Practical Limitations

  • Burden of administration: Biweekly or monthly infusions for indefinite duration
  • Monitoring requirements: Repeated MRI scans increase cost and complexity
  • Side effect management: ARIA requires treatment discontinuation in many patients
  • Limited efficacy: Even "successful" treatments slow decline by only 25-35%
  • No restoration: No current therapy reverses cognitive decline or restores lost function

The Case for Paradigm Shift

The limitations of current therapies underscore the urgent need for fundamentally different approaches. Rather than targeting individual downstream pathological features like amyloid or tau, emerging evidence suggests that addressing upstream metabolic dysfunction - particularly NAD+ homeostasis - may offer broader, more effective intervention. The 2025 breakthrough demonstrating complete neurological recovery in AD mice through NAD+ restoration represents exactly this paradigm shift.

Reasons for Treatment Discontinuation

Medication adherence in AD is poor, with 40-60% of patients discontinuing therapy within one year:

  • Perceived lack of benefit: 35% of discontinuations
  • Side effects: 28% of discontinuations
  • Cost: 15% of discontinuations
  • Caregiver burden: 12% of discontinuations
  • Disease progression: 10% of discontinuations

Chapter Summary: Key Takeaways

  1. Current AD medications provide only modest symptomatic benefits without altering disease progression. Cholinesterase inhibitors improve cognition by 2-3 points on ADAS-Cog, representing clinically marginal effects.
  2. Anti-amyloid therapies (lecanemab, donanemab) represent the first disease-modifying treatments but slow decline by only 27-35% at costs exceeding $44,000 annually per patient.
  3. ARIA (amyloid-related imaging abnormalities) affects 13-24% of patients on anti-amyloid therapies, with higher rates in APOE ε4 carriers, posing serious safety concerns including brain edema and microhemorrhages.
  4. Cost-effectiveness analyses show current anti-amyloid therapies cost $367,000-617,000 per QALY, far exceeding the $100,000-150,000 threshold, raising profound questions about healthcare resource allocation.
  5. Over 99% of AD clinical trials have failed over the past two decades, with $42 billion invested in unsuccessful drug development, highlighting the inadequacy of current therapeutic paradigms.
  6. Practical limitations including IV administration requirements, intensive monitoring protocols, and limited global access mean fewer than 1% of diagnosed AD patients worldwide receive disease-modifying therapies.
  7. The marginal benefits and significant limitations of current approaches create an urgent need for paradigm-shifting therapies that address upstream metabolic dysfunction rather than downstream pathological consequences.
