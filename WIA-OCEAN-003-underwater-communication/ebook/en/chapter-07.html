Chapter 7

Real-World Deployments

From laboratory to ocean: case studies of operational underwater communication systems serving science, industry, and defense worldwide.

Ocean Observatories

Cabled ocean observatories provide persistent monitoring with high-bandwidth fiber-optic backbones, supplemented by wireless acoustic links to mobile platforms and sensors.

NEPTUNE Canada (Ocean Networks Canada)

800 km of fiber-optic cable off British Columbia connects 5 nodes at depths to 2,660m. Each node provides power and data to instruments via junction boxes. Wireless acoustic modems (10-25 kHz, 5 kbps) link mobile AUVs and gliders to the cabled infrastructure. Real-time data streams to shore via fiber, enabling remote instrument control and adaptive sampling.

DONET Japan (Dense Ocean Network for Earthquakes and Tsunamis)

Seafloor network off Japan's Pacific coast monitors seismic activity for earthquake/tsunami early warning. 51 observation points connected by fiber; acoustic links relay data from temporary instruments. DONET's 3-second warning improvement (versus land-based detection) has saved lives during recent earthquakes.

Offshore Oil and Gas

Subsea production systems require reliable communication for monitoring wellheads, manifolds, and pipelines. Safety-critical applications demand <10⁻⁹ BER.

Subsea Control Systems

Acoustic telemetry (15-35 kHz) provides wireless monitoring where fiber or copper umbilicals are impractical. Commands (valve open/close, flow rate changes) use low-rate (100 bps) highly-reliable transmission with triple redundancy. Status monitoring (pressure, temperature, flow) tolerates higher BER at higher rates (1-5 kbps).

Autonomous Underwater Vehicles

AUVs conduct surveys, inspections, and scientific missions requiring intermittent communication for mission updates and data offload.

Hybrid Communication Strategy

Long-range acoustic (5-10 km, 100-1000 bps) for command/control during mission. Short-range optical (50m, 10 Mbps) for high-speed data download during docking or rendezvous. Surface WiFi/cellular when vehicle surfaces for position fixes and mission data upload.

KIOST AUV Deployments

KIOST operates multiple AUVs in the East Sea and Yellow Sea for fishery surveys, oceanographic monitoring, and underwater archaeology. Their ISiMI AUV uses K-MAC acoustic modems (see Chapter 2) achieving 15 kbps at 5 km in challenging shallow-water multipath conditions.

Military Applications

Defense applications demand covert, jam-resistant communication with minimal detectability.

Submarine Communication

VLF/ELF broadcast (16-76 kHz) from shore transmitters penetrates seawater to communicate with submerged submarines. Extremely low data rates (<100 bps) limit messages to brief status updates or recall orders. Submarines must approach periscope depth for high-bandwidth satellite communication.

Underwater Sensor Networks for Area Denial

Distributed acoustic sensors detect and track submarines, surface vessels, and UUVs. Nodes communicate via CDMA acoustic modems (20-30 kHz) for interference resistance. DTN protocols handle intermittent connectivity as mobile nodes enter/exit range.

Challenges and Lessons Learned

Real-world deployments reveal challenges absent from laboratory tests:

  • Biofouling: Marine growth on transducers degrades performance; requires cleaning or antifouling coatings
  • Shipping Noise: Commercial traffic creates impulsive noise; adaptive noise filtering essential
  • Thermoclines: Temperature layers refract acoustic signals unpredictably; requires environmental adaptation
  • Battery Life: Power constraints limit communication duty cycle; sleep/wake protocols conserve energy
  • Cost: Deep-rated acoustic modems ($10k-$50k) limit large-scale deployments; low-cost LED optical systems emerging

Best Practices for Deployment

  • Environmental Characterization: Measure sound speed profile, ambient noise before deployment
  • Link Budget Analysis: Calculate SNR margins accounting for worst-case conditions
  • Redundancy: Multi-path routing and backup communication modes prevent single-point failures
  • Testing: Extensive pool and field trials before operational deployment
  • Monitoring: Continuous performance metrics (BER, SNR, link quality) enable proactive maintenance
  • Standardization: WIA-OCEAN-003 compliance ensures interoperability between vendors

Summary: Key Takeaways

  • Observatory Networks: Fiber backbones with wireless acoustic/optical for mobile platforms
  • Industrial Systems: High reliability for safety-critical offshore oil/gas operations
  • AUV Communication: Hybrid approach—acoustic for command, optical for data, RF at surface
  • Military Applications: VLF/ELF for submarines; covert UUV communication
  • Real-World Challenges: Biofouling, shipping noise, thermoclines, power constraints
  • Best Practices: Environmental characterization, link budgets, redundancy, WIA-OCEAN-003 compliance
  • KIOST Leadership: East Sea deployments demonstrate Korean expertise
