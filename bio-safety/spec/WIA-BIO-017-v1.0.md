## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Purpose

This specification defines comprehensive biosafety standards for handling biological materials, pathogens, and genetically modified organisms (GMOs) in laboratory, clinical, and industrial settings.

### 1.2 Scope

The standard covers:
- Biosafety level classifications (BSL-1 through BSL-4)
- Risk assessment and hazard identification
- Containment strategies and facility design
- Personal protective equipment requirements
- Decontamination and waste disposal procedures
- Emergency response and incident management

### 1.3 Philosophy

**弘益人間 (Benefit All Humanity)** - This standard aims to protect laboratory personnel, the public, and the environment from biological hazards while enabling scientific advancement that benefits all of humanity.

### 1.4 Terminology

- **Biosafety Level (BSL)**: Containment precautions required to safely handle biological agents
- **Risk Group**: Classification of biological agents based on pathogenicity and transmissibility
- **Biological Safety Cabinet (BSC)**: Primary containment device for aerosol protection
- **Decontamination**: Process of reducing or eliminating pathogenic microorganisms
- **Exposure**: Contact with infectious agents through inhalation, ingestion, or skin penetration
- **Containment**: Physical barriers and practices to prevent release of biological agents

## 2. Biosafety Levels (BSL)

### 2.1 BSL-1: Minimal Risk

#### 2.1.1 Agents
- Not known to consistently cause disease in healthy adults
- Examples: *E. coli* K-12, *Bacillus subtilis*, non-pathogenic yeast

#### 2.1.2 Practices
- Standard microbiological practices
- Work performed on open benchtops
- No special containment devices required
- Hand washing facilities available

#### 2.1.3 Safety Equipment
- Laboratory coats
- Gloves when handling biological materials
- Eye protection if splashes anticipated

#### 2.1.4 Facility Requirements
- Easily cleanable surfaces
- Sink for hand washing
- Doors that separate laboratory from public areas

### 2.2 BSL-2: Moderate Risk

#### 2.2.1 Agents
- Associated with human disease; exposure hazards include percutaneous injury, ingestion, mucous membrane exposure
- Examples: *Staphylococcus aureus*, hepatitis B virus, HIV, *Salmonella*

#### 2.2.2 Practices
- BSL-1 practices plus:
  - Limited access during work
  - Sharps precautions
  - Biosafety manual defining waste decontamination
  - Medical surveillance as appropriate

#### 2.2.3 Safety Equipment
- Class I or II Biological Safety Cabinets (BSC) for procedures generating aerosols
- Protective laboratory clothing
- Gloves and eye/face protection

#### 2.2.4 Facility Requirements
- BSL-1 plus:
  - Autoclave available (in facility or nearby)
  - Self-closing, lockable laboratory doors
  - Eyewash station readily available

### 2.3 BSL-3: Serious or Lethal Infection Risk

#### 2.3.1 Agents
- Indigenous or exotic agents with potential for aerosol transmission; disease may have serious or lethal consequences
- Examples: *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*, SARS-CoV-2, yellow fever virus, *Coxiella burnetii*

#### 2.3.2 Practices
- BSL-2 practices plus:
  - Controlled access (only authorized personnel)
  - Decontamination of laboratory clothing before laundering
  - Baseline serum samples for personnel
  - Respiratory protection as needed

#### 2.3.3 Safety Equipment
- Class I or II BSC for all open manipulations
- Protective laboratory clothing (solid-front gowns, scrub suits)
- Gloves in two layers if needed
- Respiratory protection (fit-tested N95 or higher)

#### 2.3.4 Facility Requirements
- BSL-2 plus:
  - Physical separation from access corridors
  - Self-closing, double-door access
  - Sealed penetrations
  - Directional inward airflow
  - HEPA-filtered exhaust air
  - Autoclave in the laboratory
  - Hands-free sink near exit

### 2.4 BSL-4: Maximum Containment

#### 2.4.1 Agents
- Dangerous and exotic agents posing high risk of life-threatening disease; aerosol transmission or unknown transmission pathways
- Examples: Ebola virus, Marburg virus, Lassa fever virus, smallpox virus

#### 2.4.2 Practices
- BSL-3 practices plus:
  - Clothing change before entering
  - Shower on exit
  - All material decontaminated before exit
  - Detailed operations manual
  - Personnel competency verification

#### 2.4.3 Safety Equipment
- Class III BSC (glovebox), or
- Positive pressure personnel suit with life support system
- Double gloves
- Full-body protective suit

#### 2.4.4 Facility Requirements
- BSL-3 plus:
  - Isolated zone (separate building or isolated zone within building)
  - Dedicated supply and exhaust air systems
  - HEPA filtration and/or incineration of exhaust air
  - Effluent decontamination system
  - Chemical shower for decontamination of suit
  - Airlock entry
  - Continuous negative pressure
  - Multiple containment barriers

## 3. Risk Group Classification

### 3.1 Risk Group Framework

Biological agents are classified into four risk groups based on:
- Pathogenicity
- Mode of transmission
- Availability of prophylaxis or treatment
- Host range

### 3.2 Risk Group 1 (Low Individual and Community Risk)

**Criteria:**
- Unlikely to cause human or animal disease
- No or minimal hazard to laboratory workers or community
- Examples: *Bacillus subtilis*, *Lactobacillus* spp., baker's yeast

**Containment:** BSL-1

### 3.3 Risk Group 2 (Moderate Individual Risk, Low Community Risk)

**Criteria:**
- Can cause human or animal disease
- Unlikely to spread in the community
- Effective treatment and prophylaxis usually available
- Examples: Hepatitis B/C, *Toxoplasma*, *Schistosoma*

**Containment:** BSL-2

### 3.4 Risk Group 3 (High Individual Risk, Low Community Risk)

**Criteria:**
- Usually causes serious human or animal disease
- May spread to the community
- Effective treatment and prophylaxis usually available
- Examples: HIV, *M. tuberculosis*, West Nile virus, SARS-CoV-2

**Containment:** BSL-3

### 3.5 Risk Group 4 (High Individual and Community Risk)

**Criteria:**
- Usually causes serious human or animal disease
- May be readily transmitted from one individual to another
- Effective treatment and prophylaxis not usually available
- Examples: Ebola, Marburg, Lassa fever, smallpox

**Containment:** BSL-4

## 4. Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

### 4.1 PPE Selection Matrix

| BSL Level | Gloves | Lab Coat/Gown | Eye Protection | Respiratory Protection | Footwear |
|-----------|--------|---------------|----------------|----------------------|----------|
| BSL-1 | Optional | Lab coat | If splash risk | None | Closed-toe shoes |
| BSL-2 | Required | Lab coat/gown | Required | N95 if aerosols | Closed-toe shoes |
| BSL-3 | Double gloves | Solid-front gown | Required | Fit-tested N95/PAPR | Dedicated shoes |
| BSL-4 | Double gloves | Positive pressure suit | Full face shield | Supplied air | Dedicated boots |

### 4.2 Glove Selection

#### 4.2.1 Material Selection
```
Glove Selection Index (GSI) = (C × P × D) / R
```

Where:
- `C` = Chemical resistance rating (0-10)
- `P` = Puncture resistance rating (0-10)
- `D` = Dexterity rating (0-10)
- `R` = Risk level (1-4)

**Materials:**
- **Latex**: Good dexterity, moderate chemical resistance
- **Nitrile**: Excellent chemical resistance, good puncture resistance
- **Neoprene**: Broad chemical resistance
- **Butyl rubber**: Best for ketones and esters
- **Silver Shield**: Multi-chemical resistance

#### 4.2.2 Double Gloving Protocol
- Inner glove: Nitrile or latex
- Outer glove: Extended cuff, different color for breach detection
- Change outer gloves between tasks
- Change both gloves if breach suspected

### 4.3 Respiratory Protection

#### 4.3.1 N95 Respirators (BSL-2/3)
- Filters ≥95% of airborne particles ≥0.3 μm
- Fit testing required annually
- Seal check before each use
- Replace when breathing resistance increases

#### 4.3.2 Powered Air-Purifying Respirators (PAPR)
- Motorized fan draws air through HEPA filter
- Provides higher protection factor
- Useful for personnel who cannot be fit-tested for N95
- Battery maintenance critical

#### 4.3.3 Supplied Air Systems (BSL-4)
- Positive pressure suit with external air supply
- Provides highest level of protection
- Requires extensive training
- Buddy system mandatory

### 4.4 Eye and Face Protection

- **Safety glasses**: Minimum for BSL-1/2
- **Goggles**: Required for splash hazards
- **Face shields**: Required for BSL-3, use with safety glasses/goggles
- **Full face respirator**: Provides eye and respiratory protection

## 5. Decontamination Procedures

### 5.1 Decontamination Methods

#### 5.1.1 Autoclaving (Steam Sterilization)
**Principle:** Moist heat denatures proteins and nucleic acids

**Standard Cycle:**
- Temperature: 121°C (250°F)
- Pressure: 15 psi
- Time: 15-30 minutes (depending on load)
- Validation: Biological indicators (*Geobacillus stearothermophilus* spores)

**Kill Efficiency:**
```
Log Reduction = log₁₀(N₀/N)
```

Where:
- `N₀` = Initial microbial load
- `N` = Final microbial load
- Target: ≥6 log reduction

**Applications:**
- Laboratory glassware and equipment
- Biohazardous waste
- Contaminated liquids
- Surgical instruments

#### 5.1.2 Chemical Disinfection

**Disinfectant Selection:**
```
Disinfection Efficacy (DE) = (C × T) / R
```

Where:
- `C` = Concentration (% or ppm)
- `T` = Contact time (minutes)
- `R` = Resistance factor (organism-specific)

**Common Disinfectants:**

| Agent | Concentration | Contact Time | Spectrum | BSL |
|-------|---------------|--------------|----------|-----|
| 70% Ethanol | 70% v/v | 10 min | Bacteria, enveloped viruses | BSL-1/2 |
| 10% Bleach | 5,000-10,000 ppm | 10 min | Broad spectrum including spores | BSL-2/3 |
| Phenolics | 2-5% | 10 min | Bacteria, fungi, enveloped viruses | BSL-2 |
| Quaternary Ammonium | 0.1-0.5% | 10 min | Bacteria, some viruses | BSL-1/2 |
| Peracetic Acid | 0.2-0.35% | 5-10 min | Broad spectrum including spores | BSL-3/4 |
| Hydrogen Peroxide | 3-7.5% | 10-30 min | Bacteria, viruses, fungi | BSL-2/3 |

#### 5.1.3 Dry Heat Sterilization
- Temperature: 160-170°C
- Time: 1-2 hours
- Applications: Glassware, metal instruments, powders, oils
- Validation: *Bacillus atrophaeus* spores

#### 5.1.4 UV-C Irradiation
- Wavelength: 254 nm (germicidal)
- Mechanism: DNA/RNA damage
- Applications: Surface decontamination, BSC decontamination
- Limitations: No penetration, shadowing effects

**UV Dose Calculation:**
```
UV Dose (mJ/cm²) = Intensity (μW/cm²) × Time (seconds) / 1000
```

Recommended doses:
- Bacteria: 10-20 mJ/cm²
- Viruses: 20-40 mJ/cm²
- Spores: 50-100 mJ/cm²

### 5.2 Spill Response

#### 5.2.1 Minor Spill (<10 mL, BSL-1/2)
1. Alert nearby personnel
2. Don appropriate PPE (gloves, lab coat, eye protection)
3. Cover spill with absorbent material
4. Apply disinfectant (10% bleach or equivalent)
5. Allow 20-30 min contact time
6. Clean up with disposable materials
7. Dispose in biohazard waste
8. Decontaminate area again
9. Document incident

#### 5.2.2 Major Spill (>10 mL or BSL-3/4)
1. Evacuate area immediately
2. Close doors, post warning signs
3. Notify supervisor and biosafety officer
4. Allow aerosols to settle (30 min)
5. Don full PPE (include respirator for BSL-3/4)
6. Enter with buddy system
7. Apply absorbent pads
8. Apply disinfectant in concentric circles (outside to inside)
9. Extended contact time (30-60 min)
10. Clean up methodically
11. Decontaminate all surfaces and equipment
12. Document thoroughly
13. Investigate root cause

### 5.3 Waste Decontamination

#### 5.3.1 Solid Waste
- Autoclave at 121°C for 30 min
- Validate with biological indicators
- Red biohazard bags for autoclaving
- Clear bags prohibited (may melt)
- Maximum fill: 2/3 full

#### 5.3.2 Sharps
- Rigid, puncture-resistant containers
- Autoclave before disposal
- Never recap needles
- Fill to marked line only

#### 5.3.3 Liquid Waste
- Chemical disinfection (10% bleach, 30 min) then drain
- Or autoclave in appropriate containers
- Neutralize corrosive wastes before disposal

## 6. Risk Assessment Methodology

### 6.1 Quantitative Risk Assessment

#### 6.1.1 Risk Score Calculation
```
Risk Score (R) = (H × P × E) / C
```

Where:
- `H` = Hazard level (1-4, corresponding to Risk Group)
- `P` = Probability of exposure (0-1)
- `E` = Extent of potential impact (1-10)
- `C` = Containment effectiveness (0.1-1)

**Risk Score Interpretation:**
- **0-10**: Low risk (acceptable with standard precautions)
- **10-30**: Medium risk (enhanced precautions required)
- **30-60**: High risk (BSL upgrade or additional controls needed)
- **>60**: Extreme risk (reconsider procedure or implement maximum controls)

#### 6.1.2 Probability of Exposure Estimation
```
P = (F × D) × (1 - PPE_eff) × (1 - EC_eff)
```

Where:
- `F` = Frequency of operations (events/year)
- `D` = Duration of each operation (hours)
- `PPE_eff` = PPE effectiveness (0-1)
- `EC_eff` = Engineering control effectiveness (0-1)

#### 6.1.3 Impact Extent Scoring

| Score | Impact | Description |
|-------|--------|-------------|
| 1-2 | Minimal | Mild symptoms, self-limiting |
| 3-4 | Low | Moderate illness, full recovery expected |
| 5-6 | Moderate | Serious illness, possible complications |
| 7-8 | High | Severe illness, long-term sequelae |
| 9-10 | Critical | Life-threatening or fatal |

### 6.2 Containment Effectiveness

#### 6.2.1 Calculation
```
Containment Effectiveness (C) = (BSC_eff × PPE_eff × Proc_eff)^(1/3)
```

Where:
- `BSC_eff` = Biological Safety Cabinet effectiveness (0.8-0.999)
- `PPE_eff` = Personal Protective Equipment effectiveness (0.5-0.99)
- `Proc_eff` = Procedural adherence effectiveness (0.6-0.95)

**Typical Values:**
- BSL-1: C = 0.3 - 0.5
- BSL-2: C = 0.5 - 0.7
- BSL-3: C = 0.7 - 0.9
- BSL-4: C = 0.9 - 0.99

### 6.3 Exposure Assessment

#### 6.3.1 Quantitative Exposure Model
```
Exposure Assessment (EA) = (D × F × T) × SF
```

Where:
- `D` = Dose concentration (CFU/ml or viral particles/ml)
- `F` = Frequency of exposure events
- `T` = Time duration of exposure (minutes)
- `SF` = Safety factor (typically 0.01-0.1)

#### 6.3.2 Infectious Dose Considerations

**ID₅₀ (Infectious Dose for 50% of exposed):**
- Risk Group 1: >10⁶ organisms
- Risk Group 2: 10³ - 10⁶ organisms
- Risk Group 3: 10 - 10³ organisms
- Risk Group 4: 1 - 10 organisms

**Route-Specific Multipliers:**
- Inhalation: 1.0 (most efficient)
- Ingestion: 0.1 - 0.5
- Percutaneous: 0.01 - 0.1
- Mucous membrane: 0.1 - 0.5

## 7. Incident Reporting and Response

### 7.1 Incident Classification

#### 7.1.1 Severity Levels

| Level | Description | Examples | Response Time |
|-------|-------------|----------|---------------|
| 1 | Minor | Small spill BSL-1, minor equipment malfunction | 24 hours |
| 2 | Moderate | BSL-2 spill, needlestick with low-risk agent | 4 hours |
| 3 | Serious | BSL-3 exposure, major equipment failure | 1 hour |
| 4 | Critical | BSL-4 breach, aerosol release in BSL-3 | Immediate |

### 7.2 Exposure Response Protocol

#### 7.2.1 Immediate Actions
1. **Stop work immediately**
2. **Remove from exposure source**
3. **Decontaminate affected area:**
   - Skin: Wash with soap and water for 15 minutes
   - Eyes: Irrigate with water/saline for 15 minutes
   - Inhalation: Move to fresh air
4. **Remove contaminated clothing**
5. **Notify supervisor**
6. **Seek medical attention**

#### 7.2.2 Medical Evaluation
- Assess exposure route and severity
- Identify infectious agent and risk group
- Determine infectious dose
- Review immunization status
- Consider post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP)
- Establish follow-up schedule
- Document thoroughly

### 7.3 Incident Investigation

#### 7.3.1 Root Cause Analysis
Use 5 Whys methodology:
1. What happened?
2. Why did it happen? (immediate cause)
3. Why did that happen? (contributing factors)
4. Why did that happen? (systemic issues)
5. Why did that happen? (root cause)

#### 7.3.2 Corrective Actions
- Immediate: Stop recurrence
- Short-term: Fix immediate causes
- Long-term: Address systemic issues
- Preventive: Enhance safety culture

### 7.4 Documentation Requirements

**Incident Report Must Include:**
- Date, time, and location
- Personnel involved
- Biological agents involved
- Type of incident (spill, exposure, equipment failure)
- Severity classification
- Immediate actions taken
- Medical evaluation summary
- Root cause analysis
- Corrective and preventive actions
- Follow-up plan
- Lessons learned

## 8. International Biosafety Regulations

### 8.1 World Health Organization (WHO)

#### 8.1.1 Laboratory Biosafety Manual (LBM)
- Definitive international biosafety reference
- Current edition: 4th edition (2020)
- Provides framework for national regulations
- Risk assessment and biosafety levels

#### 8.1.2 Biorisk Management Standards
- **CWA 15793:2011**: Laboratory Biorisk Management
- **CEN Workshop Agreement**: Voluntary standard
- Systematic approach to biorisk management

### 8.2 United States Regulations

#### 8.2.1 Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC)
**Biosafety in Microbiological and Biomedical Laboratories (BMBL)**
- Current edition: 6th edition (2020)
- Companion to WHO LBM
- Specific to U.S. practices

**Select Agent Program**
- Regulates possession and use of biological select agents and toxins
- Registration required
- Security risk assessments
- Inspections and compliance

#### 8.2.2 National Institutes of Health (NIH)
**NIH Guidelines for Research Involving Recombinant or Synthetic Nucleic Acid Molecules**
- Governs recombinant DNA research
- Institutional Biosafety Committee (IBC) required
- Four containment levels parallel to BSL

#### 8.2.3 Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA)
**Bloodborne Pathogens Standard (29 CFR 1910.1030)**
- Protects workers from bloodborne pathogens
- Exposure Control Plan required
- Universal precautions
- Hepatitis B vaccination

### 8.3 European Union Regulations

#### 8.3.1 Directive 2000/54/EC
- Protection of workers from biological agents
- Classification of biological agents
- Risk assessment requirements
- Containment measures

#### 8.3.2 Directive 2009/41/EC
- Contained use of genetically modified microorganisms
- Four containment levels
- Notification and authorization procedures

### 8.4 International Organizations

#### 8.4.1 International Air Transport Association (IATA)
**Dangerous Goods Regulations (DGR)**
- Shipping infectious substances
- UN 2814 (Category A) and UN 3373 (Category B)
- Packaging and labeling requirements
- Training certification

#### 8.4.2 Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD)
**Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety**
- Regulates transboundary movement of living modified organisms (LMOs)
- Advanced Informed Agreement procedure
- Risk assessment and management

## 10. Safety Protocols

### 10.1 Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs)

Every biosafety program must have written SOPs for:
- Agent-specific handling procedures
- Use of biosafety cabinets
- Waste decontamination
- Spill response
- Equipment maintenance
- Emergency procedures

### 10.2 Training Requirements

#### 10.2.1 Initial Training
- Biological hazards awareness
- Biosafety level requirements
- PPE selection and use
- Decontamination procedures
- Incident response
- Hands-on BSC training
- Emergency procedures

#### 10.2.2 Refresher Training
- Annually for BSL-1/2
- Semi-annually for BSL-3
- Quarterly for BSL-4
- After incidents or procedure changes

### 10.3 Medical Surveillance

#### 10.3.1 Pre-Employment
- Baseline health assessment
- Immunization review and updates
- TB testing (for BSL-3/4)
- Serum banking (BSL-3/4)

#### 10.3.2 Periodic Monitoring
- Annual health assessment
- TB testing (annual for BSL-3, semi-annual for BSL-4)
- Agent-specific serology if indicated
- Occupational health records

### 10.4 Emergency Preparedness

#### 10.4.1 Emergency Equipment
- Eyewash stations (tested weekly)
- Safety showers (tested monthly)
- Spill kits (checked monthly)
- First aid supplies
- Emergency contact information posted

#### 10.4.2 Evacuation Procedures
- Emergency exits clearly marked
- Evacuation routes posted
- Assembly points designated
- Regular drills (annually minimum)

## Appendix A: Biosafety Cabinet Types

### A.1 Class I BSC
- Protects personnel and environment
- Does NOT protect product
- Airflow: 75 fpm minimum face velocity
- HEPA-filtered exhaust
- Use: Low to moderate risk, non-sterile procedures

### A.2 Class II BSC
**Type A1:**
- 70% recirculated, 30% exhausted
- Minimum 75 fpm face velocity
- HEPA-filtered supply and exhaust
- Most common type

**Type A2:**
- 70% recirculated, 30% exhausted
- Can be ducted or unducted
- Most versatile

**Type B1:**
- 30% recirculated, 70% exhausted
- Must be hard-ducted
- For use with small amounts of chemicals

**Type B2 (Total Exhaust):**
- 0% recirculated, 100% exhausted
- Must be hard-ducted
- For use with volatile chemicals and radionuclides

### A.3 Class III BSC (Glovebox)
- Totally enclosed, gas-tight
- Negative pressure
- Operations through attached gloves
- Maximum protection for BSL-4
- HEPA-filtered supply and exhaust
