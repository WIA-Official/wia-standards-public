Chapter 03: WIA Rainforest Conservation Standard at a Glance

3.1 Standard at a glance

WIA Rainforest Conservation is a four-phase open standard for forest-condition observations and federated forest data exchange. Phase 1 fixes JSON envelopes. Phase 2 fixes the HTTP surface. Phase 3 fixes federation and indigenous community consent semantics. Phase 4 bridges to REDD+ MRV, FAO FRA, GBIF biodiversity, satellite imagery pipelines, and the WIA family.

3.2 Object families

  • Forest condition observation — one observation tying a coordinate location to a forest-state classification
  • Deforestation event — a localised forest-loss event with geometry, area, and detection method
  • Biodiversity survey — species-occurrence records (compatible with GBIF Darwin Core)
  • Indigenous land claim — a community-claimed territory boundary with custodian attribution
  • Carbon stock estimate — above-ground / below-ground biomass + soil carbon estimate for a polygon
  • Restoration plan — a planned reforestation/restoration intervention with milestones

3.3 Roles in the federation

Phase 3 defines six roles: satellite vendor (publishes deforestation events and forest condition observations), ground survey team (publishes biodiversity surveys and ground-truth observations), indigenous community custodian (publishes community land claims and signs consent envelopes for observations within community territory), national forest agency (consumes and publishes per-jurisdiction reports), REDD+ verification body (consumes for MRV verification), research aggregator (consumes de-identified data for forest-science research).

3.5 Where it sits in the WIA family

WIA Rainforest Conservation composes with WIA-OMNI-API for credentials (community custodian identity, government agency credentials, satellite vendor accounts), WIA-AIR-SHIELD for transport hardening (cross-organisation peer reputation), and WIA-ACCESSIBILITY for community-facing UI accommodations (low-bandwidth modes for remote community access, multilingual labels in indigenous languages).

Indigenous-Sovereignty First Design

Every choice in this standard is filtered through the question: does this make community-claimed territory data more useful while honouring indigenous sovereignty, or just more accessible to outside actors? When the two conflict, sovereignty wins. That is why the standard mandates explicit community consent before cross-host data flow within community territory, refuses to allow national agencies to bypass community custodians, and requires implementations to surface custodian provenance so downstream consumers can verify whose data they are working with.
