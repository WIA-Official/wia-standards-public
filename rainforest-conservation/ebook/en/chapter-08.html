Chapter 08: Conformance, Indigenous Sovereignty, Roadmap

8.1 Conformance test suite

A black-box conformance test suite is published at https://github.com/WIA-Official/wia-rainforest-conservation-conformance and walks discovery document round-trip, observation publish/read/withdraw, bbox query, deforestation event stream subscribe/reconnect, biodiversity survey Darwin Core round-trip, indigenous land claim publish with custodian verification, community consent envelope enforcement, federation handshake, rate-limit headers and exhaustion behaviour. Hosts publishing bridge_profile=Full SHOULD additionally pass the suite extension tests for at least one supported satellite imagery source (Sentinel-2 or Landsat) and the GBIF Darwin Core bridge.

8.2 Reference container

The wia/rainforest-conservation-host:1.0.0 container image implements every Phase 2 endpoint with mock data. The container ships with a SQLite-backed store suitable for community-organisation-scale deployments (up to 100 000 observations per claim) and a built-in FAO FRA 2020 vocabulary translator. Production deployments typically swap in PostGIS for spatial query optimisation.

8.3 Indigenous Sovereignty Considerations

The standard takes indigenous community consent as a Phase 3 primitive rather than an afterthought. This has practical consequences: a satellite vendor publishing a deforestation alert within a community-claimed territory MUST verify the consent envelope before serving the alert to a national agency or REDD+ verification body audience. The default is no consent; the community must opt in.

UNDRIP Article 18 (right of indigenous peoples to participate in decision-making) and Article 31 (right to maintain control over their cultural heritage) inform the standard\x27s design. The community custodian DID is the source of truth for consent; the standard does not allow national agencies to bypass the custodian.

8.5 Backwards-compatibility promise

Within the 1.x line every Phase 2 endpoint MUST remain reachable and MUST continue to honour documented status codes and content shapes. Hosts MAY add optional query parameters, response fields, new endpoints, or media types. Hosts MUST NOT remove or repurpose existing ones. Breaking changes ride a major version bump and MUST be preceded by a 12-month deprecation window per IETF RFC 8594 and RFC 9745.

8.6 Closing note

Rainforest conservation is a global commons problem with deeply local stakeholders. The dominant pattern today is per-vendor and per-NGO silos that prevent a unified picture and that frequently bypass the indigenous communities whose territories these datasets describe. WIA Rainforest Conservation exists to close both gaps — open wire format for the data, indigenous community consent as a baked-in primitive for the social contract.

Indigenous-Sovereignty First Design

Every choice in this standard is filtered through the question: does this make community-claimed territory data more useful while honouring indigenous sovereignty, or just more accessible to outside actors? When the two conflict, sovereignty wins. That is why the standard mandates explicit community consent before cross-host data flow within community territory, refuses to allow national agencies to bypass community custodians, and requires implementations to surface custodian provenance so downstream consumers can verify whose data they are working with.
