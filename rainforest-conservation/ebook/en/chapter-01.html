Chapter 01: Introduction & Why Open Standards Matter for Forests

1.1 What this book covers

The WIA Rainforest Conservation standard provides an open wire format and federation protocol for forest-condition observations: deforestation events, degradation gradients, biodiversity surveys, indigenous community land claims, carbon-stock estimates. This book is the practitioner introduction. It walks the four-phase architecture (Data Format, API Interface, Federation Protocol, Integration) with worked examples that an implementer needs to build a conformant component without reading the specification cold.

1.2 Why rainforest data needs an open standard

Forest-condition data is currently produced by a fragmented set of actors: national forest agencies (each with their own format), satellite-monitoring vendors (Planet Labs, Maxar, Sentinel Hub), academic remote-sensing labs, NGO ground surveys, and indigenous community land-mapping projects. A policy maker or auditor trying to assemble a coherent picture of a single forest landscape must reconcile half-a-dozen incompatible formats, and the indigenous communities whose territories these datasets describe rarely have a seat at the technical table.

1.3 What WIA Rainforest Conservation changes

The standard collapses the vendor surfaces into one open wire format with explicit indigenous-community-consent gating built into Phase 3. A satellite vendor publishing deforestation alerts is welcome on the same wire as a community ground-survey team — but the receiver can filter on consent scope and make decisions on appropriate evidence. The patient-consent pattern from the medical-data WIA standards is reused here as community consent.

1.4 The 4-Phase architecture

Phase 1 (Data Format) defines JSON envelopes for forest condition observations, deforestation events, biodiversity surveys, indigenous land claims, and carbon stock estimates. Phase 2 (API Interface) specifies the HTTP surface for publish, query, stream, and federation. Phase 3 (Federation Protocol) handles cross-host data flow with community consent envelopes as the gating control. Phase 4 (Integration) bridges to FAO Global Forest Resources Assessment vocabularies, REDD+ MRV (Measurement, Reporting, Verification) frameworks, GBIF biodiversity data, Sentinel-2 / Landsat satellite imagery pipelines, and the WIA family standards.

1.5 Indigenous sovereignty as a baked-in primitive

Earlier forest-data standards have treated indigenous community consent as an afterthought. The WIA Rainforest Conservation standard treats it as a Phase 3 primitive: every cross-host data flow involving an observation tied to a community-claimed territory MUST be gated on a fresh consent envelope signed by the community custodian. The default is no consent — the community must opt in.

1.6 How this book is organised

Chapters 2-3 cover landscape and standard overview. Chapters 4-7 walk the four phases with worked envelope examples. Chapter 8 closes with conformance, indigenous-sovereignty considerations, and the roadmap.

Indigenous-Sovereignty First Design

Every choice in this standard is filtered through the question: does this make community-claimed territory data more useful while honouring indigenous sovereignty, or just more accessible to outside actors? When the two conflict, sovereignty wins. That is why the standard mandates explicit community consent before cross-host data flow within community territory, refuses to allow national agencies to bypass community custodians, and requires implementations to surface custodian provenance so downstream consumers can verify whose data they are working with.
