Chapter 03: WIA Water Scarcity Response at a Glance

3.1 Standard at a glance

WIA Water Scarcity Response is a four-phase open standard for cross-jurisdiction water scarcity coordination. Phase 1 fixes JSON envelopes. Phase 2 fixes the HTTP surface. Phase 3 fixes federation and audience controls. Phase 4 bridges to WMO drought indices, USGS NWIS, EU WISE-WFD, FAO AquaStat, and the WIA family.

3.2 Object families

  • Drought index observation — SPI, SPEI, or PDSI value for a basin at a timestamp
  • Water allocation — formal water rights allocation per jurisdiction, year, purpose, priority class
  • Restriction declaration — voluntary or mandatory water-use restriction per jurisdiction
  • Emergency response — formal emergency declaration with associated actions
  • Supply augmentation — desalination, water reuse, interbasin transfer, or storage release records
  • Public consultation — record of public comment period and disposition for major allocation changes

3.3 Roles in the federation

Phase 3 defines six roles: jurisdiction agency (state/provincial/national water authority), basin coordinator (interstate compact body, international river commission), supplier (water utility, irrigation district), regulator (national environmental agency, EU Commission, etc.), research consortium (academic hydrology research), public (anonymous read for transparency dashboards).

3.5 Where it sits in the WIA family

WIA Water Scarcity Response composes with WIA-OMNI-API for credentials (jurisdiction agency identity, basin coordinator credentials), WIA-AIR-SHIELD for transport hardening (cross-organisation peer reputation), and WIA-ACCESSIBILITY for public-facing dashboard accommodations.

Watershed-First Design

Every choice in this standard is filtered through: does this make the basin\x27s aggregate water-scarcity picture more visible to the people in the basin, or just more accessible to the bureaucratic centre? When the two conflict, basin-population visibility wins. That is why the standard mandates public read for aggregate basin data, refuses to centralise the data into a single coordinator, and requires every cross-jurisdiction read to be audit-logged so basin populations can verify what their water data is doing.

Implementer Notes

Three operational concerns recur in water-scarcity infrastructure and are baked into the standard. First, jurisdiction authority verification: writes to allocation/restriction/emergency endpoints require the writer to demonstrate authority for the jurisdiction (verified via WIA-OMNI-API jurisdictional_authority claims). Second, replay defence for emergency endpoints: a duplicated emergency declaration triggers duplicate response activations, so the seen-nonce cache MUST be persistent across host restarts. Third, public dashboard rate limits: drought-index streams attract dashboard traffic during dry seasons; hosts SHOULD provision read replicas to keep ingest paths responsive.
