Chapter 02: Drought Monitoring: SPI, SPEI, PDSI

2.1 The Standardised Precipitation Index (SPI)

The Standardised Precipitation Index (SPI), developed by McKee, Doesken, and Kleist in 1993 and standardised by the World Meteorological Organization, is the most widely used drought index globally. It measures precipitation deficit relative to long-term climatology over a configurable time window: SPI-1 (one month), SPI-3 (three months), SPI-6, SPI-12, SPI-24, SPI-36, SPI-48. Short-window SPI captures meteorological drought; long-window SPI captures hydrological drought.

2.2 SPEI — accounting for evapotranspiration

The Standardised Precipitation Evapotranspiration Index (SPEI), developed by Vicente-Serrano in 2010, extends SPI by including potential evapotranspiration. This matters for warming-driven drought: a region can experience normal precipitation but still drought-stressed because rising temperatures push evapotranspiration above the water-balance threshold. SPEI is increasingly preferred for climate-change attribution studies.

2.3 PDSI — Palmer Drought Severity Index

The Palmer Drought Severity Index (PDSI), developed by Palmer in 1965, uses a soil-moisture water balance model rather than a pure precipitation deficit. It is more responsive to short-term variability and historically dominant in US National Weather Service products. The standard supports PDSI alongside SPI/SPEI; jurisdictions select the index appropriate to their operational context.

2.4 Index computation in WIA-compliant hosts

The standard does not re-implement index computation. Hosts compute SPI/SPEI/PDSI per the WMO standardised methods and emit the result as a Phase 1 drought_index_observation envelope. The envelope carries the index_kind, value, classification, computation_window, and data_source so downstream consumers can verify methodology.

2.5 Multi-index ensemble approach

Modern operational drought monitoring (e.g., US Drought Monitor) uses an ensemble of indices rather than relying on a single one. WIA-compliant hosts can publish multiple drought_index_observation envelopes for the same basin and timestamp; downstream consumers can render an ensemble dashboard.

Watershed-First Design

Every choice in this standard is filtered through: does this make the basin\x27s aggregate water-scarcity picture more visible to the people in the basin, or just more accessible to the bureaucratic centre? When the two conflict, basin-population visibility wins. That is why the standard mandates public read for aggregate basin data, refuses to centralise the data into a single coordinator, and requires every cross-jurisdiction read to be audit-logged so basin populations can verify what their water data is doing.

Implementer Notes

Three operational concerns recur in water-scarcity infrastructure and are baked into the standard. First, jurisdiction authority verification: writes to allocation/restriction/emergency endpoints require the writer to demonstrate authority for the jurisdiction (verified via WIA-OMNI-API jurisdictional_authority claims). Second, replay defence for emergency endpoints: a duplicated emergency declaration triggers duplicate response activations, so the seen-nonce cache MUST be persistent across host restarts. Third, public dashboard rate limits: drought-index streams attract dashboard traffic during dry seasons; hosts SHOULD provision read replicas to keep ingest paths responsive.
