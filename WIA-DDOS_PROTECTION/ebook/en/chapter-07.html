Phase 4: Incident Response and Recovery

1. DDoS Response Playbooks

A DDoS response playbook is a documented, tested procedure that guides incident response teams through detection, mitigation, communication, and recovery phases. Well-designed playbooks reduce mean time to recovery (MTTR) from hours to minutes by eliminating decision paralysis and ensuring consistent, practiced responses.

1.1 Playbook Components

Component Contents Owner Update Frequency
Detection Criteria Thresholds, alerts, indicators of compromise Security Operations Quarterly
Escalation Matrix Contact list, on-call rotations, severity levels Incident Management Monthly
Mitigation Steps Runbooks for rate limiting, scrubbing, WAF, CDN Network/Security Teams Bi-annually
Communication Templates Status page, customer email, internal updates, PR Communications Team Annually
Vendor Contacts ISP, DDoS mitigation, CDN, security vendors Vendor Management Quarterly
Post-Incident Checklist Forensics, analysis, remediation, report Security Engineering Per incident

📋 Sample DDoS Response Playbook (Volumetric Attack)

Phase 1: Detection (T+0 to T+60s)

  1. Alert triggered: Traffic >3σ above baseline (e.g., 100 Gbps vs. 20 Gbps normal)
  2. Automated systems verify anomaly across multiple data sources (firewall, router, CDN)
  3. Classification: Volumetric attack (UDP flood) based on protocol distribution
  4. Severity assessment: P1 (Critical) - Service degraded by 60%

Phase 2: Initial Response (T+1min to T+5min)

  1. PagerDuty escalates to on-call Security Engineer (phone call + SMS)
  2. Security Engineer opens incident war room (Zoom + Slack #incident-response)
  3. Activate rate limiting: 1000 req/s per IP (reduce from 5000 normal limit)
  4. Notify Network Team to prepare BGP Flowspec rules
  5. Status page updated: "Investigating connectivity issues"

Phase 3: Mitigation (T+5min to T+15min)

  1. Deploy BGP Flowspec: Drop UDP traffic >1 Gbps from any /24 subnet
  2. Engage scrubbing center: BGP announcement diverts traffic to Cloudflare
  3. Monitor traffic drop: 100 Gbps → 15 Gbps within 3 minutes
  4. Service restored to 95% availability within 10 minutes
  5. Status page updated: "Mitigation in progress, service recovering"

Phase 4: Recovery & Analysis (T+15min to T+24h)

  1. Attack subsides after 45 minutes, scrubbing center traffic normalized
  2. Gradual traffic restoration to direct routing over 15 minutes
  3. Forensic analysis: Identify attack sources (botnet IPs, ASNs, geos)
  4. Post-incident report drafted within 24 hours
  5. Lessons learned meeting scheduled (within 48 hours)

2. Incident Response Team Structure

2.1 DDoS Incident Response Roles

Role Responsibilities Skills Required On-Call
Incident Commander Overall coordination, decision authority, stakeholder updates Leadership, crisis management, technical knowledge 24/7 rotation
Security Engineer Attack analysis, WAF/firewall config, mitigation implementation Security tools, threat analysis, scripting 24/7 rotation
Network Engineer BGP routing, Flowspec rules, scrubbing center coordination Routing protocols, NetFlow, ISP relationships 24/7 rotation
SRE/DevOps Service health monitoring, scaling, application-level fixes Cloud platforms, observability, automation 24/7 rotation
Communications Lead Status page, customer emails, social media, PR Crisis communications, customer empathy, writing Business hours + on-call
Executive Sponsor Business decisions, vendor escalations, budget approval Executive judgment, vendor relationships As needed (P1 only)

⚠️ Common Team Structure Mistakes

  • Too Many Cooks: 10+ people in war room leads to chaos. Limit to 5-7 active responders, others observe silently.
  • Unclear Authority: Without designated Incident Commander, decisions stall. One person must have final say.
  • No Backup: If primary on-call is unavailable (out of coverage, sick), escalation fails. Always have backup on-call.
  • Vendor Dependency: Relying entirely on external DDoS vendor without internal skills creates single point of failure.
  • Burnout: Same engineers on-call 24/7 without rotation leads to fatigue errors. Rotate weekly, limit consecutive weeks.

3. Communication Strategies

3.1 Communication Timeline and Channels

Timeline Audience Channel Message Type
T+0 (Alert) Internal Response Team PagerDuty, Slack #incident Alert notification with severity, initial data
T+5min Leadership Team Email, Slack #leadership Incident declared, impact assessment, ETA
T+10min All Employees Slack #general, email Brief status, "team is working on it"
T+15min Customers Status page (statuspage.io) "Investigating connectivity issues"
T+30min Customers Status page, Twitter/X "Identified root cause, mitigation in progress"
T+1hr Customers Email (if outage >30min) Detailed explanation, apology, estimated resolution
T+24hr Customers Email, blog post Post-incident report, root cause, preventive measures
T+1week Stakeholders Executive summary Financial impact, lessons learned, roadmap updates

✅ Communication Best Practices

  • Transparency: Admit the issue honestly. "We're experiencing a DDoS attack" is better than vague "network issues"
  • Timeliness: Update every 30-60 minutes even if no progress. Silence creates customer anxiety and support ticket flood
  • Empathy: Acknowledge customer impact. "We know this is frustrating" shows you care
  • Specificity: Provide ETAs if confident, otherwise say "working around the clock" to avoid missed deadlines
  • Single Source of Truth: Status page is canonical. All other channels (Twitter, email) link back to status page
  • No Blame: Never blame vendors publicly ("our CDN failed"). Appears unprofessional, damages vendor relationships

🎓 Key Takeaways

  • DDoS response playbooks contain 6 components (detection criteria, escalation matrix, mitigation steps, communication templates, vendor contacts, post-incident checklist) updated quarterly-annually to ensure practiced, consistent responses reducing MTTR from hours to minutes
  • Incident response team requires 5-7 core roles (Incident Commander, Security Engineer, Network Engineer, SRE/DevOps, Communications Lead, Executive Sponsor) with 24/7 on-call rotation to prevent burnout while ensuring coverage and clear decision authority
  • Communication follows 8-stage timeline from T+0 (internal alert) through T+1week (stakeholder summary) across channels (PagerDuty, Slack, status page, email, social media) with transparency, timeliness (30-60min updates), and empathy prioritized over blame
  • Post-incident analysis quantifies impact metrics (MTTD 47s, MTTR 4min, downtime 45min, revenue loss $127K, users affected 85K), response effectiveness, root cause, and generates prioritized action items with owners and deadlines to prevent recurrence
  • Business continuity planning establishes RTO targets (failover data center <15min, multi-CDN <5min, DNS failover <2min, read-only mode <1min) with RPO <5min for data-critical systems ensuring service continuity during sustained attacks
