Chapter 2

Qubit Technologies and Implementations

The race to build practical quantum computers has spawned diverse qubit technologies, each exploiting different physical phenomena to create and manipulate quantum states.

The Qubit Implementation Landscape

A qubit is the fundamental unit of quantum information, but there are many ways to physically realize one. Each implementation approach offers distinct advantages in terms of coherence time, gate fidelity, connectivity, scalability, and operating conditions. The quantum computing industry has not yet converged on a single winning technology; instead, multiple approaches are being pursued in parallel by major players and startups alike.

The choice of qubit technology profoundly affects every aspect of a quantum computer's design, from the temperature at which it operates to the algorithms it can efficiently execute. Understanding these technologies is essential for evaluating quantum computing claims, selecting development platforms, and anticipating future breakthroughs.

Superconducting Qubits

Superconducting qubits are the most mature and widely deployed quantum computing technology, used by IBM, Google, and several other major players. These qubits exploit the quantum mechanical behavior of superconducting circuits cooled to temperatures near absolute zero (typically 10-20 millikelvin, colder than outer space).

How Superconducting Qubits Work

At their core, superconducting qubits are artificial atoms created from superconducting materials (typically aluminum or niobium). The key component is the Josephson junction—a thin insulating barrier between two superconductors that exhibits quantum tunneling effects. When cooled below the superconducting critical temperature, electrical current can flow through the circuit without resistance, and the circuit exhibits quantized energy levels.

The two lowest energy levels of this circuit serve as the |0⟩ and |1⟩ states of the qubit. These states can be manipulated using precisely calibrated microwave pulses, typically in the 4-8 GHz frequency range. The transmon qubit, developed at Yale University, is the most common variant, designed to be insensitive to charge noise while maintaining sufficient anharmonicity to distinguish the qubit states from higher energy levels.

IBM Quantum Systems

IBM has been a leader in superconducting qubit development, with a clear roadmap extending to 2033. Their 2025 Nighthawk processor features 120 qubits connected by 218 next-generation tunable couplers, enabling circuits with 30% more complexity than previous generations. IBM's Quantum Loon experimental processor demonstrated all key components needed for fault-tolerant quantum computing, including real-time error decoding in less than 480 nanoseconds.

Maturity: Excellent Gate Fidelity: 99.5%+ Coherence: ~100μs

Google Quantum AI

Google's Willow chip represents a breakthrough in quantum error correction, featuring 105 superconducting qubits that achieved "below threshold" performance—meaning errors decrease as more qubits are added. The chip completed a benchmark computation in 5 minutes that would take classical supercomputers 10 septillion years. Google's Quantum Echoes algorithm demonstrated 13,000x speedup over classical methods for specific computational tasks.

Error Correction: Below Threshold Speedup: 13,000x Qubits: 105

Advantages and Challenges

Superconducting Qubit Advantages:
  • Fast gate operations (10-100 nanoseconds)
  • Mature fabrication using semiconductor industry techniques
  • Individual qubit addressability and readout
  • Demonstrated error correction below threshold
  • Strong industry investment and ecosystem
Challenges:
  • Requires extreme cooling (15 millikelvin), expensive cryogenic infrastructure
  • Limited coherence times (microseconds to milliseconds)
  • Nearest-neighbor connectivity limits algorithm efficiency
  • Each qubit requires dedicated control lines, creating wiring complexity
  • Susceptible to electromagnetic interference and cosmic rays

Trapped Ion Qubits

Trapped ion quantum computers use individual atoms suspended in electromagnetic fields as qubits. The internal energy states of these ions serve as the |0⟩ and |1⟩ states, manipulated using precisely controlled laser beams. This approach leverages decades of atomic physics research and offers exceptional coherence times and gate fidelities.

How Trapped Ion Qubits Work

Ions (typically ytterbium, calcium, or barium) are confined in a vacuum chamber using oscillating electromagnetic fields in a structure called a Paul trap or linear ion trap. The ions are cooled to near absolute zero using laser cooling techniques, where carefully tuned lasers slow the ions' motion by absorbing and re-emitting photons. Once cooled, the ions form a crystal-like structure, with each ion serving as a qubit.

Quantum gates are implemented using laser pulses that couple the internal states of the ions. Single-qubit gates rotate individual ions' states, while two-qubit entangling gates use the shared motional modes of the ion chain to create correlations between ions. Because all ions in a trap interact through these shared motional modes, trapped ion systems offer all-to-all connectivity—any qubit can directly interact with any other qubit.

IonQ

IonQ has developed some of the highest-fidelity quantum gates in the industry, with two-qubit gate fidelities exceeding 99.9%. Their systems use ytterbium ions and have demonstrated 32-qubit systems with full connectivity. IonQ has focused on algorithmic performance rather than raw qubit count, achieving strong results on real-world applications.

Gate Fidelity: 99.9%+ Connectivity: All-to-All Coherence: Seconds

Quantinuum

Formed from the merger of Honeywell Quantum Solutions and Cambridge Quantum, Quantinuum has developed the H-Series trapped ion quantum computers. Their System Model H2 features 32 qubits with 99.8% two-qubit gate fidelity. Quantinuum has pioneered techniques for moving ions between different zones of the trap, enabling more flexible quantum circuit execution.

Gate Fidelity: 99.8% Connectivity: Reconfigurable Qubits: 32

Advantages and Challenges

Trapped Ion Advantages:
  • Longest coherence times (seconds to minutes)
  • Highest gate fidelities (>99.9%)
  • All-to-all connectivity eliminates SWAP operations
  • Identical qubits (natural atoms are perfectly reproducible)
  • Room for improvement in gate speed through new techniques
Challenges:
  • Slower gate operations (microseconds vs nanoseconds)
  • Scaling beyond ~100 ions in single trap is challenging
  • Complex optical systems for laser control
  • Vacuum system requirements add complexity
  • Photon interconnects needed for modular scaling

Photonic Quantum Computing

Photonic quantum computers use particles of light (photons) as qubits. This approach offers unique advantages: photons naturally travel at the speed of light, don't interact with each other (reducing errors), and can operate at room temperature. However, creating deterministic two-photon interactions is challenging, leading to different computational models.

How Photonic Qubits Work

Photonic qubits can be encoded in various properties of light: polarization (horizontal vs vertical), path (which waveguide the photon travels), time-bin (early vs late arrival), or more exotic degrees of freedom. Single-photon sources generate individual photons, which are then manipulated using optical components like beam splitters, phase shifters, and waveguides.

Because photons don't naturally interact, photonic quantum computers often use measurement-based approaches. In one paradigm, called linear optical quantum computing (LOQC), entanglement is created probabilistically when photons meet at beam splitters, with successful operations heralded by detector clicks. Another approach, Gaussian boson sampling, uses squeezed light states and doesn't require the difficult step of generating single photons.

Xanadu

Xanadu's Borealis system uses photonic qubits for Gaussian boson sampling, demonstrating quantum advantage on problems related to graph theory and molecular simulation. Their approach uses squeezed light rather than single photons, making it more scalable with current technology. Xanadu also develops the PennyLane software framework for quantum machine learning.

Room Temperature Scalability: Promising Universal: Limited

PsiQuantum

PsiQuantum is taking a different approach, aiming to build million-qubit fault-tolerant quantum computers using photonics. Rather than demonstrating small-scale devices, they're focused on developing manufacturing processes in partnership with semiconductor foundries. Their approach requires advances in single-photon sources, detectors, and integrated photonics.

Scale Target: 1M+ Qubits Timeline: Long-term Manufacturing: Foundry-Based

Neutral Atom Qubits

Neutral atom quantum computers use individual atoms (not ions) trapped in optical tweezers—highly focused laser beams that hold atoms in place. This approach has gained momentum recently due to its potential for scaling to thousands of qubits with reconfigurable geometry.

How Neutral Atom Qubits Work

Arrays of optical tweezers can trap hundreds to thousands of individual atoms in precisely controlled positions. Unlike ions, neutral atoms don't repel each other, allowing them to be packed more densely. The qubit states are encoded in the hyperfine ground states of the atoms, which have extremely long coherence times.

Two-qubit gates are implemented by exciting atoms to Rydberg states—highly excited electronic states with exaggerated properties. When an atom is in a Rydberg state, it strongly interacts with nearby atoms through dipole-dipole interactions, enabling entangling operations. The atoms can be rearranged during computation, allowing flexible connectivity.

QuEra Computing

QuEra has demonstrated a 256-qubit neutral atom system, one of the largest qubit arrays in the industry. Their approach uses rubidium atoms and has shown promising results for optimization problems and quantum simulation. The ability to dynamically reconfigure atom positions during computation offers unique algorithmic advantages.

Qubit Count: 256 Reconfigurable Coherence: Good

Topological Qubits

Topological quantum computing represents the most exotic approach, aiming to encode qubits in the global properties of quantum systems rather than local properties of individual particles. If successful, topological qubits would be inherently protected from local errors, potentially requiring far less error correction overhead.

Microsoft's Majorana 1 Chip

In February 2025, Microsoft unveiled the Majorana 1 chip, which contains a special material capable of creating a new state of matter: topological superconductivity. This state hosts Majorana zero modes—exotic quasiparticles that are their own antiparticles. Information encoded in pairs of Majorana modes is protected by topology, meaning it cannot be disturbed by local perturbations.

Microsoft's approach represents a long-term bet on fundamentally more stable qubits. While still in early stages, the Majorana 1 chip demonstrates the feasibility of creating and controlling these exotic quantum states. If scaled successfully, topological qubits could provide a faster path to fault-tolerant quantum computing by reducing the physical-to-logical qubit overhead.

Topological Qubit Promise

  • Inherent error protection through topology
  • Potentially orders of magnitude less error correction overhead
  • Based on Nobel Prize-winning physics (2016)
  • Still in early experimental stages
  • Requires advances in materials science and nanofabrication

Summary

Key Takeaways:

  • Multiple Approaches: No single qubit technology has proven dominant; each offers distinct advantages
  • Superconducting Leadership: IBM, Google lead with mature technology and demonstrated error correction
  • Trapped Ion Precision: IonQ, Quantinuum achieve highest fidelities and longest coherence times
  • Photonic Promise: Room temperature operation and telecommunications compatibility offer unique scaling paths
  • Neutral Atom Scale: QuEra and others demonstrate hundreds of qubits with reconfigurable geometry
  • Topological Future: Microsoft's Majorana 1 represents bet on fundamentally error-protected qubits
