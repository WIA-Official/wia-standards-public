Chapter 2: RFID Technology for Animal Identification

2.1 RFID Fundamentals

Radio Frequency Identification (RFID) is the cornerstone technology for modern livestock tracking. Unlike barcodes that require line-of-sight scanning, RFID tags can be read automatically as animals pass through gates, enabling hands-free identification.

2.2 ISO 11784/11785 Standard

The international standard for livestock RFID, ensuring global interoperability.

2.2.1 Code Structure (ISO 11784)

┌──────────────┬────────────────┬─────────────────┐
│ Country Code │ Manufacturer   │ Animal ID       │
│   3 digits   │    3 digits    │   9 digits      │
└──────────────┴────────────────┴─────────────────┘
Example: 982-000-123456789
  982 = Korea
  000 = Manufacturer/Reserved
  123456789 = Unique animal number

2.3 Types of RFID Tags

2.3.1 Visual Ear Tags with RFID

  • Design: Plastic ear tag with embedded RFID chip + printed number
  • Application: Ear tag pliers, quick and easy
  • Cost: $1.50-$5 per tag
  • Lifespan: 10-15 years if properly applied
  • Pros: Visual backup, low cost, easy to apply
  • Cons: Can be torn off, requires proper application

2.3.2 Injectable Microchips

  • Design: Rice-grain sized glass capsule with RFID chip
  • Application: Injected subcutaneously (under skin) with syringe
  • Cost: $15-$30 per chip + injection
  • Lifespan: Lifetime of animal
  • Pros: Permanent, cannot be removed or lost
  • Cons: Higher cost, requires trained personnel, cannot see visually

2.3.3 Bolus Tags (Rumen Implants)

  • Design: Ceramic or metal capsule swallowed by animal
  • Application: Administered orally, stays in rumen permanently
  • Cost: $8-$15 per bolus
  • Lifespan: Lifetime of animal
  • Pros: Cannot be lost or removed, no external tag
  • Cons: More expensive, requires bolus gun, harder to confirm presence

2.5 Practical Implementation

2.5.1 Tagging Best Practices

  • Age: Tag calves at birth or within first week for disease traceability
  • Location: Right ear (most countries) or left ear (some regions) for consistency
  • Placement: Middle third of ear, avoid blood vessels and cartilage ridge
  • Hygiene: Clean tag applicator between animals to prevent infection
  • Verification: Scan tag immediately after application to confirm readability
  • Backup: Consider dual tagging (two ear tags) or ear tag + microchip for valuable animals

2.5.2 Common Problems and Solutions

ProblemCauseSolution

Infection at tag siteDirty applicator, poor placementAntibiotic spray, monitor for abscesses

2.6 Advanced RFID: UHF vs LF

2.6.1 Low Frequency (LF) - ISO 11784/11785

  • Frequency: 134.2 kHz
  • Read Range: 0-30 cm
  • Advantages: Penetrates tissue/water well, works in metal environments, global standard
  • Disadvantages: Short range, slow data rate, requires close proximity
  • Best For: Individual animal ID, close-range reading, veterinary use

2.6.2 Ultra High Frequency (UHF) - EPC Gen2

  • Frequency: 860-960 MHz (varies by country)
  • Read Range: 1-10 meters
  • Advantages: Long range, fast reading (hundreds of tags/second), smaller tags
  • Disadvantages: Absorbed by water (animals are 60% water), interference from metal
  • Best For: Warehouse inventory, pallet tracking, supply chain (less common for live animals)

Summary

RFID technology, standardized by ISO 11784/11785, provides reliable electronic identification for livestock. Low-frequency (134.2 kHz) RFID tags—whether ear tags, microchips, or bolus implants—offer permanent animal IDs that work globally. Proper tagging technique, quality readers, and integration with farm management software ensure accurate, automated record-keeping.
