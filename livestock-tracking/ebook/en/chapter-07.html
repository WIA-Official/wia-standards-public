7.2 Regional Regulations

7.2.1 European Union

  • Regulation (EC) No 1760/2000: Mandatory cattle identification and registration
  • Requirements: Individual ear tags, cattle passport, database registration within 7 days of birth
  • TRACES: EU-wide animal movement and traceability system
  • Penalty: Loss of CAP subsidies for non-compliance

7.2.2 United States

  • Animal Disease Traceability (ADT): USDA rule effective 2013
  • Requirements: Official ID for cattle crossing state lines
  • Methods: RFID tags (recommended), metal tags, tattoos, brands
  • Voluntary: National Animal Identification System (NAIS) pilot ended, but state-level programs vary

7.2.3 South Korea

  • 축산물이력제 (Livestock Traceability System): Mandatory since 2008
  • Coverage: All cattle (beef and dairy)
  • Authority: 축산물품질평가원 (Korea Institute for Animal Products Quality Evaluation - KAPE)
  • Public Access: Consumers can check cattle history online via 12-digit traceability number
  • Penalty: Fines up to ₩10 million for violations

7.2.4 Australia

  • NLIS (National Livestock Identification System): World's most comprehensive system
  • Requirements: RFID tags for all cattle, sheep, goats (mandatory since 2005)
  • Coverage: 28 million cattle, 70 million sheep
  • Success: Enabled rapid BSE/FMD response, maintains export access to 100+ countries

7.4 Export Certification

For meat exports, countries require verified disease-free status:

  • EU Requirements: TRACES system export health certificate, traceability from birth
  • Japan/Korea: Strict FMD/BSE certification, feed records (no animal protein), age verification
  • Middle East (Halal): Slaughter certification, traceability to ensure compliance
  • USA (Beef): FSIS export certificates, hormone-free verification for EU

7.6 Data Protection & Farmer Privacy

As tracking systems collect detailed farm data, privacy regulations apply:

  • GDPR (EU): Farmers have right to access, correct, delete their data
  • Data Ownership: Farmers own livestock data, not software vendors
  • Third-Party Sharing: Requires explicit consent (e.g., sharing data with meat buyers)
  • Government Access: Limited to regulatory purposes (disease control, food safety)

Summary

Livestock tracking is increasingly mandated worldwide, from the EU's 1998 cattle passport system to Korea's 2008 traceability law. Compliance ensures market access, prevents disease outbreaks, and builds consumer trust—making regulatory adherence a business necessity, not just a legal obligation.
