Phase 4 — Post-Quantum Readiness and Operational Hardening

Phase 4 looks forward to the post-quantum transition and inward to the operational disciplines that keep a confidential computing fleet honest in production.

5.1 Post-quantum signature suites

The current Phase 1 signature suite is Ed25519. The post-quantum migration plan tracks NIST PQC selections: ML-DSA (FIPS 204) is the primary candidate for envelope signatures, with hybrid schemes (Ed25519 + ML-DSA in parallel) during the transition window. Phase 4 specifies the envelope extension that lets a single envelope carry parallel signatures so consumers can verify either suite during the migration.

5.2 Formal verification of TEE firmware

Several TEE families have benefited from formal verification of their firmware: seL4 for ARM TrustZone, formal models of SGX's enclave entry / exit, machine-checked proofs for the Intel TDX module's cryptographic operations. The standard does not mandate formal verification but specifies a metadata field in the enclave identity envelope that names the verification artefact when one exists, so a tenant policy engine can require verified firmware for its highest-trust workloads.

5.3 Hardware roadmap

The next decade brings several inflection points: SGX2 / TDX ubiquity, ARM CCA fully deployed across server and edge silicon, RISC-V Keystone reaching production, and dedicated confidential-AI accelerators. The standard's envelope schema is designed to absorb new TEE families without breaking existing verifiers — the tee_family token is extensible and the native evidence is opaque to the envelope.

5.4 Performance optimisation

The dominant performance costs in TEE workloads are EPC paging (SGX1), VM-launch attestation latency (SEV-SNP, TDX), and the syscall-shim overhead for I/O-heavy workloads. The standard's operational guide lists vendor-published mitigation patterns: EPC pre-fault, attestation-result caching with explicit refresh, and zero-copy enclave I/O via ring buffers in shared memory with integrity tags.

5.5 Security hardening checklist

  • Side-channel mitigations enabled on every confidential node (LFENCE on Intel, equivalent on AMD/ARM); enforced via attestation policy.
  • Microcode and firmware refresh on a documented cadence; emit warning envelopes when nodes fall behind.
  • HSM-backed key wrapping for every tenant; HSM identity appears in the enclave identity envelope.
  • Constant-time signature verification mandatory on verifiers; non-constant-time implementations refused at conformance.
  • Audit log replicated across at least two storage backends; append-only with cryptographic chain.

Implementer notes

Three operational concerns recur across TEE implementations and are baked into the standard. First, attestation collateral freshness — verifiers MUST refresh ahead of expiry and emit warning envelopes when seven days from expiry, because operations teams routinely miss month-out collateral expiry warnings buried in cloud-provider dashboards. Second, encrypted-memory budgeting — EPC and TDX private memory are finite and the runtime MUST refuse over-budget allocations rather than silently swap to host memory, which would defeat the confidentiality guarantee that motivates the entire deployment. Third, side-channel mitigation reporting — the runtime MUST report applied mitigations as part of the launch attestation so a downstream verifier can refuse pods running on unmitigated hardware, even if the operations team forgot to patch.

Worked example

Consider a regulated bank running confidential workloads across two clouds. The bank requires per-tenant nodes (no co-residency with other tenants), HSM-backed key wrapping, and federation of verifiers across both clouds plus an independent third-party verifier. Without this standard, the bank reimplements attestation glue per cloud, maintains three separate verifier libraries, and accepts that a workload migration between clouds is a multi-week project. With this standard, the bank publishes one capacity envelope per cloud, registers three verifiers in the federation envelope, and migration becomes a single signed event with audit-grade evidence on both sides.
