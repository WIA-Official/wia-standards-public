Incident Response and Forensics

Even hardened TEE deployments experience incidents: collateral expiry, side-channel disclosures, key compromise, multi-tenant isolation breach. This chapter walks the three incident classes most likely to surface and the forensic discipline the standard requires for each.

7.1 Class I: Collateral expiry

The most common incident class. A verifier's trust anchor collateral expires; live attestations start failing; workloads fail to launch or fail mid-operation. The standard's mitigation is the warning-envelope cadence (≤ 7 days from expiry); a verifier that emits warnings is operating correctly even when the operator misses the warning.

Forensic discipline: every verifier MUST persist the warning envelopes in the audit log so a post-incident review can establish whether the operator received timely warnings. "We didn't know" is not a defensible explanation if the audit log shows seven days of warnings.

7.2 Class II: Side-channel disclosure

A new side-channel vulnerability is published; the vendor issues microcode and firmware updates; some fraction of the fleet is unpatched. Conformant runtimes refuse to schedule new confidential workloads on unpatched nodes (the attestation policy fails the SVN check) and emit lifecycle envelopes for every refusal.

Forensic discipline: the policy version applied at every attestation is in the audit log. A post-incident review can determine which workloads ran on which nodes during the unpatched window and apply the appropriate remediation (rotate secrets, re-attest, re-seal).

7.3 Class III: Multi-tenant isolation breach

The most serious class. Either a side-channel disclosure or a hypervisor escape lets one tenant's workload observe another tenant's data. The standard's mitigation is the node-isolation declaration (Phase 2 §3.4): a tenant policy that requires per-tenant nodes refuses mixed-tenant nodes, limiting blast radius.

Forensic discipline: the multi-tenancy mode of every node is in the audit log. A post-incident review can enumerate exactly which tenants co-resided on which nodes during the incident window. Affected tenants receive a forensic packet containing the relevant audit entries; unaffected tenants receive an attestation that they were not co-resident.

7.4 Forensic envelope schema

Phase 4 §5.5 specifies an incident envelope schema: incident class, affected enclave identities, time window, remediation steps, signed by the operator's incident response team. The envelope is published to affected tenants under TLP:AMBER unless tenant policy requires a higher restriction. Non-affected tenants receive a signed attestation of non-impact — a small but reassuring piece of evidence that prevents speculative loss of trust.

Implementer notes

Three operational concerns recur across TEE implementations and are baked into the standard. First, attestation collateral freshness — verifiers MUST refresh ahead of expiry and emit warning envelopes when seven days from expiry, because operations teams routinely miss month-out collateral expiry warnings buried in cloud-provider dashboards. Second, encrypted-memory budgeting — EPC and TDX private memory are finite and the runtime MUST refuse over-budget allocations rather than silently swap to host memory, which would defeat the confidentiality guarantee that motivates the entire deployment. Third, side-channel mitigation reporting — the runtime MUST report applied mitigations as part of the launch attestation so a downstream verifier can refuse pods running on unmitigated hardware, even if the operations team forgot to patch.

Worked example

Consider a regulated bank running confidential workloads across two clouds. The bank requires per-tenant nodes (no co-residency with other tenants), HSM-backed key wrapping, and federation of verifiers across both clouds plus an independent third-party verifier. Without this standard, the bank reimplements attestation glue per cloud, maintains three separate verifier libraries, and accepts that a workload migration between clouds is a multi-week project. With this standard, the bank publishes one capacity envelope per cloud, registers three verifiers in the federation envelope, and migration becomes a single signed event with audit-grade evidence on both sides.
