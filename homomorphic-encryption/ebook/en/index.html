CHAPTER 1

Introduction to Homomorphic Encryption

In our increasingly digital world, data privacy has become one of the most critical challenges of the 21st century. Every day, billions of people entrust their most sensitive information—medical records, financial data, personal communications—to cloud services and online platforms. Yet this convenience comes with a troubling trade-off: to process our data, these services must first decrypt it, exposing it to potential breaches, insider threats, and unauthorized access.

What if there was a way to compute on data without ever seeing it? What if hospitals could collaborate on patient data analysis without exposing individual records? What if you could query an encrypted database and get encrypted results, maintaining privacy throughout the entire process?

This is the promise of homomorphic encryption.

What is Homomorphic Encryption?

Homomorphic encryption is a form of encryption that allows computations to be performed on encrypted data without decrypting it first. The results of these computations remain encrypted, and when eventually decrypted, match the results of operations performed on the original plaintext.

Enc(a) ⊕ Enc(b) = Enc(a + b)

This mathematical property, called homomorphism, enables unprecedented privacy guarantees. Unlike traditional encryption, which requires decryption before any processing, homomorphic encryption maintains data confidentiality throughout the entire computational pipeline.

Real-World Analogy

Imagine a locksmith who can modify a locked box's contents without ever opening it. You hand them a locked box containing the number 5, they perform some operation (say, multiply by 2), and hand you back a locked box. When you unlock it, you find the number 10 inside. The locksmith never saw the number 5, yet successfully performed the computation. This is homomorphic encryption.

Historical Development

The concept of homomorphic encryption is not new. Certain encryption schemes have exhibited homomorphic properties for decades:

  • 1978: RSA encryption demonstrated multiplicative homomorphism
  • 1999: Paillier cryptosystem introduced additive homomorphism
  • 2009: Craig Gentry's breakthrough—the first fully homomorphic encryption scheme
  • 2011-2013: Practical improvements (BGV, BFV schemes)
  • 2017: CKKS scheme for approximate arithmetic
  • 2020s: Production deployments and standardization efforts

Gentry's 2009 achievement, based on ideal lattices, was revolutionary. For the first time, both addition and multiplication could be performed on encrypted data, enabling arbitrary computation—true fully homomorphic encryption (FHE). This solved a 30-year-old cryptographic challenge and opened entirely new possibilities for privacy-preserving computation.

Types of Homomorphic Encryption

Partially Homomorphic Encryption (PHE)

Supports only one type of operation (either addition or multiplication) unlimited times:

  • RSA: Multiplicative homomorphism
  • Paillier: Additive homomorphism
  • ElGamal: Multiplicative homomorphism

Somewhat Homomorphic Encryption (SHE)

Supports both addition and multiplication, but only for a limited number of operations before noise accumulation makes decryption impossible.

Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE)

Supports unlimited addition and multiplication operations through a process called bootstrapping, which refreshes ciphertexts to reduce noise. Modern FHE schemes include:

  • BFV: Optimized for exact integer arithmetic
  • CKKS: Optimized for approximate real/complex number arithmetic
  • TFHE: Optimized for fast bootstrapping on binary operations

Why Homomorphic Encryption Matters

The implications of practical homomorphic encryption are profound:

Healthcare

Hospitals can collaborate on medical research using patient data without ever exposing individual records. Genomic analysis can be performed on encrypted DNA sequences, protecting the most sensitive personal information.

Finance

Banks can detect fraud patterns across institutions without sharing customer data. Investment firms can analyze encrypted portfolio data without revealing holdings to cloud providers.

Machine Learning

Models can be trained on encrypted data, enabling privacy-preserving AI. Users can query ML models without revealing their queries, and get encrypted predictions back.

Cloud Computing

Sensitive computations can be outsourced to untrusted cloud providers who never see the data they're processing. This enables new business models and regulatory compliance.

CHAPTER 2

Fundamentals of Homomorphic Encryption

Mathematical Foundations

To understand homomorphic encryption, we must first understand its mathematical underpinnings. Modern HE schemes are built on lattice-based cryptography, specifically the Ring Learning With Errors (RLWE) problem.

Rings and Polynomials

The foundation of RLWE-based encryption is the polynomial ring:

R = ℤ[x] / (xn + 1)

This represents polynomials with integer coefficients, where xn = -1. For example, with n=4:

a(x) = 5 + 3x + 7x² + 2x³
b(x) = 1 + 4x + 6x² + 8x³

a(x) + b(x) = 6 + 7x + 13x² + 10x³
a(x) × b(x) = ... (computed modulo x⁴ + 1)

Modular Arithmetic

Operations are performed modulo q (the coefficient modulus):

Rq = R / qR

This bounds coefficient sizes and provides the security foundation. Typical values of q are very large primes or products of primes.

The RLWE Problem

Security of modern HE schemes relies on the hardness of RLWE:

Given: Pairs (a, b) where a is random and b = a·s + e

Find: The secret polynomial s

Here, e is small "error" sampled from a discrete Gaussian distribution. This error makes the problem hard—even for quantum computers. The problem is believed to be as hard as worst-case lattice problems, providing strong security guarantees.

Encryption and Decryption

Key Generation

To generate a key pair:

1. Sample secret key: s ← R_q (small coefficients)
2. Sample error: e ← χ (discrete Gaussian)
3. Sample random: a ← R_q (uniform)
4. Compute: b = -a·s - e (mod q)
5. Public key: pk = (b, a)
6. Secret key: sk = s

Encryption

To encrypt a message m:

1. Encode m as polynomial in R_t
2. Sample small error: e₀, e₁ ← χ
3. Sample small random: r ← χ
4. Compute:
   c₀ = b·r + e₀ + Δ·m (mod q)
   c₁ = a·r + e₁ (mod q)
5. Ciphertext: ct = (c₀, c₁)

Where Δ = ⌊q/t⌋ is the scaling factor between ciphertext and plaintext moduli.

Decryption

To decrypt a ciphertext (c₀, c₁):

1. Compute: noisy = c₀ + c₁·s (mod q)
2. Scale down: scaled = ⌊(t/q)·noisy⌉ (mod t)
3. Decode: m = decode(scaled)

The magic: The noise terms cancel during decryption, recovering the message!

Homomorphic Operations

Addition

Adding two ciphertexts is component-wise addition:

ct₁ = (c₀⁽¹⁾, c₁⁽¹⁾) encrypts m₁
ct₂ = (c₀⁽²⁾, c₁⁽²⁾) encrypts m₂

ct_sum = (c₀⁽¹⁾ + c₀⁽²⁾, c₁⁽¹⁾ + c₁⁽²⁾)
ct_sum encrypts m₁ + m₂

Noise growth is minimal—just the sum of individual noise terms.

Multiplication

Multiplication is more complex:

ct₁ = (c₀⁽¹⁾, c₁⁽¹⁾) encrypts m₁
ct₂ = (c₀⁽²⁾, c₁⁽²⁾) encrypts m₂

After multiplication:
ct_mult has 3 components: (d₀, d₁, d₂)

This requires relinearization to reduce back to 2 components.

Noise growth is significant—approximately the product of the two noise levels. This limits multiplication depth.

Noise Management

Noise Budget

Every ciphertext has a "noise budget"—the amount of noise it can tolerate before decryption fails:

Operation Noise Growth
Fresh Encryption σ√n bits
Addition +1 bit
Multiplication ×2 (doubles)
Relinearization +few bits

A fresh ciphertext might have 100 bits of noise budget. After 6-7 multiplications, this budget is exhausted.

Modulus Switching

Technique to reduce noise by scaling down the modulus:

ct_q → ct_{q/2}
Reduces noise by ~1 bit while maintaining correctness

Bootstrapping

The most powerful technique—homomorphically evaluate the decryption circuit to refresh the ciphertext:

ct_noisy → Bootstrap(ct_noisy) → ct_fresh

Same plaintext, fresh noise budget!

Bootstrapping is expensive (100-1000x slower than regular operations) but enables unlimited computation depth.

Parameter Selection

Choosing parameters involves balancing security, performance, and functionality:

Parameter Effect Typical Values
n (poly degree) Security, slots, speed 4096, 8192, 16384
q (coeff modulus) Noise budget, security 109-881 bits
t (plain modulus) Message space 1024, 2048, 4096
σ (std dev) Error size, security 3.2

The security level is estimated using the Lattice Estimator, which considers all known attacks on RLWE.

Practical Example

For 128-bit security with moderate depth:

  • n = 8192 (polynomial degree)
  • q ≈ 218 bits (coefficient modulus chain)
  • t = 1024 (plaintext modulus)
  • Supports ~5 multiplications
  • 4096 batching slots
