CHAPTER 1

Introduction to Homomorphic Encryption

The Privacy Paradox of Modern Computing

In the digital age, we face a fundamental paradox: to use cloud services, we must trust them with our most sensitive data. Every time you upload a photo, send an email, or store a document in the cloud, you're implicitly trusting that the service provider won't misuse your information. Yet data breaches, insider threats, and government surveillance remind us that this trust is often misplaced.

Consider the healthcare industry. Hospitals possess vast amounts of patient data that could revolutionize medical research and enable life-saving discoveries. Yet privacy regulations and ethical concerns prevent sharing this data, even in anonymized form. Or consider financial institutions that could collaborate to detect fraud patterns, but cannot share customer information without violating privacy laws.

Traditional encryption provides security at rest and in transit, but requires decryption for processing. This creates a vulnerability window where data is exposed. For decades, cryptographers dreamed of a solution: encryption that permits computation without decryption.

What is Homomorphic Encryption?

Homomorphic encryption (HE) is a revolutionary cryptographic technique that enables computations on encrypted data without requiring decryption. The results remain encrypted and, when decrypted, match the results of operations performed on the original plaintext.

Enc(a) ⊕ Enc(b) = Enc(a + b)

This mathematical property, called homomorphism, transforms how we think about data security. Instead of choosing between security and functionality, homomorphic encryption provides both simultaneously.

The Lockbox Analogy

Imagine a magical lockbox where you can insert mathematical operations through a special slot. You place the number 5 inside, lock it, and hand it to someone. They insert an instruction "multiply by 3" through the slot, never seeing the number inside. When you unlock the box, you find 15. The computation happened without the box ever being opened. This is homomorphic encryption.

Historical Journey to Modern HE

The quest for homomorphic encryption spans over four decades:

Early Discoveries (1978-2008)

The first homomorphic properties were discovered accidentally. In 1978, RSA encryption was found to have multiplicative homomorphic properties:

Enc(m₁) × Enc(m₂) = Enc(m₁ × m₂)

This meant encrypted values could be multiplied without decryption. Similarly, Paillier's cryptosystem (1999) provided additive homomorphism:

Enc(m₁) × Enc(m₂) = Enc(m₁ + m₂)

These partial homomorphic encryption (PHE) schemes were useful but limited—they supported only one operation type (either addition or multiplication, but not both).

The Breakthrough (2009)

Craig Gentry's 2009 PhD thesis changed everything. He constructed the first fully homomorphic encryption (FHE) scheme based on ideal lattices. For the first time, both addition and multiplication could be performed on encrypted data unlimited times, enabling arbitrary computation.

The key innovation was bootstrapping—a technique to "refresh" ciphertexts by homomorphically evaluating the decryption circuit. This prevented noise accumulation that previously limited computation depth.

The Bootstrapping Revolution

Bootstrapping works by taking a "noisy" ciphertext (one that's accumulated too much noise from operations) and homomorphically decrypting it using an encrypted version of the secret key. The result is a fresh ciphertext of the same plaintext with reduced noise, ready for more operations.

Practical Evolution (2011-Present)

Gentry's original scheme was theoretical—far too slow for practical use. The following years brought dramatic improvements:

Year Development Impact
2011-2012 BGV and BFV schemes 100-1000x faster than Gentry's original
2013 NTRU-based FHE Simpler structure, better performance
2016 TFHE (Fast bootstrapping) Bootstrapping in ~0.1 seconds
2017 CKKS scheme Enabled approximate arithmetic for ML
2020+ Production deployments Real-world applications in healthcare, finance

Types of Homomorphic Encryption

Partially Homomorphic Encryption (PHE)

Supports unlimited operations of one type:

  • RSA: Unlimited multiplications, used in blind signatures
  • Paillier: Unlimited additions, ideal for encrypted voting and financial applications
  • ElGamal: Multiplicative homomorphism, used in mix networks

PHE schemes are fast and efficient but limited in the complexity of computations they support.

Somewhat Homomorphic Encryption (SHE)

Supports both addition and multiplication, but only for a limited number of operations before noise accumulation prevents decryption. SHE is a stepping stone to FHE—every FHE scheme without bootstrapping is essentially SHE.

Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE)

Supports unlimited addition and multiplication through bootstrapping. Modern FHE schemes include:

  • BFV (Brakerski-Fan-Vercauteren): Optimized for exact integer arithmetic, ideal for database queries and secure computation
  • BGV (Brakerski-Gentry-Vaikuntanathan): Similar to BFV, with different noise management approach
  • CKKS (Cheon-Kim-Kim-Song): Supports approximate arithmetic on real/complex numbers, perfect for machine learning
  • TFHE (Torus FHE): Fast bootstrapping on binary operations, excellent for encrypted logic circuits

How Homomorphic Encryption Works: A Simplified View

The Noise-Based Approach

Modern HE schemes rely on a clever idea: adding controlled noise to encrypted data. This noise makes decryption impossible without the secret key, providing security. However, operations on ciphertexts increase the noise:

Noise Growth Characteristics

  • Addition: Noise grows additively (slowly)
  • Multiplication: Noise grows multiplicatively (quickly)
  • Bootstrapping: Resets noise to initial level (expensive but necessary)

The Computation Pipeline

1. Encryption
   Plaintext → [Add controlled noise] → Ciphertext
   Example: 42 → Enc(42) [with noise level: 10]

2. Homomorphic Operations
   Enc(42) + Enc(17) → Enc(59) [noise level: 20]
   Enc(42) × Enc(2) → Enc(84) [noise level: 100]

3. Noise Management
   If noise too high → Bootstrap → Fresh ciphertext

4. Decryption
   Ciphertext → [Remove noise using secret key] → Plaintext
   Enc(59) → 59

Key Applications and Use Cases

Healthcare: Privacy-Preserving Medical Analysis

Hospitals can collaborate on patient data analysis without exposing individual records. Researchers can perform statistical analysis, train diagnostic AI models, and conduct genomic studies on encrypted data.

Real Example: Genomic Privacy

A patient's DNA sequence contains the most sensitive personal information imaginable. Using HE, genetic testing companies can analyze encrypted DNA for disease markers, ancestry, and other traits without ever seeing the actual sequence. The patient receives encrypted results that only they can decrypt.

Finance: Secure Multi-Party Computation

Banks can detect fraud patterns across institutions without sharing customer data. Investment firms can perform risk analysis on encrypted portfolios. Credit bureaus can compute credit scores without seeing individual financial details.

Cloud Computing: Confidential Data Processing

Users can outsource computation to cloud providers who never see the data they're processing. This enables compliance with strict regulations while maintaining cloud efficiency and scalability.

Machine Learning: Private AI

Training models on encrypted data protects training set privacy. Users can query AI models without revealing their queries. Models can make predictions on encrypted inputs, returning encrypted results.

Secure Voting Systems

Votes can be encrypted, tallied homomorphically, and the total computed without ever decrypting individual votes. This provides both privacy and verifiability.

Challenges and Limitations

Performance Overhead

HE operations are significantly slower than plaintext operations:

Operation Performance Impact
Addition 10-100x slower
Multiplication 100-1000x slower
Bootstrapping 10,000-100,000x slower

However, hardware acceleration, algorithmic improvements, and careful application design are making HE increasingly practical.

Ciphertext Expansion

Encrypted data is much larger than plaintext—typically 1000-10000x expansion. This impacts storage and bandwidth requirements.

Programming Complexity

Implementing applications with HE requires understanding noise budgets, parameter selection, and optimization techniques. Tools and libraries are improving, but it remains more complex than traditional programming.

The WIA-SEC-012 Standard

WIA-SEC-012 provides a comprehensive framework for implementing homomorphic encryption in production systems:

  • Scheme Selection: Guidelines for choosing BFV, CKKS, or PHE based on application requirements
  • Parameter Standards: Pre-configured parameter sets for common security levels (128-bit, 192-bit, 256-bit)
  • Interoperability: Standardized encoding, serialization, and key management
  • Performance Optimization: Best practices for batching, parallelization, and hardware acceleration
  • Security Analysis: Threat models, attack vectors, and mitigation strategies
  • Compliance: Integration with GDPR, HIPAA, and other privacy regulations

Our Philosophy: 弘益人間 (Benefit All Humanity)

Privacy is a fundamental human right. By standardizing and simplifying homomorphic encryption, WIA-SEC-012 makes advanced privacy-preserving technology accessible to developers, organizations, and ultimately, everyone. We believe technology should empower individuals while enabling collective progress.
