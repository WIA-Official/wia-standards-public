Chapter 6: Ongoing Consent

Chapter Overview

This chapter addresses the dynamic nature of consent for brain-computer interface technologies. Unlike traditional one-time consent, BCI applications require ongoing consent processes that adapt to changing circumstances, technology updates, new information, and evolving user preferences. We present frameworks for consent refresh, modification, and withdrawal throughout the BCI lifecycle.

6.1 The Need for Dynamic Consent

Traditional informed consent models treat consent as a single event occurring before an intervention. For BCI applications, this model is inadequate for several reasons:

  • Long-term relationships: BCI use often extends over years or decades, during which circumstances change
  • Technology evolution: BCI capabilities may change through software updates, adding new functions or risks
  • New information: Research may reveal new risks or benefits not known at initial consent
  • User experience: Actual experience with the BCI may differ from expectations, requiring reassessment
  • Life changes: Users' circumstances, values, and preferences may evolve over time
  • Data implications: New uses for neural data may emerge that require fresh consent

Dynamic consent frameworks address these challenges by treating consent as an ongoing relationship rather than a discrete event.

6.3 Periodic Consent Review

Organizations SHALL establish regular intervals for consent review. The frequency depends on the nature of the BCI application and associated risks.

Annual Review (Minimum)

All BCI applications with ongoing data collection or device use require at minimum an annual consent review. This review should include:

  • Confirmation that user wishes to continue
  • Update on any changes to the BCI system or data practices
  • Disclosure of any new risks or benefits discovered
  • Review of user's current circumstances and concerns
  • Documentation of continued consent

Quarterly Review (High-Risk Applications)

For invasive BCIs, research applications, or other high-risk scenarios, more frequent review is required:

  • Assessment of user experience and any adverse effects
  • Evaluation of ongoing risk-benefit balance
  • Opportunity for questions and concerns
  • Assessment of whether circumstances have changed

Continuous Consent (Research)

Active research protocols may require ongoing consent verification at each encounter or data collection session, particularly when:

  • Procedures vary between sessions
  • New interventions are introduced
  • The protocol is modified
  • Participants show signs of distress or reluctance

6.4 Event-Triggered Review

Certain events should automatically trigger consent review and potential re-consent:

EVENT-TRIGGERED CONSENT REVIEW
===============================

TECHNOLOGY CHANGES
□ Major software updates affecting function or data collection
□ Hardware modifications or replacements
□ New features or capabilities added
□ Changes to connected systems or platforms
□ Security updates addressing vulnerabilities

INFORMATION CHANGES
□ Discovery of new risks
□ Publication of significant safety data
□ Regulatory warnings or advisories
□ Changes in evidence about benefits
□ New understanding of long-term effects

ORGANIZATIONAL CHANGES
□ Change in ownership or control of organization
□ New data sharing arrangements
□ Changes in data storage or processing location
□ Business model changes affecting data use
□ Discontinuation of support or services

USER CHANGES
□ Significant change in health status
□ Change in capacity or decision-making ability
□ Life changes affecting risk tolerance
□ User-raised concerns or questions
□ Signs of distress or desire to withdraw

6.5 Consent Modification

Users may wish to modify their consent without full withdrawal. Organizations must support granular consent management:

6.5.1 Types of Consent Modification

  • Scope limitation: Restricting data use to certain purposes while continuing device use
  • Feature selection: Opting out of optional features while maintaining core function
  • Data sharing adjustment: Changing permissions for data sharing with third parties
  • Research participation: Withdrawing from research while continuing clinical use
  • Temporary suspension: Pausing certain activities for a defined period

6.5.2 Modification Process

User-Friendly Modification

Organizations SHOULD provide accessible mechanisms for consent modification that do not require extensive documentation or approval processes. Users should be able to adjust consent preferences through:

  • Digital consent management platforms
  • Simple request forms
  • Direct communication with consent coordinators
  • Periodic review checkboxes

Complex modifications with significant implications may require discussion to ensure understanding.

6.6 Consent Withdrawal

The right to withdraw consent is fundamental and must be preserved throughout BCI use. However, BCI withdrawal is more complex than withdrawal from many other medical interventions.

6.6.1 Withdrawal Principles

  • Unconditional right: Withdrawal does not require justification
  • No penalty: Withdrawal should not result in penalty or loss of unrelated services
  • Informed decision: Implications of withdrawal should be explained
  • Supported process: Assistance should be provided throughout withdrawal
  • Documentation: Withdrawal and its terms must be documented

6.6.2 BCI-Specific Withdrawal Considerations

WITHDRAWAL COMPLEXITY FACTORS
==============================

IMPLANTED DEVICES
• Surgical removal risks vs. risks of retention
• Neural adaptation and potential withdrawal effects
• Device deactivation as alternative to removal
• Ongoing monitoring requirements if device remains

DATA WITHDRAWAL
• What happens to already-collected neural data
• Limitations on complete data deletion
• Data in aggregated or anonymized datasets
• Third-party data already shared

DEPENDENCY
• Psychological adaptation to BCI function
• Loss of restored or enhanced capabilities
• Transition planning and support
• Alternative arrangements for lost function

PRACTICAL CONSIDERATIONS
• Financial implications
• Insurance coverage for removal
• Availability of removal services
• Timeline for withdrawal completion

6.6.3 Withdrawal Process

Organizations SHALL establish clear withdrawal procedures that include:

  1. Request receipt: Acknowledgment of withdrawal request within 48 hours
  2. Information provision: Detailed explanation of withdrawal implications
  3. Options discussion: Review of alternatives to full withdrawal
  4. Deliberation period: Opportunity to consider decision (not to delay legitimate withdrawal)
  5. Final confirmation: Documented confirmation of withdrawal decision
  6. Implementation: Execution of withdrawal according to agreed terms
  7. Follow-up: Post-withdrawal support and monitoring as appropriate

6.7 Data Rights Upon Withdrawal

Withdrawal of consent raises important questions about already-collected neural data. Organizations must clearly define data handling upon withdrawal:

Data Deletion Rights

Users generally have the right to request deletion of their neural data upon withdrawal. However, complete deletion may not always be possible or required:

  • Data already used in published research may not be recallable
  • Data in aggregated, anonymized datasets may be retained
  • Legal retention requirements may override deletion requests
  • Technical limitations may prevent complete deletion

These limitations must be disclosed at initial consent and confirmed at withdrawal.

Data Export Rights

Users should have the option to receive copies of their neural data in usable format before deletion, subject to:

  • Technical feasibility
  • Data format standards
  • Reasonable timeframes
  • Any applicable legal restrictions

Digital platforms can facilitate ongoing consent management:

USER DASHBOARD

NOTIFICATION SYSTEM

• Secure messaging with organization

• Appointment scheduling for discussions

6.9 Chapter Summary

Ongoing consent for BCI applications requires dynamic frameworks that extend beyond initial authorization. Organizations must implement periodic consent review, respond to triggering events with fresh consent processes, support consent modification, and respect withdrawal rights while managing the unique complexities of BCI technology.

Digital consent platforms can facilitate these processes, but human interaction remains essential for complex decisions. The goal is to maintain genuine, informed consent throughout the BCI lifecycle rather than treating initial consent as permanent authorization.
