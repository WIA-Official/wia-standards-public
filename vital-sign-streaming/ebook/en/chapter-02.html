[MED-003] Chapter 2: Current Medical Device Challenges

"Understanding the problem is the first step to creating a solution"

2.1 Market Fragmentation

Current medical device market suffers from severe fragmentation with each manufacturer creating isolated ecosystems.

Issue Impact Consequence
Proprietary formats Data silos No comprehensive health view
Platform lock-in Limited choice Higher costs for users
Non-standard APIs Integration difficulty Reduced innovation

2.2 Technical Barriers

2.2.1 Data Format Inconsistency

Each manufacturer uses different data formats, making integration impossible without conversion.

2.2.2 Communication Protocol Diversity

  • Bluetooth LE: Wearable devices
  • WiFi: Home monitors
  • Proprietary protocols: Hospital equipment
  • Result: Incompatibility and complexity

2.3 Security and Privacy Issues

Major vulnerabilities:

  • Plaintext data transmission
  • Weak authentication (default PINs)
  • No firmware updates
  • Insufficient cloud security

2.7 The Fragmentation Trap in Practice

Consider a common scenario: a patient is treated at hospital A, transferred to hospital B's ICU, then discharged to home monitoring. With today's vendor-locked stack:

  • The bedside monitor at hospital A speaks a proprietary serial protocol that hospital B's monitor does not understand. The transferring ambulance crew cannot view the original waveform; they re-instrument the patient on arrival.
  • Hospital B's ICU emits FHIR Observation resources but only at the per-minute aggregate level required by the EHR vendor's plugin; the full waveform stays trapped on bedside storage.
  • The home monitoring service the patient is discharged to runs a third vendor stack with its own cloud silo; the EHR cannot subscribe to it because the vendor charges a per-seat data export fee.

The clinical record across the patient's journey is therefore a stitched-together summary, never a continuous physiological narrative. WIA-MED-003 addresses this by making the wire format itself portable: any host along the journey can replay or stream to any other host that honours the standard, without licence negotiation.

2.8 Why Earlier Standardisation Attempts Stalled

This is not the first attempt to unify medical device communication. IEEE 11073 (the family of personal-health-device profiles) is the most mature prior effort. WIA-MED-003 does not replace 11073; it composes with it. The differentiators:

  • Streaming-native rather than session-based. 11073 was designed in an era of episodic measurement (one cuff inflation, one weigh-in). WIA-MED-003 treats continuous streaming as the default, with episodic measurement as a degenerate case.
  • Federation-aware rather than point-to-point. 11073 assumes a manager/agent pair on the same local network. WIA-MED-003 specifies how multiple hosts hand off ownership of a stream as a patient moves across care boundaries.
  • Audience-typed access controls. The Phase 3 audience matrix (clinician, nurse station, EHR bridge, research, family, emergency service) is enforced at the standard level, not as a vendor extension.
  • Open governance. The standard is published under MIT licence; conformance test suites are open-source; no vendor pays for the right to claim conformance.

The resulting standard is positioned as a thin layer on top of existing transport protocols (BLE, MQTT, WebSocket) rather than a wholesale replacement, so that vendor adapters can keep their existing on-device firmware and gain WIA conformance through bridge software.
