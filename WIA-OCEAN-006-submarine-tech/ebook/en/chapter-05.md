# Chapter 5: Weapons and Defense Systems

## 5.1 Submarine Combat Power

Submarines are among the most lethal weapons platforms ever developed. Their combination of stealth, mobility, and devastating firepower makes them formidable opponents capable of sinking surface ships, attacking other submarines, striking land targets hundreds of miles inland, and deploying special operations forces. Modern submarines carry sophisticated weapons systems ranging from torpedoes to cruise missiles to ballistic missiles, integrated through advanced combat management systems.

The submarine's primary advantage is stealth—the ability to approach undetected, launch weapons, and evade counterattack. This capability has evolved from crude torpedoes in WWI to precision-guided weapons with ranges exceeding 1,000 kilometers. Combat systems have transformed from manual targeting to automated systems integrating sensors, weapons, and countermeasures.

The WIA-OCEAN-006 standard establishes comprehensive requirements for submarine weapons systems, including torpedo specifications, missile integration, launch systems, fire control, and safety protocols.

## 5.2 Torpedo Systems

Torpedoes remain the submarine's primary weapon for anti-submarine and anti-surface warfare:

**Modern Torpedo Characteristics**:

**Heavyweight Torpedoes**: Standard submarine torpedoes are heavyweight (533mm/21-inch diameter, 450-650 kg warhead) weapons capable of sinking any surface ship or submarine. Examples include:

- **Mk 48 ADCAP** (U.S.): 650 kg warhead, 55+ km range, 55+ knot speed, wire-guided with active/passive homing
- **Spearfish** (UK): 300 kg shaped charge warhead, 65+ km range, 80+ knot speed, wire-guided
- **Black Shark** (Italy/France): 250 kg warhead, 50 km range, 50 knot speed, fiber-optic guided
- **K-Torpedo** (Korea): Indigenous heavyweight torpedo with 250+ kg warhead, advanced guidance

**Guidance Systems**:

**Wire-Guided**: Most torpedoes deploy thin wire connecting torpedo to submarine, allowing course corrections and target updates during attack. The operator can steer the torpedo, select active or passive homing, or even abort the attack.

**Fiber-Optic Guided**: Advanced torpedoes use fiber-optic cable enabling two-way communication, transmitting sonar data from torpedo to submarine. This provides detailed target information and precise control.

**Autonomous Homing**: Terminal phase typically uses autonomous active/passive acoustic homing. The torpedo's sonar detects the target and guides itself to impact.

**Smart Torpedoes**: Modern torpedoes incorporate sophisticated processing, enabling:
- Multiple attack modes (straight run, snake search, circular search)
- Target discrimination (avoiding decoys, selecting specific targets)
- Re-attack capability if first pass misses
- Environmental adaptation (shallow water, deep water, ice)

**Warhead Types**:

**High-Explosive**: Most torpedoes use large high-explosive warheads designed to break the target's keel through direct impact or proximity detonation beneath the hull.

**Shaped Charge**: Some torpedoes use shaped charge warheads focusing explosive energy to penetrate heavy armor or double hulls.

## 5.3 Torpedo Tubes and Launch Systems

Torpedo tubes are large-diameter pressure-resistant tubes penetrating the submarine hull:

**Tube Configuration**: Submarines typically carry 4-8 torpedo tubes in the bow or amidships. Tubes are usually 533mm (21 inch) diameter and 6-7 meters long. The KSS-III has 6 torpedo tubes.

**Launch Methods**:

**Water Ram (Swim-Out)**: Modern submarines typically use water ram systems where high-pressure water pushes the torpedo out of the tube. The torpedo's motor starts outside the submarine, reducing acoustic signature. This method is quieter than air impulse systems.

**Air Impulse**: Older systems use compressed air to eject torpedoes. Louder but enables launch from greater depths.

**Countermeasure Launch**: Some tubes can launch acoustic decoys and countermeasures in addition to torpedoes.

**Weapon Loading**: Reloading torpedo tubes underwater requires careful procedure:
1. Open inner door (breech)
2. Load torpedo from weapon stowage
3. Close and secure inner door
4. Perform system checks
5. Flood tube and equalize pressure
6. Open outer door
7. Launch when ready

Automated loading systems reduce crew requirements and speed reload times to under 10 minutes per tube.

## 5.4 Cruise Missiles

Submarine-launched cruise missiles extend striking power hundreds of kilometers inland:

**Tomahawk Land Attack Missile (TLAM)**: U.S. submarines carry Tomahawk missiles with 450 kg warhead and 1,600+ km range. Launched from torpedo tubes or vertical launch systems (VLS), Tomahawks use GPS/INS guidance with terminal imaging infrared seeker for precision strikes.

**Vertical Launch Systems (VLS)**: Modern submarines integrate VLS tubes behind the bow or sail, enabling missile launch without using torpedo tubes. VLS advantages include:
- More weapons carried
- Faster reaction time
- Launch while moving
- Multiple simultaneous launches
- No torpedo tube occupied during launch

The KSS-III incorporates 6 VLS tubes capable of launching cruise missiles and potentially submarine-launched ballistic missiles (SLBMs).

**Anti-Ship Missiles**: Submarine-launched anti-ship missiles like Harpoon or Exocet engage surface targets at 100+ km range. Launched from torpedo tubes in waterproof capsules, these missiles break surface, ignite rocket motors, and fly to targets using active radar guidance.

**Hyunmoo SLCM**: Korea has developed submarine-launched variants of the Hyunmoo cruise missile family for KSS-III submarines, providing strike capability against land and sea targets.

## 5.5 Ballistic Missiles

Strategic submarines carry ballistic missiles for nuclear deterrence:

**SLBM Characteristics**: Submarine-launched ballistic missiles combine submarine stealth with ballistic missile range and power:

- **Trident II D5** (U.S./UK): Range >12,000 km, carries 8-14 warheads with pinpoint accuracy
- **M51** (France): Range 10,000+ km, up to 10 warheads
- **JL-3** (China): Range 10,000+ km, multiple warheads

SLBMs launch vertically from tubes integrated into the submarine hull, breaking the surface and igniting rocket motors. Submarines can launch from underwater depths of 30-50 meters.

**Korean SLBM Development**: Korea has developed submarine-launched ballistic missiles for KSS-III submarines:
- Indigenous SLBM with estimated range of 500-1,000+ km
- Cold launch system ejecting missile before motor ignition
- Integration with KSS-III VLS
- Enables credible second-strike deterrent capability

The KSS-III Batch-II submarines will reportedly carry 3 SLBMs in addition to cruise missiles.

## 5.6 Mines

Submarines can covertly lay mines in enemy waters:

**Mine Types**:
- **Bottom Mines**: Rest on the seafloor in shallow waters
- **Moored Mines**: Anchored to float at specific depth
- **Mobile Mines**: Propelled mines that can reposition

**Mine Sensors**:
- Acoustic sensors detect ship propeller noise
- Magnetic sensors detect hull magnetic signature
- Pressure sensors detect pressure wave from ship passage

Modern mines use sophisticated target discrimination, attacking only designated vessel types while ignoring neutral traffic.

Submarines deploy mines through torpedo tubes or dedicated mine storage bays. A submarine can quickly create mine fields blocking harbors or chokepoints.

## 5.7 Defense Systems

Submarines employ multiple defense systems:

**Acoustic Countermeasures**:

**Decoys**: Mobile acoustic decoys launched from signal ejectors create false targets or mask submarine signature. Advanced decoys replicate submarine acoustic characteristics, deceiving enemy torpedoes.

**Jammers**: Acoustic jammers disrupt torpedo guidance systems through jamming signals or acoustic denial.

**Evasive Maneuvers**: Submarines use radical maneuvers, speed changes, and depth changes to evade torpedoes. Modern torpedoes are fast, but submarines can exploit acoustic conditions and terrain.

**Torpedo Countermeasures**: Hard-kill systems detect incoming torpedoes and launch interception torpedoes or explosive devices to destroy them. These systems are experimental but show promise.

**Electronic Warfare**:

**Radar Warning Receivers**: Detect radar emissions when at periscope depth, warning of surface threats.

**Communication Intercept**: Monitor enemy communications for intelligence and threat warning.

**Laser Warning**: Detect laser targeting or communications interception attempts.

**Hull Coating and Stealth**: The submarine's acoustic quieting serves as the primary defense—undetected submarines cannot be attacked.

## 5.8 Combat Management Systems

Modern combat systems integrate sensors, weapons, and countermeasures:

**Jangbogo Combat System** (KSS-III): Korea's indigenous combat management system provides:

**Situational Awareness**: Integrates sonar, periscope/photonics mast, electronic surveillance, and navigation into unified tactical picture.

**Automatic Target Tracking**: Tracks multiple contacts simultaneously, classifying threats and prioritizing engagement.

**Fire Control**: Computes firing solutions for torpedoes and missiles, managing weapon inventory and preparing launches.

**Communication**: Integrates radio, satellite, and underwater communication systems.

**Decision Support**: Provides tactical recommendations, threat assessment, and course of action analysis.

The Jangbogo system represents sophisticated software integrating hundreds of sensors and systems into operator-friendly displays that provide commanders with comprehensive awareness and control.

**International Combat Systems**:
- **AN/BYG-1** (U.S.): Integrated combat system for Virginia and Seawolf-class
- **SUBTICS** (France): Combat system for French submarines
- **MSI-90U** (Germany): Combat system for Type 212/214 submarines

## 5.9 Special Operations Support

Submarines support special operations forces:

**Dry Deck Shelter (DDS)**: Cylindrical hangar mounted on submarine deck houses SEAL Delivery Vehicles (SDVs) or swimmer lockout chamber. SEALs can deploy and recover covertly from submerged submarine.

**Lockout Chambers**: Allow divers to exit and enter submarine underwater for reconnaissance, demolition, or clandestine operations.

**Mini-Submarines**: Some submarines carry mini-submarines for covert infiltration.

The KSS-III reportedly includes special operations capabilities for ROK Navy special warfare units.
