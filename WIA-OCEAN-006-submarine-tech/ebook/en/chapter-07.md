# Chapter 7: Commercial and Research Submarines

## 7.1 Submarines Beyond Naval Warfare

While military applications dominate submarine development, civilian submarines serve critical roles in scientific research, commercial operations, tourism, and underwater construction. These vessels range from small tourist submarines carrying dozens of passengers to sophisticated research submersibles exploring the deepest ocean trenches. Commercial submarines enable underwater work impossible from the surface, while research submarines have revolutionized our understanding of ocean depths.

Unlike military submarines prioritizing stealth and weapons, civilian submarines emphasize visibility, endurance, safety, and specialized capabilities. Tourist submarines feature large viewing windows. Research submersibles carry sophisticated scientific instruments and sampling equipment. Work submarines integrate manipulator arms and tools for underwater construction and maintenance.

The WIA-OCEAN-006 standard addresses civilian submarine requirements, including safety protocols, operational standards, environmental protection, and specialized equipment integration.

## 7.2 Research Submarines and Deep Submergence Vehicles

Scientific research submarines have enabled groundbreaking discoveries in ocean depths:

**Deep Submergence Vehicles (DSVs)**:

**DSV Alvin**: Operated by Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution since 1964, Alvin can dive to 6,500 meters carrying three people. Alvin has made over 5,000 dives, discovering hydrothermal vents in 1977, exploring the Titanic wreck, and conducting countless scientific missions. The vessel features:
- Titanium pressure sphere
- Advanced manipulator arms
- High-definition cameras and lighting
- Sampling equipment and sensors
- 10 hours underwater endurance

**DSV Limiting Factor**: The world's deepest-diving crewed submarine, reaching 10,928 meters in the Mariana Trench in 2019. This privately-operated submersible demonstrates advanced materials and engineering enabling human exploration of the ocean's deepest points.

**Shinkai 6500** (Japan): Operates to 6,500 meters depth, conducting research on deep-sea ecosystems, geology, and biology. Operated by JAMSTEC (Japan Agency for Marine-Earth Science and Technology).

**Jiaolong** (China): Deep-sea research submersible rated to 7,000 meters, supporting Chinese oceanographic research and resource exploration.

**Research Capabilities**:

**Geological Sampling**: Manipulator arms collect rock samples, sediment cores, and mineral specimens from the seafloor. These samples reveal Earth's geological history and processes.

**Biological Research**: Scientists observe deep-sea life in natural habitat, discovering new species and ecosystems. Hydrothermal vent communities, cold seeps, and abyssal plains harbor unique organisms adapted to extreme conditions.

**Oceanographic Measurements**: Instruments measure temperature, salinity, pressure, chemistry, and currents throughout the water column and at depth.

**Archaeology**: Research submarines explore shipwrecks and underwater archaeological sites with minimal disturbance. The Titanic, ancient shipwrecks, and submerged settlements have been documented using research submarines.

## 7.3 Autonomous Underwater Vehicles (AUVs)

Unmanned submersibles conduct research and surveys without crew:

**Characteristics**: AUVs operate autonomously following pre-programmed missions, carrying sensors and instruments for data collection. Modern AUVs can operate for days or weeks, covering hundreds of kilometers while mapping, surveying, or monitoring.

**Applications**:

**Oceanographic Research**: AUVs collect data on ocean conditions, marine life, and underwater features. They can survey large areas efficiently.

**Seafloor Mapping**: Equipped with sonar and sensors, AUVs map seafloor topography and features at high resolution.

**Pipeline and Cable Inspection**: AUVs inspect underwater infrastructure, identifying damage or potential problems.

**Environmental Monitoring**: AUVs monitor water quality, pollution, and ecosystem health over time.

**Military Applications**: Naval AUVs conduct mine detection, harbor security, and intelligence gathering.

**Notable AUVs**:

**REMUS**: Family of AUVs used for research, surveys, and military applications. Compact, versatile platforms.

**Bluefin**: Large AUVs capable of extended missions, used for seafloor mapping and search operations.

**Autosub**: British research AUVs that have operated under Antarctic ice and in deep ocean.

**Wave Gliders**: Surface/subsurface AUVs using wave energy for propulsion, enabling unlimited endurance missions.

## 7.4 Remotely Operated Vehicles (ROVs)

Tethered underwater robots controlled from surface vessels:

**Characteristics**: ROVs connect to surface vessels via tether carrying power and control signals. Operators control the ROV in real-time, viewing video feeds and sensor data. This enables precise control for complex tasks.

**Classifications**:

**Observation Class**: Small ROVs for inspection and survey work, typically under 100 kg.

**Work Class**: Large, powerful ROVs with manipulator arms for construction, maintenance, and heavy work. Can weigh several tons.

**Applications**:

**Offshore Energy**: ROVs install, maintain, and repair underwater oil and gas infrastructure. They can work at depths exceeding 3,000 meters, performing complex tasks like valve operation, pipeline connection, and structure inspection.

**Submarine Cable**: ROVs lay, inspect, and repair submarine communication cables and power cables.

**Salvage**: ROVs survey wrecks, attach rigging, and recover objects from the seafloor.

**Scientific Research**: ROVs collect samples, deploy instruments, and conduct observations.

**Advantages over Crewed Submarines**:
- Unlimited underwater time (tethered power)
- No crew risk
- Lower operational cost
- Can work in hazardous environments
- Precise control for delicate tasks

**Disadvantages**:
- Tether limits range and mobility
- Requires surface support vessel
- Less autonomous than AUVs

## 7.5 Tourist Submarines

Passenger submarines provide underwater viewing experiences:

**Atlantis Submarines**: Operates tourist submarines in Hawaii, Caribbean, and other locations. Vessels carry 48-64 passengers to 30-40 meters depth, featuring large acrylic viewports for viewing marine life and coral reefs.

**DeepFlight Dragon**: Personal luxury submarine for yacht owners, carrying two people to 120 meters depth with 360-degree visibility.

**Triton Submarines**: Manufactures luxury private submarines, including models diving to 1,000 meters and the Triton 36000/2 that can reach full ocean depth (10,908 meters).

**Design Features**:

**Safety**: Multiple redundant systems ensure passenger safety:
- Positive buoyancy (surfaces automatically if power lost)
- Emergency oxygen supplies
- Emergency drop weights
- Communication systems
- Fire suppression

**Visibility**: Large acrylic viewports provide panoramic views. Some designs feature transparent spheres for maximum visibility.

**Comfort**: Air conditioning, seating, and amenities ensure passenger comfort during tours typically lasting 45-90 minutes.

**Environmental Protection**: Tourist submarines operate to minimize coral and ecosystem damage, maintaining safe distances from sensitive areas.

**Economic Impact**: Tourist submarines generate revenue supporting local economies and raise awareness about marine conservation.

## 7.6 Work Submarines and Submersibles

Specialized submarines for commercial underwater work:

**Diving Support Vessels (DSVs)**: Small submarines transport and support commercial divers to work sites, providing:
- One-atmosphere chambers eliminating decompression
- Life support during extended underwater work
- Tool and equipment transport
- Emergency shelter

**Submarine Rescue Vehicles**: Purpose-built submarines rescue crews from disabled submarines:

**DSRV (Deep Submergence Rescue Vehicle)**: U.S. and other navies operate DSRVs capable of mating with disabled submarines up to 600 meters depth, evacuating up to 16 personnel per trip.

**NATO Submarine Rescue System**: International cooperative rescue system with transportable rescue submarine and support equipment.

**Lock-Out Submersibles**: Enable divers to exit and enter underwater for inspection, maintenance, or construction work while the submarine maintains pressure.

## 7.7 Korean Commercial and Research Submarine Development

Korea is developing civilian submarine capabilities:

**Hemire (Hanhae)**: Korea's first deep-sea research ROV, operated by Korea Institute of Ocean Science and Technology (KIOST), capable of operations to 6,000 meters depth. Equipped with manipulators, cameras, and sampling equipment for marine research.

**Future Crewed Submersible**: Plans exist for Korean crewed deep submergence vehicle supporting domestic ocean research and resource exploration.

**Commercial Applications**: Korean submarine technology developed for military programs has potential civilian applications in:
- Offshore energy support
- Submarine cable maintenance
- Marine research
- Underwater tourism
- Ocean resource development

## 7.8 Environmental and Safety Considerations

Civilian submarines must address environmental and safety concerns:

**Environmental Protection**:
- Minimize disturbance to marine ecosystems
- Prevent pollution and contamination
- Avoid damage to coral reefs and sensitive habitats
- Support marine conservation through education and research

**Safety Standards**:
- Rigorous certification and inspection
- Crew training and qualification
- Emergency procedures and equipment
- Passenger safety briefings
- Regular maintenance and testing

**Regulations**: Civilian submarines operate under:
- Classification society rules (Lloyd's, ABS, etc.)
- National maritime regulations
- International safety standards
- Insurance requirements
- Environmental regulations

Tourist submarines introduce thousands of people to underwater environments, supporting marine conservation awareness and local economies. Work submarines enable commercial operations in offshore energy, telecommunications, and construction.
