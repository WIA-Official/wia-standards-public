CHAPTER 2

⚠️ Current Challenges in Vertical Transportation

Examining the technical limitations, safety concerns, inefficiencies, and economic impacts of fragmented elevator systems that necessitate the WIA standard.

2.1 The Fragmentation Problem

The elevator industry suffers from severe fragmentation. While smartphones, computers, and even smart home devices have coalesced around common standards (USB, Wi-Fi, Bluetooth), elevators remain islands of proprietary technology. This fragmentation creates cascading problems throughout the building lifecycle.

Proprietary Control Systems

Each major manufacturer—Otis, Schindler, KONE, ThyssenKrupp, Mitsubishi Electric, Hitachi—uses proprietary protocols for elevator control. These systems are deliberately designed to be incompatible with competitors, creating vendor lock-in. A building owner who installs an Otis elevator cannot use Schindler maintenance services without completely replacing the control system.

The protocols differ fundamentally in architecture, message formats, and communication methods. Some use CAN bus networks, others use proprietary Ethernet variants. Data encoding varies from binary proprietary formats to custom XML schemas. Even when the physical layers are similar, the application layer protocols are completely incompatible.

The building management must contract with three separate service providers, maintain three monitoring platforms, and stock three separate parts inventories. Coordinated building-wide optimizations (like energy management during off-peak hours) are impossible.

2.2 Safety and Compliance Challenges

While mechanical safety has improved dramatically, digital safety protocols lag behind. The lack of standardization creates risks during emergencies.

Emergency Response Complications

Fire Department Interface Issues: During fires, elevators must enter "fireman service mode," allowing manual control by firefighters. However, the interface varies by manufacturer. A firefighter trained on Otis systems may struggle with Schindler controls, costing precious seconds during evacuations.

Some manufacturers use key switches, others use dedicated panels, and newer systems use digital interfaces. The location of emergency controls, the sequence of operations, and even the visual indicators differ. First responders have reported confusion and delays when entering unfamiliar buildings.

Accessibility Compliance Gaps

The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) and similar regulations worldwide mandate elevator accessibility features. However, implementation varies dramatically:

Feature Requirement Implementation Variability Problem
Audio Announcements Floor and direction callouts 5+ different voice systems Inconsistent user experience
Braille Buttons Raised and tactile markings Layout varies by manufacturer Relearning required
Visual Indicators LED/LCD displays Different icon sets, colors Confusion for hearing-impaired
Door Reopening Sensors Obstruction detection Different sensitivity levels Safety variability

Cybersecurity Vulnerabilities

Modern elevators are increasingly connected to the internet for remote monitoring, but security practices are inconsistent. A 2024 study found serious vulnerabilities across all major manufacturers:

  • Unencrypted Communications: 38% of tested systems transmitted data without encryption, exposing elevator status and control commands to eavesdropping
  • Weak Authentication: Default passwords ("admin"/"admin") remained active on 22% of systems
  • No Intrusion Detection: 67% lacked any system to detect unauthorized access attempts
  • Outdated Software: 45% ran operating systems no longer receiving security patches
  • Remote Exploits: Researchers demonstrated remote car stopping, door jamming, and false alarm triggering
Security Incident: In 2023, hackers accessed an elevator monitoring system in a New York office building through an unpatched vulnerability. They didn't cause physical harm, but demonstrated they could stop elevators between floors, triggering panic. The building was evacuated, costing $500,000 in lost productivity and requiring a complete control system replacement.

2.3 Operational Inefficiencies

Beyond safety, fragmentation causes daily operational headaches that frustrate building managers and occupants.

Suboptimal Dispatching

In buildings with elevators from multiple manufacturers, each group operates independently. An Otis elevator on the east side and a Schindler elevator on the west side cannot coordinate to minimize passenger wait times. This leads to situations where one elevator is overcrowded while another nearby is empty.

Advanced destination dispatch algorithms that could optimize building-wide traffic flow are impossible to implement. Studies show that coordinated multi-manufacturer dispatch could reduce average wait times by 18% and energy consumption by 12%, but fragmentation prevents this.

Maintenance Scheduling Conflicts

Different manufacturers have different maintenance schedules, parts availability, and service windows. A building might have:

  • Otis elevators serviced Tuesday mornings
  • Schindler elevators serviced Wednesday afternoons
  • KONE elevators serviced Thursday evenings

This creates three separate service disruptions weekly instead of one coordinated maintenance window. Occupants face reduced capacity throughout the week rather than concentrated downtime during low-traffic periods.

Energy Waste

Modern elevators can regenerate electricity during descent, feeding power back into the building. However, uncoordinated systems waste opportunities:

Scenario Coordinated System Fragmented System Energy Waste
Morning Rush (Up Traffic) Some elevators stay at ground floor All elevators return to home floors +15% energy use
Evening Rush (Down Traffic) Coordinated regenerative braking Uncoordinated regeneration +12% waste
Lunch Hour Some elevators parked All elevators in standby +8% waste
Night Mode Minimal elevators active All systems in low-power mode +20% waste

2.4 Maintenance and Modernization Barriers

The 25-30 year lifecycle of elevators means modernization is a major industry segment. Proprietary systems make this process unnecessarily expensive and disruptive.

Forced Complete Replacements

When a manufacturer discontinues a control system, building owners face a dilemma. Spare parts become unavailable, forcing premature replacement of otherwise functional mechanical components. A 15-year-old elevator with a perfectly good motor, cables, and car might need a $80,000 modernization simply because the controller is obsolete.

Standardized systems would allow controller upgrades while retaining mechanical components, reducing costs by 60-70%.

Technician Training Challenges

Elevator technicians must become certified on each manufacturer's systems. Training programs can take 6-12 months per manufacturer. A technician certified on Otis systems cannot service Schindler elevators without additional training.

This creates artificial labor shortages. A building with mixed manufacturers might wait days for the right specialist, even when general technicians are available. The specialized knowledge requirement also drives up labor costs.

Diagnostic Tool Fragmentation

Each manufacturer provides proprietary diagnostic tools:

Schindler PORT Technology $9,200 Predictive maintenance, traffic analysis
KONE KONE Care $7,800 Real-time monitoring, fault prediction
ThyssenKrupp MAX $10,500 Cloud analytics, mobile app

A service provider maintaining elevators from all four manufacturers must invest $36,000+ in diagnostic tools, plus annual subscription fees of $12,000+. These costs are passed to building owners through higher maintenance contracts.

2.5 Data Silos and Analytics Limitations

The IoT revolution promises predictive maintenance and optimization, but proprietary systems prevent full realization of these benefits.

Trapped Performance Data

Each manufacturer's cloud platform captures valuable data—ride counts, wait times, energy usage, component wear patterns. However, this data remains siloed in vendor-specific systems. Building managers cannot:

  • Compare performance across manufacturers in the same building
  • Export data for custom analytics or academic research
  • Integrate elevator data with other building systems (HVAC, lighting, security)
  • Switch analytics providers without losing historical data
  • Train AI models on cross-manufacturer datasets

Limited Third-Party Innovation

Closed APIs stifle innovation. Startups cannot develop advanced analytics, optimization algorithms, or predictive maintenance tools that work across all elevators. Each solution must be custom-developed for each manufacturer, making products economically unviable.

Compare this to the smartphone industry: open APIs allow thousands of app developers to create value on iOS and Android platforms. Imagine if each iPhone required apps written specifically for it, incompatible with other iPhones. That's the current state of elevator software.

Missed Opportunity: A university research team developed a machine learning algorithm that predicted brake failures 4-6 weeks in advance with 92% accuracy by analyzing vibration patterns. They trained it on data from 500 elevators in their campus buildings. However, the algorithm only works on KONE elevators because that's what the campus uses. Adapting it to Otis or Schindler would require entirely new datasets and retraining—work the small research team cannot afford. The innovation remains limited to one manufacturer's ecosystem.

2.6 Integration with Smart Building Systems

Modern buildings are becoming intelligent ecosystems where HVAC, lighting, security, and vertical transportation should work together. Fragmentation prevents this vision.

Building Management System Integration Failures

Building Management Systems (BMS) like Honeywell, Johnson Controls, and Siemens coordinate HVAC, lighting, and energy management. Ideally, elevators would integrate seamlessly:

  • During high elevator usage, HVAC could pre-cool upper floors
  • Energy-intensive operations could be scheduled to avoid simultaneous peak loads
  • Occupancy data from elevators could optimize lighting and HVAC in office areas
  • Fire alarm integration could automatically recall elevators and manage evacuation

In practice, each manufacturer provides a different BMS integration gateway, using incompatible protocols (some use BACnet, others Modbus, others proprietary APIs). Integration projects become expensive custom development, costing $50,000-$150,000 per building. Many building owners skip integration entirely, leaving systems disconnected.

Access Control Integration Challenges

Modern buildings use access control systems (HID, Lenel, AMAG) to restrict floor access based on employee badges. The elevator should automatically send employees only to authorized floors. However:

Integration Challenge Impact Workaround Cost
Proprietary protocols Cannot connect without custom gateway $25,000-$40,000
Real-time sync issues Access changes take hours to propagate Security risk
No standardized badge formats Must reprogram for each elevator brand $15,000
Limited visitor management Temporary access difficult to grant Manual overrides needed

2.7 Regulatory Compliance Complexity

Elevators are heavily regulated, but regulations vary by jurisdiction. Proprietary systems make compliance verification difficult.

Multi-Jurisdiction Challenges

A global corporation might have offices in 50 countries, each with different elevator safety regulations. With proprietary systems, each building must be individually verified for local compliance. Standardized systems could provide universal compliance reporting, with adapters for regional variations.

Inspection Data Inconsistency

Elevator inspectors must verify safety systems, but access to diagnostic data varies. Some manufacturers provide detailed inspection reports, others only basic summaries. Inspectors cannot compare data across manufacturers or identify industry-wide trends. This limits the effectiveness of safety oversight.

2.8 Environmental Impact

The sustainability movement demands more efficient buildings, but elevator fragmentation wastes resources.

Energy Inefficiency

As shown earlier, uncoordinated systems waste 12-20% more energy than optimized fleets. For 18+ million elevators worldwide, this represents billions of kWh annually—equivalent to the power consumption of a medium-sized country.

Electronic Waste from Premature Replacements

When controllers become obsolete, entire systems are scrapped despite mechanical components having decades of useful life. Each elevator replacement generates 2-5 tons of electronic waste, much containing hazardous materials. Standardization would extend component lifespans by 40%, reducing waste by millions of tons.

2.9 The Cost of Inaction

Continuing with the status quo isn't sustainable. As buildings become smarter and populations more urban, elevator inefficiencies will compound:

  • Urban Growth: By 2050, 68% of humanity will live in cities, increasing demand for high-rise buildings and efficient elevators
  • Aging Infrastructure: 40% of elevators are over 20 years old and approaching modernization, representing a $280 billion market opportunity or waste, depending on approach
  • Cybersecurity Risks: Connected elevators without security standards become attractive targets for hackers and terrorists
  • Climate Commitments: Buildings account for 40% of global energy use; elevator inefficiency undermines carbon reduction goals
  • Public Safety: Inconsistent emergency protocols could cost lives during disasters

  • Cybersecurity vulnerabilities in 67% of systems expose buildings to hacking and disruption
