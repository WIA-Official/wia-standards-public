Chapter 2: System Architecture

2.1 Overview of Bionic Eye Architecture

A complete bionic eye system consists of multiple interconnected components working in harmony to capture, process, transmit, and deliver visual information. The architecture must balance competing requirements: high performance, low power consumption, miniaturization, biocompatibility, reliability, and manufacturability. This chapter provides a comprehensive examination of each system component and how they integrate into a functional visual prosthesis.

System Philosophy: The WIA-BIONIC_EYE architecture follows a modular design principle, allowing independent upgrade paths for external and implanted components while maintaining backward compatibility and interoperability across manufacturers.

2.2.3 Power Management

External power systems must balance capacity with portability. Current specifications require:

  • Battery capacity: Minimum 8 hours continuous operation
  • Recharge time: Maximum 2 hours to full charge
  • Hot-swap capability for uninterrupted operation
  • Low-battery warning with 30-minute reserve
  • USB-C charging compatibility
  • Wireless charging option (Qi standard)

2.4 Implanted Component Subsystem

2.4.1 Hermetic Enclosure

The implanted electronics require protection from body fluids while remaining biocompatible. The hermetic package must:

  • Maintain seal integrity for minimum 10-year design life
  • Withstand autoclave sterilization temperatures
  • Use biocompatible materials (titanium, ceramic, platinum)
  • Minimize size and weight (<1 cm³, <2g)
  • Provide electrical feedthroughs for electrode connections
  • Include RF-transparent windows for wireless communication

2.5 Implant Approaches Comparison

2.5.1 Epiretinal Implants

Positioned on the inner surface of the retina, stimulating ganglion cells directly. Advantages include simpler surgical access and ability to cover larger retinal areas. Disadvantages include the need to bypass natural retinal processing and potential for ganglion cell damage from tacking.

2.5.2 Subretinal Implants

Placed between the photoreceptor layer and retinal pigment epithelium, stimulating bipolar cells. Advantages include leveraging remaining retinal processing circuitry and natural fixation. Disadvantages include more complex surgery and limited power delivery options (some designs use ambient light).

2.5.3 Suprachoroidal Implants

Positioned between the sclera and choroid, outside the retina. Advantages include simpler, less invasive surgery and reduced risk of retinal damage. Disadvantages include higher stimulation thresholds due to distance from target neurons and lower spatial resolution.

2.5.4 Cortical Implants

Bypass the eye entirely, stimulating the visual cortex directly. Advantages include applicability to patients with damaged eyes or optic nerves. Disadvantages include invasive brain surgery, complex electrode-cortex mapping, and long-term electrode stability challenges.

Selection Criteria: The choice of implant approach depends on the patient's specific pathology, remaining visual pathway function, surgical risk tolerance, and desired outcomes. A thorough pre-operative assessment determines candidacy for each approach.

2.7 Reliability and Redundancy

Medical implant reliability requirements demand exceptional engineering:

  • Mean Time Between Failures (MTBF): >100,000 hours
  • Redundant communication channels for critical commands
  • Fail-safe stimulation shutdown on communication loss
  • Error detection and correction in all data paths
  • Built-in self-test (BIST) capabilities
  • Graceful degradation with electrode failures

2.8 Summary

The bionic eye system architecture encompasses external image capture and processing components, wireless data and power links, and implanted stimulation electronics with electrode arrays. Each subsystem must meet stringent performance, safety, and reliability requirements while integrating seamlessly with other components. The modular design philosophy enables independent advancement of each subsystem while maintaining system-wide interoperability. Understanding this architecture is essential for device developers, clinicians, and regulatory professionals working with visual prostheses.
