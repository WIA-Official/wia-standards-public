Chapter 4: Surgical Procedures and Implantation

4.1 Overview of Bionic Eye Surgery

Implantation of a bionic eye system is a complex microsurgical procedure requiring specialized training, equipment, and facilities. The surgery involves placing electronic components in or near the eye while ensuring proper positioning, secure fixation, and preservation of remaining visual structures. This chapter details the surgical procedures for different implant types, from patient preparation through post-operative care.

Surgical Philosophy: The primary goals are accurate electrode placement for optimal phosphene mapping, secure long-term fixation, and minimal trauma to ocular tissues. Every surgical decision prioritizes patient safety and long-term device function.

4.2.2 Pre-Operative Imaging

Comprehensive imaging guides surgical planning:

  • Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT): Retinal layer analysis, macular status
  • B-scan Ultrasonography: Vitreous status, retinal detachment screening
  • Fundus Photography: Documentation, landmark identification
  • Axial Length Measurement: Implant sizing calculations
  • CT/MRI (for cortical implants): Brain anatomy, visual cortex mapping

4.3 Epiretinal Implantation Procedure

4.3.1 Surgical Setup

The procedure is performed under general anesthesia in a sterile ophthalmic operating room:

  • Standard vitreoretinal surgical setup with microscope
  • Wide-angle viewing system for peripheral retina visualization
  • Specialized implant insertion instruments
  • Intraoperative testing equipment for electrode verification
  • Backup implant system available

4.3.2 Step-by-Step Procedure

  1. Conjunctival Peritomy: 360-degree conjunctival dissection to expose sclera
  2. Scleral Pocket Creation: Partial-thickness scleral dissection for electronics housing placement
  3. Electronics Case Fixation: Secure attachment of hermetic electronics package to sclera using non-absorbable sutures
  4. Pars Plana Vitrectomy: Complete removal of vitreous gel using standard three-port approach
  5. Posterior Vitreous Detachment: Ensure complete separation of vitreous from retina
  6. Electrode Array Insertion: Introduction of flexible electrode array through sclerotomy
  7. Array Positioning: Careful placement over target retinal area (usually macular region)
  8. Retinal Tack Fixation: Securing array to retina using titanium tack(s)
  9. Intraoperative Testing: Verification of electrode function and stimulation thresholds
  10. Wound Closure: Sclerotomy closure, conjunctival reapproximation
Critical Point: Electrode array positioning is the most critical step. The array must be centered on functional retina, lie flat against the retinal surface, and be secured without excessive retinal trauma from the tack.

4.4.2 Special Considerations

  • Smaller implant size compared to epiretinal devices
  • Natural fixation by RPE adhesion (no tack needed)
  • Risk of subretinal hemorrhage during insertion
  • Potential for device migration before adhesion stabilizes
  • Positioning for optimal light capture (photovoltaic designs)

4.6 Cortical Implant Surgery

Visual cortex implants require neurosurgical expertise:

4.6.1 Pre-Surgical Brain Mapping

  • Functional MRI to locate primary visual cortex (V1)
  • Retinotopic mapping to identify electrode placement targets
  • Blood vessel mapping to avoid vascular structures
  • 3D surgical planning and navigation setup

4.7 Intraoperative Testing

4.7.1 Electrode Impedance Measurement

All electrodes are tested for proper function before wound closure:

  • Impedance should be 1-50 kΩ at 1 kHz
  • Open circuit indicates broken connection
  • Short circuit indicates electrode damage
  • Asymmetric impedance suggests poor tissue contact

4.7.2 Stimulation Threshold Testing

When patient cooperation permits, intraoperative stimulation confirms device function and helps optimize array position.

4.8.2 Complication Management

Potential Complications: Retinal detachment, infection (endophthalmitis), device migration, elevated IOP, corneal problems, and chronic inflammation must be promptly recognized and treated according to established protocols.

4.9 Surgical Training Requirements

Surgeons performing bionic eye implantation must complete:

  • Fellowship training in vitreoretinal surgery
  • Device-specific certification course
  • Supervised surgical proctorship (minimum 3 cases)
  • Wet lab and simulation training
  • Continuing education requirements

4.10 Summary

Bionic eye implantation requires meticulous surgical technique, comprehensive pre-operative assessment, and diligent post-operative care. Different implant approaches (epiretinal, subretinal, suprachoroidal, cortical) have distinct surgical requirements and risk profiles. Successful outcomes depend on proper patient selection, expert surgical execution, and systematic follow-up care. The WIA-BIONIC_EYE standard establishes minimum requirements for surgical facilities, surgeon training, and procedural protocols to ensure patient safety and optimal device function.
