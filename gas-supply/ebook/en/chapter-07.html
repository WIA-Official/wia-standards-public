Chapter 7: Phase 4: System Integration

7.1 Inter-operator Pipeline Integration

Gas transmission often involves multiple operators managing interconnected pipeline segments. Integration requirements address nomination and scheduling for gas deliveries, balancing calculations at interconnection points, operational flow control coordination, and emergency response procedures. Data exchange protocols enable automated processing of nominations and confirmations. Shared visibility into pipeline status improves operational coordination during normal and emergency conditions.

7.2 LNG Terminal Integration

LNG import/export terminals connect global LNG markets with pipeline networks requiring sophisticated integration. Regasification scheduling coordinates ship arrivals with pipeline capacity. Storage management optimizes tank levels considering ship schedules, pipeline demand, and weather forecasts. Send-out control manages gas flow from terminals into transmission networks. Marine traffic systems integration supports vessel scheduling and berth allocation.

7.3 Smart Meter Integration

Advanced metering infrastructure (AMI) connects millions of smart meters for automated reading and demand response. Meter data management systems aggregate consumption data from distributed meters. Remote disconnect capabilities support non-payment and emergency shutoffs. Leak detection algorithms analyze consumption patterns identifying potential leaks. Demand response integrates with pricing systems and grid management.

7.4 Renewable Gas Interconnection

Biomethane and hydrogen injection requires special integration procedures. Gas quality monitoring ensures injected gas meets specifications before entering pipelines. Flow measurement and custody transfer procedures establish ownership at interconnection points. Production forecasting integrates renewable gas availability into system balancing. Hydrogen compatibility assessments evaluate infrastructure suitability for hydrogen blending.

7.5 Cross-Standard WIA Integration

Gas infrastructure connects with other infrastructure systems covered by WIA standards. Electric power integration supports combined-cycle generation and sector coupling. Water infrastructure integration addresses cooling water for LNG terminals. Smart city integration enables coordinated infrastructure planning and emergency response. Digital identity standards support workforce credentials and equipment certification.

7.6 Legacy System Integration

Most gas operators maintain legacy systems that must coexist with modern infrastructure. Protocol translation gateways bridge proprietary and standard protocols. Data transformation middleware converts between legacy and standard formats. Phased migration strategies allow gradual replacement without service disruption. Backwards compatibility provisions ensure new systems work with existing equipment.

📌 Key Takeaways

  • Inter-operator integration enables coordinated operation of interconnected pipeline networks
  • LNG terminal integration connects global gas markets with regional pipeline systems
  • Smart meter integration provides detailed consumption data and demand response capabilities
  • Renewable gas interconnection supports biomethane and hydrogen injection
  • Cross-standard integration creates coherent smart infrastructure ecosystems
  • Legacy system integration preserves existing investments while enabling modernization
