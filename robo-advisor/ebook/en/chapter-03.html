Chapter 3: Modern Portfolio Theory and Optimization

3.1 The Efficient Frontier

The efficient frontier represents the set of portfolios that offer the highest expected return for a defined level of risk. Any portfolio below the efficient frontier is sub-optimal because a portfolio on the frontier offers better returns for the same risk.

Constructing the Efficient Frontier

  1. Calculate expected returns for all assets
  2. Calculate the covariance matrix for all asset pairs
  3. Generate portfolios with different weight combinations
  4. Calculate return and risk for each portfolio
  5. Identify portfolios with maximum return for each risk level

3.2 Capital Market Line (CML)

The Capital Market Line extends from the risk-free rate through the market portfolio, representing the best achievable risk-return combinations when combining the risk-free asset with the market portfolio.

Key Insights

  • All efficient portfolios lie on the CML
  • The tangency portfolio has the highest Sharpe ratio
  • Investors choose positions on CML based on risk tolerance
  • Leverage can extend positions beyond the market portfolio

3.3 Asset Correlation and Diversification

Correlation measures how two assets move relative to each other. Proper diversification combines assets with low or negative correlations to reduce portfolio volatility.

Asset Pair Typical Correlation Diversification Benefit

3.4 Risk Measures in Portfolio Management

3.4.1 Standard Deviation

The most common risk measure, representing the average deviation from mean returns. Higher standard deviation indicates greater volatility and risk.

3.4.2 Value at Risk (VaR)

VaR estimates the maximum expected loss over a time period at a given confidence level. For example, 1-day 95% VaR of $10,000 means a 5% chance of losing more than $10,000 in one day.

3.4.3 Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR)

Also known as Expected Shortfall, CVaR measures the average loss exceeding the VaR threshold, providing insight into tail risk.

3.4.4 Maximum Drawdown

The largest peak-to-trough decline in portfolio value, crucial for understanding worst-case historical scenarios.

3.5 Portfolio Optimization Techniques

3.5.1 Minimum Variance Portfolio

The portfolio on the efficient frontier with the lowest possible risk. Useful for highly risk-averse investors prioritizing capital preservation.

3.5.2 Maximum Sharpe Ratio Portfolio

The tangency portfolio that maximizes risk-adjusted returns. Most robo-advisors use variations of this optimization for their model portfolios.

3.5.3 Risk Parity Optimization

Allocates capital to equalize risk contribution from each asset class, often requiring leverage on bonds to match equity risk.

3.6 Constraints in Real-World Optimization

Practical Constraints

  • Long-only: No short selling (w ≥ 0)
  • Minimum position sizes: Avoid tiny allocations for transaction efficiency
  • Maximum position sizes: Limit concentration risk (e.g., no asset > 40%)
  • Asset class limits: Minimum/maximum allocations per category
  • Turnover constraints: Limit trading to reduce transaction costs
  • Tax constraints: Avoid triggering short-term capital gains

3.7 Multi-Period Optimization

Unlike single-period optimization, multi-period models consider how decisions today affect future opportunities, incorporating:

  • Time-varying risk tolerance (glide paths)
  • Rebalancing opportunities and costs
  • Tax implications across periods
  • Withdrawal and contribution schedules
  • Regime changes and economic cycles

3.8 Goal-Based Optimization

Modern robo-advisors increasingly use goal-based approaches that optimize for specific objectives rather than generic risk-return profiles:

Goal Types

  • Retirement: Maximize terminal wealth subject to minimum income requirements
  • Education: Ensure funds availability at specific future dates
  • Home Purchase: Achieve target amount within time horizon with high probability
  • Wealth Preservation: Maintain purchasing power while minimizing downside risk

3.9 Behavioral Portfolio Theory

Behavioral Portfolio Theory (BPT) recognizes that investors have multiple mental accounts with different risk tolerances:

  • Safety layer: Capital preservation (bonds, cash)
  • Income layer: Stable cash flows (dividend stocks, bonds)
  • Growth layer: Long-term appreciation (growth stocks)
  • Aspiration layer: High-risk, high-reward bets (alternatives, crypto)

3.10 Practical Implementation

Leading robo-advisors implement portfolio optimization with these considerations:

Betterment Approach: Uses Black-Litterman optimization with market equilibrium assumptions, adjusting for investor-specific tax situations and incorporating factor tilts for value and small-cap exposure.
Wealthfront Approach: Risk parity foundation with dynamic tax-loss harvesting, direct indexing for taxable accounts, and automated rebalancing with smart order routing.
Vanguard Approach: Simple market-cap weighted indexing with minimal optimization, relying on broad diversification and ultra-low costs rather than active optimization.

3.11 Optimization Challenges

Estimation Error

Small changes in expected return estimates can dramatically alter optimal portfolios. Robust optimization and Bayesian methods help address this.

Non-Normal Returns

Asset returns exhibit fat tails, skewness, and kurtosis. Advanced optimization incorporates higher moments and copulas.

Transaction Costs

Frequent rebalancing toward theoretical optimal portfolios can destroy value through trading costs. Smart rebalancing uses tolerance bands.

Changing Correlations

Correlations increase during market stress, reducing diversification benefits when needed most. Tail-risk hedging strategies address this.
