Chapter 2: Theories of Consciousness

An overview of the major scientific theories explaining consciousness

2.1 Introduction

Multiple theories attempt to explain how and why consciousness arises from physical processes. Each offers different predictions about where consciousness exists in the brain, what conditions are necessary for consciousness, and how it might be measured. The WIA-CONSCIOUSNESS standard draws on multiple theories to provide a comprehensive measurement framework.

2.2 Integrated Information Theory (IIT)

Core Claim

Consciousness IS integrated information. Any system with non-zero integrated information (Φ) has some degree of consciousness. The quality of experience corresponds to the structure of integrated information.

Key Concepts

  • Φ (Phi): A measure of integrated information—how much a system is more than the sum of its parts
  • Intrinsicality: Information that exists for the system itself, not for an external observer
  • Integration: Information that is unified, not decomposable into independent parts
  • Exclusion: Only the "maximum" of integrated information exists as consciousness
  • Composition: Consciousness has structure built from mechanisms and their relations

Predictions

  • Posterior cortex (especially posterior hot zone) is the main substrate of consciousness
  • Split-brain patients have two separate consciousnesses
  • Cerebellum, despite many neurons, has low Φ and thus minimal contribution to consciousness
  • Simple feedforward networks cannot be conscious regardless of complexity

2.3 Global Neuronal Workspace Theory (GNW/GNWT)

Core Claim

Consciousness arises when information is broadcast globally across a "workspace" of interconnected brain regions, making it available for multiple cognitive processes simultaneously.

Key Concepts

  • Global Ignition: An all-or-none event where local activity suddenly broadcasts globally
  • Workspace: Prefrontal-parietal network that integrates and broadcasts information
  • Broadcast: Information becomes conscious when it's globally available
  • Reportability: Conscious content can, in principle, be reported

Predictions

  • Prefrontal cortex is essential for consciousness
  • Late (>300ms) activity correlates with conscious perception
  • P3b ERP component marks conscious access
  • Ignition follows non-linear dynamics (sudden threshold crossing)

2.4 Higher-Order Theories (HOT)

Core Claim

A mental state is conscious when it is the object of a higher-order representation—a thought or perception about the first-order state.

Key Concepts

  • First-order state: The basic representation (e.g., seeing red)
  • Higher-order state: The meta-representation ("I am seeing red")
  • Prefrontal Role: Prefrontal cortex generates higher-order representations

Predictions

  • Prefrontal damage should impair consciousness of specific contents
  • Consciousness requires meta-cognitive capacity
  • Animals and infants may have reduced consciousness if they lack meta-cognition

2.5 Predictive Processing / Active Inference

Core Claim

The brain constantly generates predictions about sensory input and consciousness involves the modeling of prediction errors and their causes.

Key Concepts

  • Generative Model: The brain's model of the world that generates predictions
  • Prediction Error: Mismatch between prediction and actual input
  • Free Energy: Mathematical quantity that the brain minimizes
  • Active Inference: Acting to make predictions come true

Predictions

  • Consciousness involves high-level predictions about causes of sensory input
  • Attention modulates precision of prediction errors
  • Psychedelic states involve disruption of normal predictive processing

2.6 Attention Schema Theory (AST)

Core Claim

Consciousness is the brain's simplified model of its own attention. We experience awareness because the brain models attention in a schematic way.

Key Concepts

  • Attention: The brain's mechanism for enhancing certain signals
  • Attention Schema: A simplified model of what attention is
  • Awareness: The subjective experience arising from the attention schema

Predictions

  • Damage to attention schema areas impairs awareness but not attention itself
  • AI systems with attention mechanisms could develop proto-awareness if they model their own attention

2.8 The Nature 2025 Adversarial Study

In 2025, a landmark study published in Nature directly tested IIT and GNW predictions using a pre-registered adversarial collaboration:

Study Design:
  • 256 human participants
  • Multi-modal: fMRI, MEG, and intracranial EEG
  • Theory proponents agreed on predictions beforehand
  • Independent consortium analyzed data
Key Results:
  • IIT: 2/3 predictions passed pre-registered threshold
  • GNW: 0/3 predictions passed threshold
  • Both theories were partially challenged by the data
Implications:
  • Neither theory is fully correct as currently formulated
  • Hybrid or revised theories may be needed
  • Posterior cortex importance supported (partial IIT support)
  • Pure prefrontal involvement less supported (GNW challenge)

2.9 Implications for Measurement

The WIA-CONSCIOUSNESS standard incorporates insights from multiple theories:

  • IIT-based measures: Φ estimation, PCI for clinical use
  • GNW-based measures: Global ignition detection, broadcast strength
  • Comprehensive approach: Measure multiple indicators rather than relying on one theory
  • Theory-neutral where possible: Report empirical measures alongside theoretical interpretations

This multi-theory approach ensures that the standard remains useful even as our theoretical understanding evolves.
