CHAPTER THREE

Somatic Cell Nuclear Transfer

Understanding SCNT: The Core of Cloning

Somatic Cell Nuclear Transfer (SCNT) is the scientific technique at the heart of reproductive cloning. This remarkable procedure takes the nucleus from an adult body cell (somatic cell) and transfers it into an egg cell (oocyte) that has had its own nucleus removed. The reconstructed embryo is then stimulated to begin developing as if it were a naturally fertilized egg.

What makes SCNT so extraordinary is that it demonstrates the concept of cellular totipotency—the idea that a differentiated adult cell can be "reprogrammed" back to an embryonic state capable of developing into a complete organism. This was once thought impossible, as scientists believed that once cells became specialized (differentiated), they could never return to a pluripotent state.

The success of Dolly the sheep in 1996 shattered this assumption and opened the door to modern cloning technology. Today, SCNT has been successfully used to clone numerous mammalian species, including dogs, cats, horses, cattle, pigs, and even endangered species.

The Cellular Reprogramming Miracle

How Differentiated Cells Return to Embryonic State

In natural development, a fertilized egg (zygote) contains genetic information from both parents and is totipotent—capable of becoming any cell type in the body. As the embryo develops, cells progressively differentiate, activating some genes while silencing others. A skin cell, for example, has the same DNA as a liver cell, but different sets of genes are active.

This differentiation was long thought to be irreversible. However, SCNT exploits the egg cell's remarkable ability to reprogram adult cell nuclei. The egg cytoplasm contains factors—proteins and RNA molecules—that can erase the epigenetic marks of differentiation and reactivate the embryonic developmental program.

This reprogramming process involves:

  • DNA demethylation—removing chemical tags that silence genes
  • Histone modification—changing protein packaging around DNA
  • Chromatin remodeling—restructuring how DNA is organized in the nucleus
  • Reactivation of pluripotency genes—turning on embryonic development genes
  • Silencing of somatic cell genes—turning off adult cell-specific genes

Oocyte Collection and Preparation

High-quality oocytes (egg cells) are critical for SCNT success. The egg provides not just the machinery for development but also the reprogramming factors needed to transform the adult nucleus back to an embryonic state.

Donor Animal Selection

Oocyte donors must be carefully selected based on several criteria:

  • Age: Young adult females (2-6 years for dogs/cats) with proven fertility
  • Health Status: Excellent general health, no reproductive disorders
  • Genetic Compatibility: Same species, ideally same breed as clone
  • Reproductive History: Normal estrous cycles, good ovarian response
  • Temperament: Calm disposition, tolerant of handling and procedures

Ovarian Stimulation Protocol

To obtain multiple oocytes in a single collection, donor animals undergo hormonal stimulation to induce superovulation—producing more oocytes than would naturally occur in a single cycle.

Canine Ovarian Stimulation Protocol

Day 0-5: Pre-treatment

  • Baseline ultrasound to assess ovarian status
  • Initiate FSH (follicle-stimulating hormone) injections
  • Dose: 2-4 IU per kg body weight, twice daily

Day 6-9: Follicular Development

  • Continue FSH administration
  • Daily ultrasound monitoring of follicle growth
  • Target: Multiple follicles reaching 5-8mm diameter

Day 10: Ovulation Trigger

  • hCG injection (500-1000 IU) to trigger final maturation
  • Schedule collection for 48-72 hours post-hCG

Day 12-13: Oocyte Collection

  • Ultrasound-guided transvaginal follicle aspiration
  • Light sedation and epidural anesthesia
  • Target: 10-30 mature oocytes per collection

Oocyte Maturation and Quality Assessment

Collected oocytes are assessed for maturation stage and quality. For SCNT, metaphase II (MII) oocytes are ideal—these are fully mature eggs that have completed their first meiotic division and arrested at the second division, awaiting fertilization.

Quality Grading Criteria:

Grade Characteristics SCNT Suitability
A (Excellent) Uniform cytoplasm, distinct perivitelline space, single polar body, intact zona pellucida Optimal - use first
B (Good) Minor cytoplasmic irregularities, slight perivitelline debris, intact zona Acceptable - good success rate
C (Fair) Cytoplasmic granulation, perivitelline space irregularities, zona intact Marginal - lower success rate
D (Poor) Severe irregularities, fragmented cytoplasm, zona defects Not recommended

Enucleation: Removing the Egg's Genetic Material

Enucleation is the precise removal of the oocyte's own chromosomes, creating a "blank slate" that will accept the donor cell nucleus. This microsurgical procedure requires exceptional skill and specialized equipment.

Micromanipulation Setup

Enucleation is performed on a micromanipulation workstation consisting of:

  • Inverted Microscope: High-magnification optics with DIC (differential interference contrast)
  • Micromanipulators: Precision instruments controlling micropipettes at micrometer scale
  • Holding Pipette: Stabilizes the oocyte during manipulation (80-100 μm diameter)
  • Enucleation Pipette: Removes nuclear material (15-20 μm diameter)
  • Piezo Actuator: Generates microscopic vibrations to penetrate zona pellucida
  • Temperature Control: Heated stage maintaining 37°C

Step-by-Step Enucleation Protocol

  1. Oocyte Preparation: Transfer MII oocytes to manipulation medium containing cytochalasin B (5 μg/ml), which temporarily disrupts the cytoskeleton and makes the membrane more pliable. Incubate for 10-15 minutes.
  2. Positioning: Using the holding pipette, position the oocyte so the metaphase plate (visible as a slight darkening in the cytoplasm) is at the 3 o'clock position, clearly visible and accessible.
  3. Zona Penetration: Using the piezo-actuated enucleation pipette, create a small opening in the zona pellucida through controlled pulses that mechanically drill through the protein shell.
  4. Membrane Penetration: Advance the pipette through the zona opening and penetrate the oocyte membrane, again using piezo pulses to minimize damage.
  5. Nuclear Aspiration: Apply gentle suction to draw the metaphase plate and a small amount of surrounding cytoplasm into the enucleation pipette. Minimize cytoplasm removal to preserve oocyte viability.
  6. Pipette Withdrawal: Carefully withdraw the enucleation pipette, bringing the nuclear material with it. The oocyte membrane typically self-seals immediately.
  7. Enucleation Confirmation: Examine the removed material under UV light with Hoechst 33342 staining to confirm presence of DNA. Simultaneously verify no fluorescence remains in the enucleated oocyte.
  8. Recovery: Transfer enucleated oocytes to fresh medium without cytochalasin B and allow 30-60 minutes recovery before nuclear transfer.

Critical Success Factor: Enucleation typically takes 15-30 seconds per oocyte for skilled technicians. The entire process requires a delicate balance—removing all nuclear material while preserving oocyte viability and maintaining sufficient cytoplasm with its crucial reprogramming factors.

Nuclear Transfer: The Central Act

With enucleated oocytes prepared, the next step is introducing the donor cell nucleus. Two primary methods exist: direct injection and electrofusion. Electrofusion has become the preferred method in pet cloning due to its higher success rates and better reproducibility.

Donor Cell Preparation

The preserved fibroblast cells are revived from cryostorage and cultured to optimal condition for nuclear transfer. Key preparation steps include:

  • Serum Starvation: Cultured in low-serum medium (0.5% FBS) for 2-5 days to synchronize cells in G0/G1 phase of cell cycle. This quiescent state is optimal for nuclear reprogramming.
  • Cell Selection: Individual cells are isolated, preferably those that are small (10-15 μm diameter) with high nucleus-to-cytoplasm ratio and smooth membranes.
  • Trypsinization: Cells are detached from culture vessels using mild trypsin treatment, then suspended in manipulation medium.
  • Quality Check: Viability staining ensures only healthy cells are selected for transfer.

Electrofusion Protocol

Electrofusion uses electric fields to merge the donor cell with the enucleated oocyte, creating a reconstructed embryo.

Standard Electrofusion Procedure

Step 1: Couplet Formation

  • Using micromanipulator, place donor cell in perivitelline space of enucleated oocyte
  • Position cell in contact with oocyte membrane
  • Ensure tight apposition between cell and oocyte

Step 2: Chamber Transfer

  • Transfer oocyte-cell couplets to fusion chamber
  • Chamber contains mannitol-based fusion medium (0.3 M mannitol, 0.1 mM CaCl₂, 0.1 mM MgSO₄)
  • Position couplets between parallel platinum electrodes (1 mm apart)
  • Orient so donor cell contacts oocyte perpendicular to electrodes

Step 3: Electric Pulse Application

  • Apply alignment pulse: 3-5 V AC for 5-10 seconds (aligns couplet between electrodes)
  • Apply fusion pulse: 1.75 kV/cm DC for 20 μsec, repeated twice with 1-second interval
  • Electric field creates transient pores in membranes (electroporation)
  • Membranes fuse at contact points, integrating donor cell into oocyte

Step 4: Post-Fusion Incubation

  • Immediately transfer to culture medium
  • Incubate 30-60 minutes at 37°C
  • Assess fusion success microscopically (donor cell disappears into oocyte)
  • Fusion efficiency typically 75-85% with optimized parameters

Artificial Activation

Unlike natural fertilization where sperm entry triggers activation, reconstructed embryos must be artificially activated to begin development. Activation involves mimicking the calcium oscillations that naturally occur at fertilization.

Calcium-Based Activation Protocol

The most successful activation method combines calcium ionophore treatment with protein synthesis or degradation inhibitors:

  1. Calcium Ionophore Treatment: Incubate fused embryos in medium containing calcium ionophore (typically ionomycin at 5 μM) for 5 minutes. This triggers a surge of intracellular calcium, initiating the activation cascade.
  2. Wash: Remove ionophore by washing in fresh medium to prevent toxicity.
  3. Cycloheximide Exposure: Culture embryos in medium with cycloheximide (10 μg/ml) for 4 hours. This protein synthesis inhibitor prevents premature exit from meiosis and improves developmental competence.
  4. Recovery: Wash and transfer to standard embryo culture medium.

Alternative Activation Methods

Several other activation protocols exist, each with species-specific advantages:

  • Electrical Activation: DC pulses similar to fusion protocol can trigger activation. Often combined with chemical methods.
  • Ethanol Treatment: Brief exposure to 7% ethanol can activate embryos, particularly effective in bovine cloning.
  • Strontium Chloride: Prolonged exposure (1-2 hours) creates calcium oscillations more similar to natural fertilization.

Success Factors and Optimization

SCNT success depends on numerous variables. Understanding and optimizing these factors is crucial for achieving acceptable success rates.

Key Success Determinants

Factor Optimal Condition Impact on Success
Oocyte Quality A-grade, MII stage Critical - determines reprogramming capacity
Donor Cell Passage Passage 2-5 High - lower passages have better DNA integrity
Cell Cycle Synchronization G0/G1 phase High - affects reprogramming efficiency
Technician Experience 500+ procedures High - skill dramatically affects outcomes
Time from Enucleation to Fusion <2 hours Medium - prolonged delay reduces viability
Culture Conditions Species-optimized media, controlled atmosphere High - supports early development

Species-Specific Challenges

Different species present unique challenges in SCNT:

Dogs: Particularly difficult due to oocytes maturing in the oviduct rather than ovary, requiring surgical collection. Additionally, dogs have unique reproductive physiology with only 1-2 estrous cycles per year, limiting oocyte availability.

Cats: Easier than dogs but coat color can be unpredictable due to X-chromosome inactivation in female cats, particularly affecting calico and tortoiseshell patterns.

Horses: Large animal handling, expensive surrogates, long gestation (11 months), and low oocyte availability present logistical and economic challenges.

Chapter Summary: Somatic Cell Nuclear Transfer is the core technology of cloning, involving oocyte collection, enucleation, nuclear transfer via electrofusion, and artificial activation. Success depends on high-quality oocytes, properly prepared donor cells, skilled technicians, and species-specific protocol optimization. While technically challenging, modern SCNT protocols achieve fusion rates of 75-85% and, when combined with subsequent steps, result in cloning success rates of 20-45% depending on species.
