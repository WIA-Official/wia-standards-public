Chapter 8: Manufacturing & Testing

From Silicon Wafers to Optical Transceivers

SOI Wafer Fabrication

Silicon photonics manufacturing begins with Silicon-on-Insulator (SOI) wafers. The most common process is SOITEC's Smart Cut technology: hydrogen ions are implanted into a donor silicon wafer, creating a weakened layer. After bonding to an oxidized handle wafer and annealing, the donor wafer splits at the implanted layer, leaving a thin crystalline silicon film bonded to the oxide. The result: 220nm ± 5nm silicon on 2-3μm SiO₂ on a 725μm silicon substrate.

Wafer specifications are critical:

  • Silicon thickness uniformity: ±3nm across 300mm wafer to ensure consistent waveguide modes
  • Surface roughness: < 0.2nm RMS to minimize scattering loss
  • Crystalline quality: Low dislocation density (< 10 cm⁻²) for high photodetector performance
  • Oxide uniformity: ±1% to prevent mode leakage into substrate

Photolithography: Defining the Optical Circuit

Waveguide patterns are defined using deep-UV (DUV) lithography at 193nm wavelength, similar to leading-edge CMOS logic. Critical dimensions for silicon photonics (450nm waveguide width, 200nm gaps) are well within the capability of 65nm-node lithography tools. However, optical components have unique requirements:

  • Large-area uniformity: A single photonic die may be 5×5mm or larger, requiring excellent CD control across the field
  • Corner rounding: Sharp 90° corners cause excess scattering loss; optical proximity correction (OPC) pre-distorts mask patterns
  • Layer-to-layer alignment: Stacking Ge photodetectors, metal heaters, and electrical vias demands < 50nm overlay accuracy

Advanced immersion lithography (193nm ArF) enables sub-100nm features for ultra-compact devices, while 248nm KrF lithography suffices for less critical layers, reducing cost.

Etch Processes

After lithography, reactive ion etching (RIE) transfers the pattern into the silicon device layer. Two etch types are common:

Full Etch (220nm deep)

Removes the entire silicon device layer to define strip waveguides, grating couplers, and device isolation. Fluorine-based chemistry (SF₆, CF₄) achieves vertical sidewalls with < 3° taper and < 2nm roughness.

Partial Etch (70-150nm deep)

Creates rib waveguides and other structures requiring a remaining silicon slab. Precise etch depth control (±5nm) is critical for reproducible optical properties.

Post-etch cleaning removes polymer residue and fluorine contamination that would increase optical loss. Thermal oxidation and oxide removal ("sacrificial oxidation") can smooth sidewalls, reducing scattering from 3 dB/cm to < 1 dB/cm.

Germanium Epitaxy and Integration

Germanium photodetectors are grown by selective epitaxy in windows etched through the oxide to expose silicon. The 4% lattice mismatch between Si and Ge is accommodated using a graded Si₁₋ₓGeₓ buffer layer:

  1. Seed layer: Low-temperature Si epitaxy to provide a clean surface
  2. Graded buffer: Si₁₋ₓGeₓ with x increasing from 0 to 1 over 500-1000nm, relaxing lattice mismatch
  3. Ge layer: Pure Ge grown to final thickness (500-1000nm)
  4. Anneal: High-temperature cycling to reduce threading dislocation density

Low dislocation density (< 10⁷ cm⁻²) is essential for low dark current. Selective epitaxy allows Ge only where needed, avoiding contamination of other device areas.

III-V Heterogeneous Integration

While silicon provides passive waveguides and detectors, efficient light sources require III-V semiconductors (InP, GaAs). Two integration approaches exist:

Die-to-Wafer Bonding

Pre-fabricated III-V dies (containing laser structures) are bonded to the silicon photonic wafer using molecular bonding or adhesive bonding. After bonding, the InP substrate is removed, and the III-V mesa is etched to define laser cavities. Light couples evanescently from the III-V gain region into silicon waveguides.

Wafer-to-Wafer Bonding

Entire III-V wafers are bonded to SOI wafers, enabling parallel processing. This improves throughput but requires larger III-V wafer investments and perfect alignment across the full wafer.

Bonding quality is critical—voids or contaminants cause optical loss and thermal resistance. Infrared imaging inspects bond quality before further processing.

Metallization and Electrical Integration

Silicon photonics requires metal layers for:

  • Heaters: TiN or NiCr resistive heaters for thermal tuning
  • Modulators: Aluminum or copper interconnects to p-n junction contacts
  • RF transmission lines: Thick copper for high-speed modulator drivers, photodetector outputs
  • Wirebond pads: Gold pads for electrical I/O connections

Standard back-end-of-line (BEOL) processes from CMOS logic apply, with the constraint that photonic components must be protected from metal deposition and CMP damage.

Packaging and Fiber Attachment

Packaging is often the most expensive and yield-limiting step. The photonic chip must be:

  1. Diced: Sawed along scribe lines without damaging edge couplers (challenging with standard dicing, often requiring laser dicing or cleaving)
  2. Attached to carrier: Die bonding to ceramic or organic substrate with thermal management (epoxy, solder, or eutectic bonding)
  3. Wire bonded: Electrical connections from die to package pins (or flip-chip bonded with solder bumps for high-speed signals)
  4. Fiber aligned and attached: The most critical step—fibers must be positioned to < 1μm precision and permanently fixed with UV epoxy

Automated alignment systems use active feedback: inject light into the fiber, measure output power, maximize by adjusting position, then lock with epoxy cure. This "active alignment" is labor-intensive; emerging "passive alignment" uses precision V-grooves and mechanical stops, trading alignment precision for throughput.

Testing and Characterization

Wafer-Level Testing

Before dicing, optical probe stations characterize devices across the wafer:

  • Insertion loss: Measured using grating couplers and calibrated optical power meters
  • Spectral response: Tunable laser sweeps wavelength while measuring transmission (for filters, resonators)
  • Modulator response: Network analyzer measures S₂₁ (E-O bandwidth), bias sweeps for Vπ
  • Photodetector responsivity: Calibrated optical power in, photocurrent out, across wavelength range

Wafer maps identify known-good die (KGD) for packaging, and process monitoring detects systematic issues.

Reliability Qualification

Long-term reliability is verified through accelerated stress testing:

  • High-Temperature Operating Life (HTOL): 1000-2000 hours at 85°C, measure optical power drift and dark current increase
  • Thermal Cycling: -40°C to +85°C cycles, monitor for package delamination and fiber alignment shift
  • Humidity/Temperature: 85% RH / 85°C to detect moisture ingress and corrosion
  • Vibration: MIL-STD vibration profiles for telecom and automotive applications

Failure analysis of stressed parts provides feedback to improve design and process robustness. Target MTBF is 1-2 million hours for data center modules.

WIA-SEMI-006 Manufacturing Standards: Silicon photonics fabrication facilities shall achieve Cpk > 1.33 for critical dimensions and layer thicknesses. Wafer-level yield targets are > 80% for simple transceivers, > 60% for complex WDM modules. Module packaging yield shall exceed 95% with active fiber alignment.
