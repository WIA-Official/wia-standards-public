Chapter 4: Modulators & Photodetectors

Bridging the Electronic-Photonic Divide

The Challenge of Silicon Modulation

Silicon, despite its excellence as a waveguide material, presents a fundamental challenge: it lacks a linear electro-optic (Pockels) effect due to its centrosymmetric crystal structure. This means we cannot directly modulate light in silicon using the electric field effects that work in lithium niobate or III-V semiconductors. Instead, silicon photonics relies on three mechanisms: free carrier plasma dispersion, thermo-optic effect, and Franz-Keldysh/Quantum-Confined Stark Effect (QCSE) in Ge/SiGe structures.

Carrier Plasma Dispersion Effect

When free carriers (electrons and holes) are injected into or depleted from silicon, the refractive index changes due to the plasma dispersion effect. This index change Δn is proportional to carrier density changes ΔN:

Δn ≈ -8.8×10⁻²²ΔNe - 8.5×10⁻¹⁸(ΔNh)⁰·⁸

The refractive index change is accompanied by absorption change Δα, which limits modulation efficiency. The optimal operating point balances phase shift (proportional to Δn) against optical loss (proportional to Δα).

Mach-Zehnder Modulators (MZM)

The Mach-Zehnder interferometric modulator is the gold standard for high-performance optical modulation. It consists of two arms forming an interferometer—one arm accumulates phase shift via carrier injection/depletion while the other serves as reference. Constructive or destructive interference at the output creates intensity modulation.

PN Junction Phase Shifters

Forward-biased PN junctions inject carriers into the waveguide, changing the refractive index. This provides large phase shifts (π-shift in 1-2 mm length) but is relatively slow (few GHz bandwidth) due to carrier recombination lifetime. Forward-biased modulators are used for thermal tuning and slow control applications.

PN Depletion Mode

Reverse-biased PN junctions deplete carriers from the depletion region. This is much faster (>50 GHz bandwidth possible) because it relies on carrier drift rather than recombination. The trade-off is smaller phase shift per unit length, requiring longer devices (3-5 mm for π-shift). Most high-speed silicon modulators use depletion mode.

Ring Resonator Modulators

Ring resonator modulators offer ultra-compact footprints (10-20 μm radius) and low power consumption. By tuning the ring resonance via carrier injection/depletion, the transmission of the bus waveguide is modulated. Rings can achieve <10 fJ/bit energy efficiency, making them attractive for dense integration. However, they have narrower optical bandwidth (~10 GHz) and are temperature-sensitive, requiring thermal stabilization.

Traveling Wave Modulators

To overcome the bandwidth limitation of lumped-element modulators, traveling-wave designs distribute the capacitance along a transmission line. The RF electrical signal and optical signal co-propagate through the modulator, achieving velocity matching and broader bandwidth. Traveling-wave MZMs have demonstrated >100 GHz bandwidth, enabling 200+ Gbps data rates with advanced modulation formats.

Germanium Photodetectors

Silicon is transparent at telecom wavelengths (1260-1625 nm), so germanium—with a bandgap of 0.66 eV corresponding to 1850 nm cutoff—is integrated for photodetection. Ge photodetectors are grown epitaxially on silicon using a graded SiGe buffer layer to accommodate the 4% lattice mismatch.

Ge Waveguide Photodetectors

The most common design features Ge grown directly on the silicon waveguide. Light evanescently couples from the Si waveguide into the Ge layer, where it is absorbed and generates electron-hole pairs. Key advantages include:

  • High responsivity (> 1 A/W) due to strong absorption coefficient (α > 10⁴ cm⁻¹)
  • Compact length (10-30 μm) for complete absorption
  • High bandwidth (> 60 GHz) with careful design of RC time constant and transit time
  • Low dark current (< 100 nA) with proper passivation

Normal Incidence vs. Waveguide-Integrated

Normal-incidence Ge photodetectors (illuminated from the top surface) offer larger active area and are suitable for free-space coupling or grating coupler integration. Waveguide-integrated designs provide better bandwidth and lower capacitance due to smaller junction area.

Avalanche Photodiodes (APD)

For applications requiring ultra-high sensitivity, Ge/Si avalanche photodiodes use impact ionization to provide internal gain. Silicon's superior avalanche characteristics (low excess noise) are leveraged by absorbing light in Ge and creating avalanche multiplication in a separate Si layer. Ge/Si APDs achieve gain > 10 with noise figure < 5 dB, enabling receiver sensitivities below -30 dBm.

Advanced Modulation Formats

PAM4 Modulation

4-level Pulse Amplitude Modulation (PAM4) encodes 2 bits per symbol by using four distinct optical power levels. This doubles the data rate for a given baud rate but requires approximately 9.5 dB higher SNR than binary NRZ. PAM4 is widely adopted in 400G and 800G data center optics, paired with forward error correction (FEC) to overcome the SNR penalty.

Coherent Modulation

For long-reach applications and ultra-high spectral efficiency, coherent modulation encodes information in both amplitude and phase of the optical carrier. Silicon photonic coherent transceivers integrate I/Q modulators (dual parallel MZMs), local oscillator lasers, and 90° hybrid mixers with balanced photodetectors. Coherent detection enables QPSK, 16-QAM, and 64-QAM modulation formats, achieving > 400 Gbps per wavelength.

Driver Electronics Co-Design

High-speed modulators require sophisticated driver electronics to generate clean, high-amplitude electrical signals. Key considerations include:

  • Impedance Matching: 50Ω transmission lines to minimize reflections
  • Pre-emphasis: Boosting high-frequency components to compensate modulator roll-off
  • Equalization: Digital pre-distortion (DPD) to linearize modulator response
  • Power Efficiency: Class-AB or switched-capacitor drivers for low power consumption

The integration of driver ICs with photonic modulators is a critical packaging challenge. Flip-chip bonding, wire bonding, and through-silicon vias (TSVs) are all employed depending on bandwidth requirements and cost targets.

Testing and Characterization

Thorough characterization of modulators and photodetectors is essential for ensuring performance:

  • S-parameter measurement: Vector network analyzer (VNA) to characterize electrical-to-optical (E-O) and optical-to-electrical (O-E) frequency response
  • Eye diagram analysis: High-speed oscilloscope to assess signal quality, jitter, and noise
  • Bit error rate testing: BERT equipment to measure error rates at various optical powers and data rates
  • Linearity measurement: Multi-tone testing to quantify distortion for analog applications
