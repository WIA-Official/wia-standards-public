Chapter 5: Co-Packaged Optics (CPO)

The Future of High-Bandwidth Computing

The CPO Paradigm Shift

For decades, optical transceivers have been packaged as separate pluggable modules—SFP, QSFP, QSFP28, QSFP-DD—connected to switch ASICs or network processors via electrical SerDes links. This "pluggable optics" model offers flexibility (transceivers can be upgraded independently) but imposes fundamental limitations on power, bandwidth density, and latency. As data rates scale to 800G, 1.6T, and beyond, the electrical interface between ASIC and optics becomes a severe bottleneck.

Co-Packaged Optics (CPO) eliminates this bottleneck by integrating optical transceivers directly into the ASIC package. The photonic chip sits mere millimeters from the switch silicon, connected by ultra-short electrical traces. This proximity reduces SerDes power consumption by 40-50%, increases bandwidth density by 2-3×, and cuts latency by 10-20 nanoseconds—critical advantages for AI training, high-frequency trading, and real-time analytics.

CPO Architecture Variants

On-Package Optics

The photonic IC is mounted on the same package substrate as the switch ASIC, using standard flip-chip or wire-bonding techniques. Optical fibers attach to the package edge or through the substrate. This approach minimizes changes to existing ASIC designs and packaging flows.

Near-Package Optics

Optical transceivers are positioned immediately adjacent to the ASIC package on the PCB, connected via very short (1-2 cm) high-speed electrical traces. This "middle ground" approach offers some CPO benefits with less packaging complexity.

In-Package Optics

The most aggressive integration embeds photonic chiplets within a multi-die package alongside ASIC, HBM memory, and other components. Silicon interposers or organic substrates with optical through-vias route light signals. This enables chip-to-chip optical interconnects within the package—the ultimate goal for exascale computing.

Thermal Management Challenges

Integrating high-power ASICs (300-500W) with temperature-sensitive photonics creates severe thermal management challenges. Lasers shift wavelength by ~0.1 nm/°C, filters drift, and photodetector dark current increases exponentially with temperature. Several strategies address this:

  • Thermal Isolation: Placing photonics on a separate thermal domain with independent cooling
  • Athermal Design: Using temperature-insensitive optical components (athermalized AWGs, broadband filters)
  • Active Temperature Control: Thermoelectric coolers (TECs) or micro-channel liquid cooling
  • Wavelength Stabilization: Feedback loops to lock laser wavelengths and filter passbands

Optical Fiber Attachment

Connecting fibers to CPO packages requires new connector technologies optimized for high density and reliability:

Edge-Mount Fiber Arrays

Fiber ribbon cables attach to polished facets at the package edge via precision V-groove arrays. This approach scales to 32-64 fibers per connector with < 1 dB insertion loss. Expanded-beam optics relax alignment tolerances.

Top-Attach via Grating Couplers

Grating couplers on the photonic chip surface allow fibers to attach vertically through windows in the package lid. This enables wafer-scale testing and field replaceability but increases insertion loss and polarization dependence.

Optical Through-Silicon Vias (Optical TSV)

Micro-holes etched through the silicon substrate act as optical vias, routing light from bottom-side fiber connectors to top-side photonic waveguides. This is still largely in research but promises ultra-dense optical I/O.

Reliability and Lifetime Considerations

One concern with CPO is that optical components cannot be upgraded or replaced independently from the ASIC. This requires:

  • Rigorous Qualification: Accelerated lifetime testing (HTOL, thermal cycling, humidity) to ensure >10 year MTBF
  • Redundancy: Extra optical lanes or wavelengths to tolerate failures
  • Field Monitoring: On-chip sensors for optical power, temperature, and BER to detect degradation
  • Graceful Degradation: Adaptive data rates and FEC to maintain connectivity even as components age

Industry Adoption Roadmap

Major cloud providers and networking vendors are actively developing CPO solutions:

  • Microsoft (Azure): Demonstrated CPO prototypes for 1.6T switch ASICs, targeting 2026 deployment
  • Google: Developed optical circuit switching (OCS) with CPO for data center fabric reconfigurability
  • Meta: Consortium member driving CPO Multi-Source Agreement (MSA) standards
  • Amazon: Custom CPO designs for Nitro networking cards and Graviton processors
  • Broadcom: Integrated CPO with Tomahawk 5 and next-gen switching silicon
  • Intel: Silicon photonics engines designed for CPO integration with Xeon processors

Industry analysts predict CPO will represent 20% of data center optics shipments by 2027, growing to 60%+ by 2030 as 1.6T and 3.2T platforms ramp.

Standards and Interoperability

The Consortium for On-Board Optics (COBO) and Optical Internetworking Forum (OIF) are developing standards for CPO mechanical interfaces, electrical specifications, and thermal management. WIA-SEMI-006 aligns with these efforts while providing additional guidance on photonic chip design, testing protocols, and qualification procedures.

WIA-SEMI-006 CPO Requirements: Co-packaged photonic chips shall operate with < 15W total power for 800G aggregate bandwidth. Thermal dissipation shall not exceed 0.8°C/W to maintain junction temperature below 85°C. Optical connectors shall support > 1000 mating cycles with < 0.5 dB degradation.

Beyond Data Centers: CPO for HPC and AI

While data center networking is the initial driver, CPO has transformative potential for high-performance computing and AI accelerators. GPU-to-GPU interconnects are transitioning from electrical NVLink/Infinity Fabric to optical links supporting 800G-1.6T per direction. CPO enables dense optical interconnect matrices for distributed training across thousands of GPUs, a prerequisite for training trillion-parameter foundation models.
