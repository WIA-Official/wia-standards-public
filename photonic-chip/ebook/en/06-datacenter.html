Chapter 6: Data Center Interconnects

800G, 1.6T, and the Optical Fabric Revolution

The Hyperscale Bandwidth Crisis

Modern data centers are bandwidth-constrained ecosystems. A single rack of AI training servers can generate 100+ Terabits per second of east-west traffic, overwhelming traditional electrical switching fabrics. Video streaming platforms serve petabytes per day. Cloud gaming requires millisecond latencies. And the explosion of large language models (LLMs) demands distributed training across tens of thousands of GPUs.

This insatiable demand is driving data center networks toward optical interconnects at every layer—from chip-to-chip within servers to rack-to-rack spine-leaf networks to data center-to-data center DCI links spanning hundreds of kilometers. Photonic chips are at the heart of this transformation, enabling 800G and 1.6T transceivers that were unimaginable just five years ago.

800G Transceiver Technology

800G optical modules (OSFP, QSFP-DD800) use 8 optical lanes at 100 Gbps each (8×100G). Each lane employs PAM4 modulation running at 53.125 GBaud symbol rate. The photonic chip integrates:

  • 8-channel laser array or tunable laser with WDM multiplexer
  • 8 high-speed Mach-Zehnder modulators (>50 GHz bandwidth)
  • Driver amplifiers and pre-emphasis circuits
  • 8 Germanium photodetectors with transimpedance amplifiers
  • DSP for FEC, equalization, and performance monitoring

Power consumption targets are aggressive: < 15W total for 800G (18.75 mW/Gbps), a 50% improvement over earlier 400G modules through co-design of photonics and electronics.

1.6T: The Next Frontier

Doubling to 1.6T requires either doubling the lane count (16×100G) or doubling the per-lane rate (8×200G). The 8×200G approach is preferred as it maintains compatibility with existing fiber counts and connector form factors. Achieving 200G per lane demands:

  • 106.25 GBaud PAM4: Pushing baud rates near the limits of silicon photonics bandwidth
  • Advanced FEC: Low-overhead codes like RS-KP4 to maintain net coding gain while minimizing latency
  • Linear-Drive Modulators: Segmented MZMs or linearized rings to improve PAM4 eye linearity
  • DSP-Assisted Equalization: Feed-forward and decision-feedback equalization to combat ISI and chromatic dispersion

An alternative path uses coherent modulation (QPSK, 16-QAM) to pack more bits per symbol while maintaining lower baud rates. Coherent 1.6T transceivers are more complex but offer superior performance over longer distances (>10 km).

Optical Network Topologies

Spine-Leaf Architecture

The dominant data center architecture uses a two-tier Clos network: leaf switches connect to servers, while spine switches provide non-blocking inter-leaf connectivity. Every leaf connects to every spine, creating multiple equal-cost paths. As server speeds increase (100G, 200G, 400G NICs), the uplink from leaf to spine must scale proportionally—driving demand for 800G and 1.6T optics.

Dragonfly and Dragonfly+

For extreme-scale HPC clusters, dragonfly topologies use all-to-all connections within groups and sparse global links between groups. This minimizes hop count and latency at the cost of more complex routing. Optical circuit switching allows dynamic reconfiguration of the optical layer to adapt to traffic patterns.

Optical Circuit Switching (OCS)

Rather than packet-switching every frame, OCS establishes semi-permanent lightpaths between endpoints for long-lived flows (e.g., ML training jobs). Silicon photonic switches with nanosecond reconfiguration times enable hybrid packet/circuit architectures that combine flexibility with optical bypass of congested switches.

Fiber Infrastructure

Multi-Mode Fiber (MMF)

OM3/OM4/OM5 multi-mode fiber supports short-reach (< 100m) connections using VCSEL or silicon photonics with multimode coupling. MMF is lower cost and easier to terminate but limits reach and bandwidth-distance product.

Single-Mode Fiber (SMF)

OS1/OS2 single-mode fiber extends reach to 2-10 km for intra-data-center links and 40+ km for metro DCI. All silicon photonics transceivers targeting > 500m use SMF to avoid modal dispersion penalties.

Parallel vs. WDM Optics

Parallel optics use multiple fiber pairs (4, 8, or 16 fibers per direction), with each fiber carrying one lane. WDM optics multiplex multiple wavelengths onto a single fiber pair, reducing fiber count at the expense of more complex photonics. For 800G, both 8-fiber parallel and 4-wavelength WDM approaches exist; 1.6T increasingly favors WDM to manage fiber density.

Forward Error Correction (FEC)

As data rates push the limits of modulator and detector bandwidth, FEC becomes essential to achieve target BER (< 10⁻¹²). Modern data center optics use Reed-Solomon codes:

The trade-off is coding overhead (increasing required baud rate) versus coding gain (improving sensitivity). Sophisticated decoders use soft-decision and iterative decoding to extract maximum performance.

Power and Cooling

A fully-loaded 64-port 800G spine switch consumes 3-5 kW, with 30-40% of that in the optical modules. Cooling at this power density requires precision airflow management or even liquid cooling for next-generation platforms. Silicon photonics benefits from integration with ASIC thermal solutions (heat pipes, vapor chambers, micro-channel cold plates), another advantage of CPO architectures.

WIA-SEMI-006 Compliance: Data center transceivers shall meet IEEE 802.3 standards for 400GBASE, 800GBASE, and emerging 1.6T specifications. Modules shall operate across -5°C to 70°C ambient with < 0.5 dB optical power variation. BER shall remain < 10⁻¹² with FEC enabled under all specified conditions.

Deployment Challenges

  • Cost Scaling: While 800G optics are 2× the speed of 400G, cost targets aim for only 1.5× the price—requiring manufacturing efficiency improvements
  • Interoperability: Multi-vendor networks demand rigorous testing of gray optics, FEC modes, and link training
  • Lifecycle Management: Data centers expect 10+ year optics lifetimes, necessitating robust qualification and field monitoring
  • Fiber Capacity: Legacy data centers with 12-fiber MTP connectors cannot support 16-fiber 1.6T modules without infrastructure upgrades
