Chapter 3: WDM & Multiplexing Technology

Scaling Bandwidth Through Wavelength Parallelism

Introduction to Wavelength Division Multiplexing

Wavelength Division Multiplexing (WDM) is the optical equivalent of frequency division multiplexing in RF communications. By transmitting multiple independent data streams on different wavelength channels through the same fiber or waveguide, WDM multiplies the aggregate bandwidth without requiring additional physical media. A single fiber carrying 80 wavelength channels at 100 Gbps each delivers 8 Tbps total capacity—bandwidth that would require 80 separate fibers without WDM.

In photonic integrated circuits, WDM enables massively parallel optical interconnects within a compact footprint. Rather than routing dozens of individual waveguides across a chip, a single waveguide can carry all channels, dramatically simplifying layout and reducing device area. Silicon photonics platforms are particularly well-suited for WDM integration due to the availability of compact, low-loss wavelength filters and multiplexers.

ITU-T Frequency Grid Standards

The International Telecommunication Union (ITU-T) has standardized WDM channel spacing in Recommendation G.694.1 to ensure interoperability between vendors. The standard defines a fixed-frequency grid anchored at 193.1 THz (approximately 1552.52 nm) with channel spacings of:

Grid Type Spacing (GHz) Spacing (nm @ 1550nm) Typical Use Case
Dense WDM (DWDM) 12.5 / 25 / 50 / 100 0.1 / 0.2 / 0.4 / 0.8 Long-haul, metro networks
Coarse WDM (CWDM) ~2500 (20 nm) 20 Short-reach enterprise
LAN WDM (LWDM) ~430 (1310nm band) 4.5 Data center intra-rack

Most photonic chip WDM systems use 100 GHz channel spacing as a balance between spectral efficiency and filter requirements. At 100 Gbps per channel with 100 GHz spacing, spectral efficiency is 1 bps/Hz—a practical target for intensity modulation direct detection (IMDD) systems.

Arrayed Waveguide Gratings (AWG)

The arrayed waveguide grating is the workhorse wavelength multiplexer/demultiplexer in silicon photonics. An AWG consists of three sections:

  1. Input Free Propagation Region (FPR): A star coupler that diffracts input light into multiple waveguides
  2. Arrayed Waveguide Section: An array of waveguides with linearly increasing lengths, creating wavelength-dependent phase shifts
  3. Output FPR: A second star coupler where different wavelengths converge at different spatial positions

The operating principle relies on the array creating a wavelength-dependent phase difference between adjacent waveguides. Light of different wavelengths diffracts at different angles in the output FPR, focusing onto different output waveguides. The channel spacing Δλ and free spectral range (FSR) are determined by the array design:

Δλ = (λ² / n_g) × (d / L) × (1 / m)

where n_g is the group index, d is the waveguide spacing in the FPR, L is the FPR length, and m is the diffraction order.

AWG Design Considerations

  • Insertion Loss: Typical values of 2-4 dB for silicon AWGs, dominated by star coupler loss and waveguide propagation
  • Crosstalk: -25 to -30 dB adjacent channel isolation required for most applications, achieved through careful apodization
  • Passband Shape: Flattened passbands increase tolerance to laser wavelength drift and temperature variations
  • Polarization Dependence: TE and TM modes have different group indices, causing wavelength shift; compensated with birefringence management
  • Temperature Sensitivity: Silicon's high thermo-optic coefficient (1.86×10⁻⁴ /K) causes ~0.1 nm/°C wavelength drift, requiring thermal stabilization

Ring Resonator-Based Multiplexers

An alternative to AWGs is cascaded ring resonator filters. Each ring is designed to resonate at one WDM channel wavelength, selectively dropping that channel to a dedicated output port. Compared to AWGs, ring-based multiplexers offer:

  • Smaller footprint (rings can be as compact as 5 μm radius)
  • Potential for reconfigurability via thermal or carrier tuning
  • Scalability to large channel counts through cascading

However, rings have narrower passbands, higher sensitivity to fabrication variations, and accumulated loss for channels passing through many stages. For high-port-count static multiplexers, AWGs remain the preferred choice.

Echelle Gratings

Echelle gratings are another wavelength dispersive element used in silicon photonics. Similar to AWGs, echelle gratings diffract different wavelengths to different angles, but use a concave grating reflector rather than an arrayed waveguide section. The grating consists of facets etched into silicon, with facet spacing determining the dispersion.

Echelle gratings can achieve lower loss than AWGs (insertion loss < 2 dB) and are more compact, but are more sensitive to fabrication imperfections and have higher polarization dependence. They are particularly attractive for wavelength monitoring and optical spectrum analyzers on chip.

WDM System Architectures

Broadcast-and-Select

In this architecture, all wavelength channels are broadcast to all receivers, and each receiver uses a tunable filter to select its desired channel. This provides flexibility and reconfigurability, but wastes optical power and requires complex tunable filters at every receiver.

Route-and-Select

Here, a wavelength-selective switch (WSS) routes each wavelength channel to a specific output port. This is more power-efficient than broadcast-and-select and is the dominant architecture for optical cross-connects and reconfigurable add-drop multiplexers (ROADMs).

Fixed Wavelength Assignment

The simplest approach assigns each transmitter-receiver pair a dedicated wavelength channel using fixed multiplexers/demultiplexers. This is common in point-to-point links and passive optical networks where reconfigurability is not required.

Thermal Wavelength Management

Silicon's strong temperature dependence presents both a challenge and an opportunity. The challenge is that wavelength drift of 0.1 nm/°C means a ±5°C temperature variation causes ±0.5 nm wavelength shift—significant compared to a 100 GHz (0.8 nm) channel spacing. The opportunity is that resistive heaters can precisely tune resonances and filter passbands.

Thermal Stabilization Techniques

  • Athermalization: Combining materials with opposite thermo-optic coefficients (e.g., silicon with polymers) to create temperature-insensitive devices
  • Active Thermal Control: Using integrated heaters and temperature sensors with feedback loops to stabilize device temperature
  • Wavelength Locking: Monitoring the output spectrum and adjusting laser wavelengths or filter tuning to maintain channel alignment

Laser Sources for WDM

WDM systems require multiple single-wavelength laser sources at precisely defined wavelengths. Several approaches exist:

Discrete Laser Arrays

Individual DFB or DBR lasers, each fabricated to emit at a specific ITU channel, are assembled into arrays and coupled to the photonic chip. This is the most mature approach but requires careful wavelength binning and complex packaging.

Comb Sources

Optical frequency combs generate many equally-spaced wavelengths simultaneously. Techniques include:

  • Mode-locked lasers producing picosecond pulses with comb-like spectra
  • Cascaded four-wave mixing in nonlinear waveguides
  • Microresonator-based soliton combs (Kerr combs)

While promising, comb sources for WDM are still largely in research phase, with challenges in per-channel power, stability, and integration.

Tunable Lasers

A single broadly tunable laser can sequentially emit at each WDM wavelength, useful for testing and flexible networking. External cavity tunable lasers (ECDLs) with on-chip Vernier ring filters or MEMS mirrors achieve tuning ranges exceeding 100 nm.

Advanced Modulation for WDM

As WDM systems scale to hundreds of channels, spectral efficiency becomes critical. Advanced modulation formats pack more bits per symbol, increasing the data rate within a fixed channel bandwidth:

Modulation Format Bits/Symbol Spectral Efficiency OSNR Requirement
NRZ (On-Off Keying) 1 1 bps/Hz ~15 dB
PAM4 2 2 bps/Hz ~21 dB
PAM8 3 3 bps/Hz ~27 dB
16-QAM 4 4 bps/Hz ~20 dB (coherent)

The trade-off is that higher-order modulation requires better signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and more sophisticated digital signal processing (DSP). For data center interconnects within 2 km, PAM4 modulation at 100 GHz spacing is the current sweet spot.

WIA-SEMI-006 Specification: WDM photonic chips shall support ITU-T G.694.1 grid spacing of 100 GHz (0.8 nm) or finer. Multiplexer insertion loss shall not exceed 4 dB, and adjacent channel crosstalk shall be below -25 dB. Temperature stabilization or athermal design is required for ±10°C operating range.

Future WDM Scaling

The roadmap for WDM in photonic chips targets ever-increasing channel counts and aggregate bandwidth:

  • 2025: 32-64 channels × 100 Gbps = 3.2-6.4 Tbps per fiber
  • 2027: 64-128 channels × 200 Gbps = 12.8-25.6 Tbps per fiber
  • 2030: 128+ channels × 400 Gbps = 51+ Tbps per fiber

Achieving these targets requires advances in low-loss silicon nitride waveguides, heterogeneous integration of III-V gain media, advanced modulation with coherent detection, and sophisticated wavelength stabilization. The convergence of WDM with space-division multiplexing (multiple fibers or spatial modes) and polarization multiplexing promises to sustain bandwidth growth for decades to come.
