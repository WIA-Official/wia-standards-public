Chapter 4: Ice Sheet and Glacier Dynamics

Frozen Giants in Flux: The Greenland and Antarctic ice sheets contain enough frozen water to raise global sea levels by over 65 meters. Understanding their dynamics, mass balance, and vulnerability to climate change is critical for predicting future sea level rise and planning coastal adaptation strategies.

4.1 Introduction to Ice Sheet Dynamics

Ice sheets are vast masses of glacial ice covering more than 50,000 square kilometers. Only two ice sheets exist today: Greenland and Antarctica. These frozen reservoirs contain 99% of Earth's freshwater ice and exert profound influence on global sea level, ocean circulation, and climate. Ice sheets are dynamic systems, constantly gaining mass through snowfall and losing mass through melting and iceberg calving.

The behavior of ice sheets is governed by complex physical processes including snow accumulation, ice flow, basal sliding, ice shelf dynamics, and interactions with the atmosphere and ocean. Understanding these processes and how they respond to climate change is essential for projecting future ice sheet contributions to sea level rise—one of the most significant long-term consequences of global warming.

4.2 Mass Balance Concepts

Ice sheet mass balance refers to the difference between mass gains (primarily snowfall) and mass losses (surface melting, ice discharge, and sublimation). When gains exceed losses, the ice sheet grows and sea level falls; when losses exceed gains, the ice sheet shrinks and sea level rises. Contemporary observations show both Greenland and Antarctica are losing mass, contributing to accelerating sea level rise.

430 Gt/year Combined annual ice loss from Greenland and Antarctica (2010-2020) - equivalent to raising global sea level by ~1.2mm per year

Mass Balance Components

Ice sheet mass balance can be measured using three independent methods: the mass budget method (comparing inputs and outputs), altimetry (measuring elevation changes), and gravimetry (measuring mass changes directly). Each method has strengths and uncertainties, but all three show consistent patterns of accelerating mass loss from both ice sheets.

Mass Inputs

  • Snowfall accumulation
  • Refreezing of meltwater
  • Deposition of rime/frost
  • Total input: ~500-600 Gt/year (Greenland)

Mass Outputs

  • Surface meltwater runoff
  • Ice discharge (calving)
  • Sublimation
  • Basal melting (ice shelves)

Measurement Methods

  • GRACE satellites (gravity)
  • Radar/laser altimetry
  • Mass budget calculations
  • GPS surface velocity

Current Trends

  • Greenland: -280 Gt/year
  • Antarctica: -150 Gt/year
  • Both accelerating
  • Contribution: ~1.2mm/year SLR

4.3 Greenland Ice Sheet

The Greenland Ice Sheet covers 1.7 million square kilometers and contains enough ice to raise global sea level by 7.4 meters if completely melted. The ice sheet has transitioned from approximate balance in the 1990s to sustained mass loss in the 21st century, driven by both increased surface melting and accelerated ice discharge from outlet glaciers.

Surface Melt Processes

Greenland's surface melt has increased dramatically, with extreme melt events becoming more frequent and intense. In July 2012, surface melting was detected across approximately 97% of the ice sheet—an unprecedented event. The melt season has lengthened by 3-4 days per decade, and the area experiencing melting has expanded to higher elevations as temperatures rise.

Greenland Surface Melt Cascade

  1. Solar Absorption: Dark bare ice exposed as snow melts, reducing albedo from 85% to 50%
  2. Enhanced Melting: Darker surfaces absorb more radiation, creating positive feedback
  3. Meltwater Formation: Up to 500 Gt of surface meltwater generated annually
  4. Meltwater Routing: Water flows through supraglacial streams, crevasses, moulins to ice base
  5. Basal Lubrication: Meltwater at ice base enhances sliding (10-100% velocity increase)
  6. Crevasse Propagation: Hydrofracture extends crevasses, potentially destabilizing ice
  7. Runoff to Ocean: ~400 Gt/year reaches ocean, contributing to sea level rise

Greenland Outlet Glaciers

Approximately 300 outlet glaciers drain ice from Greenland's interior to the ocean. Several of these glaciers have undergone dramatic acceleration, thinning, and retreat in recent decades. Jakobshavn Isbræ, Greenland's largest outlet glacier, has doubled its speed and retreated 15 kilometers since 2000, while Petermann Glacier lost massive ice shelf sections in 2010 and 2012.

4.4 Antarctic Ice Sheet

The Antarctic Ice Sheet is Earth's largest ice mass, containing 26.5 million cubic kilometers of ice—enough to raise global sea level by approximately 58 meters. The ice sheet comprises two distinct sections: the larger East Antarctic Ice Sheet (EAIS), which is relatively stable, and the smaller West Antarctic Ice Sheet (WAIS), which is particularly vulnerable to ocean warming and may be approaching critical stability thresholds.

58 meters Potential global sea level rise if the entire Antarctic Ice Sheet melted - would flood every coastal city on Earth

West Antarctic Ice Sheet Vulnerability

The West Antarctic Ice Sheet rests on bedrock that is largely below sea level, making it a marine ice sheet vulnerable to Marine Ice Sheet Instability (MISI). Large portions of WAIS, particularly in the Amundsen Sea sector, are grounded on bedrock that slopes inland and downward. This configuration creates a positive feedback: as the grounding line retreats, ice flux increases, causing more thinning and further retreat—a potentially irreversible process.

Marine Ice Sheet Instability (MISI)

The Process: Once retreat begins on reverse-sloped bedrock, it becomes self-sustaining and potentially unstoppable with current technology.

Current Status: Thwaites and Pine Island glaciers show evidence that MISI may have been triggered; retreat is accelerating.

Timeline: Complete WAIS collapse could occur over centuries to millennia, but acceleration is possible.

Consequences: 3.3 meters of sea level rise would displace hundreds of millions of people globally.

Tipping Point: May have already passed critical threshold in Amundsen Sea sector; limited ability to reverse.

4.5 Ice Shelves and Buttressing

Ice shelves are floating extensions of ice sheets that form where glaciers and ice streams flow into the ocean. Although floating ice shelves don't directly contribute to sea level rise when they melt or calve (they're already displacing water), they play a critical role in restraining the flow of grounded ice behind them—a process called buttressing. When ice shelves thin or collapse, upstream ice accelerates, increasing ice discharge to the ocean.

Ice Shelf Collapse Events

Several Antarctic Peninsula ice shelves have collapsed in recent decades as regional temperatures increased. The Larsen B Ice Shelf collapse in 2002 was particularly dramatic: approximately 3,250 square kilometers of ice disintegrated in just 35 days. Following collapse, glaciers that had been restrained by the ice shelf accelerated by factors of 2-8, demonstrating the critical buttressing role of ice shelves.

Larsen B Ice Shelf Collapse (2002)

Pre-collapse: Ice shelf existed for at least 10,000 years, ~3,250 km² in area

Collapse trigger: Surface melt pools, hydrofracture, structural weakening from warming

Collapse duration: 35 days (January 31 - March 7, 2002)

Immediate effect: ~720 billion tonnes of ice released as icebergs

Glacier response: Tributary glaciers accelerated 2-8x within months

Long-term impact: Sustained increased ice discharge contributing to sea level rise

Warning sign: Demonstrated vulnerability of ice shelves to atmospheric warming

4.6 Glacier Calving Processes

Calving is the process by which ice breaks off from glacier termini or ice shelf fronts, forming icebergs. This process represents a major component of ice mass loss in Antarctica and from Greenland's marine-terminating glaciers. Calving rates have increased dramatically at many glaciers, contributing significantly to sea level rise and sometimes triggering glacier acceleration through loss of buttressing.

Calving Mechanisms

Tensional Fracture

  • Ice stretching over bedrock bumps
  • Crevasse formation and propagation
  • Common at fast-flowing glaciers
  • Forms tabular icebergs (Antarctica)

Buoyancy-Driven

  • Ice flotation at grounding line
  • Underlain by warm ocean water
  • Critical at marine glaciers
  • Can trigger rapid retreat

Hydrofracture

  • Surface meltwater fills crevasses
  • Water pressure propagates fractures
  • Can trigger ice shelf collapse
  • Increasingly important as warming continues

Basal Melting

  • Ocean water melts ice from below
  • Thins ice shelves/glacier tongues
  • Reduces structural integrity
  • Major driver in Antarctica

4.7 Sea Level Contribution

Ice sheets and glaciers are the largest potential contributors to future sea level rise. Over the 20th century, ice sheets contributed modestly to sea level rise compared to thermal expansion and mountain glacier melt. However, ice sheet contributions have accelerated dramatically in the 21st century and are projected to dominate sea level rise in coming decades and centuries.

1.2 mm/year Current rate of sea level rise from Greenland and Antarctic ice sheets combined (2010-2020) - accelerating from 0.3 mm/year in 1990s

Uncertainty in Projections

Projections of ice sheet contributions to sea level rise carry substantial uncertainty, particularly for Antarctica. The largest uncertainties stem from incomplete understanding of ice shelf stability, Marine Ice Sheet Instability dynamics, and ice cliff failure processes. Recent studies suggest that ice sheet contributions could be significantly larger than previously estimated if these instability mechanisms are triggered.

High-End Sea Level Rise Scenarios

Likely Range (RCP8.5 by 2100): 60-110 cm total sea level rise, with ice sheets contributing 20-60 cm

Low-Likelihood, High-Impact: 150-250 cm possible if Marine Ice Sheet Instability and ice cliff failure occur

Greenland Threshold: Above 1.5-2°C warming, irreversible long-term mass loss committed (multi-century timescale)

West Antarctica: May have crossed irreversible threshold in Amundsen Sea sector; 3m committed over centuries

Multi-Meter Rise: Continued emissions could commit the world to 10+ meters of sea level rise over coming centuries

4.8 Tipping Points and Irreversibility

Ice sheet behavior includes potential tipping points—thresholds beyond which changes become self-sustaining and effectively irreversible on human timescales. Once triggered, these processes could continue for centuries or millennia even if temperatures stabilize or decline. Understanding and avoiding these tipping points is critical for long-term climate stability.

The "Doomsday Glacier" - Thwaites

Thwaites Glacier in West Antarctica has been called the "Doomsday Glacier" due to its vulnerability and potential to trigger broader West Antarctic Ice Sheet collapse. The glacier is approximately the size of Florida and contains enough ice to raise global sea level by 0.6 meters. More importantly, Thwaites acts as a stabilizing anchor for surrounding ice; its collapse could trigger a chain reaction leading to 3+ meters of sea level rise over centuries.

Thwaites Glacier Crisis Indicators

  • Grounding Line Retreat: Retreating at 0.5-1 km/year in some sectors; now on retrograde slope (MISI conditions)
  • Ice Shelf Disintegration: Eastern ice shelf developing major fractures; could collapse within years to decade
  • Acceleration: Ice velocity increased 30-40% since 2000; further acceleration likely after ice shelf loss
  • Thinning Rates: 1-4 meters per year surface lowering; highest in grounding zone
  • Basal Melting: Warm ocean water accessing cavity at 100-200 m/year melt rates
  • Vulnerability: Bedrock geometry predisposes glacier to runaway retreat once MISI triggered
  • Research Findings: International Thwaites Glacier Collaboration (2018-2023) confirms high vulnerability

4.9 Monitoring and Research

Understanding ice sheet dynamics requires comprehensive monitoring from multiple platforms including satellites, aircraft, ground-based stations, and ocean measurements. Recent technological advances have revolutionized ice sheet observation, revealing acceleration in mass loss and identifying critical vulnerabilities. Continued monitoring is essential for refining sea level projections and informing adaptation planning.

Key Monitoring Technologies

Satellite Gravimetry

  • GRACE/GRACE-FO missions
  • Directly measure mass changes
  • Monthly global coverage
  • ±25-50 Gt accuracy

  • CryoSat-2, ICESat-2

  • cm-scale precision
  • Detailed spatial coverage
