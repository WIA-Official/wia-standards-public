Chapter 6: Governance and Treaties

Cooperative Governance in Extreme Environments: Polar regions are governed by unique international frameworks that balance national sovereignty, scientific cooperation, environmental protection, and Indigenous rights. These governance systems provide models for managing shared global resources and addressing transboundary environmental challenges.

6.1 The Antarctic Treaty System

The Antarctic Treaty System (ATS) is one of the most successful international agreements in history, establishing Antarctica as a continent dedicated to peace and science for over 60 years. Signed in 1959 and entered into force in 1961, the Treaty froze territorial claims, prohibited military activity, promoted scientific cooperation, and established a framework for environmental protection that has evolved to meet changing challenges.

54 Nations Signatories to the Antarctic Treaty - representing two-thirds of the world's population in a unique model of international cooperation

Antarctic Treaty System Components

The Antarctic Treaty System has evolved through additional agreements and protocols to address emerging challenges. These instruments create a comprehensive framework for Antarctic governance, environmental protection, and resource management. Together, they constitute one of the most robust international environmental regimes in existence.

1961: Antarctic Treaty Enters Force

Original 12 signatories establish peaceful, scientific cooperation framework. Treaty applies to area south of 60°S, covering 10% of Earth's surface.

1972: Convention for Conservation of Antarctic Seals (CCAS)

Establishes catch limits and protected species before commercial sealing begins. Precautionary approach; no commercial sealing has occurred.

1980: Convention on Conservation of Antarctic Marine Living Resources (CCAMLR)

Ecosystem-based management of Southern Ocean fisheries. Includes krill, fish, and dependent species. 27 members managing world's largest marine protected areas.

1988: Convention on Regulation of Antarctic Mineral Resource Activities (CRAMRA)

Negotiated but never entered force. Abandoned in favor of mining prohibition in Madrid Protocol.

1991: Protocol on Environmental Protection (Madrid Protocol)

Designates Antarctica as "natural reserve, devoted to peace and science." Prohibits mining for at least 50 years (until 2048). Comprehensive environmental protection.

6.2 The Madrid Protocol

The Protocol on Environmental Protection to the Antarctic Treaty, known as the Madrid Protocol, entered into force in 1998 and provides comprehensive environmental protection for Antarctica. The Protocol designates Antarctica as a "natural reserve, devoted to peace and science" and establishes environmental principles and procedures that all Antarctic activities must follow.

Madrid Protocol Key Elements

Mining Prohibition

  • Bans all mineral resource activities
  • Prohibition valid until at least 2048
  • Modification requires unanimous consent
  • Strong protection but reviewable

Environmental Impact Assessment

  • All activities require prior assessment
  • Three levels: preliminary, initial, comprehensive
  • Public review of major impacts
  • Precautionary approach required

Protected Areas

  • Antarctic Specially Protected Areas (ASPAs)
  • Antarctic Specially Managed Areas (ASMAs)
  • 73 ASPAs, 8 ASMAs designated
  • Protect scientific, environmental, historic values

Six Annexes

  • I: Environmental impact assessment
  • II: Conservation of fauna and flora
  • III: Waste disposal and management
  • IV: Marine pollution prevention
  • V: Protected areas
  • VI: Liability (not yet in force)

Committee for Environmental Protection (CEP)

The Committee for Environmental Protection advises the Antarctic Treaty Consultative Meeting on environmental issues. Established under the Madrid Protocol, the CEP reviews environmental impact assessments, monitors compliance with environmental provisions, develops recommendations for protected areas, and provides scientific and technical advice on environmental protection.

6.3 Convention on Conservation of Antarctic Marine Living Resources

The Convention on Conservation of Antarctic Marine Living Resources (CCAMLR), which entered into force in 1982, takes an ecosystem-based approach to managing Southern Ocean resources. Unlike traditional fisheries management focused solely on target species, CCAMLR considers impacts on dependent and related species, ensuring conservation of the entire Antarctic marine ecosystem.

5.5 million km² Area of marine protected areas established by CCAMLR in the Ross Sea and South Orkney Islands - world's largest MPAs

CCAMLR Management Approach

CCAMLR's ecosystem approach considers not only harvested species but also predator-prey relationships, ecosystem structure, and environmental variability. Conservation measures include catch limits, seasonal closures, gear restrictions, and protected areas. All decisions require consensus among the 27 member countries, which can make conservation progress slow but ensures broad support.

Managed Fishery Annual Catch Limit Key Conservation Measures Status
Antarctic Krill 5.61 million tonnes (precautionary limit) Catch limits, real-time management, observer program Catch ~400,000 tonnes; well below limit
Patagonian Toothfish Varies by area (~15,000 tonnes total) Vessel licensing, catch documentation, bycatch limits Sustainable; IUU fishing greatly reduced
Antarctic Toothfish ~3,000 tonnes Research fishery, strict monitoring Developing sustainable management

Marine Protected Areas

CCAMLR has established some of the world's largest marine protected areas in Antarctic waters. The Ross Sea Region MPA (2016) covers 1.55 million square kilometers with 1.12 million km² fully protected. The South Orkney Islands Southern Shelf MPA (2009) covers 94,000 km². Additional MPAs have been proposed for the Weddell Sea, East Antarctica, and Antarctic Peninsula but require consensus approval.

Challenges in Antarctic Marine Protection

  • Consensus Requirement: All CCAMLR decisions require unanimous agreement; single nation can block MPAs
  • Fishing Interests: Some members prioritize fishing access over conservation, limiting MPA expansion
  • Climate Change: Shifting species distributions complicate fixed spatial management approaches
  • Illegal Fishing: IUU fishing reduced but not eliminated; toothfish poaching continues
  • Monitoring Challenges: Vast, remote areas difficult to monitor; enforcement capacity limited

6.4 Arctic Governance

Unlike Antarctica, the Arctic has no comprehensive treaty system. Instead, Arctic governance involves overlapping layers of international law, regional cooperation, national sovereignty, and Indigenous governance. The Arctic Ocean is subject to the Law of the Sea, while Arctic lands are sovereign territory of eight nations. This creates a more complex governance landscape than Antarctica's unified treaty system.

6.5 The Arctic Council

The Arctic Council, established in 1996, is the premier forum for Arctic cooperation. It includes the eight Arctic states as members and six Indigenous organizations as Permanent Participants with full consultation rights. The Council addresses environmental protection and sustainable development but explicitly excludes military security issues. Despite lacking binding legal authority, the Council has facilitated important agreements and assessments.

Arctic Council Structure and Achievements

Members

  • 8 Arctic States (voting members)
  • 6 Indigenous Permanent Participants
  • 39 Observer states/organizations
  • Consensus-based decision making

Working Groups

  • Arctic Monitoring and Assessment Programme (AMAP)
  • Conservation of Arctic Flora and Fauna (CAFF)
  • Emergency Prevention, Preparedness and Response (EPPR)
  • Protection of Arctic Marine Environment (PAME)
  • Sustainable Development Working Group (SDWG)
  • Arctic Contaminants Action Program (ACAP)

Binding Agreements

  • Search and Rescue (2011)
  • Oil Pollution Response (2013)
  • Scientific Cooperation (2017)
  • First legally binding instruments

Major Assessments

  • Arctic Climate Impact Assessment (2004)
  • Arctic Biodiversity Assessment (2013)
  • Snow, Water, Ice and Permafrost (2011, 2021)
  • Arctic Ocean Review (2013)

Limitations of Arctic Council

While the Arctic Council facilitates cooperation and produces valuable assessments, it has significant limitations. The Council has no enforcement power, cannot address military security issues, and operates by consensus which can limit ambitious action. It produces recommendations rather than binding obligations. As Arctic geopolitical tensions increase and resource competition intensifies, questions arise about whether current governance structures are adequate for emerging challenges.

Arctic Council Geopolitical Challenges (2022-Present)

Russian Invasion of Ukraine: Seven Arctic states paused participation in Council activities involving Russia (March 2022)

Chairship Challenges: Norway assumed 2023-2025 chairship under difficult circumstances; resumed limited cooperation

Observer Tensions: China's growing Arctic interest and observer status creates strategic concerns for some members

Future Uncertainty: Questions about Council's ability to function during geopolitical tensions; need for institutional resilience

6.6 Law of the Sea and Arctic Ocean

The United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS) provides the legal framework for Arctic Ocean governance. Arctic coastal states have sovereignty over territorial seas (12 nautical miles), sovereign rights in exclusive economic zones (200 nm), and potential extended continental shelf claims beyond 200 nm. These provisions are leading to overlapping claims and boundary negotiations in the Arctic Ocean.

Central Arctic Ocean Fisheries Agreement

In 2018, a landmark agreement was reached to prevent unregulated fishing in the Central Arctic Ocean—a 2.8 million square kilometer area beyond national jurisdiction that is becoming ice-free in summer. The agreement, signed by the Arctic five coastal states plus China, EU, Iceland, Japan, and South Korea, establishes a precautionary approach: no commercial fishing until scientific research demonstrates sustainability and appropriate management is in place.

6.7 Protected Areas

Both Arctic and Antarctic regions include networks of protected areas designed to conserve biodiversity, protect ecosystems, and preserve areas for scientific research. These protected areas range from small sites protecting specific features to vast marine protected areas covering millions of square kilometers. Effective protection requires adequate designation, management, enforcement, and monitoring.

Antarctic Protected Areas

Antarctic Specially Protected Areas (ASPAs)

  • 73 sites designated
  • Protect unique ecosystems, species
  • Scientific, historic, wilderness values
  • Entry requires permit

Antarctic Specially Managed Areas (ASMAs)

  • 8 sites designated
  • Manage complex activities
  • Coordinate multiple users
  • Larger than ASPAs typically

Marine Protected Areas

Historic Sites

  • Protect exploration heritage
  • Managed under Madrid Protocol
  • Tourism impacts concern

Arctic Protected Areas

Arctic protected areas are established under national legislation of Arctic states rather than through a unified international system. Coverage and protection levels vary substantially between countries. Overall, approximately 11% of the Arctic is under some form of protection, though protection quality and enforcement vary. Major protected areas include national parks, nature reserves, and Indigenous-managed protected areas.

6.8 Tourism Management

Polar tourism has grown dramatically in recent decades, particularly in Antarctica and Arctic cruise destinations. Tourism provides economic benefits and raises awareness but also presents environmental and safety risks. Effective tourism management requires balancing access with protection, ensuring visitor safety, and minimizing environmental impacts.

74,400 Tourist visits to Antarctica in 2019-20 season (pre-COVID) - up from 6,700 in 1992-93, raising conservation concerns

International Association of Antarctica Tour Operators (IAATO)

IAATO is a self-regulatory industry organization founded in 1991 that coordinates Antarctic tourism and sets operating standards. Members commit to guidelines that exceed legal requirements, including visitor limits per site, no-disturbance rules for wildlife, waste management protocols, and expedition staff training. While voluntary, IAATO standards significantly influence Antarctic tourism practices.

Tourism Challenges and Management

Challenge Impacts Management Approach Effectiveness
Wildlife Disturbance Breeding disruption, stress, habituation Distance limits, visitor numbers, seasonal closures Moderate - compliance varies
Waste and Pollution Litter, sewage, fuel spills Pack it in/pack it out, treatment requirements Generally good for regulated operators

Cumulative Effects Ecosystem-level impacts from repeated visitation Environmental monitoring, adaptive management Limited - long-term impacts poorly understood

Emerging Tourism Concerns

  • Growth Trajectory: Antarctic tourism increased 11-fold since 1992; COVID temporarily paused but recovery underway
  • Larger Vessels: Ships carrying 500+ passengers create concentrated impacts and emergency response challenges
  • New Activities: Kayaking, camping, diving, mountaineering increase diversity of impacts
  • Year-Round Access: Arctic tourism now possible year-round; shoulder seasons emerging
  • Private Expeditions: Non-commercial trips outside IAATO framework harder to regulate
  • Climate Change: Increased access to previously unreachable areas; changing ice conditions create new risks

6.9 Future Governance Challenges

Polar governance faces significant challenges from climate change, geopolitical tensions, resource competition, and increasing human activity. Current frameworks may not be adequate for emerging challenges. Strengthening polar governance requires enhanced cooperation, adaptive mechanisms, adequate resources for monitoring and enforcement, and meaningful inclusion of all stakeholders including Indigenous peoples.

Priority Governance Needs

Climate Adaptation

  • Governance frameworks for rapid change
  • Adaptive management approaches
  • Integration of climate projections
  • Flexible protected area systems

Enhanced Protection

  • Expansion of protected area networks
  • Stricter pollution controls
  • Ecosystem-based management
  • Improved enforcement

Indigenous Inclusion

  • Meaningful participation in decisions
  • Co-management arrangements
  • Integration of traditional knowledge
  • Respect for Indigenous rights

International Cooperation

  • Maintain cooperation despite tensions
  • Strengthen existing frameworks
  • Develop new agreements as needed
  • Share monitoring data and research
