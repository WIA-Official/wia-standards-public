Chapter 2: Neural Signal Acquisition

Learning Objectives: This chapter covers the methods and technologies for acquiring neural signals, including electroencephalography (EEG), electrocorticography (ECoG), and intracortical recordings. You will learn the WIA-BCI specifications for each acquisition method and the technical requirements for certified devices.

2.1 Fundamentals of Neural Signal Generation

Neural signals originate from the electrochemical activity of neurons. When neurons communicate, they generate electrical potentials that can be detected by appropriately positioned sensors. Understanding these signals is essential for designing effective BCI systems.

The brain contains approximately 86 billion neurons, each capable of forming thousands of synaptic connections. When a neuron fires an action potential, it creates a brief electrical event lasting about 1 millisecond. The collective activity of neuronal populations generates larger-scale electrical fields that can be measured at varying distances from the source.

Types of Neural Signals

  • Action Potentials (Spikes): Individual neuron firing events, detectable only with invasive electrodes
  • Local Field Potentials: Summed activity of nearby neuronal populations
  • Cortical Surface Potentials: Electrical activity recorded from the brain surface
  • Scalp Potentials: Attenuated signals reaching the scalp surface

2.2 Non-Invasive Acquisition Methods

Electroencephalography (EEG)

EEG is the most widely used non-invasive BCI signal acquisition method. It records electrical activity from electrodes placed on the scalp surface, capturing the summed postsynaptic potentials of large neuronal populations.

WIA-BCI EEG SPECIFICATIONS
===========================

ELECTRODE_REQUIREMENTS:
  material: Ag/AgCl or dry active electrodes
  impedance: < 10 kΩ (wet), < 100 kΩ (dry)
  placement: 10-20 or 10-10 system
  minimum_channels: 8 (Level 1), 32 (Level 2)

AMPLIFIER_SPECIFICATIONS:
  input_impedance: > 100 MΩ
  common_mode_rejection: > 100 dB
  noise_level: < 1 μVrms
  bandwidth: 0.1 - 100 Hz (configurable)
  sampling_rate: ≥ 250 Hz (Level 1), ≥ 500 Hz (Level 2)

ADC_REQUIREMENTS:
  resolution: ≥ 16 bits
  dynamic_range: ± 400 μV typical
  anti-aliasing: -40 dB at Nyquist

SAFETY_REQUIREMENTS:
  patient_leakage: < 10 μA
  isolation: 4000V minimum
  electromagnetic_compatibility: IEC 60601-1-2

Functional Near-Infrared Spectroscopy (fNIRS)

fNIRS measures changes in blood oxygenation in the cerebral cortex using near-infrared light. It provides complementary information to EEG and can be combined for hybrid BCI systems.

Light Sources

Wavelengths: 690-850 nm, Power: < 2 mW/cm²

Detectors

Silicon photodiodes or avalanche photodiodes

Source-Detector Distance

25-40 mm for cortical sensitivity

Temporal Resolution

2.3 Partially Invasive Methods

Electrocorticography (ECoG)

ECoG records electrical activity directly from the surface of the cerebral cortex using electrode grids or strips placed beneath the skull. This approach provides higher signal quality than EEG while avoiding penetration of brain tissue.

ECoG Advantages: ECoG signals have 5-10 times higher amplitude than scalp EEG, broader bandwidth (up to 500 Hz), higher spatial resolution (approximately 1 cm), and significantly better signal-to-noise ratio. These characteristics make ECoG particularly suitable for high-performance BCI applications. 
WIA-BCI ECoG SPECIFICATIONS
============================

ELECTRODE_ARRAYS:
  material: platinum-iridium or stainless steel
  electrode_diameter: 2-4 mm
  inter-electrode_spacing: 5-10 mm
  array_configurations: strips (1x4 to 1x8), grids (4x4 to 8x8)
  total_channels: 16-256

BIOCOMPATIBILITY:
  materials: ISO 10993 compliant
  sterilization: gamma or ethylene oxide
  implant_duration: temporary (< 30 days) or chronic (> 30 days)

SIGNAL_SPECIFICATIONS:
  amplitude_range: 50-200 μV typical
  bandwidth: 0.1-500 Hz
  sampling_rate: ≥ 1000 Hz
  resolution: ≥ 16 bits

SURGICAL_REQUIREMENTS:
  craniotomy_size: minimized, application-specific
  placement_verification: intraoperative imaging required
  infection_prevention: strict sterile protocols

Epidural Recording

Epidural electrodes are placed between the skull and the dura mater, offering a less invasive alternative to subdural ECoG while still providing improved signal quality over scalp EEG. This approach is gaining attention for chronic BCI applications due to reduced surgical risk.

2.4 Invasive Recording Methods

Microelectrode Arrays

Intracortical microelectrode arrays penetrate the brain tissue to record from individual neurons or small neuronal populations. These devices provide the highest quality neural signals but require neurosurgical implantation and carry associated risks.

Safety Considerations: Invasive neural interfaces require Level 4 WIA-BCI certification, comprehensive biocompatibility testing, long-term stability assessment, and strict surgical protocols. These devices should only be considered when non-invasive alternatives are insufficient for the clinical need.

ARRAY_TYPES:
  utah_array:
    electrode_count: 96-128

    electrode_count: 16-256

    electrode_count: 64-1024

  spike_detection: SNR ≥ 3:1

2.5 Signal Quality Assessment

Ensuring adequate signal quality is critical for BCI performance. The WIA-BCI standard defines specific metrics and methods for signal quality assessment.

Quality Metrics

  • Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR): Ratio of signal power to noise power, measured in dB
  • Artifact Contamination: Percentage of data affected by movement, EMG, or other artifacts
  • Electrode Impedance: Indicator of electrode-tissue interface quality
  • Channel Correlation: Detection of bridged or shorted electrodes
  • Spectral Quality: Presence of expected physiological rhythms
# Signal Quality Assessment Algorithm

def assess_signal_quality(raw_data, sampling_rate):
    """
    WIA-BCI compliant signal quality assessment
    
    Returns quality score: 0-100
    Certification requires score ≥ 70
    """
    
    # Calculate noise floor (baseline period)
    noise_rms = calculate_rms(raw_data[baseline_period])
    
    # Calculate signal power (active period)
    signal_power = calculate_power(raw_data[active_period])
    
    # SNR calculation
    snr_db = 10 * log10(signal_power / (noise_rms ** 2))
    
    # Artifact detection
    artifact_ratio = detect_artifacts(raw_data) / len(raw_data)
    
    # Spectral analysis
    psd = compute_psd(raw_data, sampling_rate)
    spectral_quality = assess_spectral_features(psd)
    
    # Composite score
    quality_score = (
        0.4 * normalize_snr(snr_db) +
        0.3 * (1 - artifact_ratio) * 100 +
        0.3 * spectral_quality
    )
    
    return quality_score

2.6 Hardware Requirements

WIA-BCI Certified Hardware Categories

All neural signal acquisition hardware must meet category-specific requirements to receive WIA-BCI certification:

  • Category A: Consumer wellness devices (Level 1)
  • Category B: Research and clinical investigation devices (Level 1-2)
  • Category C: Medical diagnostic devices (Level 2)
  • Category D: Therapeutic devices with recording (Level 2-4)
  • Category E: Implantable neural interfaces (Level 3-4)

2.7 Data Formats and Interoperability

To ensure interoperability between different BCI systems and analysis tools, the WIA-BCI standard specifies required data formats and metadata standards.

WIA-NEURAL-CONTAINER (WNC) FORMAT
==================================

FILE_STRUCTURE:
  header:
    version: "WNC-1.0"
    device_info: {...}
    subject_info: {...}  # anonymized
    recording_info: {...}
    channel_info: [...]
    
  data:
    format: float32 or int16
    layout: channels x samples
    timestamps: microsecond precision
    
  annotations:
    events: [...]
    markers: [...]
    artifacts: [...]
    
  metadata:
    acquisition_software: "..."
    processing_history: [...]
    wia_certification: "..."

REQUIRED_METADATA:
  - sampling_rate (Hz)
  - channel_count
  - electrode_locations
  - reference_scheme
  - filter_settings
  - hardware_serial_number

Proper signal acquisition is essential for achieving the performance requirements outlined in subsequent chapters.
