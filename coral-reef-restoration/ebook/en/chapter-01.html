"Benefit All Humanity"

Coral reefs protect coastlines, provide food, support livelihoods, and harbor biodiversity—serving all humanity and countless marine species.

1.1 The Rainforests of the Sea

Coral reefs are often called the "rainforests of the sea" because they harbor an extraordinary concentration of biodiversity. While occupying less than 1% of the ocean floor, coral reefs support approximately 25% of all marine species, making them among the most biodiverse ecosystems on Earth.

These remarkable structures are built by tiny animals called coral polyps, which secrete calcium carbonate to form intricate limestone skeletons. Over thousands of years, generations of corals create massive reef structures that become home to millions of organisms.

1.3 The WIA-REEF Standard

The WIA Coral Reef Restoration Standard (WIA-REEF) provides a comprehensive framework for monitoring, protecting, and restoring coral reef ecosystems. This standard addresses the urgent need for standardized approaches to reef conservation across the globe.

1.5 Why Standards Matter

The Standardization Challenge

Without standardized approaches, reef conservation efforts worldwide struggle to share data, compare results, and coordinate action. The WIA-REEF standard creates a common language for reef restoration.

1.5.1 Benefits of Standardization

  • Data Interoperability: Share monitoring data across organizations
  • Comparable Metrics: Assess reef health using consistent measures
  • Best Practice Sharing: Learn from successful restoration projects
  • Funding Efficiency: Allocate resources based on verified data
  • Global Coordination: Unite conservation efforts worldwide
