Chapter 1: Understanding Autoimmunity

Autoimmune diseases occur when the immune system, designed to protect the body from foreign invaders, mistakenly attacks healthy cells and tissues. This chapter provides the foundational understanding of autoimmune mechanisms necessary for implementing WIA-AUTOIMMUNE standards.

1.1 The Immune System Overview

The human immune system is a complex network of cells, tissues, and organs that work together to defend the body against pathogens. Understanding normal immune function is essential for comprehending how autoimmunity develops and manifests.

1.2 Self-Tolerance Mechanisms

The immune system normally maintains tolerance to self-antigens through multiple mechanisms. Failure of these tolerance mechanisms leads to autoimmune disease:

Central Tolerance

  • Thymic Selection: T cells that strongly react to self-antigens are eliminated in the thymus (negative selection)
  • Bone Marrow Selection: B cells producing self-reactive antibodies undergo receptor editing or deletion
  • AIRE Expression: Autoimmune regulator gene enables expression of tissue-specific antigens in thymus

Peripheral Tolerance

  • Regulatory T Cells: Tregs suppress autoreactive lymphocytes in peripheral tissues
  • Anergy: Autoreactive cells become functionally unresponsive without co-stimulation
  • Immune Privilege: Certain tissues (eye, brain, testes) have limited immune access

1.4 Risk Factors and Triggers

Genetic Susceptibility

Genetic factors account for approximately 30-50% of autoimmune disease risk. Key genetic associations include:

  • HLA Genes: Strong associations with specific HLA alleles (e.g., HLA-DR4 in RA, HLA-B27 in spondyloarthritis)
  • Non-HLA Genes: PTPN22, CTLA-4, IL-2RA, STAT4 variants affect immune regulation
  • X Chromosome: May explain female predominance (X-inactivation, gene dosage)

1.6 Epidemiology

Global Autoimmune Disease Burden
================================
Total Affected: 400-600 million globally
Prevalence: 5-8% of population (varies by region)

Top Conditions by Prevalence:
1. Hashimoto's Thyroiditis: 5% of population
2. Rheumatoid Arthritis: 0.5-1%
3. Psoriasis: 2-3%
4. Type 1 Diabetes: 0.3-0.5%
5. Inflammatory Bowel Disease: 0.3%
6. Multiple Sclerosis: 0.1%
7. Systemic Lupus Erythematosus: 0.05%

Demographics:
- Female:Male ratio: 2.7:1 overall
- Peak onset: 15-45 years for most conditions
- Geographic variation: MS more common in higher latitudes

1.7 The Importance of Standardization

Given the complexity and heterogeneity of autoimmune diseases, standardized approaches to diagnosis, treatment, and monitoring are essential for improving patient outcomes and enabling meaningful research across populations and healthcare systems.
