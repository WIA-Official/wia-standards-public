Chapter 2: Architecture and Infrastructure

Learning Objectives: Master the technical architecture required for asteroid mining operations, understand spacecraft design requirements, and learn infrastructure standards for extraction facilities and communication systems.

2.1 System Architecture Overview

The WIA-ASTEROID_MINING standard defines a comprehensive system architecture that integrates multiple components into a cohesive operational framework. This architecture supports the full lifecycle of asteroid mining operations, from initial prospecting through resource extraction and processing to final delivery.

The architecture follows a modular design philosophy, enabling operators to scale their capabilities incrementally as operations expand. Core modules can be combined in various configurations to support different mission profiles, from small-scale prospecting missions to full industrial extraction operations.

2.2 Spacecraft Design Requirements

2.2.1 Prospecting Spacecraft

Prospecting spacecraft are designed to survey potential mining targets and characterize their composition, structure, and resource content. The standard specifies minimum requirements for prospecting vehicles:

  • Propulsion: Ion propulsion or chemical systems capable of delta-v requirements for target accessibility class
  • Power: Solar arrays sized for operational distance, with battery backup for eclipse periods
  • Sensors: Multi-spectral imaging, RADAR, thermal imaging, and mass spectrometer capabilities
  • Communication: X-band or Ka-band high-gain antenna with minimum 1 kbps data rate at maximum range
  • Autonomy: On-board navigation and hazard avoidance for operations with communication delays
Parameter Minimum Requirement Recommended
Operational Life 2 years 5+ years
Payload Mass 50 kg 200+ kg

2.2.2 Extraction Platforms

Extraction platforms are the workhorses of asteroid mining operations. These systems must be capable of anchoring to asteroid surfaces, excavating or processing material, and operating in the harsh space environment for extended periods. The standard defines several platform categories:

Light Extraction Platform (LEP): Designed for small-scale operations targeting near-Earth asteroids. Typical mass under 1000 kg, capable of processing 10-100 tonnes of regolith per mission. Suitable for water extraction from C-type asteroids.

Medium Extraction Platform (MEP): Industrial-scale systems for sustained operations. Mass range 1-10 tonnes, processing capacity of 100-1000 tonnes. Includes on-board beneficiation capabilities for preliminary resource concentration.

Heavy Extraction Platform (HEP): Full mining installations for major asteroid deposits. Mass exceeding 10 tonnes, often assembled in space from modular components. Capable of continuous operation with processing rates exceeding 1000 tonnes per year.

2.4 Processing Facility Architecture

Processing facilities transform raw asteroid material into usable resources. The standard defines requirements for both in-situ processing (on or near the asteroid) and centralized processing (at dedicated orbital facilities).

2.4.1 In-Situ Processing Units

In-situ processing reduces the mass that must be transported, improving mission economics. Key systems include:

  • Thermal Processing: Solar concentrators or electrical heating for volatile extraction from regolith
  • Magnetic Separation: Isolating metallic particles from silicate matrix
  • Electrostatic Separation: Sorting particles by conductivity and charge
  • Crushing and Grinding: Size reduction for improved processing efficiency
  • Electrolysis: Splitting water into hydrogen and oxygen for propellant

2.4.2 Orbital Processing Stations

For large-scale operations, dedicated orbital processing stations provide economies of scale. These facilities receive raw or partially processed material from multiple mining operations and perform advanced refining. Station requirements include:

  • Microgravity or artificial gravity processing environments
  • Vacuum and pressurized processing chambers
  • High-temperature metallurgical capabilities
  • Product storage and packaging systems
  • Crew habitation modules (for human-tended operations)
Design Philosophy: Processing facility architecture emphasizes modularity and redundancy. Critical systems must have backup capabilities, and modules should be replaceable without interrupting overall operations.

2.7 Power Systems

Power generation and distribution must be sized for the operational environment. Near-Earth operations can rely primarily on solar power, while more distant operations may require nuclear sources:

  • Photovoltaic Arrays: Primary power for operations within 3 AU of the Sun. Efficiency degrades with solar distance.
  • Solar Concentrators: Enhanced solar collection for high-power processing applications.
  • Radioisotope Generators: Reliable power for distant operations or extended eclipse periods.
  • Nuclear Fission Reactors: High-power option for industrial-scale operations beyond Mars orbit.
  • Energy Storage: Battery or fuel cell systems for eclipse operations and peak loads.

2.8 Autonomous Operations

Communication delays make real-time control impractical for most asteroid mining operations. The standard requires significant autonomous capability:

  • Autonomous navigation and hazard avoidance
  • Self-directed resource prospecting and extraction
  • Automated system health monitoring and maintenance
  • Fault detection, isolation, and recovery (FDIR)
  • Dynamic mission replanning based on discovered conditions
Autonomy Levels: The standard defines five autonomy levels (AL1-AL5), from basic teleoperation support (AL1) to fully autonomous operations capable of completing entire mission phases without ground intervention (AL5).

2.9 Chapter Summary

This chapter has detailed the technical architecture required for asteroid mining operations under the WIA-ASTEROID_MINING standard. From spacecraft design through processing facilities and communication systems, each component must meet specific requirements while integrating into the overall operational framework.

The modular architecture philosophy enables scalable deployment, allowing operators to begin with small prospecting missions and expand to industrial-scale extraction as capabilities mature. Emphasis on autonomy addresses the communication challenges inherent in deep-space operations.
