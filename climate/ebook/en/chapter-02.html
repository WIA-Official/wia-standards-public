Chapter 2: Current Challenges in Climate Data

Overview: Six unsolved frictions — inventory fragmentation, observation latency, scenario opacity, verification gaps, double-counting in voluntary carbon markets, and scope-3 dark matter — define the problem space WIA-CLIMATE-022 addresses.

2.1 Inventory fragmentation: one company, fifteen datasets

A typical multinational reports its corporate emissions to CDP, the SBTi monitoring portal, an ESG ratings agency, the SEC (where applicable), the EU CSRD digital tagging system, the Korean K-ETS exchange, and three internal ERP systems. Each consumes a slightly different cut of the same primary data. A 2024 study by the Carbon Disclosure Project found that 41% of reporting companies had material discrepancies (> 5% Scope 1) between two of their own filings in the same year — not because of fraud, but because the source data was extracted, transformed, and re-keyed seven times along the way.

2.2 Observation latency: WMO is fast, the consumer chain is not

The WMO Global Telecommunication System and its successor WIS 2.0 deliver radiosonde, surface synop, satellite radiance, and ocean buoy data within minutes of acquisition. Yet downstream, the path from a WIS 2.0 broker subscription to a usable analysis-ready cube on a corporate ESG dashboard typically involves five custom adapters, two manually maintained format conversions, and one CRON job. The result: a typhoon track is on the WMO broker at T+3 minutes but on the corporate climate-risk dashboard at T+72 hours.

2.3 Scenario opacity: which SSP, which run, which institution?

CMIP6 publishes thousands of model runs across 49 institutions. A user asking "what does SSP2-4.5 say about 2050 precipitation in Seoul?" must:

  • Pick which model(s) to use (single-model bias vs multi-model mean).
  • Pick which ensemble realization (r1i1p1f1 vs r10i1p1f1).
  • Bias-correct against an observational baseline (ERA5, CHIRPS, JRA-3Q).
  • Downscale (CORDEX, NEX-GDDP, ISIMIP).
  • Quote uncertainty (5–95% range, not a point estimate).

None of these decisions is wrong, but none is automatic — and most ESG dashboards present a single number with no provenance. The IFRS S2 standard explicitly requires "physical and transition risk scenario analysis" but is silent on which scenario, leaving practitioners to reinvent the wheel.

2.4 The verification gap

ISO 14064-3 verification requires the auditor to traverse from the disclosed total back to primary records — invoices, meter readings, fuel deliveries. In practice, that traversal happens through Excel workbooks emailed between the operator and the verifier. A 2023 IETA survey reported that 68% of verifiers spend the majority of their engagement reconciling spreadsheets, not assessing methodology. A standard wire format with cryptographic provenance turns weeks of reconciliation into hours of audit.

2.5 Double-counting in voluntary carbon markets

The same metric ton of CO₂ removed by a Brazilian reforestation project can be claimed by the host country in its NDC, by a corporate buyer of voluntary credits, and by a Verra registry retirement record — unless an Article 6.2 corresponding adjustment is applied. The 2023 ICVCM Core Carbon Principles and 2024 VCMI Claims Code now require traceable corresponding-adjustment flags, but the data plumbing to do so reliably across registries is still ad hoc.

2.6 Scope-3 dark matter

For most non-extractive companies, Scope 3 (value-chain) emissions are 70-95% of the corporate footprint, but disclosed Scope 3 totals carry estimated uncertainties of ±40-200% (CDP 2024). The reasons are knowable: supplier emissions are estimated by spend-based factors, transport emissions by distance approximations, and product use-phase emissions by lifecycle assumptions. ISO 14083:2023 (transport) and the GHG Protocol Land Sector and Removals Guidance (2024) provide methodology, but the data exchange to populate those methodologies is precisely what WIA-CLIMATE-022 standardises.

Chapter Summary

Key Takeaways

  1. One company files essentially the same data fifteen times, and they don't quite match.
  2. Observation networks are fast; the path from WIS 2.0 to a usable corporate dashboard is slow.
  3. CMIP6 is rich but unstructured for non-specialist consumption.
  4. Verification is bottlenecked by spreadsheet reconciliation, not methodology.
  5. Voluntary carbon markets need machine-readable corresponding-adjustment trails.
  6. Scope 3 carries 70-95% of corporate footprints with ±40-200% uncertainty — the standard's biggest target.
