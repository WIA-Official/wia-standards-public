Chapter 7

Regulations and Airspace Management

弘益人間 (홍익인간) - Responsible Operation Serving Public Safety

7.1 FAA Part 107 - United States Regulations

The Federal Aviation Administration's Part 107 regulation, effective August 29, 2016, established the framework for commercial drone operations in the United States. Understanding these regulations is mandatory for legal commercial operations and exemplifies the 弘益人間 principle by balancing innovation with public safety.

Part 107 Requirements

To operate commercially under Part 107, remote pilots must:

  • Be at least 16 years old
  • Pass the FAA Aeronautical Knowledge Test (recurrent every 24 months)
  • Pass TSA vetting
  • Register their drone(s) with the FAA if over 0.55 lbs (250g)

The knowledge test covers airspace classifications, weather, aircraft loading and performance, emergency procedures, crew resource management, radio communication, airport operations, and maintenance. The test has 60 multiple-choice questions with a 70% passing score required.

Operational Limitations

Part 107 establishes specific operational rules:

  • Visual Line of Sight (VLOS): The remote pilot must be able to see the drone with unaided vision (corrective lenses permitted)
  • Altitude Limit: 400 feet AGL (Above Ground Level) or within 400 feet of a structure
  • Speed Limit: 100 mph (87 knots) maximum groundspeed
  • Daylight Operations: Official sunrise to sunset, plus 30 minutes civil twilight with anti-collision lighting
  • Right-of-Way: Must yield to all manned aircraft
  • People Restrictions: Cannot fly over people unless they're directly participating, under covered structure, or using drone with appropriate certification
  • Moving Vehicles: Cannot operate from a moving vehicle except over sparsely populated areas

Part 107 Waivers

Operators can request waivers from certain operational rules by demonstrating equivalent level of safety. Common waivers include:

  • Night operations: Allowing flight outside daylight hours (now baseline authorized with anti-collision lighting and recency training)
  • Operations over people: Enabling various degrees of operation over non-participants
  • Beyond Visual Line of Sight (BVLOS): Operating without direct visual contact with the drone
  • Operations from moving vehicles: For applications like pipeline or power line inspection

BVLOS waivers are particularly challenging to obtain, requiring demonstrated safety through redundant systems, observers, detect-and-avoid technology, or restricted airspace. Only a few hundred BVLOS waivers have been granted despite thousands of applications.

7.2 EASA Drone Regulations - European Union

The European Union Aviation Safety Agency (EASA) implemented harmonized drone regulations across EU member states effective December 31, 2020. The Open, Specific, and Certified categories provide a risk-based framework.

Open Category

The Open category covers low-risk operations requiring no authorization from aviation authorities. It's divided into three subcategories:

A1 (Low risk): Drones under 250g or C0 class, can fly over uninvolved people. Requires online training and test.

A2 (Medium risk): Drones under 2kg or C2 class, must maintain 30m distance from uninvolved people (5m with low-speed mode). Requires theoretical knowledge and self-practical training.

A3 (Higher risk): Drones under 25kg or C3 class, must maintain 150m distance from residential, commercial, industrial, or recreational areas. Requires online training.

All Open category operations are limited to 120m altitude, VLOS, and require the operator to register as a UAS operator.

Specific Category

The Specific category addresses medium-risk operations requiring authorization based on operational risk assessment. Operators submit an Operational Authorization request demonstrating how risks will be mitigated.

The SORA (Specific Operations Risk Assessment) methodology helps operators and authorities evaluate risk across dimensions including:

  • Ground risk (probability and severity of collision with people on ground)
  • Air risk (probability and severity of mid-air collision)
  • Strategic mitigations (reducing exposure to risk)
  • Tactical mitigations (reducing probability of collision)

Standard Scenarios (STS) are predefined operation types (like STS-01 for VLOS over controlled ground area, STS-02 for BVLOS over controlled ground area) that streamline authorization for common use cases.

Certified Category

The Certified category addresses high-risk operations requiring drone certification, operator certification, and licensed remote pilots. This includes:

  • Transport of people
  • Transport of dangerous goods
  • Operations over assemblies of people where no tactical mitigation exists

Passenger-carrying drones (eVTOL air taxis) will fall under this category, requiring certification similar to traditional aircraft.

7.3 International Regulations and ICAO Standards

The International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO), a UN specialized agency with 193 member states, develops global standards for aviation safety, security, efficiency, and environmental protection.

ICAO Remotely Piloted Aircraft Systems (RPAS) Framework

ICAO's Annex 19 addresses safety management for RPAS, while various documents provide guidance on:

  • Classification and certification of RPAS
  • Licensing of remote pilots and crew
  • Operational procedures and rules of the air
  • Detect and Avoid (DAA) requirements
  • Communication, Navigation, and Surveillance (CNS) standards

While ICAO standards aren't directly enforceable (member states implement them through national regulations), they provide harmonization enabling international operations and trade.

Regional Variations

Canada (Transport Canada) requires Basic or Advanced RPAS Pilot Certificates depending on operation type, with different requirements for drones under/over 250g and operations near people or controlled airspace.

Australia (CASA) distinguishes between excluded category (< 250g, recreational), recreational flyers (< 2kg), and Remote Pilot License holders for commercial operations.

China (CAAC) has comprehensive regulations requiring licensing for drones over 7kg or operations requiring beyond visual line of sight. China's domestic market is the world's largest, with DJI holding dominant position.

Japan revised its Aviation Act in 2022, requiring registration and licensing for drones over 100g, with specific rules for urban operations and Level 4 (BVLOS over populated areas) authorizations.

7.4 Airspace Classifications and Restrictions

Understanding airspace structure is critical for safe and legal operations. The 弘益人間 principle demands that drone operators respect airspace rules protecting manned aviation and sensitive locations.

US Airspace Classes

The FAA classifies airspace from A through G:

  • Class A: 18,000 ft MSL to FL600, IFR only (drones never operate here)
  • Class B: Major airports, typically surface to 10,000 ft MSL, requires ATC authorization
  • Class C: Busy airports, typically surface to 4,000 ft AGL, requires ATC authorization
  • Class D: Towered airports, typically surface to 2,500 ft AGL, requires ATC authorization
  • Class E: Controlled airspace not A/B/C/D, starts at various altitudes, authorization needed if below 400 ft in surface area
  • Class G: Uncontrolled airspace, no ATC authorization required

LAANC - Low Altitude Authorization and Notification Capability

LAANC is an automated system providing near-real-time authorization to fly in controlled airspace (Class B, C, D, E surface areas). Rather than waiting days or weeks for manual authorization, LAANC approvals arrive in seconds to minutes.

LAANC defines UAS Facility Maps showing altitude ceilings in different areas. Apps like AirMap, Aloft, and Kittyhawk integrate LAANC, allowing operators to check restrictions, request authorization, and receive approval without leaving the app.

No-Fly Zones and Restricted Areas

Various locations are prohibited or restricted for drone operations:

  • Airports: Variable restrictions extending 5+ miles from runway ends
  • National Security Areas: Washington DC has a permanent flight restriction radius
  • Military Bases: Prohibited areas with severe penalties for violation
  • Wildfires: Temporary Flight Restrictions (TFRs) to allow firefighting aircraft
  • Stadiums: One-hour before through one-hour after major sporting events
  • National Parks: Generally prohibited by park service regulations
  • Power Plants: Critical infrastructure protection zones

7.5 Remote ID and Tracking Requirements

Remote ID is the digital license plate for drones, broadcasting identification and location information that authorities and other airspace users can receive. Following 弘익人間, it balances accountability with operational freedom.

FAA Remote ID Rule

Effective September 16, 2023, the FAA requires all drones operated under Part 107 to broadcast Remote ID unless:

  • Operating in an FAA-recognized Identification Area (FRIA)
  • Drone was manufactured before March 16, 2019 and weighs under 250g
  • Operating under Part 89 limitations for unmanned aircraft

Remote ID broadcasts:

  • Drone serial number or session ID
  • Drone position (latitude, longitude, altitude, velocity)
  • Control station location
  • Emergency status indication
  • Time mark

Two implementation methods exist:

Standard Remote ID: Built into the drone, broadcasts both drone and control station locations via Bluetooth and WiFi.

Broadcast Remote ID: External module that broadcasts drone location only (control station location optional). Retrofits older drones for compliance.

European Union Remote ID

EU regulations require drones in Open category (A2 and A3 subcategories) to have direct remote identification allowing broadcast of registration number and real-time location.

Network remote identification sends information to a network service accessible by authorities, enabling tracking without requiring line-of-sight reception.

Privacy and Security Considerations

Remote ID raises privacy concerns as anyone with appropriate receiver can detect drone operations. However, it doesn't identify the operator by name - only the registration number, which authorities can link to operator records.

The 弘益人間 principle suggests this represents appropriate balance: accountability for safety and security while not enabling mass surveillance. Law enforcement and security personnel benefit from identifying unauthorized drones near critical infrastructure or emergency operations.

7.6 UTM - Unmanned Aircraft System Traffic Management

As drone operations scale to thousands of simultaneous flights, traditional air traffic control approaches don't scale. UTM (Unmanned Aircraft System Traffic Management) provides the framework for managing low-altitude airspace.

NASA and FAA UTM Concept

NASA developed the UTM concept through extensive research and testing. The system operates separately from traditional ATC but interfaces at boundaries. Core functions include:

  • Registration and identification: Database of all drones and operators
  • Flight planning and authorization: Operators submit flight plans, system checks conflicts with airspace restrictions and other flights
  • Strategic deconfliction: Preventing conflicts before flight through planning
  • Tactical deconfliction: Real-time collision avoidance if aircraft paths converge unexpectedly
  • Tracking and monitoring: Real-time position reporting and conformance monitoring
  • Contingency management: Handling lost link, low battery, or other emergencies

U-Space in Europe

U-Space is Europe's UTM equivalent, providing services supporting safe drone integration:

  • U1 Services: E-registration, e-identification, geofencing
  • U2 Services: Flight planning, tracking, authorization
  • U3 Services: Advanced operations including traffic management and collaborative interfaces
  • U4 Services: Full integration with manned aviation

U-Space became mandatory in designated areas throughout the EU as of January 2023, with certified U-Space Service Providers (USSPs) operating systems.

Commercial UTM Providers

Companies like AirMap, Airspace Link, and Altitude Angel provide UTM platforms integrating airspace data, weather, notifications, flight authorization, and operator services. These platforms enable the scaling needed for commercial drone delivery and urban air mobility.

Key Takeaways

  • FAA Part 107 requires remote pilot certification (knowledge test every 24 months), registration for drones >250g, and adherence to VLOS, 400ft altitude limit, 100mph speed limit, daylight operations, and yielding to manned aircraft
  • EASA's risk-based framework includes Open category (A1/A2/A3 subcategories based on drone weight and proximity to people), Specific category (operational authorization via SORA risk assessment), and Certified category (high-risk operations requiring aircraft-level certification)
  • LAANC (Low Altitude Authorization and Notification Capability) provides near-real-time automated airspace authorization for controlled airspace (Classes B/C/D/E surface areas), transforming days-long approval processes into seconds
  • Remote ID effective September 2023 broadcasts drone serial number, position, velocity, and control station location via Bluetooth/WiFi, serving as digital license plate enabling accountability following 弘益人間 principles
  • Airspace classifications (Classes A-G) determine authorization requirements, with Class B/C/D requiring ATC approval, Class E requiring approval in surface areas below 400ft, and Class G being uncontrolled
  • UTM (Unmanned Aircraft System Traffic Management) and U-Space provide frameworks for managing thousands of simultaneous drone flights through registration, flight planning, strategic/tactical deconfliction, tracking, and contingency management
  • International harmonization through ICAO standards enables cross-border operations while regional variations (FAA Part 107, EASA Open/Specific/Certified, Transport Canada, CASA, CAAC) address local needs and risk tolerance
