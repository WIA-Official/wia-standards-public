Chapter 2: Audit Frameworks and Standards

Navigating Global Compliance Requirements

The Global Regulatory Landscape

AI decision-making sits at the intersection of multiple regulatory domains: data protection, consumer rights, fair lending, healthcare privacy, and emerging AI-specific legislation. Organizations operating internationally must navigate a complex patchwork of requirements, each with distinct audit implications.

This chapter examines major regulatory frameworks affecting AI audit, providing practical guidance for compliance implementation. The principle of 弘益人間 reminds us that these regulations exist to protect people from algorithmic harm—compliance is not merely legal obligation but ethical imperative.

GDPR: The European Data Protection Regime

The European Union's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), effective since May 2018, established the world's most comprehensive data protection framework. Its implications for AI audit are profound and far-reaching.

Right to Explanation (Article 22)

Article 22 grants individuals the right not to be subject to decisions based solely on automated processing that produce legal or similarly significant effects. When such decisions occur, individuals have the right to obtain meaningful information about the logic involved.

Audit Requirements:

Organizations must maintain detailed records of automated decision logic, enabling generation of explanations upon request. Audit logs should capture:

  • Decision logic and algorithmic reasoning
  • Features and data points influencing the decision
  • Model confidence and alternative outcomes
  • Human oversight mechanisms (if applicable)

Data Protection Impact Assessments (Article 35)

GDPR mandates Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIAs) for high-risk processing activities, including systematic monitoring and automated decision-making. DPIAs must evaluate risks to individual rights and describe safeguards.

// GDPR-compliant decision log structure
interface GDPRDecisionLog {
  decision_id: string;
  timestamp: string;
  data_subject_id: string;  // Pseudonymized

  processing_details: {
    purpose: string[];

  decision: {
    outcome: string;
    reasoning: string;  // Human-readable explanation

  rights_metadata: {
    explanation_available: boolean;

    rectification_procedure: string;
  };
}

Accountability Principle (Article 5)

Controllers must demonstrate compliance with GDPR principles. Audit trails provide essential evidence of accountability, documenting lawful processing, purpose limitation, data minimization, and accuracy.

CCPA: California Consumer Privacy Act

The California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA), effective January 2020, grants California residents extensive privacy rights. The California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA), its successor, strengthens requirements further.

Right to Know

Consumers have the right to know what personal information is collected, how it's used, and whether it's sold or disclosed. For AI systems, this extends to understanding how data influences automated decisions.

Automated Decision-Making Disclosure

CPRA requires businesses to disclose automated decision-making technology, including profiling, enabling consumers to understand and challenge decisions. Audit systems must support:

  • Disclosure of automated decision-making use
  • Explanation of decision logic and significance
  • Consumer access to decision records
  • Opt-out mechanisms where required

EU AI Act: Risk-Based AI Regulation

The proposed EU AI Act represents the world's first comprehensive AI regulatory framework. It categorizes AI systems by risk level and imposes requirements proportional to risk.

Risk Categories

Risk Level Examples Requirements
Unacceptable Social scoring, real-time biometric surveillance Prohibited
High Risk Credit scoring, hiring, law enforcement Strict compliance, audit trails, human oversight
Limited Risk Chatbots, emotion recognition Transparency obligations
Minimal Risk Spam filters, recommendation systems Voluntary codes of conduct

High-Risk System Requirements

High-risk AI systems must implement comprehensive audit capabilities:

  • Technical Documentation: Complete documentation of system design, training data, and decision logic
  • Logging Capabilities: Automatic recording of events throughout the AI lifecycle
  • Human Oversight: Mechanisms for human review and intervention
  • Accuracy and Robustness: Regular testing and validation with documented results
  • Transparency: Clear information for users and affected individuals

Financial Services Regulations

Financial institutions face sector-specific AI audit requirements stemming from fair lending laws, prudential regulation, and consumer protection mandates.

Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA)

ECOA prohibits credit discrimination based on protected characteristics. AI-driven lending decisions must be auditable for fairness and bias. The Federal Reserve and other regulators expect:

  • Adverse action notices explaining credit denials
  • Model validation and bias testing
  • Ongoing monitoring for disparate impact
  • Documentation of model development and deployment

Model Risk Management (SR 11-7)

The Federal Reserve's SR 11-7 guidance establishes model risk management expectations for banking organizations. AI models used in credit, pricing, or risk decisions require:

  • Independent model validation
  • Performance monitoring and backtesting
  • Documentation of limitations and assumptions
  • Escalation procedures for model issues

Healthcare Regulations

HIPAA: Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act

AI systems processing protected health information (PHI) must comply with HIPAA's privacy and security rules. Audit requirements include:

  • Access logs tracking all PHI disclosures
  • Security incident documentation
  • Risk analysis and mitigation
  • Business associate agreements for AI vendors

FDA Medical Device Regulation

AI-powered medical devices require FDA approval and post-market surveillance. Clinical decision support systems must maintain:

  • Clinical validation studies
  • Adverse event reporting
  • Software updates and change documentation
  • Real-world performance monitoring

Industry Standards and Frameworks

ISO/IEC 27001: Information Security Management

ISO 27001 provides a framework for information security management systems (ISMS). AI audit systems should align with controls addressing:

  • Access control (A.9)
  • Cryptography (A.10)
  • Operations security (A.12)
  • Compliance (A.18)

NIST AI Risk Management Framework

The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) AI Risk Management Framework provides voluntary guidance for trustworthy AI development. It emphasizes:

  • Valid and reliable outcomes
  • Safe operation
  • Security and resilience
  • Accountability and transparency
  • Explainability and interpretability
  • Privacy enhancement
  • Fairness with harmful bias management

IEEE Standards

IEEE develops technical standards for AI ethics and transparency:

  • IEEE 7000: Systems design addressing ethical concerns
  • IEEE 7001: Transparency of autonomous systems
  • IEEE 7010: Wellbeing metrics for ethical AI

Implementing Multi-Framework Compliance

Organizations operating globally must satisfy multiple frameworks simultaneously. A unified audit architecture addresses overlapping requirements efficiently:

class MultiFrameworkAuditSystem {
  async logDecision(decision: Decision): Promise {
    // Core decision logging
    const auditLog = this.createBaseLog(decision);

    // GDPR enhancements
    if (this.isGDPRApplicable(decision)) {
      auditLog.gdpr = this.addGDPRMetadata(decision);
    }

    // CCPA enhancements
    if (this.isCCPAApplicable(decision)) {
      auditLog.ccpa = this.addCCPAMetadata(decision);
    }

    // EU AI Act enhancements
    if (this.isEUAIActApplicable(decision)) {
      auditLog.euAIAct = this.addEUAIActMetadata(decision);
    }

    // Sector-specific enhancements
    if (decision.domain === 'finance') {
      auditLog.ecoa = this.addECOAMetadata(decision);
    } else if (decision.domain === 'healthcare') {
      auditLog.hipaa = this.addHIPAAMetadata(decision);
    }

    // Persist with integrity verification
    await this.persistWithVerification(auditLog);
  }
}

Compliance Verification Automation

Automated compliance checking reduces manual audit burden and enables real-time detection of violations:

interface ComplianceRule {
  framework: string;
  requirement: string;
  check: (log: AuditLog) => ComplianceResult;
}

const gdprRightToExplanation: ComplianceRule = {
  framework: "GDPR",
  requirement: "Article 22 - Right to explanation",
  check: (log) => {
    if (log.decision.automated && log.decision.significant_effect) {
      if (!log.decision.reasoning || log.decision.reasoning.length < 50) {
        return {
          compliant: false,
          message: "Insufficient explanation for significant automated decision"
        };
      }
    }
    return { compliant: true };
  }
};

const ccpaAutomatedDecisionDisclosure: ComplianceRule = {
  framework: "CCPA",
  requirement: "Automated decision-making disclosure",
  check: (log) => {
    if (!log.disclosure || !log.disclosure.automated_decision_disclosed) {
      return {
        compliant: false,
        message: "Automated decision-making not disclosed to consumer"
      };
    }
    return { compliant: true };
  }
};

Documentation Requirements

Comprehensive documentation demonstrates compliance and facilitates regulatory audits:

  • System Documentation: Architecture, data flows, model descriptions
  • Policy Documentation: Governance policies, acceptable use, escalation procedures
  • Validation Documentation: Testing results, bias audits, performance metrics
  • Incident Documentation: Malfunctions, security breaches, bias incidents
  • Training Documentation: Staff training on compliance and audit procedures

Regulatory Audit Preparation

When regulators request audit information, preparedness is critical:

Audit Readiness Checklist:
  1. Maintain up-to-date system inventory of all AI decision systems
  2. Ensure audit logs are complete, accessible, and verified
  3. Prepare summary reports demonstrating compliance
  4. Document all policy changes and their effective dates
  5. Designate trained personnel to interface with regulators
  6. Establish secure channels for data transmission to regulators
  7. Conduct regular internal compliance audits

Chapter Summary

  • Financial services regulations (ECOA, SR 11-7) mandate fairness testing and model risk management
  • Healthcare regulations (HIPAA, FDA) require strict privacy controls and clinical validation
  • Industry standards (ISO 27001, NIST, IEEE) provide frameworks for trustworthy AI development
  • Multi-framework compliance requires unified audit architecture addressing overlapping requirements
  • Automated compliance checking enables real-time violation detection and reduces manual audit burden
  • Comprehensive documentation and audit readiness preparation are essential for regulatory engagements
