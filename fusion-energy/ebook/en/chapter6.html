Chapter 6: Tritium Breeding and Materials

"A fusion power plant must be self-sustaining not just in energy, but in fuel. The lithium blanket transforms neutrons into tritium, closing the fuel cycle and making fusion truly sustainable."

6.1 The Tritium Challenge

While deuterium is abundant in seawater, tritium is radioactive with a 12.3-year half-life and exists naturally only in trace amounts. Current world tritium inventory is about 25 kilograms, mostly from nuclear weapons programs and heavy-water reactors. This is far insufficient for fusion energy deployment - ITER alone will require several kilograms for initial operations.

Fortunately, fusion reactors can breed their own tritium. Each D-T fusion reaction produces one 14.1 MeV neutron. When this neutron strikes lithium in a breeding blanket surrounding the plasma, it produces tritium through nuclear reactions. If the blanket produces more than one tritium atom per fusion reaction consumed, the fuel cycle becomes self-sustaining.

Lithium-based Tritium Breeding Reactions

Two lithium isotopes can breed tritium:

Lithium-6 (7.5% natural abundance):

⁶Li + n → ⁴He + ³H + 4.8 MeV

This exothermic reaction works efficiently with thermal (slow) neutrons and produces additional energy.

Lithium-7 (92.5% natural abundance):

⁷Li + n → ⁴He + ³H + n - 2.5 MeV

This endothermic reaction requires fast neutrons (>2.5 MeV) but produces an additional neutron, potentially breeding more than one tritium per fusion neutron.

The Tritium Breeding Ratio (TBR)

The tritium breeding ratio (TBR) is defined as the number of tritium atoms produced per tritium atom consumed in fusion reactions. A TBR > 1.0 is essential for self-sufficiency, but practical power plants require TBR ≥ 1.05-1.15 to account for:

  • Radioactive decay during storage and processing
  • Incomplete tritium extraction from breeding material
  • Tritium permeation losses through pipes and structures
  • Inventory requirements for startup and operational reserve
  • Need to supply tritium to other plants during startup

6.2 Breeding Blanket Concepts

The breeding blanket is one of the most challenging fusion reactor components, simultaneously performing multiple critical functions:

  1. Breeding tritium through neutron-lithium reactions
  2. Extracting heat from fusion neutrons for electricity generation
  3. Shielding superconducting magnets from neutron radiation
  4. Surviving intense neutron bombardment for years

Solid Breeder Blankets

Solid breeder concepts use ceramic lithium compounds (lithium orthosilicate Li₄SiO₄, lithium metatitanate Li₂TiO₃, or lithium oxide Li₂O) as the breeding material. Advantages include:

  • Chemical stability and compatibility with structural materials
  • No lithium fire hazard (ceramics are chemically stable)
  • Simpler tritium extraction (by purge gas flow)
  • Well-understood materials properties

Challenges include adequate heat removal from ceramics and achieving sufficient TBR without prohibitively thick blankets. Neutron multipliers like beryllium or lead are typically needed to boost the TBR above 1.0.

Liquid Breeder Blankets

Liquid breeders use molten lithium, lithium-lead eutectic (Pb-Li), or lithium-fluoride salts (FLiBe). These offer:

  • Self-healing (no radiation damage accumulation)
  • Excellent heat transfer properties
  • Dual function as breeder and coolant
  • Good neutron multiplication (Pb-Li produces extra neutrons from lead)

However, liquid metals pose challenges: corrosion of structural materials, tritium retention in the liquid, magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) effects when flowing through magnetic fields, and safety concerns with reactive lithium.

ITER Test Blanket Modules

6.3 Fusion Materials Science

The 14.1 MeV neutrons from D-T fusion create the most demanding materials environment known to engineering. Understanding neutron damage is essential for designing blankets and structures that survive decades of operation.

Neutron Damage Mechanisms

High-energy neutrons damage materials through several mechanisms:

Atomic Displacement

Neutron collisions knock atoms out of their lattice positions, creating vacancies and interstitials. These defects cluster into voids, dislocation loops, and other structures that weaken the material, reduce ductility, and cause swelling. The damage is quantified in "displacements per atom" (dpa) - fusion first walls will accumulate 50-100 dpa over their lifetime, far exceeding fission reactor experience (~10-20 dpa).

Transmutation

Neutrons can be absorbed by nuclei, transforming them into different elements. In steel, nickel transmutes to helium and hydrogen, which accumulate in grain boundaries and cause embrittlement. Tungsten transmutes to rhenium and osmium, changing mechanical properties. These transmutations are unavoidable and unique to fusion (fission neutrons have lower energy and different transmutation cross-sections).

Helium Production

Helium generation is particularly pernicious. Unlike hydrogen, helium is insoluble in metals and forms bubbles at grain boundaries, causing severe embrittlement and reducing fracture toughness. Fusion materials may accumulate thousands of ppm helium, levels never encountered in fission reactors.

Structural Material Candidates

Several material classes are being developed for fusion applications:

Reduced Activation Ferritic-Martensitic (RAFM) Steels

RAFM steels like EUROFER and F82H are variants of 9% chromium steels optimized for fusion. Molybdenum, niobium, and nickel (which activate to long-lived isotopes) are replaced with tungsten, vanadium, and tantalum. These steels offer:

  • Good mechanical properties up to 550°C
  • Mature manufacturing technology
  • Reduced activation (recyclable after ~100 years)
  • Adequate performance up to 20-50 dpa

RAFM steels are the baseline structural material for DEMO blankets, though temperature limits restrict coolant temperatures and thermal efficiency.

Oxide Dispersion Strengthened (ODS) Steels

Adding nanoscale yttrium oxide particles to steel dramatically improves high-temperature strength and radiation resistance. The oxide particles trap helium in tiny bubbles, preventing grain boundary embrittlement. ODS steels can operate to 650-700°C and tolerate higher damage levels, but manufacturing is challenging and expensive.

Silicon Carbide Composites (SiC/SiC)

Silicon carbide fiber-reinforced silicon carbide composites offer revolutionary properties:

  • Very low activation (recyclable in decades)
  • High-temperature capability (>1000°C)
  • Low density, high strength
  • Good radiation resistance

However, SiC/SiC faces challenges in hermeticity (gas tightness), joining technology, and thermal conductivity degradation under irradiation. It's a promising long-term option for advanced reactors.

Tungsten and Tungsten Alloys

Pure tungsten or tungsten alloys are used for plasma-facing components due to:

  • Highest melting point of any metal (3422°C)
  • Low tritium retention
  • Low erosion rate
  • Good thermal conductivity

The main challenge is brittleness, especially after neutron irradiation. Tungsten also recrystallizes under heat flux, changing its microstructure and properties.

6.4 First Wall and Divertor

The first wall and divertor face the plasma directly, experiencing extreme heat fluxes, particle bombardment, and neutron damage simultaneously.

First Wall Loading

ITER's first wall will experience:

  • Neutron flux: 0.78 MW/m² (averaged), up to 1 MW/m² peak
  • Surface heat flux: 0.5 MW/m² from radiation and particles
  • Neutron damage: ~3 dpa per full-power year
  • Cyclic loading: Thermal stress from pulsed operation

ITER's first wall uses beryllium tiles bonded to copper heat sinks, backed by water-cooled steel structures. Beryllium was chosen for its low atomic number (less plasma contamination if eroded), good thermal properties, and low tritium retention. However, beryllium is toxic, limiting maintenance operations.

Divertor Extreme Conditions

The divertor experiences the most extreme conditions in fusion:

  • Heat flux: 10-20 MW/m² steady state, potentially 50+ MW/m² during transients
  • Particle flux: 10²⁴ particles/m²/s
  • Erosion: Sputtering and erosion requiring periodic replacement
  • Temperature: Surface temperatures up to 1500-2000°C

ITER's divertor uses tungsten monoblock design: tungsten blocks brazed to copper alloy cooling tubes. High-pressure water cooling (5 MPa, 150°C inlet temperature) removes the intense heat. Even with this sophisticated design, ITER's divertor will require replacement every few years of operation.

Future reactors may use advanced divertor concepts: liquid metal divertor (lithium or tin flowing over the surface), advanced geometry (Super-X divertor, snowflake divertor), or gaseous divertor (dense neutral gas shielding).

6.5 Tritium Fuel Cycle

A complete tritium fuel cycle includes breeding, extraction, separation, purification, storage, fueling, and recovery. ITER will have extensive tritium handling systems processing kilograms of tritium, though it won't breed significant amounts (only test blanket modules).

Tritium Extraction

From solid breeders, tritium is extracted by flowing helium purge gas through the ceramic pebbles at 300-500°C. The tritium diffuses out of the ceramics, is swept away by the gas, and then separated from the helium.

From liquid breeders, tritium dissolves in the liquid and must be continuously extracted to prevent buildup and permeation losses. Extraction methods include vacuum permeation through membranes or chemical gettering.

Isotope Separation

The fuel mixture contains hydrogen, deuterium, and tritium isotopes in various molecular forms (H₂, D₂, T₂, HD, HT, DT). These must be separated and purified. Methods include:

  • Cryogenic distillation: Exploiting different boiling points of isotopes
  • Thermal cycling absorption: Using metal hydrides with isotope-dependent properties
  • Gas chromatography: For small-scale separation and analysis

Tritium Accountability and Safety

Tritium is a radioactive material requiring careful accounting and containment. While tritium beta radiation is low-energy (unable to penetrate skin), it's hazardous if inhaled or ingested. Fusion facilities must:

  • Track all tritium inventory to within a few grams
  • Prevent tritium release to environment (triple-barrier containment)
  • Detritiate air and water streams before release
  • Monitor worker exposure and environmental tritium levels
  • Plan for accident scenarios (worst-case tritium release)

6.6 The Materials and Blanket Challenge

Materials science is often called the "pacing item" for fusion energy - the critical path determining when fusion power plants can be deployed. While ITER will operate with materials adequate for a few dpa of neutron damage, DEMO and commercial reactors need materials surviving 50-150 dpa over 20-40 year lifetimes.

The International Fusion Materials Irradiation Facility (IFMIF), planned for construction in Japan, will address this need. Using high-current accelerators to generate intense deuterium-lithium neutron sources, IFMIF will test materials at fusion-relevant damage rates, qualifying materials for DEMO within 2-3 years instead of the decades required using fission reactors.

Computational materials science also plays an increasing role. Atomistic simulations can now predict defect evolution and helium bubble formation, while machine learning accelerates the discovery of radiation-resistant alloys. The integration of experiments, simulation, and data-driven materials discovery offers a path to fusion materials qualified for commercial deployment.
