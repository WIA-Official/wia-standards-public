Chapter 3: Plasma Physics and Heating

"Plasma - the fourth state of matter - is the most common form of visible matter in the universe, comprising 99% of what we can see. Yet on Earth, it requires extraordinary effort to create and control."

3.1 Understanding Plasma: The Fourth State of Matter

We're all familiar with the three classical states of matter: solid, liquid, and gas. Add enough energy to ice and it melts to water; add more energy and it boils to steam. But if we keep adding energy to steam, heating it to millions of degrees, something remarkable happens: the water molecules break apart into individual atoms, and then the atoms themselves are torn apart as electrons are stripped away. What remains is a soup of electrically charged particles - ions and free electrons - called plasma.

Plasma is not just hot gas. Its electrical properties give it fundamentally different behavior. The free charges respond to electric and magnetic fields, creating collective effects that don't exist in neutral gases. Plasma can conduct electricity, generate its own magnetic fields, and support waves and instabilities that have no counterpart in ordinary matter.

Plasma Parameters and Conditions

Several key parameters characterize a plasma:

Parameter Definition Fusion Plasma Value
Electron Temperature (Te) Average kinetic energy of electrons 10-20 keV (120-230 million °C)
Ion Temperature (Ti) Average kinetic energy of ions 10-20 keV (120-230 million °C)
Density (n) Number of particles per volume 10¹⁹-10²¹ m⁻³
Pressure (p) p = nT (in appropriate units) 100 kPa - 1 MPa
Beta (β) Ratio of plasma to magnetic pressure 2-10%

Why Plasma for Fusion?

Fusion requires bringing atomic nuclei close enough for the strong nuclear force to overcome electrostatic repulsion. At room temperature, this is impossible - the positive charges repel too strongly. But at 150 million degrees Celsius, particles move so fast that a small fraction have enough energy to overcome the repulsion when they collide head-on.

This is where plasma becomes essential. At fusion temperatures, matter cannot exist in any other state. Moreover, the electrical nature of plasma allows us to heat it with electromagnetic fields and confine it with magnetic fields, avoiding material contact that would instantly cool the plasma.

3.2 Plasma Confinement and Energy Loss

The central challenge of magnetic fusion is keeping the plasma hot enough, dense enough, and stable long enough for fusion reactions to occur at a useful rate. This is quantified by the Lawson criterion, but understanding why confinement is difficult requires examining how plasmas lose energy.

Energy Loss Mechanisms

Fusion plasmas lose energy through several channels:

1. Radiation Losses

Charged particles emit electromagnetic radiation when accelerated. In plasma, electrons spiral around magnetic field lines (synchrotron radiation) and collide with ions (bremsstrahlung). Light impurities like carbon and oxygen radiate strongly through line emission. Heavy impurities like tungsten can be catastrophic, radiating so much that the plasma cools and terminates.

2. Transport Losses

Heat and particles diffuse from the hot plasma core to the cooler edge, where they escape to the divertor. In an ideal plasma, this transport would occur only through classical collisions between particles. However, turbulence driven by plasma instabilities causes anomalous transport that is 10-100 times faster than classical predictions. Reducing turbulent transport is key to improving confinement.

3. Charge Exchange Losses

Neutral atoms entering the plasma can exchange electrons with hot ions, creating a hot neutral that escapes (not confined by magnetic fields) and a cold ion that cools the plasma. Minimizing neutral density in the core is essential.

4. Convective Losses

Large-scale plasma movements (MHD activity, edge localized modes) can eject hot plasma to the walls, causing rapid energy loss.

The Energy Confinement Time

The energy confinement time (τE) quantifies how long energy remains in the plasma before being lost. It's defined as the total plasma energy divided by the power loss rate. For ITER to achieve Q=10, it requires τE ≈ 3-5 seconds with plasma temperatures of 10-20 keV and densities around 10²⁰ m⁻³.

Confinement time improves with plasma size, magnetic field strength, and current. The empirical scaling law for high-confinement mode (H-mode) tokamaks is:

τE ∝ I⁰·⁹³ B⁰·¹⁵ n⁰·⁴¹ Pheat⁻⁰·⁶⁹ R¹·⁹⁷

This scaling, based on data from dozens of tokamaks, shows that larger machines (bigger radius R) with higher current (I) and magnetic field (B) confine energy much better. This is why ITER is so large - size really matters for fusion.

3.3 Plasma Heating Methods

Achieving fusion temperatures requires massive heating power - typically 50-100 MW for ITER-scale devices. Three complementary methods are used, each with distinct advantages.

Ohmic Heating: The Plasma Toaster

When current flows through any resistive conductor, it generates heat (P = I²R). The tokamak plasma, despite being a good conductor at high temperatures, still has some resistance. When 15 million amperes flow through ITER's plasma, ohmic heating deposits about 20 MW.

Ohmic heating is effective for initial heating up to 20-30 million degrees. However, plasma resistivity decreases rapidly with temperature (roughly as T⁻³/²). As the plasma gets hotter, it becomes such a good conductor that ohmic heating becomes negligible. This is actually beneficial - once the plasma is hot enough for fusion, the current flows with minimal resistance, reducing the power needed to sustain it.

Neutral Beam Injection: Particle Bombardment

Neutral beam injection (NBI) works like heating a pool by throwing hot rocks into it. Hydrogen isotope ions are accelerated to high energies (100-1000 keV, corresponding to velocities of 4-10 million meters per second), then neutralized by passing through a gas cell. The resulting high-energy neutral atoms can penetrate the magnetic field (which only affects charged particles) and enter the plasma core.

Once inside, the neutral atoms quickly collide with plasma ions and electrons, ionizing and transferring their energy. The newly created ions are now confined by the magnetic field and contribute to the plasma current. Modern NBI systems can deliver 30-40 MW per beamline, with ITER planning to use three beamlines for up to 100 MW total.

NBI has several advantages: it heats ions directly (important since fusion occurs between ions), drives plasma current, and can be aimed to control rotation. The main challenge is achieving high neutralization efficiency at the high energies needed to penetrate large plasmas. ITER's NBI will use negative ion sources, which can be neutralized more efficiently at 1 MeV energies.

Radio Frequency Heating: Resonant Energy Transfer

Radio frequency (RF) heating uses electromagnetic waves to transfer energy to plasma particles by resonating with their natural motion in the magnetic field. Two primary methods are employed:

Ion Cyclotron Resonance Heating (ICRH)

Ions in a magnetic field spiral around field lines at a characteristic frequency called the cyclotron frequency, which depends on the ion mass, charge, and magnetic field strength. By launching radio waves at 40-80 MHz (similar to FM radio), we can match the ion cyclotron frequency at a specific location in the plasma.

When the wave frequency matches the ion motion, energy is efficiently absorbed, accelerating the ions to higher energies. ICRH preferentially heats minority ion species (e.g., heating hydrogen in a deuterium plasma), creating a hot ion population that transfers energy to the bulk plasma through collisions. ITER's ICRH system will provide 20 MW at 40-55 MHz.

Electron Cyclotron Resonance Heating (ECRH)

Electrons, being much lighter than ions, spiral around magnetic field lines at much higher frequencies - typically 140 GHz for ITER (millimeter waves, similar to radar). ECRH systems use powerful gyrotron tubes that generate these waves, which are launched into the plasma via carefully positioned antennas.

ECRH has unique advantages: the waves can be focused like a laser beam, allowing localized heating and current drive at specific plasma locations. This is crucial for controlling plasma instabilities and optimizing the current profile. ECRH is also used for plasma startup, creating the initial ionization that begins the discharge. ITER will use 20 MW of ECRH at 170 GHz.

Lower Hybrid Current Drive

A third RF technique, lower hybrid current drive (LHCD), uses waves at 3-6 GHz to drive plasma current efficiently. The waves transfer momentum to electrons, causing them to flow preferentially in one direction. LHCD can sustain significant plasma current without the central solenoid, enabling steady-state operation.

3.4 Plasma Instabilities: Taming the Beast

Plasmas are inherently unstable. The free energy in temperature gradients, pressure gradients, and current density drives instabilities that can degrade confinement or even terminate the plasma. Understanding and controlling these instabilities is crucial for fusion success.

Magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) Instabilities

MHD theory treats plasma as a magnetized fluid, describing large-scale collective motions. Several MHD instabilities can occur in tokamaks:

Kink Modes

External kink modes occur when the plasma column bends or kinks, particularly if the plasma current is too high relative to the confining magnetic field. Internal kink modes cause the plasma core to shift or develop a helical structure. The safety factor q must be above certain critical values (typically q > 2 at the edge) to avoid these modes.

Tearing Modes

Tearing modes occur when magnetic field lines break and reconnect, forming magnetic islands. These islands create regions where particles and heat can escape easily, degrading confinement. Neoclassical tearing modes (NTMs) are self-sustaining: the island causes local pressure flattening, which drives more island growth, in a positive feedback loop.

Edge Localized Modes (ELMs)

In high-confinement mode, the plasma edge develops a steep pressure gradient that periodically becomes unstable, expelling energy and particles in bursts. Large ELMs can deposit 10+ MJ of energy on the divertor in milliseconds, potentially damaging plasma-facing components. Managing ELM size and frequency is a major challenge for ITER.

Disruptions: The Plasma's Termination

The most serious instability is a disruption - a catastrophic loss of plasma confinement occurring in milliseconds. During a disruption, the plasma thermal energy (hundreds of megajoules in ITER) is deposited on the walls, the plasma current (15 MA in ITER) decays rapidly, and the associated electromagnetic forces can damage the structure.

Disruptions are triggered by various causes: excessive density, too many impurities, MHD instabilities growing too large, or loss of position control. Modern tokamaks have sophisticated systems to predict and mitigate disruptions:

  • Disruption prediction: Machine learning algorithms analyze plasma parameters to predict disruptions 30-300 ms in advance
  • Active avoidance: When a disruption is predicted, heating, fueling, and magnetic fields are adjusted to steer back to stability
  • Mitigation: If avoidance fails, massive gas injection or shattered pellet injection spreads the heat load and reduces electromagnetic forces

Microinstabilities and Turbulence

While MHD instabilities operate at the scale of the entire plasma, microinstabilities occur at much smaller scales (millimeters to centimeters) but have profound effects on energy and particle transport. These instabilities, driven by temperature and density gradients, create turbulence that mixes hot core plasma with cooler edge plasma, increasing transport.

The most important microinstabilities are:

  • Ion temperature gradient (ITG) modes: Driven by steep ion temperature gradients
  • Trapped electron modes (TEM): Associated with electrons trapped in magnetic field ripple
  • Electron temperature gradient (ETG) modes: Occur at very small scales, affecting electron heat transport

Understanding and reducing turbulent transport is a major research focus. Advanced diagnostics using beam scattering and reflectometry can now directly measure turbulent fluctuations, while supercomputer simulations solve the complex equations governing turbulence evolution.

3.5 High Confinement Mode: The H-mode Breakthrough

One of the most important discoveries in fusion research occurred in 1982 at the ASDEX tokamak in Germany. Physicist Friedrich Wagner and his team observed that above a certain heating power threshold, the plasma would suddenly transition to a state with dramatically improved confinement - energy confinement time doubled or tripled in milliseconds.

This high-confinement mode (H-mode) differs from the standard low-confinement mode (L-mode) by developing a transport barrier at the plasma edge. In this narrow region (a few centimeters wide), turbulence is suppressed by sheared plasma flows (imagine how stirring a fluid in different directions at different radii suppresses mixing). The steep pressure gradient that results improves overall confinement.

H-mode has become the baseline operating scenario for ITER and future fusion reactors because it provides the confinement needed for Q=10 at achievable size and magnetic field. However, H-mode comes with the challenge of edge localized modes (ELMs), requiring sophisticated control techniques.

Advanced Confinement Regimes

Beyond standard H-mode, researchers have discovered enhanced confinement regimes:

  • Improved H-mode (I-mode): Good energy confinement without particle buildup, naturally ELM-free
  • Quiescent H-mode (QH-mode): Replaces ELMs with continuous edge harmonic oscillations
  • Internal transport barriers (ITB): Enhanced confinement regions in the plasma core
  • Super H-mode: Recently discovered regime with confinement exceeding standard H-mode by 20-50%

3.6 Plasma Diagnostics: Seeing the Invisible

You cannot control what you cannot measure. Understanding and controlling fusion plasma requires dozens of diagnostic systems that measure temperature, density, magnetic fields, flows, and fluctuations across the plasma volume with millisecond time resolution.

Temperature Diagnostics

Thomson scattering: A powerful laser pulse is fired through the plasma. Electrons scatter the light, with the spectrum of scattered light revealing electron temperature and density through Doppler broadening. Modern systems use multiple laser pulses per second and fiber optics to measure profiles across the entire plasma radius.

Electron cyclotron emission (ECE): Hot electrons spiraling in the magnetic field emit radio waves at the cyclotron frequency. By measuring this emission at different frequencies, we obtain the electron temperature profile (different frequencies correspond to different magnetic field strengths, hence different radial locations).

Charge exchange recombination spectroscopy (CXRS): A diagnostic neutral beam is injected into the plasma. When beam neutrals undergo charge exchange with plasma ions, the resulting excited ions emit characteristic light. The Doppler broadening reveals ion temperature, while the Doppler shift measures plasma rotation velocity.

Density and Impurity Diagnostics

Interferometry: Measures line-integrated electron density by detecting the phase shift of a laser or microwave beam passing through the plasma. Multiple chords provide density profiles.

Reflectometry: Radio waves sent into the plasma reflect when the wave frequency matches the local plasma frequency, which depends on density. By sweeping frequency, we can probe different densities and obtain density profiles with high spatial and temporal resolution.

Spectroscopy: Light from plasma impurities (carbon, oxygen, tungsten) provides information about impurity concentration, temperature, and flow velocities through detailed analysis of spectral lines.

Magnetic Diagnostics

Magnetic sensors (pickup coils, Rogowski coils, Hall probes) measure magnetic fields inside and around the plasma. These measurements, combined with real-time equilibrium reconstruction codes, determine the plasma shape, position, current profile, and safety factor profile - essential for plasma control.
