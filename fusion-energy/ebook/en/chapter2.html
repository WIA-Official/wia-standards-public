Chapter 2: Tokamak Reactors and ITER

"The tokamak represents humanity's most successful attempt to bottle a star. Born in the Soviet Union during the Cold War, it has become the foundation for international cooperation on fusion energy."

2.1 The Birth of the Tokamak

In the early 1950s, as the world grappled with the possibilities and perils of nuclear energy, Soviet physicists Igor Tamm and Andrei Sakharov began exploring ways to confine plasma using magnetic fields. Their groundbreaking insight was that a twisted magnetic field in a toroidal (donut-shaped) configuration could provide stable plasma confinement. They called their design "tokamak," an acronym from the Russian "toroidalnaya kamera s magnitnymi katushkami" (toroidal chamber with magnetic coils).

The tokamak concept was revolutionary. Previous attempts at magnetic confinement suffered from instabilities that caused the plasma to quickly escape. The tokamak's combination of a strong toroidal magnetic field (created by external coils) and a poloidal magnetic field (created by current flowing through the plasma itself) created nested, twisted magnetic field lines that dramatically improved stability.

For years, Soviet fusion research remained largely secret behind the Iron Curtain. Western scientists pursued alternative approaches, particularly stellarators (invented by Lyman Spitzer at Princeton) and magnetic mirrors. The breakthrough came in 1968 at the Kurchatov Institute in Moscow, where the T-3 tokamak achieved electron temperatures of 10 million degrees Celsius - ten times higher than any previous magnetic confinement device.

When Soviet scientists announced these results at an international conference, Western researchers were skeptical. However, when British scientists brought their own diagnostic equipment to Moscow and confirmed the measurements, it sparked a revolution in fusion research. Within a few years, tokamaks were being built around the world, and they have dominated magnetic confinement fusion research ever since.

2.2 How Tokamaks Work: Magnetic Topology

Understanding the tokamak requires grasping how magnetic fields control plasma. Charged particles in a plasma - electrons and ions - cannot move freely across magnetic field lines; instead, they spiral along them like beads on a string. By shaping the magnetic field appropriately, we can guide plasma particles in a closed loop, preventing them from hitting the reactor walls.

The Toroidal Field

The primary component is the toroidal field, which runs around the donut like wire through the hole of a bead. In modern tokamaks, this field is generated by 16-20 large D-shaped superconducting magnets arranged around the torus. These magnets, cooled to -269°C using liquid helium, carry enormous currents (tens of thousands of amperes) to produce fields of 5-13 Tesla - strong enough to levitate a frog (as was famously demonstrated in 2000 by Andre Geim, who later won the Nobel Prize for graphene).

The toroidal field alone is not sufficient for confinement because particles would drift vertically due to the curvature of the magnetic field and the field gradient (the field is stronger on the inside of the torus than the outside). This leads to charge separation and electric fields that would cause the plasma to expand and hit the walls within milliseconds.

The Poloidal Field and Plasma Current

The solution is to add a poloidal magnetic field - one that runs the "short way" around the donut. This is generated by driving millions of amperes of current through the plasma itself, effectively turning the plasma into the secondary winding of a giant transformer. The primary winding is a central solenoid that runs through the hole in the donut.

When the toroidal and poloidal fields combine, the result is a helical (spiral) magnetic field. Plasma particles follow these twisted field lines, which form nested toroidal surfaces. A particle starting anywhere in the plasma will eventually visit all parts of that magnetic surface before returning to its starting point, but it will never cross onto a different surface. This topology is crucial for confinement stability.

The Safety Factor

A key parameter in tokamak physics is the safety factor, denoted q. This describes how many times a field line goes around the torus the long way (toroidally) for each trip around the short way (poloidally). The safety factor varies from the plasma center to the edge, and certain values (particularly q = 2, 3, 4) can lead to instabilities.

Most tokamaks operate with q between 2 and 4 at the plasma edge. Higher safety factors generally improve stability but require stronger magnetic fields or larger plasma currents, increasing cost and engineering complexity. The art of tokamak design involves optimizing q profiles for stable, high-performance operation.

2.3 Tokamak Components and Systems

A modern tokamak is an extraordinarily complex machine, integrating systems for magnetic confinement, plasma heating, fueling, diagnostics, and energy extraction. Let's examine the major components.

The Vacuum Vessel

The vacuum vessel is the tokamak's containment structure, a massive double-walled steel torus that maintains ultra-high vacuum (10⁻⁷ Pa, a billion times less than atmospheric pressure). In ITER, the vacuum vessel weighs 5,200 tonnes and is 19.4 meters in diameter. It must withstand enormous forces from the magnets, thermal stresses from the hot plasma, and electromagnetic loads during plasma disruptions, all while maintaining vacuum integrity.

The Divertor: Handling the Exhaust

One of the greatest engineering challenges in tokamak design is handling the plasma exhaust. As the plasma is heated and fusion reactions occur, helium "ash" accumulates and must be removed. Additionally, hydrogen fuel ions and impurities must be continuously exhausted to maintain plasma purity.

The divertor is a specialized region at the bottom of the tokamak where the magnetic field lines are diverted to intersect material surfaces. Here, the plasma particles are neutralized and pumped away. The divertor experiences heat fluxes exceeding 20 MW/m² - similar to a rocket engine nozzle - and must survive this punishment for years. Advanced materials like tungsten are used, but divertor technology remains an active research area.

Heating Systems

Getting the plasma to fusion temperatures requires massive heating power. Three primary methods are used:

Ohmic Heating

The plasma current flowing through the resistive plasma generates heat, just like a toaster element. This is effective up to about 20-30 million degrees, but at higher temperatures the plasma becomes such a good conductor that ohmic heating becomes ineffective.

Neutral Beam Injection (NBI)

High-energy neutral atoms (typically deuterium accelerated to 100-1000 keV) are injected into the plasma. Being neutral, they are not deflected by the magnetic field and penetrate deep into the core. Once inside, they collide with plasma ions, transferring their energy and becoming ionized and confined. NBI can deliver 30+ MW of heating power.

Radio Frequency (RF) Heating

Radio waves at specific frequencies can resonate with the natural motions of ions or electrons in the magnetic field, transferring energy like pushing a swing at just the right moment. Two primary RF methods are used: Ion Cyclotron Resonance Heating (ICRH) at 40-80 MHz and Electron Cyclotron Resonance Heating (ECRH) at 140+ GHz. Each can contribute 20+ MW of heating.

2.4 Operating a Tokamak: The Plasma Discharge

Operating a tokamak involves executing a carefully choreographed sequence called a discharge or shot. Let's walk through a typical high-performance discharge:

Pre-discharge Preparation (Hours to Days)

Before each discharge, the vacuum vessel must be cleaned and conditioned. This typically involves:

  • Glow discharge cleaning: Low-temperature plasma removes impurities from walls
  • Baking: Heating walls to 200-350°C to remove adsorbed water and gases
  • Wall conditioning: Coating walls with boron or lithium to reduce oxygen and carbon
  • Pumping to ultra-high vacuum: Achieving base pressure < 10⁻⁷ Pa

Breakdown and Current Ramp-up (0-10 seconds)

The discharge begins by applying a rapidly changing magnetic flux through the central solenoid, inducing a toroidal electric field. A small amount of gas (typically hydrogen or deuterium) is puffed into the vessel. When the electric field exceeds the breakdown threshold, the gas ionizes, creating an initial plasma current of a few kiloamperes.

The plasma current is then ramped up to its full value - typically 5-15 million amperes in large tokamaks. As the current increases, the plasma is compressed and shaped by the poloidal field coils into the desired cross-sectional shape (usually D-shaped or elliptical, which provides better confinement than circular).

Flat-top: Steady-state Operation (10-1000 seconds)

Once the plasma reaches its target current, shape, and position, the flat-top phase begins. Here, the plasma is maintained in steady state while heating systems bring it to fusion temperatures. In current tokamaks, this phase lasts 10-100 seconds, limited by the flux swing available from the central solenoid (the plasma current must be continuously driven by changing magnetic flux).

During flat-top, sophisticated control systems continuously adjust:

  • Plasma shape and position (using poloidal field coils)
  • Plasma density (via gas puffing and pellet injection)
  • Heating power (NBI and RF systems)
  • Current profile (using off-axis current drive)
  • Impurity levels (via divertor pumping and wall conditioning)

The goal is to achieve and maintain high-performance conditions: high temperature (150+ million °C), high density (10²⁰ particles/m³), good confinement (several seconds), and stability (avoiding disruptions). When conditions are optimal, fusion reactions occur and the plasma becomes self-heated by the energetic alpha particles produced.

Ramp-down and Termination (10-20 seconds)

When the desired flat-top duration is reached or the central solenoid flux is exhausted, the plasma current is ramped down in a controlled manner. Heating systems are reduced, gas fueling is adjusted, and the plasma cools and shrinks. Finally, the plasma is terminated by shutting off the toroidal field, and the remaining gas is pumped away.

2.5 ITER: The International Fusion Megaproject

The ITER project represents the culmination of decades of tokamak development and the largest international scientific collaboration in history. ITER (originally "International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor," now simply ITER, meaning "the way" in Latin) is being built in Cadarache, southern France, by a partnership of 35 nations representing over half the world's population.

The Q=10 Goal

ITER's primary mission is to demonstrate fusion energy gain with Q ≥ 10, meaning it will produce at least 10 times more fusion power than the heating power input to the plasma. If 50 MW of external heating creates plasma conditions that produce 500 MW of fusion power, that's Q=10. This represents a crucial threshold: the plasma becomes dominated by self-heating from fusion alpha particles rather than external heating, approaching the conditions needed for a power plant.

Previous tokamaks have achieved Q ≈ 1 (breakeven). The Joint European Torus (JET) in the UK achieved Q = 0.67 with 16 MW of fusion power in 1997. ITER's factor-of-10 improvement comes from:

  • Size: Larger plasma volume improves confinement (confinement scales favorably with size)
  • Magnetic field: Stronger fields confine plasma better and allow higher pressure
  • Superconducting magnets: Enable long pulses (400-600 seconds vs. 10-20 seconds)
  • Advanced plasma shapes: Elongated, D-shaped plasma optimizes confinement
  • Modern heating systems: 50+ MW of NBI and RF heating

ITER's Timeline and Challenges

ITER's scale and complexity have led to a lengthy construction schedule. The project officially began in 2007, with construction starting in 2010. Major milestones include:

  • 2010-2020: Site preparation and major component manufacturing
  • 2020-2025: Tokamak assembly, installation of major components
  • 2025-2027: First plasma (hydrogen) operations, system commissioning
  • 2027-2033: Progressive enhancement, deuterium operations
  • 2033-2038: Deuterium-tritium operations, achieving Q=10
  • 2038-2045: Extended high-performance operation, technology validation

The project has faced challenges including budget increases (now ~€22 billion), schedule delays, and technical obstacles. However, construction is now well advanced, with most major components delivered and assembly proceeding. The tokamak building, a 60-meter-tall structure, now houses the cryostat (the giant thermos that contains the superconducting magnets) and many of the major components.

2.6 Beyond ITER: The Path to DEMO

While ITER will demonstrate fusion energy gain, it is not designed to generate electricity for the grid. That task falls to DEMO (DEMOnstration) power plants, the next step toward commercial fusion. Several nations are developing DEMO concepts:

European DEMO

The European fusion program is designing a DEMO that would generate 300-500 MW of electricity for the grid. Key features include a larger plasma than ITER (major radius ~9 meters), stronger magnetic fields (5.7 T), and a breeding blanket that produces tritium fuel. Target operation is the 2050s.

Other National DEMO Programs

  • China CFETR: China Fusion Engineering Test Reactor, targeting 1000 MW fusion power
  • Japan DEMO: Steady-state operation with 1.5 GW fusion power
  • Korea K-DEMO: 1 GW fusion power with advanced superconductors
  • India DEMO: Building on domestic tokamak experience

These DEMO plants must address challenges beyond ITER's scope:

  • Tritium self-sufficiency: Breeding tritium from lithium in the blanket
  • High availability: Operating 70-80% of the time (vs. a few percent for ITER)
  • Remote maintenance: Replacing components in highly radioactive environment
  • Materials qualification: Surviving 20-40 years of neutron bombardment
  • Economic viability: Generating electricity at competitive costs

2.7 Tokamak Innovations: Advanced Scenarios

While ITER uses a conventional tokamak design with a pulsed central solenoid, research continues on advanced operating scenarios:

Steady-state Operation

Power plants cannot operate in pulses; they need continuous operation. This requires driving the plasma current without the central solenoid. Two approaches are being pursued:

  • Bootstrap current: The pressure gradient in the plasma naturally drives current due to plasma physics effects. In optimized conditions, 50-90% of the required current can be self-generated.
  • Non-inductive current drive: Neutral beams and RF waves can drive current by transferring momentum to plasma particles. Combined with bootstrap current, 100% steady-state operation is possible.

Advanced Tokamak Scenarios

Modern tokamaks explore advanced scenarios with improved confinement and stability:

  • High β operation: Increasing plasma pressure to extract more fusion power from the magnetic field
  • Internal Transport Barriers: Creating regions of reduced turbulence for better confinement
  • Hybrid scenarios: Combining good confinement with steady-state capability
  • Super H-mode: Enhanced confinement regimes discovered in recent experiments
