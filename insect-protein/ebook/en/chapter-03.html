CHAPTER 3

Farming & Production Systems

Introduction to Insect Farming Systems

Insect farming represents a paradigm shift from traditional agriculture. Unlike conventional livestock, insects can be farmed vertically in climate-controlled facilities, achieving production densities impossible with cattle, pigs, or chickens. This chapter explores facility design, environmental management, feeding systems, and operational best practices for commercial insect production.

Facility Design and Infrastructure

Scale Considerations

Insect farms range from small-scale operations (10-50 sq meters) producing a few kilograms weekly to industrial facilities (1,000+ sq meters) producing tons monthly. Starting small allows operators to master the biology and processes before scaling.

Climate Control Systems

Insects are ectothermic, making temperature and humidity control critical. Commercial facilities require:

  • HVAC Systems: Maintain optimal temperature ranges (25-32°C depending on species). Many farms use zone heating to reduce energy costs.
  • Humidity Control: Dehumidifiers and humidifiers maintain species-specific ranges (40-80%). Improper humidity causes molting problems and disease.
  • Ventilation: Fresh air exchange prevents ammonia buildup and maintains air quality. Minimum 4-6 air changes per hour recommended.
  • Backup Systems: Temperature/humidity alarms and backup power prevent catastrophic losses during failures.

Rearing Container Design

Container choice impacts labor efficiency, space utilization, and animal welfare. Common systems include:

  • Plastic Bins: Stackable, easy to clean, ideal for small to medium operations. Standard sizes: 40x60cm, 20-30cm deep.
  • Vertical Racks: Maximize floor space utilization. Climate control more challenging with height.
  • Automated Systems: Large operations use conveyor systems for feeding, cleaning, and harvesting.
  • Breeding Enclosures: Separate, optimized spaces for breeding stock with egg-laying substrates.

Environmental Management

Temperature Management

Temperature directly affects growth rate, feed conversion, and reproduction. Cricket optimal range: 28-32°C. Each degree below optimal can extend production time by 10-15%. Thermal imaging helps identify cold spots. Many farms use programmable thermostats with different day/night temperatures to reduce costs while maintaining growth.

Humidity Control

Proper humidity prevents desiccation and ensures successful molting. Too high promotes mold and bacterial growth; too low causes developmental problems. Cricket farms target 40-50% RH, mealworm farms 50-70% RH. Hygrometers in multiple locations monitor conditions. Some farms use saturated salt solutions to buffer humidity locally.

Lighting Regimens

Most insects require photoperiod management. Standard: 12-14 hours light, 10-12 hours dark. LED lights reduce heat and energy costs. Red lights during dark periods allow observation without disturbing insects. Some species require specific light intensities for proper behavior and reproduction.

Feeding Systems and Nutrition

Feed Formulation Principles

Insect feed must balance protein, carbohydrates, fats, vitamins, and minerals for optimal growth and nutrition:

  • Protein: 18-25% for most species. Sources: soy meal, wheat gluten, fish meal, brewers yeast.
  • Carbohydrates: Energy source. Wheat bran, cornmeal, oat flour commonly used.
  • Fats: 3-5% for essential fatty acids. Can influence omega-3/omega-6 ratios in harvested insects.
  • Vitamins/Minerals: Supplements ensure complete nutrition. Calcium particularly important for chitin synthesis.
  • Moisture: Many insects obtain water from fresh vegetables (carrots, potatoes) rather than open water.

Commercial Feed Options

Many farmers start with commercial poultry or fish feed, modifying moisture content. As operations scale, custom formulations optimize for cost and nutritional output. Some farms partner with food manufacturers to use by-products (brewery spent grain, bakery waste), reducing feed costs dramatically.

Feeding Schedules

Crickets: Feed daily, remove uneaten food after 24 hours to prevent mold. Mealworms: Feed 2-3 times weekly, they store nutrients in substrate. Black soldier fly larvae: Continuous feeding possible with appropriate waste management. Overfeeding wastes feed and increases moisture problems; underfeeding slows growth and increases cannibalism.

Breeding Programs

Genetic Selection

Professional farms maintain breeding colonies selected for:

  • Fast growth rates
  • High feed conversion efficiency
  • Disease resistance
  • Reduced aggression/cannibalism
  • High egg production
  • Uniformity in size and quality

Breeding Colony Management

Maintain 10-20% of production as breeding stock. Select the largest, healthiest individuals. Prevent inbreeding by introducing new genetics every 6-12 months. Record-keeping tracks lineage and performance. Some operations maintain multiple lines and cross-breed for hybrid vigor.

Egg Collection and Incubation

Cricket eggs laid in moist substrate (vermiculite, coconut coir). Collect egg containers every 2-3 days. Incubate separately at optimal temperature (28-30°C) and humidity (80-90%). Hatching occurs 10-14 days. Prevent desiccation of eggs - this is a common failure point. Mealworm beetles lay eggs in flour/bran substrate; sifting separates eggs from adults.

Health Management and Biosecurity

Disease Prevention

Prevention is critical; treatment options limited once disease establishes:

  • Quarantine: New stock isolated for 2-4 weeks before introduction to main population.
  • Sanitation: Regular cleaning of containers, removal of dead insects, waste management.
  • Density Management: Overcrowding stresses insects and spreads disease rapidly.
  • Quality Control: Daily observation identifies problems early. Remove sick/weak individuals promptly.

Common Health Issues

  • Viral Infections: Particularly problematic in crickets. Disinfection and biosecurity essential.
  • Mites: Can devastate colonies. Prevent with low humidity and good sanitation.
  • Fungal Issues: Usually from excessive moisture. Improve ventilation, reduce humidity.
  • Bacterial Problems: Often from spoiled feed or poor sanitation.

Biosecurity Protocols

  • Footbaths with disinfectant at facility entrances
  • Dedicated clothing and boots for farm work
  • Hand washing stations and protocols
  • Visitor restrictions and logging
  • Equipment sterilization between uses
  • Waste management to prevent pest attraction

Harvesting and Post-Harvest Handling

Harvest Timing

Crickets harvested at 6-8 weeks when fully grown but before mass die-off. Mealworms at late larval stage before pupation. Timing affects yield, nutrition, and product quality. Most farms harvest in morning when insects less active.

Harvest Methods

  • Manual Collection: Small operations pick/scoop insects. Labor intensive but gentle.
  • Cold Stunning: Refrigeration (4°C) for 1-2 hours immobilizes insects for easier handling.
  • Sieving Systems: Separate insects from substrate and waste. Multiple sieve sizes separate life stages.
  • Automated Systems: Large farms use conveyor systems and mechanical separators.

Pre-Processing Protocols

  • Fasting: 24-hour fast purges gut contents, improving food safety and flavor.
  • Washing: Clean water rinse removes debris and frass.
  • Killing/Preservation: Freezing (-18°C), blanching (boiling briefly), or freeze-drying. Each method affects final product characteristics.

Scaling Operations

From Small to Commercial Scale

Most successful farms follow staged growth:

  1. Pilot Phase: 10-20 bins, learning biology and processes. Duration: 3-6 months.
  2. Small Commercial: 100-500 bins, basic automation, initial sales. 6-12 months.
  3. Medium Scale: 500-2000 bins, climate control automation, consistent production.
  4. Industrial Scale: 2000+ bins, full automation, multiple facility zones, tons monthly production.

Automation and Technology

As operations scale, automation becomes cost-effective:

  • Automated feeding systems: Reduce labor, ensure consistency
  • Climate monitoring and control: IoT sensors, automated HVAC adjustments
  • Robotic harvesting: Emerging technology, currently expensive
  • Data analytics: Track production metrics, optimize processes
  • Inventory management: Software tracks batches, ages, locations

Economic Considerations

Capital Requirements

Small-scale startup (50kg monthly production): $5,000-15,000 including facility preparation, containers, climate control, initial breeding stock. Medium commercial (500kg monthly): $50,000-150,000. Large industrial: $500,000+.

Operating Costs

  • Feed: 40-50% of operating costs
  • Energy (climate control): 15-25%
  • Labor: 20-30%
  • Facility (rent, maintenance): 10-15%
  • Other (breeding stock replacement, supplies): 5-10%

Revenue Streams

  • Whole frozen insects: $15-30/kg wholesale
  • Dried/roasted insects: $40-80/kg
  • Protein powder: $60-120/kg
  • Pet food ingredient: $10-20/kg
  • Breeding stock sales: Variable, often high margin
  • By-products (frass fertilizer): $1-3/kg

Conclusion

Successful insect farming requires mastering numerous interconnected systems: environmental control, nutrition, genetics, health management, and processing. Unlike traditional farming where nature does much of the work, insect farming is essentially a manufacturing process requiring precision and consistency. However, the rewards - environmental sustainability, nutritional value, and economic opportunity - make it an increasingly attractive agricultural enterprise.
