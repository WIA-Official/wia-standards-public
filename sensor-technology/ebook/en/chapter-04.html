🌡️ Temperature Sensors

Temperature is one of the most commonly measured physical parameters, with applications spanning from industrial process control to consumer electronics. Modern temperature sensors offer a wide range of accuracy, response time, and operating temperature capabilities to suit diverse applications.

Temperature Sensor Technologies

🔥
Thermocouples

Generate voltage from temperature difference between two dissimilar metals. Wide temperature range (-200°C to +2000°C), rugged, but require cold junction compensation. Types: K, J, T, E, N, S, R, B.

📊
RTD (Resistance Temperature Detector)

Platinum resistance changes linearly with temperature. High accuracy (±0.1°C), excellent stability and repeatability. PT100, PT1000 are standard types. Range: -200°C to +850°C.

🔌
Thermistors (NTC/PTC)

Semiconductor resistance varies exponentially with temperature. High sensitivity, fast response, low cost. NTC: negative coefficient (most common). Range: -55°C to +300°C.

💻
Digital Temperature Sensors

Integrated sensor with ADC and digital interface (I2C/SPI). Easy integration, factory calibrated. Examples: TMP117 (±0.1°C), DS18B20, BME280.

📡
Infrared (IR) Pyrometers

Non-contact measurement of surface temperature by detecting IR radiation. Essential for moving objects, hazardous environments, or very high temperatures. Range: -50°C to +3000°C.

🎯
Semiconductor Junction

Forward voltage of PN junction varies with temperature (~2mV/°C). Built into most ICs for thermal management. Limited range but very low cost when integrated.

💧 Humidity Sensors

Humidity measurement is critical for comfort control in buildings, industrial process monitoring, weather forecasting, and semiconductor manufacturing. Most modern humidity sensors measure relative humidity (RH), the ratio of actual water vapor to saturation vapor pressure at a given temperature.

Humidity Sensing Technologies

🔋
Capacitive

Polymer dielectric absorbs water, changing capacitance. Most common type for HVAC, weather stations, consumer devices. Good accuracy (±2-3% RH), wide range.

📊
Resistive

Polymer or ceramic changes resistance with humidity. Lower cost than capacitive, but less accurate and slower response. Used in disposable applications.

🌡️
Thermal Conductivity

Measures heat loss to surrounding air which varies with humidity. Used for absolute humidity measurement in industrial applications.

💎
Chilled Mirror (Dew Point)

Cools mirror until condensation forms, measuring dew point directly. Highest accuracy reference standard (±0.1°C dew point). Expensive, requires maintenance.

💡 Humidity Sensor Best Practices

Contamination Protection: Use PTFE membrane filters to protect sensors from dust, oils, and chemicals while allowing water vapor transmission.

Condensation Avoidance: Never expose sensors to condensing conditions. Heating elements can prevent condensation in high-humidity environments.

Calibration: Use saturated salt solutions (MgCl₂: 33% RH, NaCl: 75% RH, K₂SO₄: 97% RH) for periodic verification.

🌬️ Gas Sensors

Gas sensors detect and measure the concentration of specific gases in the environment. Applications range from industrial safety (detecting toxic or explosive gases) to indoor air quality monitoring and breath analysis for medical diagnostics.

Gas Sensing Technologies

🔥
Metal Oxide Semiconductor (MOS/MOX)

Heated metal oxide changes conductance in presence of gases. Low cost, detects many gases, but requires high power (heater) and has cross-sensitivity. SGP40, MQ series.

⚡
Electrochemical

Gas oxidation/reduction produces current proportional to concentration. High selectivity, good accuracy, low power. Used for CO, H2S, O2, NO2. Limited lifetime (1-3 years).

📡
NDIR (Non-Dispersive Infrared)

Measures IR absorption at gas-specific wavelengths. Excellent selectivity for CO2, hydrocarbons. Long lifetime, no consumables. Higher cost and size. Senseair, Amphenol.

🔬
Photoionization (PID)

UV lamp ionizes gas molecules, measures resulting current. Detects VOCs at ppb levels. Used in industrial hygiene and hazmat response. High sensitivity, fast response.

🔊
Photoacoustic

IR absorption creates pressure waves detected by microphone. Very high selectivity and sensitivity. Used for trace gas analysis. Expensive, complex.

🔥
Catalytic Bead (Pellistor)

Combustible gas burns on heated catalyst, changing resistance. Detects LEL of flammable gases. Industry standard for safety applications.

🏭 Air Quality Index (AQI) Monitoring

Air Quality Index systems combine measurements from multiple sensors to provide a single indicator of overall air quality. These systems are deployed in smart buildings, outdoor monitoring networks, and portable devices for health-conscious consumers.

Key Air Quality Parameters

  • PM2.5: Particulate matter under 2.5μm - penetrates deep into lungs, causes respiratory and cardiovascular effects
  • PM10: Particulate matter under 10μm - causes respiratory irritation, visibility reduction
  • CO2: Indicator of ventilation adequacy, affects cognitive function above 1000 ppm
  • VOCs: Volatile organic compounds from building materials, cleaning products, causing sick building syndrome
  • O3: Ground-level ozone from photochemical reactions, respiratory irritant
  • NO2: Nitrogen dioxide from combustion, respiratory effects

Particulate Matter Sensors

Particulate matter (PM) sensors detect and count airborne particles using optical or laser-based methods. These sensors are essential components of air quality monitoring systems.

💡
Optical (Light Scattering)

LED illuminates particles, photodiode measures scattered light intensity. Lower cost, estimates mass concentration. Examples: Sharp GP2Y, Plantower PMS5003.

🔦
Laser (Optical Particle Counter)

Focused laser beam counts individual particles, measures size distribution. Higher accuracy, counts by size bins. Examples: Sensirion SPS30, Alphasense OPC-N3.

⚖️
Gravimetric (Reference)

Collects particles on filter, weighs mass. Gold standard for PM measurement but not real-time. Used for calibration and regulatory monitoring.

// Air Quality Index Calculation (US EPA Standard)
interface AQIBreakpoint {
    concentration: { low: number; high: number };
    aqi: { low: number; high: number };
}

const PM25_BREAKPOINTS: AQIBreakpoint[] = [
    { concentration: { low: 0.0, high: 12.0 }, aqi: { low: 0, high: 50 } },     // Good
    { concentration: { low: 12.1, high: 35.4 }, aqi: { low: 51, high: 100 } },  // Moderate
    { concentration: { low: 35.5, high: 55.4 }, aqi: { low: 101, high: 150 } }, // Sensitive
    { concentration: { low: 55.5, high: 150.4 }, aqi: { low: 151, high: 200 } },// Unhealthy
    { concentration: { low: 150.5, high: 250.4 }, aqi: { low: 201, high: 300 } },// Very
    { concentration: { low: 250.5, high: 500.4 }, aqi: { low: 301, high: 500 } } // Hazardous
];

function calculateAQI(concentration: number, breakpoints: AQIBreakpoint[]): number {
    const bp = breakpoints.find(b => 
        concentration >= b.concentration.low && concentration <= b.concentration.high
    );
    if (!bp) return -1;
    
    return Math.round(
        ((bp.aqi.high - bp.aqi.low) / (bp.concentration.high - bp.concentration.low)) *
        (concentration - bp.concentration.low) + bp.aqi.low
    );
}

🏢 Smart Building Environmental Monitoring

Modern smart buildings deploy networks of environmental sensors to optimize energy efficiency, ensure occupant comfort, and maintain healthy indoor air quality. These systems integrate with building management systems (BMS) to automatically adjust HVAC, lighting, and ventilation.

Key Monitoring Applications

🌡️
Thermal Comfort

Temperature and humidity monitoring at multiple points enables zone-based climate control. Predictive algorithms anticipate occupancy patterns.

💨
Demand-Controlled Ventilation

CO2 sensors modulate fresh air intake based on actual occupancy, reducing HVAC energy consumption by 20-40% compared to fixed ventilation rates.

🌿
Indoor Air Quality

VOC, particulate, and CO2 monitoring identifies pollution sources and triggers alerts. Essential for WELL and LEED building certifications.

💡
Daylight Harvesting

Light sensors enable automatic dimming of artificial lighting based on natural daylight availability, reducing lighting energy by 30-50%.

⚠️ Sensor Placement Considerations

Temperature Sensors: Mount at breathing height (1.1-1.7m), away from windows, doors, HVAC vents, and heat sources.

CO2 Sensors: Place in breathing zone, not near air supply diffusers. Consider stratification in high-ceiling spaces.

Particulate Sensors: Avoid locations with direct airflow that may introduce artifacts. Consider sampling tube for representative air.
