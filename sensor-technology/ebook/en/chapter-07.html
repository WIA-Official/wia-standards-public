🌐 IoT Sensor Architecture

The Internet of Things (IoT) connects billions of sensors to networks, enabling smart homes, industrial automation, smart cities, and environmental monitoring. Understanding the architecture and protocols of IoT sensor networks is essential for designing scalable, efficient, and secure systems.

IoT Sensor Characteristics

🔋 Low Power

Battery-operated sensors must operate for years. Sleep modes, duty cycling, and efficient protocols are essential.

📡 Wireless

Connectivity via short-range (BLE, Zigbee) or long-range (LoRa, NB-IoT) protocols depending on application.

💰 Low Cost

Mass deployment requires sensor costs <$10, often <$1. Integration reduces BOM and assembly cost.

🔒 Secure

End-to-end encryption, secure boot, and authentication prevent tampering and data theft.

📡 Wireless Protocols

Selecting the appropriate wireless protocol depends on range, power consumption, data rate, and network topology requirements. No single protocol fits all applications.

🧠 Sensor Fusion

Sensor fusion combines data from multiple sensors to produce more accurate, reliable, or comprehensive information than any single sensor alone. Fusion algorithms range from simple averaging to sophisticated Kalman filters and neural networks.

Fusion Levels

📊 Data-Level Fusion

Combining raw sensor data directly. Requires synchronized, compatible sensors. Used for multi-camera stitching, redundant sensor averaging.

⚙️ Feature-Level Fusion

Extracting features from each sensor, then combining. More flexible than data-level. Common in pattern recognition systems.

🎯 Decision-Level Fusion

Each sensor makes independent decisions, then combined by voting or weighting. Most robust to sensor failures.

🏢 Smart Building Applications

Smart buildings use sensor networks for energy optimization, occupant comfort, security, and predictive maintenance. The global smart building market is projected to exceed $130 billion by 2027.

Building Sensor Types

👥 Occupancy Sensors

PIR motion, radar, and CO2-based occupancy detection. Enable demand-controlled ventilation and lighting.

🌡️ Environmental Sensors

Temperature, humidity, CO2, VOCs, and light levels. HVAC optimization and air quality monitoring.

⚡ Energy Meters

Electrical sub-metering, gas, and water consumption. Identify waste and optimize usage patterns.

🔒 Security Sensors

Door/window contacts, motion detectors, glass break sensors. Access control integration.

Demand-Controlled Ventilation

CO2 sensors enable ventilation systems to adjust airflow based on actual occupancy rather than fixed schedules. This can reduce HVAC energy consumption by 10-30% while improving air quality.

CO2 Thresholds for Ventilation Control

Outdoor: 400-420 ppm
Excellent: <600 ppm
Good: 600-800 ppm
Acceptable: 800-1000 ppm
Poor: >1000 ppm (increase ventilation)

🏭 Industrial IoT (IIoT)

Industrial IoT connects manufacturing equipment, processes, and infrastructure for monitoring, optimization, and predictive maintenance. IIoT requires higher reliability, determinism, and security than consumer IoT.

IIoT Applications

🔧 Predictive Maintenance

Vibration, temperature, and current sensors detect equipment degradation before failure. Reduces downtime by 30-50%.

📊 Process Monitoring

Real-time visibility into production parameters. Quality control, yield optimization, and compliance documentation.

📍 Asset Tracking

RTLS (Real-Time Location Systems) track tools, materials, and personnel. Improves utilization and safety.

⚡ Energy Management

Monitor and optimize energy consumption. Demand response, peak shaving, and sustainability reporting.

Predictive Maintenance Sensors

Sensor Type Measurement Fault Detection
Accelerometer (Vibration) Vibration spectrum, RMS Bearing wear, imbalance, misalignment
Temperature Surface/ambient temp Overheating, friction, electrical faults
Current (CT) Motor current signature Broken rotor bars, eccentricity
Ultrasonic High-frequency sound Leaks, electrical discharge, bearing faults
Oil Analysis Particle count, viscosity Wear metals, contamination

🔐 Edge AI and Security

Edge Computing

Edge computing processes sensor data locally rather than sending everything to the cloud. This reduces latency, bandwidth, and cloud costs while improving privacy.

⚡ Reduced Latency

Local processing enables real-time response (<10ms) for control applications. Critical for autonomous systems.

📡 Bandwidth Savings

Send insights, not raw data. A camera can generate 100+ Mbps; edge AI reduces this to kbps of metadata.

🔒 Privacy

Process sensitive data locally. Camera analytics without transmitting video. GDPR compliance.

IoT Security Best Practices

  • Secure boot: Verify firmware integrity before execution
  • Encryption: TLS for transport, AES for data at rest
  • Authentication: Device certificates, mutual TLS
  • Updates: Secure OTA update mechanism
  • Network segmentation: Isolate IoT devices from IT networks
  • Monitoring: Anomaly detection for compromised devices

Key Takeaways

IoT sensor networks enable smart buildings, industrial automation, and connected cities. Protocol selection depends on range, power, and data requirements. Sensor fusion improves accuracy and reliability. Edge computing reduces latency and bandwidth while improving privacy. Security must be built in from the beginning.
