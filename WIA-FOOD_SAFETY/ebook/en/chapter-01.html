Chapter 1: Food Safety Fundamentals & Hazards

Introduction to Food Safety

Food safety is the assurance that food will not cause harm to consumers when prepared and consumed according to its intended use. In the United States alone, foodborne illnesses affect approximately 48 million people annually, resulting in 128,000 hospitalizations and 3,000 deaths (CDC, 2023).

Key Concept: Food safety is not just about preventing illness—it's about protecting public health, building consumer trust, and ensuring economic stability of the food industry.

The Three Categories of Food Hazards

1. Biological Hazards

Biological hazards are the most common cause of foodborne illness. They include bacteria, viruses, parasites, and fungi that can contaminate food at any point in the supply chain.

Pathogen Common Sources Symptoms Onset Time
E. coli O157:H7 Undercooked beef, raw vegetables, unpasteurized milk Severe stomach cramps, diarrhea (often bloody), vomiting 1-8 days
Salmonella Poultry, eggs, raw fruits/vegetables Diarrhea, fever, abdominal cramps 6-72 hours
Listeria monocytogenes Deli meats, soft cheeses, raw sprouts Fever, muscle aches, headache, confusion 1-4 weeks
Campylobacter Raw/undercooked poultry, unpasteurized milk Diarrhea, cramping, fever 2-5 days

Case Study: 2018 Romaine Lettuce E. coli Outbreak

In 2018, contaminated romaine lettuce from Yuma, Arizona caused an E. coli O157:H7 outbreak affecting 36 states. The outbreak resulted in:

  • 210 confirmed cases
  • 96 hospitalizations
  • 5 deaths
  • Economic losses exceeding $100 million

The Problem: Traditional traceability systems took 6+ days to identify the source, allowing widespread distribution of contaminated lettuce.

2. Chemical Hazards

Chemical hazards in food can occur naturally or be introduced during production, processing, or storage. Common chemical hazards include:

  • Pesticides: Residues on fruits and vegetables exceeding safe limits
  • Heavy Metals: Lead, mercury, cadmium, arsenic in fish, rice, and water
  • Allergens: Undeclared milk, eggs, peanuts, tree nuts, soy, wheat, fish, shellfish
  • Food Additives: Excessive use of preservatives, colorings, or flavorings
  • Cleaning Chemicals: Residues from sanitizers and detergents

3. Physical Hazards

Physical hazards are foreign objects that can cause injury or choking. Examples include:

  • Glass: From broken light bulbs, bottles, or jars
  • Metal: Staples, nails, machinery fragments
  • Plastic: Packaging materials, gloves, equipment parts
  • Stones: In grains, dried beans, or vegetables
  • Bone fragments: In meat products

Risk Assessment

Food safety professionals use risk assessment to prioritize hazards based on two factors:

  1. Likelihood: How likely is this hazard to occur? (Low, Medium, High)
  2. Severity: How serious would the consequences be? (Minor, Moderate, Severe)

Risk = Likelihood × Severity

Hazard Likelihood Severity Risk Level
E. coli in ground beef Medium Severe HIGH
Glass fragments in jarred products Low Severe MEDIUM
Mild pesticide residue on apples Medium Minor LOW
