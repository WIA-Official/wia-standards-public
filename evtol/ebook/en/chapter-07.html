7.1 Manufacturing Strategy Overview

7.2 Composite Structures Manufacturing

Automated Fiber Placement (AFP)

Robots lay carbon fiber tape onto molds following computer-controlled paths. 16-32 tows of pre-impregnated (pre-preg) carbon fiber are placed simultaneously at speeds up to 1 meter/second. Laser heating activates resin adhesion. AFP achieves repeatable 0.1mm accuracy while reducing labor 70% versus hand layup.

Joby's wing manufacturing uses AFP for primary structures. The system operates 24/7, producing multiple wing sets daily. Initial robot programming takes weeks, but once validated, produces thousands of identical parts with minimal variation—critical for certification compliance.

Autoclave Curing

Laid-up parts cure in autoclaves at 350°F and 90 psi pressure for 4-8 hours. Pressure consolidates layers eliminating voids; heat crosslinks resin creating structural strength. Large autoclaves cost $2-5 million but are essential for certified aerospace composites.

Out-of-autoclave (OOA) processes using vacuum bagging save cost and energy but require careful validation. Some eVTOL manufacturers use OOA for non-critical structures (fairings, interior panels) while autoclaving primary structural components (wings, fuselage).

Quality Inspection

  • Ultrasonic Testing: High-frequency sound waves detect voids, delaminations, or foreign objects in cured composites
  • Thermography: Infrared cameras identify defects during curing through differential heat signatures
  • X-Ray CT Scanning: 3D imaging reveals internal structure, critical for complex joints and thick sections
  • Visual Inspection: Trained technicians examine surface finish, fiber alignment, resin content

7.3 Battery Pack Assembly

Battery pack manufacturing borrows heavily from automotive industry while meeting aviation safety standards:

Cell Assembly

  1. Cell Testing (100%): Every cell undergoes capacity, internal resistance, and voltage testing. 0.5-1% rejected for out-of-spec performance
  2. Module Assembly: 10-20 cells arranged in modules with busbars, sensors, and thermal interfaces. Automated welding or bonding
  3. Module Testing: Electrical, thermal, and mechanical validation before pack integration
  4. Pack Integration: Modules mounted in structural housing with BMS, cooling plumbing, high-voltage connectors
  5. Final Testing: Charge/discharge cycles, thermal performance, isolation testing, vibration qualification

Safety Features

  • Thermal Propagation Barriers: Fire-resistant materials between cells prevent cascading failures
  • Pressure Relief Vents: Controlled venting prevents explosive cell rupture
  • High-Voltage Isolation: Multiple layers of insulation with continuous monitoring
  • Crash Protection: Energy-absorbing structures shield battery from impact forces

7.5 Final Assembly

Final assembly brings together major components on moving production line inspired by automotive manufacturing:

Assembly Stations

  1. Fuselage Integration (2 days): Install seats, avionics, wiring harnesses, flight controls
  2. Wing Attachment (1 day): Mate wings to fuselage, install propulsion systems, connect electrical
  3. Systems Integration (2 days): Complete wiring, plumbing, install batteries, calibrate sensors
  4. Ground Testing (3 days): Power-up, software loading, functional tests, calibrations
  5. Paint and Finish (2 days): Final paint, decals, protective coatings, interior installation
  6. Flight Testing (2-3 days): Production test flights, final adjustments, sign-off

Total assembly cycle: 12-15 days per aircraft at mature production rates. Contrast with Boeing 737: 9-12 days for aircraft with 50X more parts and 100X more labor hours—demonstrating eVTOL design simplicity.

7.6 Supply Chain Management

eVTOL manufacturers must balance aerospace reliability with automotive cost efficiency in supply chains:

  • Dual Sourcing: Critical components have two qualified suppliers preventing single-point failures
  • Long-Term Agreements: 5-10 year contracts with volume commitments secure capacity and pricing
  • Supplier Development: Technical support helping suppliers meet aerospace quality standards
  • Vertical Integration: In-house manufacturing of truly differentiating components (motors, flight computers)
  • Global Sourcing: Components from lowest-cost regions while maintaining quality (batteries from Asia, avionics from US/Europe)

7.7 Quality Management Systems

AS9100 aerospace quality standard requires:

  • Process Documentation: Every manufacturing step documented with work instructions, inspections, acceptance criteria
  • Traceability: Every part tracked from raw material to installation in specific aircraft serial number
  • Calibration: All measurement equipment calibrated regularly against NIST-traceable standards
  • Non-Conformance Management: Documented investigation, corrective action, preventive action for any defects
  • Continuous Improvement: Statistical process control, root cause analysis, Lean/Six Sigma methodologies

Archer and Joby combine AS9100 with Toyota Production System lean manufacturing principles. Daily production meetings review metrics: first-pass yield, cycle time, quality escapes. Cross-functional teams rapidly solve issues before they impact multiple aircraft.

7.8 Scaling Production

Path from prototype to mass production involves staged facility expansion:

Phase 1: Low-Rate Production (2024-2026)

  • Produce 10-50 aircraft/year in pilot production facility
  • Validate manufacturing processes, identify improvement opportunities
  • Train workforce, develop supplier base
  • Investment: $50-100 million

Phase 2: Full-Rate Production (2027-2030)

  • Dedicated manufacturing campus producing 1,000-2,000 aircraft/year
  • Automated material handling, robotic assembly
  • Co-located supplier park for just-in-time delivery
  • Investment: $500 million - $1 billion

Phase 3: Global Expansion (2030+)

  • Regional manufacturing in major markets (US, Europe, Asia)
  • Capacity 5,000+ aircraft/year globally
  • Local supply chains, reduced shipping costs and emissions
  • Investment: $2-3 billion

Key Takeaways

  • Volume Challenge: eVTOL requires 10-100X production volumes versus traditional aerospace, demanding automotive-inspired manufacturing methods.
  • Automated Composites: Automated fiber placement and autoclave curing enable repeatable high-quality carbon fiber structures at scale.
  • Battery Manufacturing: Automotive battery production adapted for aviation safety requirements with 100% cell testing and thermal propagation barriers.
  • In-House Motors: Critical propulsion components manufactured internally for IP protection and quality control with rigorous testing.
  • Flow Production: 12-15 day assembly cycles using moving production lines contrast with months-long traditional aerospace builds.
  • Hybrid Quality Systems: AS9100 aerospace rigor combined with Toyota Production System lean principles for efficiency without compromising safety.
  • Staged Scaling: Production ramps from 10-50 units/year (pilot production) to 1,000-2,000 (full-rate) to 5,000+ (global expansion) over 2024-2030.
