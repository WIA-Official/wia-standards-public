Chapter 5

Job Transformation in the AI Era

Why Korean Youth Are Hit 3x Harder Than Global Average

Introduction: The Age of Paradox

Three years have passed since ChatGPT was unveiled to the world on November 30, 2022. In that time, dramatic changes have occurred in labor markets worldwide. The numbers presented in the World Economic Forum's "Future of Jobs Report 2025" are shocking: By 2030, 170 million new jobs will be created globally, but 92 million jobs will disappear. The net increase is only 78 million.

+78M
Net Global Job Increase by 2030 (170M Created - 92M Lost)

But the real story is hidden behind the numbers. According to McKinsey's analysis, by 2030, 30% of work hours in the U.S. economy could be automated, and 3.75 million workers will need to transition careers. This is the most rapid labor market restructuring since the Industrial Revolution.

The Paradox of Job Transformation

We've believed that technological advancement replaces simple labor and increases demand for high-skilled workers. But the AI era shows the opposite phenomenon.

Stage 1: Collapse of Formalized Knowledge - AI first replaces "the ability to quickly find predetermined answers"

Stage 2: Rise of Tacit Knowledge - "Tacit knowledge" accumulated through experience is not easily replaced by AI

Stage 3: Widening Generational Gap - Early-career youth lose jobs while mid-to-late career seniors gain value

Korea's Unique Situation

The Bank of Korea's report "AI Expansion and Youth Employment Decline" released in October 2025 reveals shocking facts: Over the 3 years from July 2022 to July 2025, Korea's youth (ages 15-29) lost 211,000 jobs. Of these, 98.6% (208,000) disappeared from AI high-exposure industries.

-211K
Korean Youth Jobs Lost in 3 Years (98.6% from AI High-Exposure Industries)

Even more surprising is that during the same period, jobs for those over 50 increased by 209,000. Of these, 69.9% (146,000) grew in AI high-exposure industries.

1. Regional Job Change Disparities

North America: The Frontline of Speed

North America is taking the most aggressive stance on AI automation adoption. Automation adoption rate reached 70% in 2025. In the U.S. alone, 77,999 people lost jobs due to AI-related restructuring at 342 tech companies in 2025 — an average of 491 people losing jobs to AI daily.

Asia: China's Robot Revolution and India's Service Transition

China has maintained #1 in industrial robot market since 2013, with half of all global robot installations occurring in China. Over 90% of Chinese companies recognize AI and robots as core transformation technologies. India is moving in a completely different direction — U.S. companies are hiring low-cost skilled workers there, shifting significant back-office operations to India.

2. New Job Creation: The Reality of 170 Million

The 170 million new jobs predicted by WEF are not fiction. Already in 2025, 350,000 AI-related new positions have been created. The question is: who are these jobs for?

Prompt Engineer: Average salary $136,141 in 2025, top 10% exceed $200,000
AI Ethics Officer: $120,000-180,000
AI Product Manager: $130,000-180,000

However, entry barriers are high: 77% of AI jobs require a master's degree, 18% require a PhD. Only 8% of positions are accessible with just a high school education.

3. The Reskilling Paradox

Reskilling investment for the AI era is at an all-time high. U.S. companies spent $101.8 billion on training in the past 12 months. Amazon has invested over $1.2 billion to train more than 700,000 people globally. But the question remains: is this investment reaching those who need it?

According to NASSCOM, only 10% of emerging market workers have access to AI training. In Europe, 45% of the population lacks even basic digital skills. The WEF estimates 59% of the global workforce will need reskilling by 2030, but only 1 in 6 will have access to appropriate training opportunities before 2027.

Key Takeaways

  • AI replaces formalized knowledge first, making early-career workers more vulnerable
  • Korean youth hit 3x harder than global average (211K jobs lost in 3 years)
  • New AI jobs require high qualifications (77% need master's degrees)
  • Reskilling investments aren't reaching those who need them most
  • Data abundance, not job complexity, determines automation speed
