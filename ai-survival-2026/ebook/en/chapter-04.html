Global AI Regulation and Ethics

Three Paradigms and Korea's Choice

Introduction: The Era When Regulation Determines Innovation

In 2025, artificial intelligence regulation has become a strategic weapon that determines national competitiveness, going beyond simple legal issues. The European Union's AI Act went into effect in August 2024, establishing the most comprehensive AI regulatory system in history. The U.S. saw a 180-degree shift in regulatory philosophy with the change of administration, and China is completing a strict state-led control system.

However, this number indicates chaos rather than harmony. AI regulations in each country adopt different philosophies, different priorities, and different enforcement methods.

Three Paradigms of AI Regulation

1. EU's 'Regulation-First' (Risk-First Paradigm)
Philosophy: Risk prevention takes priority over innovation
Core: Risk-based classification + Pre-regulation
Strategy: Exporting global standards (Brussels Effect)

2. US's 'Market-First' (Market-First Paradigm)
Philosophy: Innovation over safety (varies by administration)
Core: Decentralization + Post-regulation
Strategy: Private-led + Federal-state mix

3. China's 'State-First' (State-First Paradigm)
Philosophy: Social stability and state control first
Core: Centralization + Content control
Strategy: Algorithm registration + Mandatory labeling

1. Global AI Regulation Status: Three Contrasting Strategies

1.1 European Union: World's First Comprehensive AI Regulation

The EU's AI Act came into effect on August 1, 2024, with phased implementation. Regulations are classified into 4 levels based on AI system risk: Unacceptable, High-risk, Limited, and Minimal.

Key dates: February 2, 2025 - Unacceptable AI systems banned; August 2, 2025 - GPAI model regulations begin; August 2, 2026 - Full high-risk AI system regulations; August 2, 2027 - Complete implementation.

1.2 United States: Deregulation and Innovation-First Policy Shift

U.S. AI regulation underwent a dramatic reversal in January 2025. On inauguration day, January 20, 2025, President Trump repealed the Biden administration's comprehensive AI Executive Order 14110.

Biden's EO emphasized AI safety, security, and trustworthiness with mandatory red team testing for high-risk AI models and enhanced cybersecurity protocols. Trump's new EO "Removing Barriers to American Leadership in Artificial Intelligence" presents an entirely different philosophy, focusing on removing regulatory barriers and maintaining America's global AI dominance.

1.3 China: Preemptive Regulation Centered on Content Control

China implemented the world's first administrative regulation on generative AI services on August 15, 2023 — one year ahead of the EU AI Act. Key features include algorithm registration requirements, content labeling obligations (effective September 2025), and strict content regulations prohibiting content that "incites subversion of state power" or "threatens national security."

2. Korea's AI Basic Law

Korea's AI Basic Law was promulgated on January 21, 2025, and will take effect on January 22, 2026. While borrowing the EU's high-risk AI concept, Korea uses its own term "high-impact AI," designating areas that significantly affect human life and rights, such as medical devices, transportation facilities, and hiring/loan screening.

However, Korea's maximum fine of 30 million won is significantly lower than the EU's maximum of 35 million euros, raising questions about effectiveness.

Key Takeaways

  • Global AI regulation divides into three paradigms: EU (Risk-First), US (Market-First), China (State-First)
  • EU AI Act represents the world's most comprehensive regulation with strict fines
  • US under Trump administration pivoted to deregulation
  • Korea's AI Basic Law emphasizes promotion over regulation
  • Companies need multi-compliance strategies for global operations
