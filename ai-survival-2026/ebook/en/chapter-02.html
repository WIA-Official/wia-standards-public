Introduction: The Paradox of an 8-Year-Old Technology Changing the World in 2025

In June 2017, Google published the paper "Attention is All You Need" — just 11 pages. Most researchers who read it at the time evaluated it as "interesting but practicality is unknown." Yet in 2025, the global AI market has reached $758 billion, with the Transformer architecture at its center. What's the secret behind this explosive annual growth rate of 29.2%?

What's more remarkable is that from 2017 to 2024, Transformer's core structure remained virtually unchanged. Self-attention, residual connections, layer normalization — these three basic building blocks are still the same. What changed were just fine-tuned optimizations: Pre-normalization for training stability, Rotary Positional Embeddings for position information, and Grouped-Query Attention for inference speed.

1. Transformer Architecture: From the 2017 Revolution to 2025 Evolution

1.1 Self-Attention: Why Was It Revolutionary?

The core of Transformer is the Self-Attention mechanism. While existing RNNs processed sentences sequentially and gradually forgot past information, Self-Attention allows every word in a sentence to directly reference each other.

For example, in the sentence "The judge issued a sentence," determining whether "sentence" is a legal term or a grammatical term requires simultaneously considering the context of "judge" and "issued." Self-Attention calculates the relationship of each word with all words in the sentence to accurately capture this context.

1.2 Multi-Head Attention: Seeing from Multiple Perspectives Simultaneously

Transformer goes one step further by introducing Multi-Head Attention. Using just one Attention mechanism could lead to bias toward specific patterns. So multiple Attention "heads" run in parallel, with each head learning different aspects of the sentence (positional relationships, grammatical structure, semantic relationships).

Key Insight: "More Heads ≠ Better Performance"

Initially, it was thought that using more Attention heads was always better. But recent research has revealed that qualitative diversity of heads is more important than quantity. Some heads focus on positional information (positional heads), some understand grammatical structure (syntactic heads), and others detect rare patterns (rare heads).

2. Large Language Models (LLM): The End of Parameter Wars and the Era of Efficiency

2.1 The Parameter Number Game: Is Bigger Always Better?

In the early 2020s, the AI industry entered "parameter wars." When GPT-3's 175 billion parameters showed amazing performance, everyone rushed to build bigger models. But by 2025, the tide has turned.

GPT-4's estimated parameters reach 1.7 trillion (MoE structure, 8 experts × 220B). But 2025 teaches us that parameter size alone doesn't win. For example, Mistral's Mixtral 8x7B has 46.7 billion total parameters, but actually uses only 12.9 billion parameters per token through its MoE structure, showing equal or better performance than models 5x larger.

2.3 MoE (Mixture of Experts): The Game Changer for Efficiency

The core trend in 2025 LLM design is MoE (Mixture of Experts). Traditional "dense" models always activate all parameters. In contrast, MoE selectively activates only relevant "expert" modules based on input.

For example, DeepSeek R1 has 67.1 billion parameters, but only 37 billion are actually activated per token. This achieves 20x cost efficiency relative to model size.

"The most important change in the 2025 AI market is the 'performance leveling' of models. As major benchmark scores improved by 18-67 percentage points in just one year, performance gaps between models are rapidly shrinking."

3. The Rise of Multimodal AI

In 2025, AI technology has entered the multimodal era beyond single modality. The transition from LLMs that only processed text to multimodal AI that simultaneously understands images, audio, and video represents a fundamental change in how AI perceives the world.
