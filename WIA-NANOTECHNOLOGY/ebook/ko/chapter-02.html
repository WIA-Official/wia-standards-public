2.1 Classification by Dimensionality

Nanomaterials are classified based on the number of dimensions confined to the nanoscale. This classification scheme, based on dimensionality, provides insight into the material's properties and potential applications. The four main categories are 0D, 1D, 2D, and 3D nanostructures.

Type Dimensions in Nanoscale Examples Key Properties
0D (Zero-dimensional) All three dimensions Quantum dots, nanoparticles, fullerenes Quantum confinement in all directions, discrete energy levels
1D (One-dimensional) Two dimensions Nanotubes, nanowires, nanorods Quantum confinement in two directions, high aspect ratio
2D (Two-dimensional) One dimension Graphene, nanosheets, thin films Quantum confinement in one direction, large surface area
3D (Three-dimensional) Bulk with nanofeatures Nanocomposites, nanoporous materials Bulk properties with nanoscale features, structural hierarchy

2.1.1 Zero-Dimensional (0D) Nanostructures

0D nanomaterials have all three dimensions confined to the nanoscale, resulting in complete quantum confinement. These materials exhibit atom-like properties with discrete energy levels rather than continuous energy bands. Examples include:

  • Quantum Dots: Semiconductor nanocrystals with size-tunable optical properties
  • Nanoparticles: Metallic or oxide particles with sizes from 1-100 nm
  • Fullerenes: Cage-like carbon molecules (C60, C70, etc.)
  • Nanoclusters: Small aggregates of atoms or molecules

2.1.2 One-Dimensional (1D) Nanostructures

1D nanomaterials have two dimensions in the nanoscale and one dimension significantly larger, creating high aspect ratio structures. These materials exhibit quantum confinement in the radial direction while allowing free electron movement along the length:

  • Carbon Nanotubes (CNTs): Single-walled or multi-walled cylindrical structures
  • Nanowires: Conductive wires with diameters in the nanometer range
  • Nanorods: Short, rod-shaped nanostructures
  • Nanofibers: Continuous fibers with nanoscale diameters

2.1.3 Two-Dimensional (2D) Nanostructures

2D nanomaterials have one dimension in the nanoscale (thickness) while the other two dimensions are much larger. These materials exhibit quantum confinement in the thickness direction:

  • Graphene: Single layer of carbon atoms in hexagonal lattice
  • Transition Metal Dichalcogenides (TMDs): MoS₂, WS₂, etc.
  • Nanosheets: Exfoliated or synthesized thin layers
  • Thin Films: Deposited layers with nanometer thickness

2.2 Carbon Nanotubes (CNTs)

Carbon nanotubes are cylindrical nanostructures composed of carbon atoms arranged in a hexagonal lattice. Discovered by Sumio Iijima in 1991, CNTs represent one of the most studied nanomaterials due to their exceptional mechanical, electrical, and thermal properties.

2.2.1 Structure and Types

CNTs can be visualized as rolled-up graphene sheets. The way the sheet is rolled determines the nanotube's properties and is characterized by a chiral vector:

Chiral Vector: Ch = na₁ + ma₂, where n and m are integers and a₁, a₂ are unit vectors of the graphene lattice. The (n,m) indices define the nanotube's structure and properties.
  • Armchair (n,n): Metallic conductors, symmetric structure
  • Zigzag (n,0): Can be metallic or semiconducting depending on n
  • Chiral (n,m): Most general form, usually semiconducting

2.2.2 Single-Walled vs. Multi-Walled CNTs

Property Single-Walled CNTs (SWCNTs) Multi-Walled CNTs (MWCNTs)
Structure Single graphene cylinder Multiple concentric cylinders
Diameter 0.4 - 3 nm 5 - 100 nm
Electrical Properties Metallic or semiconducting Generally metallic
Purity Easier to purify More defects between layers
Cost More expensive Less expensive, easier synthesis
Applications Electronics, sensors, composites Mechanical reinforcement, batteries, field emission

2.2.3 Exceptional Properties

  • Mechanical: Tensile strength ~100 GPa (100x stronger than steel), Young's modulus ~1 TPa
  • Electrical: Current density >10⁹ A/cm² (1000x better than copper)
  • Thermal: Thermal conductivity ~3000 W/m·K (better than diamond)
  • Chemical: Highly stable, resistant to chemical attack

2.2.4 Synthesis Methods

  • Arc Discharge: Vaporizes graphite electrodes in inert atmosphere, produces high-quality CNTs
  • Laser Ablation: Uses laser to vaporize graphite target, good for SWCNTs
  • Chemical Vapor Deposition (CVD): Most scalable method, grows CNTs on catalyst particles

2.2.5 Applications

  • Composite materials for aerospace and automotive industries
  • Transparent conductive films for displays and solar cells
  • Field emission displays and X-ray sources
  • Sensors for gases, biomolecules, and pressure
  • Energy storage in batteries and supercapacitors
  • Drug delivery and tissue engineering scaffolds

2.3 Graphene and 2D Materials

Graphene, isolated in 2004 by Andre Geim and Konstantin Novoselov (Nobel Prize 2010), is a single layer of carbon atoms arranged in a hexagonal lattice. It represents the ultimate 2D material and has sparked a revolution in materials science.

2.3.1 Structure and Properties

Graphene is the building block for all graphitic materials. It can be wrapped into 0D fullerenes, rolled into 1D nanotubes, or stacked into 3D graphite. Key properties include:

  • Atomic Thickness: One atom thick (~0.34 nm)
  • Mechanical: Strongest material known (strength 130 GPa, Young's modulus 1 TPa)
  • Electrical: Ultra-high electron mobility (>200,000 cm²/V·s at room temperature)
  • Optical: Absorbs only 2.3% of white light despite being one atom thick
  • Thermal: Thermal conductivity >5000 W/m·K
  • Surface Area: Theoretical surface area of 2630 m²/g

2.3.2 Synthesis Methods

Method Quality Scalability Cost Use Cases
Mechanical Exfoliation Highest Low High (lab scale) Research, fundamental studies
CVD on Metal High Medium-High Medium Electronics, large-area films
Liquid-Phase Exfoliation Medium High Low Composites, coatings, inks
Reduction of Graphene Oxide Low-Medium Very High Very Low Energy storage, composites

2.3.3 Beyond Graphene: Other 2D Materials

Graphene's success has led to discovery and development of numerous other 2D materials:

  • Hexagonal Boron Nitride (h-BN): "White graphene," insulator with high thermal conductivity
  • Transition Metal Dichalcogenides (TMDs): MoS₂, WS₂, WSe₂ - semiconductors for electronics and optoelectronics
  • Black Phosphorus: Direct bandgap semiconductor, high carrier mobility
  • MXenes: Transition metal carbides/nitrides, excellent for energy storage
  • 2D Oxides: Various metal oxides with unique electronic and optical properties

2.3.4 Applications of Graphene

  • Electronics: High-frequency transistors, flexible displays, touch screens
  • Energy: Supercapacitor electrodes, battery anodes, solar cells
  • Composites: Mechanical reinforcement, thermal management, anti-corrosion coatings
  • Sensors: Gas sensors, biosensors, strain sensors
  • Water Treatment: Desalination membranes, filtration
  • Photonics: Optical modulators, photodetectors, transparent conductors

2.4 Quantum Dots

Quantum dots (QDs) are semiconductor nanocrystals that exhibit quantum confinement effects in all three dimensions. Their unique size-tunable optical properties make them valuable for displays, imaging, and photovoltaics.

2.4.1 Structure and Quantum Confinement

When a semiconductor crystal is reduced to the nanoscale (typically 2-10 nm), its electronic structure changes from continuous energy bands to discrete, atom-like energy levels. This quantum confinement leads to size-dependent optical properties.

Key Phenomenon: As QD size decreases, the bandgap increases, causing a blue-shift in absorption and emission spectra. This allows precise tuning of emission wavelength by controlling particle size during synthesis.

2.4.2 Core-Shell Structures

To improve optical properties and stability, QDs are often synthesized with core-shell structures:

  • Type I: Shell has larger bandgap than core (e.g., CdSe/ZnS), confines both electrons and holes in core
  • Type II: Staggered bandgap alignment, spatial separation of electrons and holes
  • Alloyed: Graded composition reduces lattice strain and improves stability

2.4.3 Common Quantum Dot Materials

Material Emission Range Advantages Challenges
CdSe/ZnS Visible (450-650 nm) High quantum yield, narrow emission Cadmium toxicity, RoHS restrictions
InP/ZnS Visible (500-700 nm) Cd-free, less toxic Lower quantum yield, broader emission
PbS, PbSe Near-IR (900-2000 nm) IR applications, solution processing Lead toxicity, air sensitivity
Carbon Dots Blue-green (400-550 nm) Low toxicity, simple synthesis Low quantum yield, broad emission
Perovskite QDs Visible (400-800 nm) High efficiency, facile synthesis Stability issues, lead content

2.4.4 Applications

  • Displays: QLED TVs with enhanced color gamut and brightness
  • Bioimaging: Fluorescent labels for cellular and tissue imaging
  • Solar Cells: Multiple exciton generation, solution-processed photovoltaics
  • LEDs: Efficient electroluminescent devices
  • Lasers: Low-threshold lasing, tunable wavelength
  • Photodetectors: Sensitive light detection across broad spectrum

2.5 Metal Nanoparticles

Metal nanoparticles exhibit unique optical, electronic, and catalytic properties distinct from their bulk counterparts. The three most widely studied are gold, silver, and titanium dioxide.

2.5.1 Gold Nanoparticles (AuNPs)

Gold nanoparticles display strong optical absorption due to localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR), where incident light causes collective oscillation of conduction electrons. Properties include:

  • Size-dependent color: 5nm (red) to 100nm (purple-blue)
  • Biocompatibility: Non-toxic, easily functionalized
  • Chemical stability: Inert to oxidation and most chemicals
  • Applications: Drug delivery, photothermal therapy, biosensors, catalysis

2.5.2 Silver Nanoparticles (AgNPs)

Silver nanoparticles are valued for their antibacterial properties and plasmonic behavior:

  • Antimicrobial activity: Releases Ag⁺ ions that disrupt bacterial cell membranes
  • Strong LSPR: Intense optical properties for sensing applications
  • Conductivity: Used in conductive inks and printed electronics
  • Applications: Wound dressings, food packaging, water purification, SERS substrates

2.5.3 Titanium Dioxide Nanoparticles (TiO₂)

TiO₂ nanoparticles are semiconducting metal oxides with photocatalytic properties:

  • Crystal structures: Anatase (more active), rutile, brookite
  • Photocatalysis: Generates reactive oxygen species under UV light
  • UV absorption: Transparent sunscreen ingredient
  • Applications: Photocatalytic water splitting, air purification, self-cleaning surfaces, dye-sensitized solar cells

2.6 Nanocomposites and Hybrid Materials

Nanocomposites combine nanomaterials with bulk matrices to create materials with enhanced or novel properties. The nanoscale reinforcement provides improvements that exceed simple rule-of-mixtures predictions due to interfacial effects and nanoscale phenomena.

2.6.1 Types of Nanocomposites

  • Polymer Nanocomposites: CNTs or graphene in polymers for mechanical reinforcement, conductivity
  • Metal Matrix Nanocomposites: Ceramic nanoparticles in metals for strength and wear resistance
  • Ceramic Nanocomposites: Nanoparticles in ceramic matrix for toughness improvement
  • Hybrid Organic-Inorganic: Combine properties of both components (e.g., perovskite solar cells)

2.6.2 Property Enhancements

Property Enhancement Mechanism Example
Mechanical Strength Load transfer to high-strength nanofillers CNT-reinforced epoxy: 3-5x strength increase
Thermal Conductivity Percolating network of conductive nanofillers Graphene-polymer: 10-100x increase
Electrical Conductivity Percolation above critical volume fraction CNT-polymer: Insulator to conductor transition
Barrier Properties Tortuous path for diffusing molecules Clay nanocomposites: Improved gas barrier
Optical Properties Plasmonic or quantum effects QD-polymer: Luminescent materials

2.6.3 Fabrication Methods

  • In-situ polymerization: Nanoparticles present during polymer synthesis
  • Solution mixing: Disperse nanoparticles in polymer solution, then cast
  • Melt blending: Mix nanoparticles into molten polymer
  • Layer-by-layer assembly: Sequential deposition of components

  • Nanomaterials are classified by dimensionality: 0D (quantum dots), 1D (nanotubes), 2D (graphene), 3D (nanocomposites)
  • Carbon nanotubes exhibit exceptional mechanical (100 GPa strength), electrical (10⁹ A/cm² current density), and thermal (3000 W/m·K) properties
  • CNTs can be metallic or semiconducting depending on their chiral vector (n,m) indices
  • Graphene is the strongest material known with electron mobility >200,000 cm²/V·s and thermal conductivity >5000 W/m·K
  • Multiple synthesis methods exist for graphene: mechanical exfoliation (highest quality), CVD (scalable), liquid-phase exfoliation (economical)
  • Quantum dots exhibit size-dependent bandgap due to quantum confinement, enabling tunable emission wavelengths
  • Core-shell QD structures (e.g., CdSe/ZnS) improve quantum yield and photostability
  • Gold nanoparticles show LSPR-based color changes and are biocompatible for medical applications
  • Silver nanoparticles provide antimicrobial activity through Ag⁺ ion release
  • TiO₂ nanoparticles are photocatalytic semiconductors used in environmental remediation and solar cells
  • Nanocomposites combine nanomaterials with bulk matrices to achieve property enhancements beyond rule-of-mixtures predictions
  • Beyond graphene, numerous 2D materials exist including h-BN, TMDs, and MXenes with diverse applications
