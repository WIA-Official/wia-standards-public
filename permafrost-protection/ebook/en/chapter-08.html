8.1 International Policy Landscape

Permafrost protection requires coordinated action across scales from local to global. While no single international treaty specifically addresses permafrost, several frameworks are relevant:

United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC)

Paris Agreement and Permafrost
  • Global temperature goals: Limiting warming to 1.5-2°C is essential for permafrost protection; each additional 0.5°C of warming means significantly more permafrost loss
  • Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs): Countries set emission reduction targets; permafrost carbon feedback makes targets harder to achieve
  • Adaptation frameworks: Paris Agreement recognizes adaptation as equal priority to mitigation; permafrost adaptation eligible for international support
  • Loss and damage: Permafrost communities experiencing irreversible impacts; Loss and Damage fund could support relocation and adaptation
  • Gap: Permafrost carbon feedback not fully incorporated in IPCC models or national inventories; needs better integration

Arctic Council

The Arctic Council is the leading intergovernmental forum for Arctic issues, comprising eight Arctic states and six indigenous Permanent Participants:

  • Permafrost monitoring: Arctic Monitoring and Assessment Programme (AMAP) coordinates permafrost observation and assessment
  • Knowledge synthesis: Regular assessment reports on permafrost status and trends (e.g., Snow, Water, Ice and Permafrost in the Arctic - SWIPA)
  • Best practices: Working groups develop guidelines for sustainable development in permafrost regions
  • Limitations: Council is a forum for cooperation, not a regulatory body; recommendations are non-binding
  • Opportunity: Could develop Arctic-wide permafrost protection framework or guidelines

Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD)

  • Protected area targets: Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework aims for 30% of land protected by 2030 (30×30); could drive permafrost ecosystem protection
  • Ecosystem-based adaptation: CBD promotes EbA approaches relevant to permafrost regions
  • Indigenous rights: Strong recognition of indigenous peoples and local communities (IPLCs) in conservation

8.2 National and Regional Policies

Countries with substantial permafrost have developed various policy approaches:

National Policy Examples

Country Key Policies/Programs Strengths Gaps/Challenges

Nordic Countries Coordinated mountain permafrost monitoring; climate adaptation strategies; Saami rights recognition Good coordination across countries; integration with broader climate policy; indigenous rights frameworks Limited permafrost area (mainly alpine); less urgency than Arctic nations

Policy Gaps and Needs

Despite progress, significant policy gaps remain:

  • Fragmented governance: Permafrost crosses jurisdictions; need for coordinated regional approaches
  • Inadequate funding: Adaptation costs in billions; current funding in tens to hundreds of millions
  • Short-term focus: Political cycles favor short-term projects; permafrost protection requires multi-decade commitment
  • Limited regulation: Few countries have mandatory climate risk assessment for infrastructure in permafrost regions
  • Carbon accounting gaps: Permafrost carbon emissions not consistently included in national greenhouse gas inventories
  • Indigenous rights implementation: While many countries recognize indigenous rights on paper, implementation often inadequate

8.3 Research Priorities

Advancing permafrost protection requires continued research addressing critical knowledge gaps:

High-Priority Research Areas

1. Permafrost Carbon Feedback

Critical Questions:

  • What are regional patterns of carbon release?
  • What controls CO₂ vs. CH₄ emissions?
  • How do abrupt thaw processes affect carbon release?
  • Can permafrost carbon emissions be meaningfully reduced?

Status: Active research but high uncertainty remains

2. Abrupt Thaw Processes

Critical Questions:

  • What are spatial extent and rates of abrupt thaw?
  • How to predict thermokarst development?
  • Can abrupt thaw be prevented or slowed?
  • What are ecosystem and carbon impacts?

Status: Growing recognition; needs better quantification

3. Infrastructure Adaptation

Critical Questions:

  • Effectiveness of adaptation technologies under future climate?
  • Cost-benefit analysis of adaptation vs. relocation?
  • Design standards for climate uncertainty?
  • Lifecycle assessment of different foundation types?

Status: Engineering research advancing but needs climate integration

4. Improved Projections

Critical Questions:

  • How much permafrost will be lost under different scenarios?
  • Regional patterns of permafrost change?
  • Timescales of permafrost response?
  • Tipping points and non-linear changes?

Status: Models improving; need better ground ice representation

5. Ecosystem Responses

Critical Questions:

  • How will species and communities respond to permafrost loss?
  • What are ecosystem tipping points?
  • Effectiveness of ecosystem-based adaptation?
  • How to maintain biodiversity as climate changes?

Status: Descriptive understanding growing; predictive capacity limited

6. Social Dimensions

Critical Questions:

  • What are full social costs of permafrost degradation?
  • How to support community adaptation and relocation?
  • How to integrate indigenous knowledge effectively?
  • Mental health and cultural impacts?

Status: Understudied; needs more social science research

Research Infrastructure Needs

Essential Research Capabilities:
  • Expanded monitoring networks: More sites, better spatial coverage, long-term commitment to measurements
  • Coordinated experiments: Standardized experimental manipulations across sites (e.g., warming experiments, vegetation removal trials)
  • Improved modeling frameworks: Earth system models with better permafrost representation; high-resolution regional models
  • Data infrastructure: Open access databases; standardized data formats; data preservation and curation
  • Synthesis and integration: Programs synthesizing diverse data; meta-analyses; model-data intercomparison
  • Interdisciplinary collaboration: Bridging natural sciences, engineering, and social sciences
  • Indigenous research partnerships: Community-based research respecting indigenous knowledge and priorities
  • Sustained funding: Multi-year, multi-decadal commitments necessary for permafrost research

8.4 The WIA Permafrost Protection Standard

The World Certification Industry Association (WIA) Permafrost Protection Standard provides a comprehensive framework for permafrost protection integrating science, engineering, policy, and ethics. The standard is built on the principle of 弘益人間 (Hongik Ingan) — "Benefit All Humanity."

WIA Permafrost Protection Standard: Core Components

1. MONITORING AND ASSESSMENT

All permafrost regions and infrastructure should have:

  • Baseline permafrost characterization (temperature, ice content, active layer thickness)
  • Regular monitoring using standardized protocols
  • Data contribution to international networks (GTN-P, CALM)
  • Open data sharing following FAIR principles
  • Integration of indigenous knowledge and community observations
2. CLIMATE RISK ASSESSMENT

All infrastructure and development projects in permafrost regions must include:

  • Comprehensive permafrost assessment at project planning stage
  • Climate change projections for project lifetime (50-100 years)
  • Multiple scenario analysis (optimistic to pessimistic climate futures)
  • Cumulative effects assessment including existing disturbance
  • Adaptive management plan with decision triggers
3. ENGINEERING STANDARDS

Infrastructure design and construction must:

  • Follow established permafrost engineering codes and best practices
  • Incorporate climate adaptation measures appropriate to site conditions
  • Use proven permafrost protection technologies (thermosyphons, insulation, etc.)
  • Include post-construction monitoring and maintenance plans
  • Design for future climate conditions, not just current baseline
4. ECOSYSTEM PROTECTION

Conservation and sustainable development must:

  • Protect intact ecosystems through expanded protected areas (target: 30% by 2030)
  • Minimize disturbance to moss/organic layers and vegetation
  • Restore degraded sites using evidence-based techniques
  • Maintain landscape connectivity for climate-driven species movement
  • Implement ecosystem-based adaptation strategies
5. INDIGENOUS RIGHTS AND KNOWLEDGE

All permafrost protection efforts must:

  • Respect indigenous rights to land, resources, and self-determination
  • Obtain Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC) for projects
  • Integrate traditional ecological knowledge with scientific understanding
  • Support community-led monitoring and conservation initiatives
  • Ensure equitable benefit-sharing from research and development
6. CLIMATE MITIGATION

Recognizing that local protection is insufficient without global action:

  • Advocate for ambitious greenhouse gas emission reductions aligned with Paris Agreement
  • Include permafrost carbon feedback in climate policy and carbon budgets
  • Prioritize clean energy transition in permafrost regions
  • Oppose new fossil fuel development in undisturbed permafrost areas
  • Support carbon removal technologies as complement to emissions reduction
7. FINANCE AND CAPACITY BUILDING

Adequate resources and capabilities must be provided:

  • Sustained funding for permafrost monitoring, research, and adaptation
  • International support for adaptation in developing countries and indigenous communities
  • Training programs for permafrost engineering and management
  • Technology transfer of permafrost protection methods
  • Insurance and risk-sharing mechanisms for permafrost infrastructure
8. GOVERNANCE AND COOPERATION

Effective institutions and collaboration:

  • Strengthen Arctic Council and other international cooperation mechanisms
  • Develop transboundary agreements for shared permafrost regions
  • Coordinate national and subnational permafrost policies
  • Engage all stakeholders (government, indigenous peoples, industry, NGOs, scientists)
  • Establish clear accountability and enforcement mechanisms

8.5 Implementation Pathways

Translating the WIA Standard into action requires coordinated efforts across multiple sectors and scales:

Near-Term Actions (2025-2030)

  • Expand monitoring: Fill geographic gaps in permafrost monitoring; establish baseline measurements in under-monitored regions
  • Update building codes: Mandate climate risk assessment and adaptation measures for all new infrastructure in permafrost regions
  • Protect vulnerable areas: Designate additional protected areas prioritizing permafrost conservation; target 20% protection by 2030
  • Support communities: Increase adaptation funding for indigenous and remote communities; initiate planned relocations where necessary
  • Enhance research: Launch coordinated research programs addressing high-priority questions; establish synthesis centers
  • Mainstream permafrost in climate policy: Include permafrost carbon in national inventories; account for feedback in climate targets

Medium-Term Actions (2030-2050)

  • Scale up adaptation: Widespread implementation of infrastructure protection measures; retrofit vulnerable existing infrastructure
  • Ecosystem restoration: Large-scale restoration programs for degraded permafrost ecosystems
  • Achieve protection targets: 30% of permafrost regions in protected areas with effective management
  • Technology advancement: Deploy next-generation permafrost protection technologies; improve cost-effectiveness
  • Complete relocations: Support communities through planned relocation processes; ensure cultural continuity
  • Model improvement: Achieve reliable regional permafrost projections; reduce uncertainty in carbon feedback estimates

Long-Term Vision (2050-2100)

  • Climate stabilization: Limit warming to well below 2°C, ideally 1.5°C; preserve majority of current permafrost
  • Adapted infrastructure: All permafrost region infrastructure resilient to projected climate changes
  • Ecosystem resilience: Permafrost ecosystems maintained through active conservation and management
  • Thriving communities: Indigenous and local communities prospering in permafrost regions with cultural continuity
  • Complete understanding: Comprehensive knowledge of permafrost systems enabling accurate prediction and effective management
  • Global cooperation: Sustained international collaboration on permafrost protection as model for environmental governance

8.6 The Critical Decade

The next decade (2025-2035) is critical for permafrost protection. Decisions and actions taken now will determine trajectories for centuries to come. Permafrost takes thousands of years to form but can degrade in decades. Once lost, permafrost cannot be restored on human timescales.

A Call to Action

Protecting permafrost is protecting our global climate, unique ecosystems, and vulnerable communities.

Every stakeholder has a role to play:

  • Governments: Adopt and implement comprehensive permafrost protection policies; fund research and adaptation
  • Researchers: Address critical knowledge gaps; share findings openly; engage with communities and policymakers
  • Engineers: Apply best practices; innovate new solutions; design for changing climate
  • Communities: Share observations and knowledge; advocate for rights and resources; lead local conservation
  • Industry: Minimize disturbance; use proven protection technologies; invest in adaptation
  • NGOs: Advocate for protection; support communities; raise awareness
  • Individuals: Support climate action; amplify indigenous voices; demand accountability

The WIA Permafrost Protection Standard provides the framework. Implementation requires collective action.

Conclusion

Permafrost protection sits at the intersection of climate science, engineering, ecology, social justice, and international cooperation. This ebook has explored the science of permafrost, the changes already underway, the technologies for monitoring and protection, and the policy frameworks needed for comprehensive action.

Key messages bear repeating:

  • Permafrost is changing rapidly: Warming, thawing, and degrading at rates unprecedented in human history
  • The stakes are global: Permafrost carbon feedback could significantly accelerate climate change; impacts extend far beyond the Arctic
  • Solutions exist but require action: We have the knowledge and tools to protect critical infrastructure and ecosystems, but implementation lags
  • Indigenous peoples must be centered: Those who have lived sustainably in permafrost regions for millennia must lead protection efforts
  • Local action is necessary but insufficient: Even the best site-scale protection cannot succeed without global climate action
  • The window is closing: Delays in action mean more permafrost lost, more carbon released, more communities displaced
  • Hope remains: Strong climate mitigation combined with targeted adaptation can still protect most permafrost and the communities and ecosystems dependent on it

The WIA Permafrost Protection Standard provides a roadmap. Now comes the essential work of implementation—in communities across the Arctic and high mountains, in research laboratories and engineering firms, in government offices and international forums. The permafrost that has been frozen for millennia can still be protected, but only with urgent, sustained, and coordinated action.
