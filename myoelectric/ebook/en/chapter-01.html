Chapter 1: Introduction to Myoelectric Prosthetics

1.1 What is Myoelectric Control?

Myoelectric control uses surface electromyography (sEMG) — the electrical signals produced by skeletal muscle contraction — to control powered prosthetic limbs and exoskeletons. When a muscle contracts, motor units fire action potentials that sum into a detectable surface potential of 50–5000 μV at the skin surface. Electrodes placed on the residual limb capture these signals and translate them into prosthetic commands.

The clinical history of myoelectric prosthetics begins with externally powered arm prostheses in the late 1950s. Commercial myoelectric devices became widely available with the Ottobock System Electric Hand in the early 1970s. Subsequent introduction of the Hudgins TD4 time-domain feature set and LDA classifiers transformed the field from simple two-site amplitude control to multi-grip intelligent hands with 8–16 gesture classes.

1.4 Signal Generation and Physiology

The sEMG signal originates from motor unit action potentials (MUAPs). Each motor unit (a motor neuron and all muscle fibers it innervates) fires in the 5–50 Hz range during voluntary contraction. Surface electrodes capture the spatial and temporal superposition of hundreds to thousands of MUAPs — the interference pattern.

Signal characteristics:

  • Amplitude: 50–5000 μV (skin surface); increases linearly with muscle force up to ~60% MVC (Maximum Voluntary Contraction).
  • Frequency content: 20–500 Hz dominant; peak spectral frequency typically 80–150 Hz; median frequency decreases with fatigue.
  • SNR challenges: Power line interference (50/60 Hz), motion artifacts (below 20 Hz), amplifier noise. Differential electrodes with CMRR ≥ 80 dB required.

1.5 WIA Myoelectric Standard Overview

The WIA Myoelectric Standard (WIA-MYO) provides a four-phase interoperability framework:

  • Phase 1 — Data Format: JSON schema for EMG frames, Hudgins feature extraction, classifier output, session metadata, ISO 22523 classification.
  • Phase 2 — API Interface: OpenAPI 3.1 REST endpoints for device management, real-time streaming, classifier model deployment.
  • Phase 3 — Protocol: ROS 2 topics, BLE GATT profiles for wireless electrodes, USB HID for wired systems, Bluetooth Low Energy 5.4.
  • Phase 4 — Integration: Hospital EMR (FHIR R5), prosthetic fitting clinical workflows, Coapt COMPLETE CONTROL API, Ottobock OrthoConnect SDK.
