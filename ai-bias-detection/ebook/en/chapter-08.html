Case Studies and Best Practices

This chapter examines real-world case studies of AI bias, lessons learned, and best practices for building fair AI systems. Through concrete examples, we'll see how 弘익人間 principles apply in practice.

Case Study 1: Amazon's Recruiting Tool

Background

Amazon developed an AI recruiting tool to automate resume screening. The system was trained on resumes submitted to Amazon over a 10-year period, with the goal of identifying top candidates.

The Problem

The AI learned to penalize resumes containing the word "women's" (as in "women's chess club") and downgrade candidates from women's colleges. This occurred because the historical data showed a male-dominated hiring pattern in technical roles.

Root Cause

  • Historical bias: Training data reflected past hiring discrimination
  • Proxy discrimination: Gender signals existed in resumes even without explicit gender field
  • Lack of disaggregated evaluation: System wasn't tested separately by gender
  • Insufficient oversight: Bias wasn't detected before deployment testing

Outcome

Amazon ultimately scrapped the tool after failing to guarantee it wouldn't discriminate. The company stated they never used it for real hiring decisions.

Lessons Learned

  • Historical data can encode and amplify existing biases
  • Gender-blind hiring requires removing all gender proxies, not just explicit gender fields
  • Early testing with disaggregated evaluation is essential
  • Some problems may be better solved with human judgment supported by AI, not replaced by it

What Should Have Been Done

  • Audit training data for representation and label bias
  • Identify and remove proxy features correlated with gender
  • Test performance separately for different demographic groups
  • Apply debiasing techniques (reweighting, adversarial debiasing)
  • Implement human oversight for final decisions

Case Study 2: COMPAS Recidivism Risk Assessment

Background

COMPAS (Correctional Offender Management Profiling for Alternative Sanctions) is a risk assessment tool used in criminal justice to predict recidivism likelihood. It's used for bail, sentencing, and parole decisions across the United States.

The Problem

ProPublica's 2016 investigation found that COMPAS exhibited racial bias:

  • Black defendants labeled higher risk but didn't reoffend: 45%
  • White defendants labeled higher risk but didn't reoffend: 23%
  • Black defendants labeled lower risk but did reoffend: 28%
  • White defendants labeled lower risk but did reoffend: 48%

Analysis

The algorithm satisfied predictive parity (equal precision for high-risk predictions across races) but violated equalized odds (different false positive and false negative rates). This illustrates the impossibility of satisfying all fairness criteria simultaneously.

Lessons Learned

  • Different fairness metrics can give conflicting results
  • Context matters: in criminal justice, false positives (wrongly predicting recidivism) harm individuals
  • High-stakes decisions require careful selection of appropriate fairness criteria
  • Transparency is crucial but controversial in criminal justice
  • Historical arrest data may reflect biased policing, not actual crime rates

Best Practices for Criminal Justice AI

  • Prioritize equalized odds to ensure equal treatment of innocent and guilty defendants
  • Conduct intersectional analysis (race × gender, race × age, etc.)
  • Provide explanations and allow challenges to risk scores
  • Regular fairness audits by independent third parties
  • Human review required for all high-risk scores
  • Consider whether automation is appropriate for these decisions at all

Case Study 3: Healthcare Algorithm Racial Bias

Background

A widely-used healthcare algorithm assigned risk scores to over 200 million people annually, determining who would benefit from high-risk care management programs.

The Problem

The algorithm used healthcare costs as a proxy for health needs. Because Black patients faced systemic barriers to healthcare access and thus incurred lower costs, they were assigned lower risk scores despite being sicker than White patients with similar scores.

Impact

At a given risk score, Black patients were considerably sicker than White patients:

  • Black patients had 26.3% more chronic conditions
  • When the algorithm was adjusted to reduce bias, the percentage of Black patients receiving extra help increased from 17.7% to 46.5%

Root Cause

Measurement bias: Healthcare costs are not an unbiased measure of health needs when different populations have different levels of healthcare access.

Solution Implemented

  • Changed objective from predicting costs to predicting actual health conditions
  • Used number of active chronic conditions as the outcome instead of costs
  • Reduced bias by over 80%

Lessons Learned

  • Choice of target variable (what you're predicting) is crucial
  • Proxies must be valid across all demographic groups
  • Domain expertise is essential to identify biased measurements
  • Post-deployment monitoring can catch bias that wasn't apparent during development
  • Fixing bias is possible but requires rethinking fundamental modeling choices

Best Practices Summary

1. Data Collection and Preparation

Representative Sampling
  • Ensure all populations are adequately represented in training data
  • Use stratified sampling when necessary
  • Document demographic composition of datasets
  • Address underrepresentation through targeted data collection
Label Quality
  • Develop clear, unbiased labeling guidelines
  • Train annotators on fairness considerations
  • Use diverse annotation teams
  • Measure inter-annotator agreement across demographic groups
Feature Selection
  • Identify and remove unnecessary proxy features
  • Validate that features measure the same construct across groups
  • Document rationale for including potentially sensitive features
  • Test feature importance separately for each demographic group

2. Model Development

Fairness-Aware Training
  • Define fairness criteria appropriate for your use case
  • Incorporate fairness constraints into model training
  • Use techniques like reweighting, adversarial debiasing, or fairness regularization
  • Accept accuracy-fairness tradeoffs when necessary
Disaggregated Evaluation
  • Always evaluate performance separately for each demographic group
  • Report worst-case group performance, not just averages
  • Conduct intersectional analysis
  • Test on held-out data that matches deployment distribution

3. Deployment and Monitoring

Continuous Monitoring
  • Track fairness metrics in real-time
  • Set up alerts for fairness violations
  • Monitor for distribution drift
  • Conduct regular comprehensive audits
Human Oversight
  • Require human review for high-stakes decisions
  • Provide explanations for AI recommendations
  • Enable appeal processes
  • Train human reviewers on recognizing and addressing bias

4. Organizational Practices

Team Diversity
  • Build diverse development teams
  • Include domain experts and affected stakeholders
  • Establish ethics review boards
  • Encourage different perspectives in design discussions
Documentation and Transparency
  • Create comprehensive model cards
  • Document all fairness-related decisions
  • Maintain detailed audit trails
  • Be transparent about limitations and tradeoffs
Stakeholder Engagement
  • Involve affected communities in system design
  • Conduct user research across diverse populations
  • Establish feedback mechanisms
  • Respond to fairness concerns promptly

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  1. Fairness Washing: Claiming fairness without rigorous evaluation
  2. One-Time Assessment: Testing fairness once and never checking again
  3. Aggregate-Only Metrics: Reporting only overall performance without group breakdowns
  4. Ignoring Intersectionality: Not examining compound discrimination
  5. Lack of Stakeholder Input: Building systems without consulting affected communities
  6. Insufficient Documentation: Failing to document fairness-related decisions
  7. Automation Bias: Over-relying on AI without human oversight
  8. Narrow Fairness Definition: Optimizing for one metric while ignoring others
  9. Quick Fixes: Applying superficial solutions to deep-rooted bias problems
  10. Siloed Responsibility: Treating fairness as one team's job rather than organization-wide priority

Final Recommendations

Building fair AI systems is not a one-time project but an ongoing commitment. Success requires:

  • Leadership commitment: Fairness must be a priority from the top
  • Resource allocation: Dedicate time, budget, and personnel to fairness
  • Cultural change: Make fairness part of organizational values
  • Continuous learning: Stay current with research and best practices
  • Collaboration: Work with researchers, regulators, and affected communities
  • Humility: Acknowledge that perfect fairness is aspirational, not achievable
  • Transparency: Be honest about limitations and ongoing challenges
  • Action: Continuously improve rather than waiting for perfect solutions
