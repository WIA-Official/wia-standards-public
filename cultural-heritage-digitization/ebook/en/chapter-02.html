CHAPTER 2

📸 3D Scanning Technologies

At the heart of cultural heritage digitization lies the ability to capture accurate three-dimensional representations of physical objects and spaces. This chapter explores the primary technologies used to create digital twins of cultural artifacts.

Photogrammetry

Photogrammetry is the science of making measurements from photographs. In cultural heritage, it involves taking multiple overlapping photographs of an object from different angles and using software to reconstruct a 3D model.

How It Works

  • Capture 50-300+ photos from all angles around the object
  • Software identifies common points across images
  • Algorithms calculate spatial positions and geometry
  • Texture mapping applies photographic detail to the model

Advantages

  • Cost-effective: Uses standard cameras and smartphone-accessible
  • High texture quality: Captures color and surface detail excellently
  • Flexible scale: Works for small artifacts to entire buildings
  • Non-invasive: No contact with the object required

Best Uses

Sculptures, pottery, architectural facades, archaeological sites, monuments, and textured surfaces.

Case Study: Smithsonian 3D Digitization

The Smithsonian Institution has digitized thousands of artifacts using photogrammetry, from the Wright Brothers' airplane to Abraham Lincoln's life mask. These models are freely available online, allowing anyone to examine historical treasures in detail.

LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging)

LiDAR uses laser pulses to measure distances, creating highly accurate "point clouds" of millions of spatial coordinates.

Technology Overview

  • Emits rapid laser pulses (thousands to millions per second)
  • Measures time for light to return after bouncing off surfaces
  • Calculates precise distance for each point
  • Creates dense, geometrically accurate point clouds

Types of LiDAR

  • Terrestrial LiDAR: Ground-based scanners for buildings and sites
  • Aerial LiDAR: Drone or aircraft-mounted for large landscapes
  • Handheld LiDAR: Portable scanners for indoor environments

Advantages

  • Extreme accuracy: Sub-millimeter precision possible
  • Speed: Capture millions of points in minutes
  • Works in darkness: Doesn't rely on ambient light
  • Penetrates vegetation: Can reveal structures hidden by foliage

Archaeological Discovery: Lost Mayan Cities

Aerial LiDAR scanning revealed thousands of previously unknown Mayan structures hidden beneath the jungle canopy in Guatemala, revolutionizing our understanding of Mayan civilization's scale and complexity.

Structured Light Scanning

This technique projects patterns of light (grids, stripes) onto an object and analyzes how the pattern deforms to calculate 3D shape.

Process

  • Project known light patterns onto the object
  • Cameras capture the deformed pattern
  • Software calculates geometry from distortions
  • Multiple scans combined for complete coverage

Best Applications

  • Small to medium artifacts with complex geometry
  • High-detail surface capture
  • Museum collections and precious objects
  • Quality control of conservation work

CT Scanning (Computed Tomography)

Medical CT technology adapted for cultural heritage allows non-invasive internal examination of artifacts.

Unique Capabilities

  • Reveals internal structure without opening or damaging objects
  • Discovers hidden inscriptions or contents
  • Analyzes construction techniques
  • Identifies materials and damage invisible externally

Unveiling Ancient Secrets

CT scanning of Egyptian mummies has revealed cause of death, disease patterns, and even digitally "unwrapped" mummies without disturbing the wrappings. Ancient sealed scrolls have been virtually opened and read without physical contact.

Technology Comparison

Choosing the Right Technology

  • Photogrammetry: Best for textured objects, cost-sensitive projects, large sites
  • LiDAR: Best for geometric accuracy, large-scale documentation, outdoor environments
  • Structured Light: Best for small artifacts, museum objects, high detail requirements
  • CT Scanning: Best for internal examination, fragile objects, research purposes

Data Processing and Output

Point Clouds

Raw output from scanning consists of millions of 3D coordinates. Point clouds preserve maximum detail but require processing.

Mesh Generation

Converting point clouds to polygon meshes creates usable 3D models. Balance between detail and file size is crucial.

Texture Mapping

Applying photographic texture to meshes creates photorealistic models that capture material properties and surface details.

WIA-EDU-023 Scanning Standards

Resolution Requirements

  • Small artifacts (<50cm): Minimum 100 points/mm²
  • Medium objects (50cm-5m): Minimum 10 points/cm²
  • Buildings/monuments: Minimum 1cm accuracy
  • Archaeological sites: Minimum 5cm accuracy

File Formats

  • Point clouds: E57, LAS, PTS
  • Meshes: OBJ, PLY, STL, FBX
  • Web delivery: glTF 2.0, USDZ
  • Archival: X3D, COLLADA

Best Practices

  • Document scanning methodology and equipment specifications
  • Maintain original raw data in addition to processed models
  • Include color calibration targets for accurate reproduction
  • Capture multiple resolution levels for different use cases
  • Verify accuracy through control measurements
  • Store redundant copies in geographically separate locations

Understanding these technologies enables cultural institutions to choose appropriate methods for their collections and ensure high-quality digital preservation that meets international standards.
