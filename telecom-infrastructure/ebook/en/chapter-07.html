📊 Chapter 7: Spectrum Allocation and Management

The Invisible Resource

Radio frequency spectrum is a finite natural resource more valuable than gold. Its efficient allocation and management determine the success of wireless telecommunications worldwide.

7.1 Understanding Radio Frequency Spectrum

7.1.1 What is Spectrum?

Radio frequency (RF) spectrum refers to the range of electromagnetic frequencies used for wireless communications, ranging from 3 kHz to 300 GHz.

Spectrum Bands and Properties:

Band Frequency Range Propagation Primary Use
VLF (Very Low) 3-30 kHz Global, penetrates water Submarine communication, navigation
LF (Low) 30-300 kHz Long range ground wave AM radio, navigation
MF (Medium) 300 kHz - 3 MHz Regional coverage AM broadcast, maritime
HF (High) 3-30 MHz Global via ionosphere Shortwave, aviation, amateur radio
VHF (Very High) 30-300 MHz Line of sight, ~50 km FM radio, TV, public safety
UHF (Ultra High) 300 MHz - 3 GHz Line of sight, ~30 km TV, cellular (4G/5G), Wi-Fi
SHF (Super High) 3-30 GHz Short range, ~10 km 5G, satellite, radar, microwave links
EHF (Extremely High) 30-300 GHz Very short range, <1 km 5G mmWave, future 6G

7.1.2 Why Spectrum Management Matters

Scarcity:

  • Unlike fiber or copper, you can't manufacture more spectrum
  • Especially scarce below 6 GHz (ideal for mobile coverage)
  • Global demand growing 25-40% annually

Economic Value:

  • 5G spectrum auctions have raised $100+ billion globally since 2018
  • USA 2021 C-band auction: $81 billion (largest ever)
  • Inefficient allocation costs economy tens of billions in lost productivity

Technical Complexity:

  • Interference between users must be managed
  • Different frequencies have vastly different properties
  • International coordination required (satellites, border areas)

7.2 Spectrum Allocation Methods

7.2.1 Traditional Approaches

1. Command-and-Control (Administrative Allocation)

  • Government assigns spectrum to specific services/users
  • No market mechanism, based on public interest determination
  • Pros: Ensures critical services (public safety, aviation), prevents interference
  • Cons: Inefficient, slow to adapt to changing needs, can't respond to market signals
  • Example: Broadcasting, public safety, military

2. Spectrum Auctions (Market-Based)

  • Government sells licenses to highest bidders
  • Different auction formats: simultaneous ascending, combinatorial, incentive
  • Pros: Efficient allocation to those who value it most, raises revenue for government
  • Cons: Favors large incumbents, may discourage innovation, high upfront costs
  • Example: Mobile spectrum licenses in most countries

3. Beauty Contests (Comparative Selection)

  • Government evaluates applications based on criteria (coverage, investment, innovation)
  • Pros: Can prioritize policy goals (rural coverage, local ownership)
  • Cons: Subjective, slower process, prone to political influence
  • Example: Some developing countries, regional licenses

4. Unlicensed Spectrum (Commons Model)

  • Spectrum open to all users following technical rules (power limits, protocols)
  • Managed through equipment certification rather than licenses
  • Pros: Enables innovation (Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, IoT), no licensing costs
  • Cons: Interference risks, no quality of service guarantees
  • Example: ISM bands (2.4 GHz, 5 GHz), CBRS (USA)

7.2.2 Emerging Approaches

Dynamic Spectrum Access (DSA)

  • Real-time sharing between primary and secondary users
  • Uses database (geolocation) or sensing to detect spectrum availability
  • Dramatically increases spectrum efficiency
  • Example: TV White Spaces, CBRS (USA), LSA (Europe)

Spectrum Sharing

  • Multiple users/services occupy same band with coordination
  • Types: Geographic (different locations), temporal (different times), orthogonal (different codes/frequencies)
  • Example: CBRS 3.5 GHz (USA) - Navy, fixed satellite, wireless broadband share

7.3 Mobile Spectrum Bands

7.3.1 Low-Band Spectrum (Sub-1 GHz)

Key Bands:

  • 600 MHz: Repurposed from TV broadcasting (USA)
  • 700 MHz: "Digital dividend" from TV transition (global)
  • 800 MHz: Europe, Asia (formerly TV)
  • 850 MHz / 900 MHz: Original cellular bands, still widely used

Characteristics:

  • Coverage: Excellent (10-35 km per tower)
  • Bandwidth: Limited (10-30 MHz typical per operator)
  • Speed: 10-100 Mbps
  • Best For: Wide-area coverage, rural, indoor penetration, IoT

7.3.2 Mid-Band Spectrum (1-6 GHz)

Key Bands:

  • 1.8 GHz: Early LTE, 5G
  • 2.1 GHz / 2.3 GHz: 3G/4G/5G
  • 2.5 GHz / 2.6 GHz: 4G/5G, popular globally
  • 3.5 GHz (C-band): Global 5G "sweet spot"
  • 3.7-4.2 GHz: C-band (USA), being cleared from satellite

Characteristics:

  • Coverage: Good (1-5 km per tower)
  • Bandwidth: Moderate to high (50-200 MHz per operator)
  • Speed: 100-900 Mbps
  • Best For: Urban/suburban coverage + capacity, ideal balance for 5G

7.3.3 High-Band / mmWave (24-100 GHz)

Key Bands:

  • 24 GHz (n258): USA, some Asia
  • 26 GHz (n258): Europe primary mmWave band
  • 28 GHz (n261): USA, Japan, Korea
  • 39 GHz (n260): USA, indoor

Characteristics:

  • Coverage: Poor (100-500 meters)
  • Bandwidth: Huge (800+ MHz channels available)
  • Speed: 1-10 Gbps
  • Best For: Dense urban, stadiums, indoor venues, fixed wireless

7.4 Spectrum Efficiency Techniques

7.4.1 Multiple Access Technologies

Frequency Division (FDMA)

  • Users assigned different frequencies
  • Simple, but inefficient use of spectrum
  • Used in 1G analog cellular

Time Division (TDMA)

  • Users share frequency, transmit in assigned time slots
  • More efficient than FDMA
  • Used in 2G GSM

Code Division (CDMA)

  • All users transmit simultaneously on same frequency
  • Separated by unique codes
  • Higher capacity than TDMA
  • Used in 3G WCDMA/CDMA2000

Orthogonal Frequency Division (OFDMA)

  • Divides channel into many narrow subcarriers
  • Assigns subcarriers dynamically to users
  • Resistant to interference and multipath fading
  • Used in 4G LTE, 5G NR, Wi-Fi

7.4.2 Advanced Antenna Technologies

MIMO (Multiple-Input Multiple-Output)

  • Multiple antennas at transmitter and receiver
  • Spatial multiplexing: Multiple data streams on same frequency
  • Capacity increase: 2x (2x2), 4x (4x4), 8x (8x8)

Massive MIMO

  • 64, 128, or 256 antenna elements
  • Beamforming: Focus energy on specific users
  • Spatial multiplexing: Serve many users simultaneously
  • Capacity increase: 3-5x over conventional systems

Beamforming

  • Dynamic adjustment of antenna radiation pattern
  • Focus signal toward intended user, null out interference
  • Improves SNR, increases capacity, extends range

7.5 International Spectrum Coordination

7.5.1 ITU Role

The International Telecommunication Union (ITU) coordinates global spectrum use:

  • Radio Regulations: International treaty governing spectrum use
  • World Radiocommunication Conference (WRC): Held every 3-4 years to update regulations
  • Regional Groups: Region 1 (Europe/Africa), Region 2 (Americas), Region 3 (Asia/Pacific)
  • Coordination: Resolve interference between countries, satellite orbits

7.5.2 Harmonization Benefits

  • Economies of Scale: Global equipment market reduces costs 30-50%
  • Roaming: Seamless service across borders
  • Interference Management: Consistent band plans reduce cross-border issues
  • Innovation: Larger market encourages R&D investment

Example: 3.5 GHz for 5G

  • Europe, Asia, Latin America aligned on 3.4-3.8 GHz
  • USA uses 3.7-4.2 GHz (C-band)
  • Equipment can support both with minor modifications
  • Result: Rapid 5G deployment, affordable devices

7.6 WIA-SOC-012 Spectrum Management Standards

7.6.1 Allocation Principles

1. Efficiency

  • Spectrum allocated to highest-value uses
  • Unused spectrum reallocated when possible
  • Encourage sharing where feasible

2. Flexibility

  • Technology-neutral licenses when possible
  • Service-neutral allocation
  • Enable secondary markets (trading, leasing)

3. Transparency

  • Clear allocation processes
  • Public consultation on major decisions
  • Published spectrum registries

4. Equity

  • Access for new entrants and innovators
  • Rural coverage obligations
  • Reserve spectrum for public safety, emergency services

7.6.2 Recommended Spectrum Mix for 5G

Balanced Deployment:

  • Low-band (sub-1 GHz): Minimum 20 MHz per operator for coverage
  • Mid-band (1-6 GHz): 80-100 MHz per operator for capacity (3.5 GHz preferred)
  • mmWave (24+ GHz): 800-1000 MHz for dense urban capacity

Total: ~1 GHz per operator across all bands for comprehensive 5G service

7.7 Summary

Spectrum is the lifeblood of wireless communications, requiring careful allocation and management. Key takeaways:

  • Spectrum is a scarce, valuable resource with unique propagation characteristics
  • Different allocation methods (auctions, unlicensed, sharing) have distinct trade-offs
  • Mid-band spectrum (especially 3.5 GHz) is the "sweet spot" for 5G
  • Advanced technologies (OFDMA, Massive MIMO) dramatically increase spectrum efficiency
  • International harmonization benefits consumers through lower costs and better roaming
  • WIA-SOC-012 promotes efficient, flexible, transparent spectrum management
