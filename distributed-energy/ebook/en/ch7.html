Advanced Control Systems

Intelligent Optimization of Distributed Energy Resources

7.1 Model Predictive Control (MPC)

MPC Fundamentals

Model Predictive Control represents a paradigm shift from reactive control strategies that respond to current conditions toward proactive optimization that anticipates future states and optimizes actions accordingly. MPC leverages mathematical models of system dynamics, forecasts of disturbances (weather, load, prices), and optimization algorithms to determine control sequences minimizing cost functions over prediction horizons spanning hours to days.

The fundamental MPC cycle operates in four stages: First, measure current system state including battery state-of-charge, building temperatures, equipment status, and grid conditions. Second, forecast external factors including solar/wind generation, electricity prices, building occupancy, and weather over the prediction horizon. Third, solve an optimization problem determining optimal control actions (battery charge/discharge, HVAC setpoints, DER dispatch) minimizing costs while satisfying constraints (equipment limits, comfort requirements, grid obligations). Fourth, implement the first interval's control actions while discarding the remainder of the solution. This process repeats continuously, typically every 5-15 minutes, incorporating latest measurements and forecasts into updated optimizations.

MPC for Building Energy Systems

Buildings account for 40% of total energy consumption and offer substantial flexibility through thermal mass storage, adaptable equipment schedules, and DER integration. MPC transforms buildings from passive energy consumers to active grid participants optimizing across multiple timescales and objectives.

A typical building MPC system models thermal dynamics including envelope heat transfer, HVAC system performance, occupancy patterns, and internal heat gains. The controller predicts building temperature evolution under different control sequences while incorporating weather forecasts, electricity price projections, and occupancy schedules. Optimization selects HVAC control, battery operation, and non-critical load scheduling minimizing energy costs subject to comfort constraints (maintaining 68-76°F during occupied hours).

Advanced implementations achieve 15-30% energy cost savings versus conventional controls through strategies including precooling before peak electricity price periods, leveraging thermal mass for load shifting, optimizing battery charging with solar generation and price forecasts, and curtailing non-critical loads during demand response events. The University of California Merced deployed building MPC achieving 24% cooling energy reduction while improving temperature control and occupant satisfaction.

MPC for Microgrid Energy Management

Microgrid MPC coordinates diverse DER resources including renewable generation, energy storage, backup generators, and flexible loads to optimize energy costs, maximize renewable utilization, enhance reliability, and provide grid services. The optimization formulation becomes substantially more complex than single-building systems due to coupled constraints, multiple objectives, and larger decision spaces.

The microgrid MPC problem can be formulated as minimizing total operating cost over horizon T equal to the sum of grid electricity purchases, fuel costs for generators, battery degradation, demand charges, and load curtailment penalties, subject to constraints ensuring power balance at every interval (generation plus storage discharge plus grid import equals load plus storage charge plus grid export), battery state-of-charge limits and charge/discharge rate constraints, generator minimum stable operation levels and ramp rate limits, network capacity constraints on line flows, and voltage bounds throughout the distribution network.

Solving this mixed-integer nonlinear program (MINLP) poses computational challenges, particularly for large microgrids requiring solutions every 5-15 minutes. Practical implementations employ convex relaxations transforming nonlinear network constraints to second-order cone programs, use rolling horizon approaches solving only near-term decisions with full detail while approximating distant intervals, implement hierarchical decomposition separating fast local controls from slower system-wide coordination, or apply heuristic methods (genetic algorithms, particle swarm optimization) trading optimality for computational speed.

7.2 Machine Learning for DER Optimization

Load and Generation Forecasting

Accurate forecasting underpins effective DER control, with forecast errors directly translating to suboptimal operation and reduced economic performance. Machine learning methods often outperform traditional statistical approaches by automatically discovering complex nonlinear relationships in high-dimensional data.

Solar Power Forecasting: Deep neural networks processing numerical weather predictions, satellite imagery, and historical solar generation achieve hour-ahead forecast errors of 5-10% RMSE and day-ahead errors of 15-20% RMSE. Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) excel at extracting spatial patterns from satellite cloud imagery, while recurrent networks (LSTMs, GRUs) capture temporal dependencies in weather evolution. Ensemble methods combining multiple models with different strengths further reduce errors by 10-15%.

Load Forecasting: Gradient boosted decision trees (XGBoost, LightGBM) and deep neural networks achieve building-level load forecasts with 5-12% MAPE (mean absolute percentage error) for day-ahead predictions. Key features include recent consumption history, temperature and humidity, calendar information (day of week, holidays), occupancy patterns, and weather forecasts. Transfer learning enables accurate forecasting for buildings with limited historical data by pre-training on large datasets then fine-tuning on specific buildings.

Price Forecasting: Electricity price forecasting supports energy arbitrage and contract optimization. Hybrid models combining ARIMA for capturing autoregressive patterns with neural networks for nonlinear weather and load relationships achieve 8-15% MAPE for day-ahead price forecasts in most markets. Price spike detection using classification models identifies extreme price events enabling risk management and strategic bidding.

Reinforcement Learning for Adaptive Control

Reinforcement learning (RL) trains control policies through interaction with environments (real systems or simulations), learning optimal actions without explicit modeling. RL agents receive observations (battery SOC, building temperature, electricity price), select actions (equipment control commands), and receive rewards (negative of costs). Through repeated interaction, agents learn policies maximizing cumulative rewards.

Deep reinforcement learning combines neural networks (for function approximation in high-dimensional state/action spaces) with RL algorithms (Q-learning, policy gradients). Applications include battery storage operation optimizing energy arbitrage and degradation tradeoffs, HVAC control balancing comfort and efficiency, EV charging coordination minimizing costs while ensuring vehicle availability, and microgrid dispatch under uncertainty with changing conditions and incomplete information.

Advantages versus MPC include no requirement for explicit system models (learning directly from data), natural handling of stochastic environments through policy learning, and potential for discovering novel strategies beyond human-designed heuristics. Challenges include sample inefficiency (requiring extensive training data), difficulty ensuring safety during training, and limited interpretability of learned policies. Hybrid approaches combining RL-learned components within MPC frameworks show promise for balancing advantages of both methods.

Anomaly Detection and Fault Diagnosis

Machine learning enables automated detection of equipment faults, performance degradation, and cybersecurity threats in distributed energy systems. Early fault detection prevents minor issues from escalating to expensive failures while maximizing equipment uptime and energy production.

Solar System Monitoring: Supervised learning models trained on normal and faulty operation distinguish healthy from degraded performance. Random forests identify module failures, soiling, shading, and inverter faults from production data and environmental conditions with 90-95% accuracy. Unsupervised approaches using autoencoders or isolation forests detect anomalies without labeled training data by learning normal operation patterns and flagging significant deviations.

Battery Diagnostics: State-of-health estimation predicts remaining battery capacity and cycle life from voltage, current, and temperature measurements. Gaussian process regression provides probabilistic capacity estimates with uncertainty quantification, enabling data-driven warranty and replacement decisions. Early detection of thermal runaway precursors using multivariate statistical methods enhances safety for large battery installations.

Cyberattack Detection: Intrusion detection systems employ machine learning to identify malicious network traffic or anomalous device behaviors indicating compromise. One-class SVM, deep autoencoders, and LSTM networks trained on normal communication patterns detect attacks including denial-of-service, man-in-the-middle, and command injection with 95-99% true positive rates and <1% false positive rates, critical for maintaining trust in remotely controlled DER systems.

7.3 Distributed Optimization and Multi-Agent Systems

Consensus Algorithms

Distributed optimization enables coordinated control of large-scale DER systems without centralized communication or computation bottlenecks. Consensus algorithms allow distributed agents (DER controllers) to agree on decisions through local information exchange with neighbors, eventually converging to globally optimal or near-optimal solutions.

The alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) provides a powerful framework for distributed optimization. ADMM decomposes global problems into smaller subproblems solved by individual agents, with coordination achieved through dual variable updates and consensus constraints. For DER coordination, each resource optimizes local operation considering local constraints and costs, exchanges information with neighbors regarding planned actions, and updates coordination variables to achieve consensus. Convergence to optimal solutions occurs typically within 10-50 iterations, compatible with 1-5 minute control intervals even with communication delays.

Multi-Agent Coordination

Multi-agent systems (MAS) model DER ecosystems as collections of autonomous agents (solar inverters, battery controllers, building management systems) that interact through communication and market mechanisms. Agent-based approaches naturally represent the decentralized ownership and operation characteristic of distributed energy while enabling emergent coordination through well-designed interaction rules.

Agent architectures typically include reactive layers (immediate responses to safety events), deliberative layers (optimization of local operation), and social layers (coordination with other agents). Communication occurs through messages following standardized ontologies (FIPA Agent Communication Language) enabling semantic interoperability. Market-based coordination implements virtual markets where agents trade energy or services, with prices emerging from supply-demand balance rather than central optimization.

Advantages include scalability to very large systems (millions of agents), robustness to individual agent failures, respect for privacy (agents need not share detailed internal information), and flexibility to accommodate heterogeneous resources and objectives. Challenges include proving stability and optimality of emergent behavior, managing communication overhead in dense networks, and addressing potential for gaming or manipulation of coordination mechanisms.

7.4 Optimal Power Flow and Network-Aware Control

Distribution System Optimal Power Flow

Optimal power flow (OPF) determines DER dispatch that optimizes objectives (minimize losses, maximize renewable hosting capacity, minimize costs) while satisfying network constraints (voltage limits, line capacities, power balance). Distribution OPF proves more challenging than transmission OPF due to unbalanced three-phase systems, high resistance causing voltage sensitivity to active power, radial topologies with limited redundancy, and limited observability from sparse sensor deployment.

Classical OPF formulations result in non-convex optimization problems that are NP-hard to solve globally. Recent advances employ convex relaxations including second-order cone programming (SOCP) and semidefinite programming (SDP) that transform OPF to convex problems solvable in polynomial time with global optimality guarantees under certain conditions. These relaxations are exact for many practical distribution networks, providing provably optimal solutions with computational efficiency suitable for real-time control.

Voltage Regulation and Reactive Power Management

High penetrations of distributed solar generation create voltage management challenges including overvoltage during high solar production, voltage sag during cloud transients, and rapid voltage variations causing flicker. Traditional voltage regulation equipment (tap-changing transformers, capacitor banks) operates too slowly to address solar intermittency, while widespread smart inverter deployment provides fast-acting distributed reactive power control.

Coordinated volt-VAR optimization (VVO) determines reactive power setpoints for all DER inverters and controllable utility equipment to minimize losses or maximize hosting capacity while maintaining voltage within ANSI C84.1 limits (0.95-1.05 pu). Advanced VVO formulates as AC-OPF with reactive power controls as decision variables, solved using SOCP or SDP relaxations. Implementation requires either centralized control with communication to all inverters or distributed approaches where inverters adapt local volt-VAR curves based on network conditions.

Field demonstrations show VVO reducing distribution losses by 3-8% while enabling 20-50% increases in solar hosting capacity compared to uncoordinated operation. The Electric Power Research Institute's smart inverter pilot on Hawaiian Electric circuits achieved 15% loss reduction and eliminated voltage violations that previously required solar curtailment.

7.5 Stochastic Optimization Under Uncertainty

Robust Optimization

DER control faces substantial uncertainty in renewable generation, load, electricity prices, and equipment availability. Robust optimization addresses uncertainty by determining decisions that perform well across all scenarios within specified uncertainty sets, providing guarantees against worst-case realizations.

Two-stage robust optimization separates here-and-now decisions (must be decided before uncertainty resolution) from wait-and-see decisions (adapted after observing uncertainty). For microgrids, day-ahead commitments of backup generators represent here-and-now decisions, while real-time dispatch adjusts to realized renewable generation. The robust formulation minimizes worst-case cost across all uncertainty realizations, trading some expected performance for protection against unfavorable outcomes.

Adjustable robust optimization improves performance by allowing wait-and-see decisions to adapt to observations through decision rules (affine or polynomial functions of uncertain parameters). This increased adaptability reduces conservatism while maintaining robust guarantees. Computational methods employ column-and-constraint generation algorithms that iteratively identify worst-case scenarios and adjust decisions to protect against them, converging to robust optimal solutions.

Stochastic Programming

Stochastic programming represents uncertainty through probability distributions and optimizes expected performance rather than worst-case robustness. Scenario-based approaches discretize continuous distributions into representative scenarios (often 10-100 scenarios for tractability) and optimize expected costs across scenarios weighted by probabilities.

Two-stage stochastic programs optimize first-stage decisions before uncertainty resolves, then optimize recourse decisions (second stage) for each scenario realization. For DER energy management, first-stage decisions include equipment scheduling and market positions, while second-stage recourse addresses deviations from forecasts through real-time adjustments, storage dispatch, and curtailment. The objective minimizes expected total costs (first-stage plus scenario-weighted recourse costs).

Multistage stochastic programming extends this framework to decision sequences across multiple time periods with uncertainty evolving over time, represented through scenario trees. This formulation naturally captures decision-dependent information revelation and option value of delaying commitments, but computational complexity grows exponentially with stages and scenarios. Decomposition methods including Benders decomposition and progressive hedging enable solution of large-scale problems by breaking overall problems into more manageable subproblems solved iteratively.

Control Evolution: DER control systems are rapidly evolving from simple rule-based approaches toward sophisticated optimization incorporating forecasting, uncertainty, and multi-objective tradeoffs. These advanced controls unlock substantial value while managing increasing system complexity. A 2023 study by NREL estimated that optimal DER control could reduce electricity costs by $15-40 billion annually in the United States while improving reliability and enabling higher renewable penetrations.
