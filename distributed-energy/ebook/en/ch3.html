Chapter 3

Microgrids and Islanding

Building Resilient, Self-Sufficient Energy Systems

3.1 Microgrid Fundamentals

Defining Microgrids

A microgrid is a localized electrical grid with defined boundaries that can operate autonomously from the main power grid. Unlike traditional distribution systems designed for unidirectional power flow from centralized generators to passive loads, microgrids actively manage distributed energy resources, storage systems, and controllable loads within their defined boundaries.

The U.S. Department of Energy defines a microgrid as "a group of interconnected loads and distributed energy resources within clearly defined electrical boundaries that acts as a single controllable entity with respect to the grid and can connect and disconnect from the grid to enable it to operate in both grid-connected or island mode." This definition emphasizes three key characteristics: defined boundaries, coordinated control, and the capability to island from the main grid.

Microgrid Architectures

AC-Coupled Microgrids: These systems connect DER components through alternating current buses, matching the standard grid architecture. AC coupling offers straightforward integration with existing electrical infrastructure and standard utility interconnection. However, AC-coupled systems require synchronization of multiple inverters and careful management of voltage and frequency in island mode. Protection coordination becomes more complex with bidirectional power flows and multiple generation sources.

DC-Coupled Microgrids: DC microgrids distribute power using direct current buses, typically at voltages ranging from 380V to 1,500V. Native DC generation sources (solar PV, batteries, fuel cells) connect directly to the DC bus through DC-DC converters, eliminating multiple AC-DC-AC conversion stages and improving overall efficiency by 5-10%. DC microgrids excel in applications with predominantly DC loads (LED lighting, electronics, EV charging, data centers) and offer simplified protection through current limiting. However, DC systems require specialized components and lack the extensive standards and equipment ecosystem available for AC systems.

Hybrid AC-DC Microgrids: These systems combine AC and DC distribution networks connected through bidirectional converters, leveraging advantages of both architectures. DC buses serve native DC generation and loads, while AC buses interface with the utility grid and AC loads. Hybrid architectures are increasingly common in modern installations, particularly where building new infrastructure allows optimization for mixed loads and diverse generation sources.

3.2 Islanding Capability and Control

Planned Islanding

Planned islanding transitions occur when the microgrid intentionally disconnects from the utility grid in response to either utility requests (for maintenance or system protection) or microgrid operator decisions (to optimize economics or reduce exposure to grid disturbances). The transition sequence typically involves:

  1. Pre-Islanding Assessment: The microgrid controller verifies sufficient generation and storage capacity to serve local loads, confirms all critical equipment is operational, and ensures grid conditions are suitable for disconnection.
  2. Load Shedding (if necessary): If generation capacity is insufficient, non-critical loads are disconnected according to predefined priorities, ensuring critical loads remain served.
  3. Synchronization Verification: The system confirms microgrid voltage, frequency, and phase align with the utility grid to enable clean disconnection without transients.
  4. Switch Operation: The point of common coupling (PCC) disconnect switch opens, electrically isolating the microgrid from the utility.
  5. Island Mode Establishment: Designated grid-forming inverters assume voltage and frequency regulation responsibilities, establishing stable island operation.
  6. Steady-State Operation: The microgrid control system manages generation, storage, and loads to maintain stable operation within acceptable voltage and frequency bounds.

Unplanned Islanding (Anti-Islanding)

Unplanned islanding occurs when the utility grid experiences a fault or outage while distributed generators remain connected. This situation creates safety hazards for utility workers who may encounter energized lines they expect to be de-energized, and can damage equipment when the grid reconnects with an out-of-phase microgrid.

IEEE 1547 and UL 1741 standards mandate anti-islanding protection for grid-connected DER systems. Modern inverters employ multiple detection methods including:

  • Passive Methods: Monitor voltage, frequency, and harmonic content for deviations exceeding normal operating ranges. Over/under voltage (typically ±10%), over/under frequency (typically ±0.5 Hz), and voltage phase angle changes indicate grid disconnection.
  • Active Methods: Deliberately inject disturbances (frequency shifts, voltage perturbations) that amplify when the grid disconnects but remain negligible when connected. Active frequency drift, Sandia frequency shift, and slip mode frequency shift are common techniques.
  • Communication-Based Methods: Use signals from the utility (transfer trip, power line carrier) to command immediate disconnection, offering the fastest and most reliable detection but requiring communication infrastructure.

For microgrids designed to island, these same detection methods trigger the planned islanding sequence rather than immediate shutdown, transitioning to autonomous operation within 2 seconds of grid loss.

Grid-Forming vs. Grid-Following Inverters

Grid-Following Inverters: Traditional grid-connected inverters operate as current sources synchronized to grid voltage and frequency. They use phase-locked loops (PLLs) to track grid phase and inject current at desired power levels. Grid-following inverters excel in grid-connected mode but cannot establish voltage and frequency independently, preventing island operation without modification.

Grid-Forming Inverters: These advanced inverters can operate as voltage sources, establishing and regulating voltage and frequency without an external reference. Grid-forming capability enables island operation and improves grid stability through virtual synchronous machine (VSM) behavior, emulating the inertial response of traditional rotating generators. Modern microgrid installations employ grid-forming inverters for at least one storage system or dispatchable generator to ensure island capability.

3.3 Black Start Capability

Black Start Fundamentals

Black start capability enables a microgrid to restore power from a completely de-energized state without external grid connection. This capability proves critical during extended utility outages or catastrophic grid failures. The process requires self-sufficient starting power, careful sequencing to manage inrush currents, and coordinated control to establish stable operation.

Black Start Sequence

A typical battery-based black start proceeds through the following stages:

  1. Battery System Energization (0-5 seconds): Battery inverter powers up using stored energy, establishing DC bus voltage and initializing control systems. Self-contained UPS power enables control electronics and communication equipment to boot.
  2. Grid-Forming Inverter Startup (5-15 seconds): The battery inverter transitions to grid-forming mode, establishing AC voltage (480V or 13.8kV typical) and frequency (60 Hz North America, 50 Hz elsewhere) at the microgrid bus.
  3. Critical Control Loads (15-30 seconds): Essential control equipment, communication systems, and monitoring devices energize first, establishing full situational awareness before proceeding.
  4. Auxiliary Power Systems (30-60 seconds): HVAC for temperature-sensitive equipment, lighting for safe operation, and auxiliary power for generators energize.
  5. Generator Starting (60-120 seconds): Diesel generators, gas turbines, or other dispatchable generation start and synchronize to the microgrid bus, supplementing battery capacity for extended operation.
  6. Load Restoration (120-300 seconds): Non-critical loads restore in stages to prevent inrush current overloading generation capacity. Motors and transformers require special consideration due to high starting currents (5-7× running current for motors).
  7. Renewable Generation Integration (300+ seconds): Solar inverters and other renewable sources synchronize once stable operation is established, maximizing clean energy generation and conserving fuel/battery capacity.

Case Study: University Campus Microgrid Black Start

System: 5 MW microgrid serving university campus with 2 MW solar, 1 MW/2 MWh battery, 3 MW natural gas generators

Event: Regional grid failure during extreme weather event

Response:

  • T+0s: Grid loss detected, microgrid isolation switch opens automatically
  • T+8s: Battery inverter establishes grid-forming operation at 13.8kV bus
  • T+25s: Control building and critical IT loads restored (200 kW)
  • T+90s: First generator online and synchronized (1 MW)
  • T+150s: Second generator online (total 2 MW generation)
  • T+240s: Priority buildings restored (classrooms, labs, dormitories - 1.5 MW load)
  • T+480s: All loads restored based on building prioritization (3.2 MW total)
  • T+600s: Solar inverters synchronized as sun rises (adding 0.8 MW)

Outcome: Campus maintained continuous operation for 36 hours until grid restoration, protecting critical research, maintaining student safety, and avoiding estimated $2M in losses from research interruption and facility damage.

3.4 Microgrid Control Systems

Hierarchical Control Architecture

Microgrid control systems employ hierarchical architectures with distinct time scales and responsibilities across three primary levels:

Primary Control (Milliseconds): Fast local controllers maintain voltage and frequency stability through droop control, current limiting, and protection functions. Implemented in distributed inverter and generator controllers using only local measurements, primary control operates without communication, ensuring stability even if communication fails. Droop control mimics traditional generator governor behavior, adjusting output power in response to frequency deviations (P-f droop) and reactive power in response to voltage changes (Q-V droop).

Secondary Control (Seconds): Centralized microgrid controller coordinates multiple DER resources to restore voltage and frequency to nominal values after disturbances, optimize power sharing among generators, and manage state transitions (grid-connected to island and vice versa). Secondary control receives measurements from throughout the microgrid, computes optimal set-points, and transmits commands to primary controllers. Communication latency requirements are moderate (1-10 seconds acceptable), allowing standard Ethernet or cellular connectivity.

Tertiary Control (Minutes to Hours): Higher-level economic dispatch and energy management functions optimize microgrid operation over longer timeframes. Tertiary control incorporates weather forecasts, electricity price signals, utility demand response requests, and load predictions to determine optimal scheduling of generators, charging/discharging of storage, and management of flexible loads. Advanced systems employ model predictive control (MPC) or stochastic optimization to handle uncertainty in renewable generation and load.

Power Sharing and Load Balancing

Effective microgrid operation requires fair distribution of load among available generators while respecting each resource's capabilities and constraints. Droop control provides the foundation for autonomous power sharing without communication:

The frequency-power droop relationship follows: f = f_nominal - K_p × (P - P_setpoint), where K_p is the droop coefficient, typically set to achieve 0.5-1% frequency drop at rated power. This linear relationship ensures generators with identical droop settings share load proportionally to their ratings, while allowing different droop coefficients to bias power sharing as desired.

Voltage-reactive power droop follows similar principles: V = V_nominal - K_q × (Q - Q_setpoint), maintaining acceptable voltage levels throughout the microgrid while sharing reactive power burden among generators. Proper droop coefficient selection balances load sharing against voltage/frequency regulation quality.

3.5 Microgrid Protection Systems

Protection Challenges in Microgrids

Traditional utility distribution protection assumes unidirectional power flow from substations to loads, with fault currents supplied entirely by the utility grid. Protective devices (circuit breakers, fuses, reclosers) coordinate in a hierarchical scheme where downstream devices clear faults before upstream devices operate, minimizing outage extent.

Microgrids fundamentally disrupt these assumptions through bidirectional power flow, multiple generation sources, and dramatic changes in fault current magnitude between grid-connected and island modes. In grid-connected mode, fault currents may exceed 10,000 A supplied primarily by the utility. In island mode, inverter-based generation typically limits fault current to 110-200% of rated current, reducing available fault current by 50-100×. This wide variation prevents traditional overcurrent protection from functioning correctly in both modes.

Adaptive Protection Schemes

Modern microgrids employ adaptive protection that adjusts settings based on operating mode, generation configuration, and network topology. Microprocessor-based relays receive commands from the microgrid controller to modify pickup settings, time delays, and coordination logic when transitioning between grid-connected and island modes or when generation sources connect/disconnect.

Differential Protection: Compares current entering and leaving protected zones, detecting faults through imbalances exceeding expected measurement errors. Differential protection works independently of fault current magnitude, providing consistent performance across operating modes. High-speed fiber optic communication between relay pairs enables fault clearing in 1-2 cycles (16-33 ms), minimizing equipment damage and improving safety.

Directional Protection: Uses current and voltage measurements to determine power flow direction, enabling coordination in networks with bidirectional flow. Directional overcurrent relays only operate for faults in designated directions, allowing proper coordination even when fault current sources are distributed.

Distance Protection: Calculates impedance to faults using voltage and current measurements, providing fault location information and backup protection for adjacent zones. Distance relays can detect high-impedance faults that produce insufficient current for overcurrent detection.

3.6 Synchronization and Grid Reconnection

Synchronization Requirements

Transitioning from island to grid-connected operation requires precise synchronization to prevent equipment damage and power quality disturbances. IEEE 1547-2018 specifies synchronization criteria:

Parameter Allowable Difference
Voltage Magnitude ±10% (±5% recommended)
Frequency ±0.3 Hz
Phase Angle ±20° (±10° recommended)

Synchronization outside these windows generates transient currents potentially exceeding 10× normal current, stressing equipment and potentially triggering protective relays. Advanced synchronization algorithms continuously adjust microgrid voltage, frequency, and phase to match utility conditions, minimizing transients when reconnection occurs.

Reconnection Sequence

  1. Grid Availability Check: Monitor utility voltage and frequency to confirm grid restoration and stable operation for a minimum period (typically 5 minutes) before attempting reconnection.
  2. Synchronization: Adjust microgrid voltage, frequency, and phase angle to match grid conditions within specified tolerances. Modern controllers achieve synchronization in 10-30 seconds.
  3. Paralleling: Close PCC disconnect switch when synchronization conditions are met, connecting microgrid to utility. Initial current flow should be minimal (less than 10% rated current) if synchronization is accurate.
  4. Mode Transition: Transition from grid-forming to grid-following control. The grid-forming inverter reduces its contribution while increasing grid dependence, smoothly transferring frequency and voltage regulation responsibility to the utility.
  5. Power Transfer: Gradually increase or decrease power exchange with utility to desired levels based on economic optimization, grid services contracts, or utility requirements.
  6. Normal Operation: Resume grid-connected operation with DER providing energy, ancillary services, or peak shaving as programmed.
Resilience Benefit: Microgrids with islanding capability provide critical resilience for facilities that cannot tolerate power interruptions: hospitals, emergency services, water treatment plants, data centers, military installations, and critical infrastructure. The 2021 Texas winter storm demonstrated this value, with microgrids maintaining power while the broader grid faced rolling blackouts affecting millions.
