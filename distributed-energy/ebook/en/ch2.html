DER Technologies and Components

Understanding the Building Blocks of Distributed Energy Systems

2.1 Solar Photovoltaic Systems

Photovoltaic Technology Fundamentals

Solar photovoltaic technology converts sunlight directly into electricity through the photovoltaic effect, discovered by French physicist Edmond Becquerel in 1839. When photons from sunlight strike a semiconductor material, they transfer their energy to electrons, exciting them to a higher energy state and allowing them to flow as electrical current.

Modern solar cells use primarily silicon-based semiconductors, though emerging technologies employ alternative materials including cadmium telluride, copper indium gallium selenide, and perovskites. Silicon cells dominate the market due to silicon's abundance, well-understood manufacturing processes, and excellent performance characteristics.

Cell Technologies and Efficiencies

Monocrystalline Silicon: These cells are cut from single-crystal silicon ingots, creating highly uniform crystalline structures. The manufacturing process involves the Czochralski method, where a silicon seed crystal is slowly drawn from a vat of molten silicon, creating a cylindrical ingot. Monocrystalline cells achieve the highest commercial efficiencies (20-26%) and perform well in low-light conditions, but cost more to manufacture due to higher silicon purity requirements and material waste from cutting cylindrical ingots into square wafers.

Polycrystalline Silicon: Also called multicrystalline, these cells are made from silicon fragments melted together, creating a material with multiple crystal boundaries. While slightly less efficient (15-20%) than monocrystalline cells due to increased electron scattering at crystal boundaries, polycrystalline cells cost less to produce and offer excellent value for large-scale installations where space is less constrained.

Thin-Film Technologies: Thin-film solar cells deposit photovoltaic materials in extremely thin layers (typically less than 1 micrometer) onto substrates including glass, plastic, or metal. Cadmium telluride (CdTe) and copper indium gallium selenide (CIGS) are the primary commercial thin-film technologies. While generally less efficient (11-13% for CdTe, 13-15% for CIGS) than crystalline silicon, thin-film panels use less material, can be manufactured in continuous processes, perform better in high temperatures, and work well in diffuse light conditions.

Perovskite Solar Cells: Named for their crystal structure (similar to the mineral perovskite), these emerging cells have achieved laboratory efficiencies exceeding 25% and offer potential for extremely low manufacturing costs through solution-based processing. Perovskite cells can be manufactured at low temperatures, enabling flexible and semi-transparent applications. However, stability and longevity challenges must be resolved before widespread commercial deployment.

Balance of System Components

Inverters: Solar panels produce direct current (DC) electricity, but most appliances and the electrical grid operate on alternating current (AC). Inverters perform the critical function of converting DC to AC while maximizing power output through maximum power point tracking (MPPT) algorithms.

Inverter Types:

  • String Inverters: Connect series-connected strings of panels to a single central inverter (1-100+ kW). Cost-effective but subject to shading losses affecting entire strings. Typical efficiency: 95-98%.
  • Microinverters: Individual inverters attached to each panel, eliminating string-level shading issues and enabling panel-level monitoring. Higher cost but improved energy harvest in partial shading conditions. Efficiency: 95-96%.
  • Power Optimizers: Hybrid approach combining panel-level optimization with central inversion, offering benefits of both approaches. Efficiency: 97-99%.
  • Hybrid Inverters: Integrate solar inversion with battery charging/discharging and grid-interactive capabilities in a single unit, simplifying system integration.

Mounting Systems: Proper mounting is critical for structural integrity, optimal orientation, and long-term performance. Roof-mounted systems use rails attached to the roof structure, with mounting methods varying by roof type (composition shingle, tile, metal, flat membrane). Ground-mounted systems offer easier installation and maintenance but require available land. Tracking systems—single-axis or dual-axis—orient panels toward the sun throughout the day, increasing energy production by 15-35% at the cost of higher system complexity and cost.

Monitoring and Control: Modern solar installations include monitoring systems that track production, detect faults, and optimize performance. Data collection occurs at panel, inverter, and system levels, with cloud-based platforms providing real-time visibility and automated alerts. Advanced systems integrate weather forecasting, utility pricing signals, and battery storage control to maximize economic value.

2.2 Wind Energy Systems

Wind Turbine Fundamentals

Wind turbines extract kinetic energy from moving air masses, converting it to rotational mechanical energy and then to electricity. The power available in wind increases with the cube of wind speed (P ∝ v³), making site selection critical—a location with 12 mph average winds produces twice the energy of an 8 mph site.

Modern wind turbines employ three-blade horizontal-axis designs that achieve optimal balance between material costs, rotor efficiency, and acoustic performance. The swept area of the rotor determines power capture capacity, with utility-scale turbines now exceeding 200 meters in rotor diameter and 14 MW in rated capacity.

Distributed Wind Applications

Small Wind Turbines (1-100 kW): These systems serve individual buildings, farms, or small communities. Modern small wind turbines incorporate pitch control, variable-speed generators, and advanced power electronics to optimize performance across varying wind conditions. Installation requires careful site assessment including wind resource evaluation, zoning compliance, and FAA clearance for tall towers.

Community Wind: Projects in the 100 kW to 10 MW range owned by local communities or cooperatives provide distributed generation while keeping economic benefits local. These mid-scale installations can power hundreds of homes while creating local jobs and revenue streams.

Wind Turbine Components

Rotor and Blades: Blades use aerodynamic profiles similar to aircraft wings, with carefully designed twist and taper to optimize performance across the blade length. Modern blades employ advanced composite materials—typically glass fiber or carbon fiber reinforced plastics—to achieve high strength-to-weight ratios. Blade pitch control adjusts the angle of attack to optimize power capture in varying winds and protect the turbine in high winds.

Generator Types: Most distributed wind turbines use permanent magnet generators, which eliminate the need for external excitation and achieve high efficiency across varying speeds. Doubly-fed induction generators (DFIGs), common in utility-scale turbines, offer excellent grid compatibility and power quality control. Direct-drive generators eliminate gearboxes, reducing maintenance requirements but increasing generator size and cost.

Control Systems: Modern wind turbines employ sophisticated control systems managing pitch, yaw, and power conversion to maximize energy capture while protecting equipment from over-speed and excessive loads. Advanced controls integrate weather forecasting, grid frequency signals, and power market prices to optimize operation for both energy production and grid support services.

2.3 Energy Storage Technologies

Electrochemical Storage

Lithium-ion Battery Systems: Lithium-ion batteries have revolutionized energy storage through their combination of high energy density, excellent cycle life, and decreasing costs. Different chemistries optimize different performance characteristics:

Chemistry Energy Density Cycle Life Primary Application
NMC (Nickel Manganese Cobalt) 200-250 Wh/kg 1,000-2,000 EVs, residential storage
LFP (Lithium Iron Phosphate) 90-160 Wh/kg 2,000-5,000 Stationary storage, EVs
NCA (Nickel Cobalt Aluminum) 200-260 Wh/kg 500-1,000 Premium EVs
LTO (Lithium Titanate) 70-90 Wh/kg 10,000-20,000 Fast charging, buses

Battery management systems (BMS) are critical for safe and effective operation, monitoring cell voltages, temperatures, and currents while balancing charge across cells. Advanced BMS incorporate state-of-charge estimation, state-of-health monitoring, and thermal management to maximize battery lifespan and performance.

Flow Batteries: Flow battery technology stores energy in liquid electrolytes contained in external tanks, with power and energy capacity independently scalable. Vanadium redox flow batteries are the most mature technology, offering several key advantages for stationary storage:

  • Power capacity determined by stack size, energy capacity by tank volume, enabling cost-effective scaling for long-duration applications
  • Deep discharge capability without degradation, unlike lithium-ion batteries that suffer accelerated aging with deep cycling
  • Extended cycle life (10,000-20,000 cycles) with minimal capacity fade
  • Non-flammable electrolytes enhancing safety for large installations
  • Operating temperature range of -5°C to 50°C without active cooling in many climates

Zinc-bromine and iron-chromium flow batteries offer potentially lower costs but face technical challenges including dendrite formation, membrane degradation, and electrolyte management. Organic flow batteries using carbon-based molecules show promise for further cost reduction and improved environmental profiles.

Thermal Energy Storage

Thermal storage systems store energy as heat or cold, offering excellent performance and economics for applications with significant thermal loads. Technologies span simple sensible heat storage (hot water tanks, rock beds) to advanced latent heat storage using phase-change materials and thermochemical storage.

Sensible Heat Storage: The simplest approach stores energy by changing the temperature of a storage medium without phase change. Water is the most common medium due to its high specific heat capacity (4.18 kJ/kg·K), availability, and safety. Stratified hot water tanks can store energy at 60-95°C for hours to days, supporting solar thermal systems, heat pumps, and CHP applications. Underground thermal energy storage (UTES) uses aquifers, boreholes, or caverns to store seasonal thermal energy for district heating systems.

Phase-Change Materials (PCMs): PCMs store energy through phase transitions (typically solid-liquid) at specific temperatures, offering higher energy density than sensible heat storage. Paraffin waxes, salt hydrates, and fatty acids serve as PCMs across different temperature ranges. A PCM melting at 28°C can store building cooling overnight for daytime release, reducing air conditioning loads during peak hours. Higher-temperature PCMs (120-180°C) integrate with industrial processes and concentrated solar power systems.

Thermochemical Storage: These systems store energy in reversible chemical reactions, achieving the highest energy densities among thermal storage technologies. Metal hydride systems, salt hydration reactions, and sorption processes can store thermal energy for months with minimal losses, enabling seasonal storage. Despite high energy density, thermochemical storage faces challenges including slow reaction kinetics, heat transfer limitations, and material degradation over repeated cycles.

2.4 Combined Heat and Power Systems

CHP Technology Overview

Combined heat and power systems achieve superior efficiency by capturing and utilizing waste heat from electricity generation. Conventional central power plants discard 50-65% of fuel energy as waste heat, achieving electrical efficiencies of only 35-45%. CHP systems, by using this thermal energy for heating, cooling, or industrial processes, achieve total system efficiencies of 65-90%.

CHP Technologies by Scale

Micro-CHP (1-10 kW): Residential and small commercial applications use micro-CHP systems based on internal combustion engines, Stirling engines, or fuel cells. These systems provide space heating, domestic hot water, and electricity, with thermal output modulated to match building loads. Modern micro-CHP units achieve electrical efficiencies of 25-35% and overall efficiencies exceeding 90%.

Commercial/Industrial CHP (100 kW - 10 MW): Mid-scale CHP employs reciprocating engines, combustion turbines, or steam turbines. Natural gas reciprocating engines are most common, offering electrical efficiencies of 35-40%, quick start-up, and good part-load performance. Gas turbines excel in larger applications (5+ MW), achieving 30-40% electrical efficiency with high-quality heat output suitable for absorption chillers or industrial processes.

Absorption Cooling: CHP systems can drive absorption chillers using waste heat to provide space cooling, creating combined cooling, heat, and power (CCHP or trigeneration) systems. This capability greatly improves CHP economics in warm climates where cooling loads are significant. Absorption chillers achieve coefficients of performance (COP) of 0.7-1.2 when driven by waste heat that would otherwise be discarded.

2.5 Electric Vehicle Charging and Vehicle-to-Grid

EV Charging Infrastructure

Electric vehicles represent both a load and a potential distributed energy resource. Charging infrastructure ranges from simple Level 1 (120V AC, 1.4-1.9 kW) for overnight residential charging to DC fast charging (50-350 kW) for rapid charging along travel corridors. Level 2 charging (240V AC, 3.3-19.2 kW) provides the optimal balance for most residential and workplace applications, fully charging vehicles in 4-8 hours.

Smart Charging and Load Management

Unmanaged EV charging can create significant grid impacts, with multiple vehicles charging simultaneously creating demand spikes exceeding transformer and circuit capacities. Smart charging systems coordinate charging to flatten load profiles, align charging with renewable generation, and respond to price signals or grid conditions. Time-of-use rates incentivize off-peak charging, while OpenADR protocols enable automated demand response.

Vehicle-to-Grid (V2G) Technology

Bidirectional charging enables EVs to provide grid services by discharging stored energy when needed. A fleet of EVs can aggregate to provide frequency regulation, voltage support, or peak shaving services. The technical requirements include bidirectional inverters, communication protocols (ISO 15118, CHAdeMO, CCS), and grid codes for DER interconnection. Economic viability depends on market structures that appropriately compensate grid services while ensuring adequate vehicle availability for transportation needs.

Vehicle-to-building (V2B) and vehicle-to-home (V2H) applications provide backup power during outages and reduce demand charges through peak shaving. A typical EV with a 60 kWh battery can power an average home for 2-3 days during an outage or significantly reduce grid consumption during daily peak periods.

2.6 Demand Response and Flexible Loads

Demand Response Fundamentals

Demand response manages electricity consumption to match generation availability, providing an alternative to building additional generation capacity. Strategies range from manual load curtailment during emergencies to automated, continuous optimization of flexible loads. The Federal Energy Regulatory Commission estimates demand response can reduce peak demand by 5-20% in well-designed programs.

Flexible Load Types

HVAC Systems: Heating, ventilation, and air conditioning loads represent 40-60% of building electricity consumption and offer excellent flexibility. Smart thermostats can pre-cool or pre-heat buildings before peak periods, shift operation to off-peak hours, or reduce set-points during demand response events. Building thermal mass provides storage, allowing temperature variations of 2-4°F without occupant discomfort while significantly reducing peak loads.

Water Heating: Electric water heaters store thermal energy, enabling load shifting without affecting hot water availability. Heat pump water heaters achieve COPs of 2.5-3.5, providing efficient operation while offering 2-4 hours of demand flexibility through tank thermal storage.

Industrial Processes: Many industrial loads offer flexibility including air compressors, refrigeration systems, water pumping, wastewater treatment, and batch processes. Automated controls can shift these loads to off-peak periods or curtail non-critical processes during grid emergencies while maintaining production schedules.

Integration Principle: The most effective distributed energy systems integrate multiple technologies—solar generation with battery storage, CHP with thermal storage, EV charging with demand response—creating resilient, efficient systems greater than the sum of their parts.
