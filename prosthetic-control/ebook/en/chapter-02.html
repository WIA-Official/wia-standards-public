2.1 EMG Signal Characteristics

Electromyography (EMG) measures the electrical activity of skeletal muscles. When motor neurons fire, they trigger action potentials in muscle fibers, creating measurable voltage differences that can be detected by surface or intramuscular electrodes. Understanding EMG signal characteristics is fundamental to effective prosthetic control.

2.1.1 Physiological Origins

The EMG signal originates from motor unit action potentials (MUAPs). A motor unit consists of a single motor neuron and all the muscle fibers it innervates. When the brain commands a muscle to contract, motor neurons begin firing action potentials that propagate along axons to the neuromuscular junction, where they trigger muscle fiber depolarization.

Key physiological factors affecting EMG signals:

  • Motor Unit Recruitment: Weak contractions recruit small motor units with few muscle fibers. As contraction force increases, larger motor units are recruited, increasing the EMG amplitude.
  • Firing Rate Modulation: Motor neurons can fire at rates from 6-8 Hz (relaxed) to 50+ Hz (maximal effort). Higher firing rates increase both force and EMG signal power.
  • Motor Unit Synchronization: Normally, motor units fire asynchronously. Synchronization (often due to fatigue or pathology) creates characteristic burst patterns in the EMG.
  • Muscle Fiber Types: Type I (slow-twitch) fibers produce smaller, longer-duration potentials. Type II (fast-twitch) fibers produce larger, shorter potentials. Muscle composition affects signal characteristics.

2.1.2 Signal Amplitude and Frequency Content

Surface EMG signals typically have:

  • Amplitude Range: 0-10 mV peak-to-peak before amplification, typically amplified to ±5V for analog-to-digital conversion
  • Dominant Frequency: 50-150 Hz, containing most signal power
  • Bandwidth: 10-500 Hz useful signal range
  • Signal-to-Noise Ratio: Highly variable, typically 10-40 dB depending on electrode quality, placement, and skin preparation

The EMG power spectrum follows a characteristic shape: low power at low frequencies (below 20 Hz), peak power in the 50-150 Hz range, and declining power at higher frequencies. The median frequency (frequency dividing the power spectrum into equal halves) typically ranges from 80-120 Hz for surface EMG.

Frequency content changes with muscle conditions:

  • Fatigue: Median frequency decreases by 5-20% during sustained contractions as metabolite accumulation alters muscle fiber conduction velocity
  • Force Level: Higher force levels recruit faster motor units, slightly increasing median frequency
  • Temperature: Cold muscles show decreased median frequency; warm muscles show increased frequency

2.1.3 Noise Sources and Artifacts

EMG signals are contaminated by various noise sources that must be addressed through proper signal conditioning:

Inherent Noise (0.01-1 μV):

  • Electrode thermal noise: Random thermal motion of electrons
  • Skin impedance noise: Electrical resistance of skin-electrode interface
  • Amplifier noise: Electronic noise from instrumentation

Ambient Noise (10-500 μV):

  • 50/60 Hz power line interference: Electromagnetic radiation from AC power systems
  • Radio frequency interference: Cell phones, Wi-Fi, other RF sources
  • Electrostatic coupling: Movement generating static charges

Physiological Artifacts (10-5000 μV):

  • ECG (electrocardiogram): Heart electrical activity can couple into limb electrodes
  • Motion artifacts: Electrode movement relative to skin creates large transient signals
  • Cross-talk: Nearby muscles' EMG signals contaminate the target muscle signal
  • Baseline wander: Low-frequency drift due to skin potential changes

2.2 Electrode Technologies and Placement

Electrode selection and placement critically affect signal quality. The WIA standard supports multiple electrode technologies to accommodate different clinical situations and cost constraints.

2.2.1 Surface Electrode Types

Wet Ag/AgCl Electrodes:

Silver/silver chloride electrodes with conductive gel provide the gold standard for EMG recording. The gel reduces skin impedance from ~100 kΩ to ~10 kΩ, improving signal quality. These electrodes offer excellent signal stability and low noise but require skin preparation (cleaning, sometimes light abrasion) and regular replacement as the gel dries out. Typical lifespan: 1-3 days of continuous wear.

Dry Contact Electrodes:

Metal (stainless steel, gold-plated) or conductive polymer electrodes that require no gel. These are more convenient and have indefinite lifespan but higher impedance (50-500 kΩ) and motion sensitivity. Modern active (amplified) dry electrodes partially compensate for high impedance by amplifying signals at the electrode before transmission. Dry electrodes work best when integrated into prosthetic sockets providing consistent pressure.

Textile Electrodes:

Conductive fabrics (silver-coated nylon, carbon-impregnated fibers) woven into garments or socket liners. Advantages include comfort, washability, and distributed contact area reducing pressure points. Disadvantages include variable impedance depending on moisture level and difficulty achieving optimal placement. Emerging technology for long-term home use.

High-Density Arrays:

Multiple small electrodes (typically 8-64) arranged in a grid pattern, providing spatial resolution of muscle activity. Arrays enable detection of individual motor unit potentials and robust pattern recognition even with electrode shift. However, they require more complex electronics and data processing. Typical electrode spacing: 5-10 mm. Array sizes: 4×4 to 8×8 electrodes.

2.2.2 Intramuscular Electrodes

For users with limited residual limb muscles or poor surface EMG signal quality, intramuscular electrodes can be surgically implanted:

Fine-Wire Electrodes:

Thin insulated wires (50-100 μm diameter) with exposed tips inserted into specific muscles using hypodermic needles. After insertion, the needle is removed, leaving wires in place. Fine-wire electrodes provide excellent selectivity (minimal cross-talk) and high amplitude signals (2-10× larger than surface EMG) but are invasive, have limited lifespan (days to weeks), and risk infection and tissue damage.

Permanent Implanted Electrodes:

Biocompatible electrodes (platinum, iridium oxide) with wireless telemetry can be permanently implanted in muscles. These provide stable long-term signals but require surgery, may trigger foreign body responses, and depend on battery-powered electronics. Systems like the Myoelectric Implantable Neuromuscular Interface (MINI) have demonstrated 5+ years of stable operation in animal studies and human trials.

2.2.3 Optimal Electrode Placement

Electrode placement follows these principles:

Muscle Belly Positioning:

Electrodes should be placed over the muscle belly (thickest part), aligned with muscle fiber direction. This maximizes signal amplitude and minimizes cross-talk from adjacent muscles. Placement near tendons or muscle edges produces weaker signals.

Bipolar Configuration:

Standard prosthetic control uses bipolar recording: two active electrodes over the muscle and one reference electrode over electrically neutral tissue (bone, tendon). The differential amplifier subtracts signals common to both electrodes (like power line interference) while amplifying the difference (the EMG signal). Electrode spacing: typically 20-30 mm for surface electrodes.

Consistency:

Precise, repeatable electrode placement is crucial for pattern recognition systems. Even 5-10 mm shifts can significantly degrade classifier performance. The WIA standard recommends anatomical landmarks, measuring tape, and permanent skin markers for consistent socket fabrication and daily donning.

Multiple Site Selection:

For pattern recognition control, electrode sites should maximize independence (low cross-talk). Recommended sites for transradial (below-elbow) amputation:

  • Flexor digitorum superficialis (finger flexion)
  • Extensor digitorum (finger extension)
  • Flexor carpi radialis (wrist flexion, radial deviation)
  • Extensor carpi radialis (wrist extension, radial deviation)
  • Flexor carpi ulnaris (wrist flexion, ulnar deviation)
  • Extensor carpi ulnaris (wrist extension, ulnar deviation)
  • Pronator teres (forearm pronation)
  • Supinator (forearm supination)

2.3 Signal Conditioning Pipeline

Raw EMG signals must be processed through a multi-stage pipeline before use in control algorithms. The WIA standard defines requirements for each processing stage to ensure consistent performance across implementations.

2.3.1 Amplification

Pre-amplification:

The first amplification stage should be as close to the electrodes as possible (ideally within 50 mm) to maximize signal-to-noise ratio before signals travel through cables where they can pick up interference. Pre-amplifier specifications per WIA standard:

  • Gain: 100-1000× (typically 500×)
  • Input impedance: >100 MΩ to minimize loading effects on high-impedance skin
  • Common mode rejection ratio (CMRR): >80 dB at 60 Hz to reject power line interference
  • Input-referred noise: <2 μV RMS
  • Bandwidth: 10-500 Hz

Main Amplification:

After pre-amplification, signals are further amplified to match the input range of analog-to-digital converters (typically ±5V or 0-3.3V). Total system gain: 1000-5000×, adjustable based on user-specific signal amplitudes.

2.3.2 Filtering

High-Pass Filtering:

Removes DC offset and low-frequency motion artifacts. The WIA standard specifies a 4th-order Butterworth high-pass filter with cutoff frequency between 10-20 Hz. Butterworth filters provide maximally flat passband response without ripples that could distort signal features.

Implementation (digital IIR filter):

Cutoff frequency: 20 Hz
Sampling rate: 1000 Hz
Filter order: 4
Type: Butterworth
Implementation: Cascaded second-order sections for numerical stability

Low-Pass Filtering:

Removes high-frequency noise and prevents aliasing. The WIA standard requires cutoff frequency of 450-500 Hz (below the Nyquist frequency for 1000 Hz sampling). Again, 4th-order Butterworth filters are recommended for their flat passband.

Notch Filtering:

Targets power line interference at 50 or 60 Hz (depending on region) and sometimes harmonics (120 Hz, 180 Hz). Narrow notch filters (Q factor: 20-30) remove interference while minimally affecting nearby frequencies.

Adaptive notch filters track slight variations in power line frequency (typically ±0.5 Hz) for optimal rejection. The WIA standard allows either fixed or adaptive notch filters based on implementation complexity tolerance.

2.3.3 Analog-to-Digital Conversion

The WIA standard specifies minimum ADC requirements:

  • Resolution: 12 bits minimum (4096 levels), 16 bits recommended (65536 levels) for better dynamic range
  • Sampling Rate: 1000 Hz minimum per channel, 2000 Hz recommended for detailed pattern recognition
  • Simultaneous Sampling: All channels should be sampled simultaneously (or within 1 μs) to preserve phase relationships important for pattern recognition
  • Dynamic Range: >60 dB to capture both weak and strong contractions without clipping or being lost in quantization noise

Higher resolution ADCs (16-24 bits) allow automatic gain adjustment, adapting to different users' signal amplitudes without manual calibration.

2.3.4 Rectification and Smoothing

For direct and proportional control (but not pattern recognition), EMG signals are rectified and smoothed to create a control envelope:

Full-Wave Rectification:

Takes the absolute value of the filtered EMG signal, converting bipolar signals (±V) to unipolar (0-V). This is necessary because EMG oscillates around zero; unrectified signals would average to zero.

Smoothing:

Applies a low-pass filter to the rectified signal to remove rapid fluctuations, creating a smooth control signal proportional to contraction strength. Common approaches:

  • Moving Average: Average of the last N samples. Simple to implement but introduces lag proportional to window size. Typical window: 100-300 ms.
  • Low-Pass Filter: Exponential moving average implemented as IIR filter. Cutoff: 3-10 Hz. Provides smooth output with minimal lag.
  • Root Mean Square (RMS): Square root of averaged squared samples. Better represents signal power and is less sensitive to outliers. Window: 100-200 ms.

The WIA standard recommends RMS with 150-200 ms window for proportional control, providing good responsiveness while filtering transient noise.

2.4 Feature Extraction for Pattern Recognition

Pattern recognition algorithms require features extracted from multi-channel EMG signals. Features should capture muscle activation patterns while being robust to noise, electrode shift, and physiological variations.

2.4.1 Time-Domain Features

Mean Absolute Value (MAV):

Average of absolute EMG values over a time window. Directly related to muscle contraction strength. Computationally simple, widely used as a baseline feature.

MAV = (1/N) * Σ|x[i]|
Where N = window length, x[i] = EMG samples
Typical window: 150-250 ms (150-250 samples at 1000 Hz)

Root Mean Square (RMS):

Square root of averaged squared samples. Represents signal power and is theoretically optimal for Gaussian noise. Slightly better performance than MAV in noisy conditions.

RMS = sqrt((1/N) * Σx[i]²)

Waveform Length (WL):

Sum of absolute differences between adjacent samples. Represents signal complexity and frequency content. Increases with muscle activation and frequency.

WL = Σ|x[i+1] - x[i]|

Zero Crossings (ZC):

Number of times the signal crosses zero amplitude. Related to frequency content. Higher contraction levels generally increase zero crossings. Often combined with a threshold to reduce noise sensitivity.

Slope Sign Changes (SSC):

Number of times the signal slope changes sign (peaks and valleys). Also represents frequency content. Less sensitive to baseline drift than zero crossings.

2.4.2 Frequency-Domain Features

Median Frequency (MDF):

Frequency dividing the power spectrum into two equal halves. Robust indicator of muscle fatigue (decreases with fatigue) and contraction intensity. Requires Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) computation.

Mean Frequency (MNF):

Average frequency weighted by power spectrum. Similar to median frequency but more sensitive to high-frequency components. Also decreases with fatigue.

Peak Frequency:

Frequency with maximum power in the spectrum. Can be unstable in noisy conditions but useful when combined with other features.

Spectral Moments:

Statistical moments (mean, variance, skewness) of the power spectrum. Capture distribution of frequency content beyond single-value metrics.

2.4.3 Time-Frequency Features

For non-stationary signals (rapidly changing contractions), time-frequency methods provide better characterization:

Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT):

Applies FFT to overlapping windows, creating a spectrogram showing how frequency content changes over time. Useful for transient movements and gesture recognition.

Wavelet Transform:

Decomposes signals into different frequency bands at different time resolutions. Provides better time resolution at high frequencies and frequency resolution at low frequencies compared to STFT. Wavelet coefficients can serve as features for classification.

2.4.4 Advanced Features

Autoregressive (AR) Coefficients:

Parameters of AR models fitted to EMG signals. AR models predict future samples based on past samples. Coefficients capture spectral characteristics efficiently. Typical AR order: 4-6.

Hjorth Parameters:

Three metrics describing signal properties:

  • Activity: Signal variance (power)
  • Mobility: Square root of variance of first derivative normalized by signal variance (mean frequency)
  • Complexity: Mobility of first derivative normalized by signal mobility (bandwidth)

Sample Entropy:

Measure of signal regularity. Regular signals (like fatigue) have low entropy; random signals have high entropy. Robust to noise and signal length variations.

2.4.5 Feature Set Selection

The WIA standard recommends a baseline feature set providing good performance across applications:

Recommended Minimal Feature Set (per channel):

  1. Mean Absolute Value (MAV)
  2. Waveform Length (WL)
  3. Zero Crossings (ZC)
  4. Slope Sign Changes (SSC)

This 4-feature-per-channel set provides ~85-90% classification accuracy for basic gestures with 8 channels (32 total features).

Recommended Enhanced Feature Set:

Adding these features improves accuracy to 90-95%:

  1. Root Mean Square (RMS)
  2. Median Frequency (MDF)
  3. 4 Autoregressive Coefficients (AR1-AR4)

Total: 10 features per channel, 80 features for 8 channels.

Implementations can use additional features for specialized applications, but all WIA-compliant systems must support at least the minimal feature set for interoperability.

2.5 Real-Time Processing Considerations

Prosthetic control requires real-time signal processing with guaranteed low latency. The WIA standard specifies performance requirements and implementation strategies.

2.5.1 Latency Requirements

Total system latency (from muscle contraction to prosthetic movement initiation) must be minimized for natural control. The WIA standard defines latency budgets:

  • Acceptable: <100 ms total latency. Most users adapt well to this delay.
  • Target: <50 ms total latency. Feels nearly instantaneous to users.
  • Excellent: <30 ms total latency. Indistinguishable from natural limb control in most tasks.

Latency sources:

  • Signal acquisition: 1-2 ms (sampling interval)
  • Filtering: 5-20 ms (depends on filter order and implementation)
  • Feature extraction: 5-10 ms (window-based features)
  • Classification: 1-5 ms (depends on algorithm complexity)
  • Communication to prosthetic: 2-10 ms (depends on protocol)
  • Prosthetic response: 10-30 ms (mechanical delays)

Careful optimization is needed to meet the <50 ms target.

2.5.2 Computational Efficiency

Real-time processing on embedded systems requires efficient algorithms:

Fixed-Point Arithmetic:

Many microcontrollers lack floating-point units. Implementing algorithms in fixed-point arithmetic (using integers to represent fractional numbers) can provide 5-10× speedup. The WIA standard provides fixed-point reference implementations of all required signal processing functions.

SIMD Operations:

Single Instruction Multiple Data operations process multiple samples simultaneously. Modern ARM processors support NEON SIMD instructions that can accelerate filtering and feature extraction by 2-4×.

Hardware Acceleration:

Dedicated hardware modules (like FFT accelerators or DSP coprocessors) can offload intensive computations from the main processor. The WIA standard defines hardware abstraction layers allowing systems to utilize available acceleration.

2.5.3 Power Consumption

Battery-powered prosthetic controllers must balance performance with power efficiency:

Adaptive Sampling:

Sample at high rates (2000 Hz) during active use for best performance, but reduce to 100-200 Hz during idle periods to save power. Motion sensors detect when the prosthetic is in use.

Processing Duty Cycle:

Instead of continuous processing, systems can process in bursts (e.g., 50 ms active, 50 ms sleep) when high responsiveness isn't needed. This can reduce power consumption by 30-50%.

Algorithm Selection:

Simple time-domain features require much less computation (and power) than frequency-domain or time-frequency features. The WIA standard supports multiple feature sets, allowing power-constrained implementations to use simpler features.

2.6 Signal Quality Assessment

Automatic assessment of signal quality enables systems to adapt to changing conditions and alert users to problems.

2.6.1 Quality Metrics

Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR):

Ratio of signal power during contraction to noise power during rest. SNR > 20 dB indicates good quality; SNR < 10 dB suggests electrode problems. Computed by comparing RMS values during active and rest periods.

Electrode Impedance:

High impedance (>100 kΩ for wet electrodes, >500 kΩ for dry) indicates poor skin contact. Can be measured by injecting small test currents (10 μA at 10 Hz) and measuring resulting voltages. Most modern prosthetic controllers include impedance measurement circuitry.

Motion Artifact Detection:

Large low-frequency components (<10 Hz) indicate motion artifacts from electrode movement. Automated detection triggers warnings or temporarily ignores corrupted data.

Power Line Interference:

Excessive power at 50/60 Hz (more than 10% of total signal power) indicates poor shielding or grounding. Can be detected by computing FFT and examining power at line frequency.

2.6.2 Adaptive Signal Processing

Modern systems adapt to signal quality in real-time:

Automatic Gain Control:

Adjusts amplifier gain to maintain optimal ADC input levels. If signals are too weak (using <20% of ADC range), gain increases. If signals clip (saturating ADC), gain decreases. Prevents loss of information due to improper gain settings.

Adaptive Filtering:

Adjusts filter parameters based on noise characteristics. If power line interference increases, notch filter depth increases. If motion artifacts are detected, high-pass cutoff increases temporarily.

Feature Weighting:

Pattern recognition systems can weight features based on their reliability. Channels with poor SNR receive lower weights in classification. This graceful degradation maintains functionality even when some channels are compromised.

2.7 WIA Data Format Specification

The WIA standard defines a JSON-based data format for EMG signal representation, ensuring interoperability between acquisition hardware and control processors.

2.7.1 Signal Packet Structure

{
  "version": "1.0.0",
  "timestamp": 1735142400000,
  "device_id": "PROS-EMG-2024-001",
  "sampling_rate": 1000,
  "channels": [
    {
      "id": 1,
      "electrode_type": "AgAgCl_wet",
      "position": "flexor_digitorum",
      "impedance_ohms": 8500,
      "gain": 500,
      "samples": [245, 238, 251, ...],
      "quality": {
        "snr_db": 28.5,
        "artifact_score": 0.12,
        "status": "good"
      }
    },
    ...
  ],
  "features": {
    "method": "time_domain_baseline",
    "window_ms": 200,
    "values": [
      {"channel": 1, "MAV": 245.6, "WL": 1823.4, "ZC": 47, "SSC": 52},
      ...
    ]
  }
}

2.7.2 Binary Format

For bandwidth-constrained applications, the WIA standard also defines a compact binary format:

Header (16 bytes):
  - Magic number (4 bytes): 0x57494145 ("WIAE")
  - Version (2 bytes): 0x0100 (v1.0)
  - Timestamp (8 bytes): Unix timestamp in milliseconds
  - Channel count (1 byte): Number of channels (1-255)
  - Sampling rate (1 byte): Encoded as 2^value * 100 Hz

Per-channel data (variable length):
  - Channel ID (1 byte)
  - Sample count (2 bytes)
  - Samples (2 bytes each, signed 16-bit integers)

This binary format reduces bandwidth by ~70% compared to JSON while maintaining all essential information.
