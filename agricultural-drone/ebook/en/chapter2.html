Chapter 2: Multispectral Imaging & Remote Sensing

Chapter Overview: Understanding multispectral cameras, spectral bands, and remote sensing principles for agricultural applications.

2.1 Introduction to Multispectral Imaging

Multispectral imaging captures light across multiple specific wavelength bands beyond human vision. While our eyes see RGB (Red, Green, Blue), agricultural cameras capture additional bands like Near-Infrared (NIR) and Red Edge crucial for assessing plant health.

Why Multispectral?

  • Early Detection: Identify crop stress 7-14 days before visible symptoms
  • Precision: Quantify health differences across fields
  • Objectivity: Numerical data vs. subjective visual assessment
  • Large Scale: Survey hundreds of hectares in hours

2.2 Spectral Bands Explained

📷 Common Agricultural Bands

1. Blue (450nm)

  • Wavelength: 450±20nm
  • Use: Chlorophyll content, water clarity
  • Absorption: High in healthy vegetation

2. Green (560nm)

  • Wavelength: 560±20nm
  • Use: Leaf greenness, crop vigor
  • Reflection: Peaks in healthy plants

3. Red (650nm)

  • Wavelength: 650±20nm
  • Use: Chlorophyll absorption (key for NDVI)
  • Absorption: Maximum in photosynthetically active vegetation

4. Red Edge (730nm)

  • Wavelength: 730±10nm
  • Use: Sensitive to chlorophyll variations, nitrogen status
  • Unique: Rapid change region between red and NIR

5. Near-Infrared (850nm)

  • Wavelength: 850±40nm
  • Use: Biomass, water content, plant structure (key for NDVI)
  • Reflection: Very high in healthy vegetation (up to 50%)

2.4 Camera Calibration

Calibration ensures imagery accuracy and consistency:

Radiometric Calibration

  • Purpose: Convert raw pixel values to reflectance
  • Method: Use calibrated reflectance panel before/after flight
  • Panel: Typically 50% gray reference target
  • Process: Capture panel image, software applies correction to all images

Geometric Calibration

  • Purpose: Correct lens distortion
  • Method: Factory calibration or field calibration with checkerboard pattern
  • Result: Accurate spatial positioning and area measurements

2.5 Image Acquisition Best Practices

Flight Parameters

  • Altitude: 50-120m AGL (affects resolution: 50m = 3cm/px, 120m = 7cm/px)
  • Speed: 5-10 m/s (slower for higher overlap)
  • Overlap: 75-80% forward, 60-70% side (ensures complete coverage)
  • Time of Day: 10am-2pm (sun angle 30-60° for consistent lighting)
  • Weather: Clear or partly cloudy (avoid harsh shadows, rain, wind >10m/s)

2.6 Spectral Indices

Mathematical combinations of bands reveal crop properties:

NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index)

NDVI = (NIR - Red) / (NIR + Red)

  • Range: -1 to +1
  • Healthy vegetation: 0.6-0.9
  • Stressed vegetation: 0.3-0.6
  • Bare soil: 0.0-0.2

NDRE (Normalized Difference Red Edge)

NDRE = (NIR - RedEdge) / (NIR + RedEdge)

  • More sensitive to chlorophyll than NDVI
  • Better for mid-to-late season crops
  • Range: 0.0-0.8

GNDVI (Green NDVI)

GNDVI = (NIR - Green) / (NIR + Green)

  • More sensitive to chlorophyll concentration
  • Useful for crops at mid-growth stage

2.7 Data Processing Workflow

  1. Image Upload: Transfer images from drone SD card
  2. Calibration: Apply radiometric calibration using reflectance panel
  3. Alignment: Stitch individual images into orthomosaic
  4. Index Calculation: Generate NDVI, NDRE, other indices
  5. Analysis: Identify zones, generate prescriptions
  6. Export: GeoTIFF, shapefile for farm management software

2.8 Applications by Crop Type

Rice

  • Monitor nitrogen levels (NDRE)
  • Detect disease (brown spot, blast) via spectral signatures
  • Assess lodging risk
  • Optimal index: NDVI, NDRE

Corn/Wheat

  • Variable rate nitrogen application
  • Early disease detection (rust, blight)
  • Emergence counting
  • Optimal index: NDVI, GNDVI

Soybeans

  • Weed detection
  • Nutrient deficiency mapping
  • Canopy coverage assessment
  • Optimal index: NDVI, SAVI

2.9 Key Takeaways

  • Multispectral cameras capture 4-6 bands including NIR and Red Edge
  • NIR band is critical for vegetation health assessment
  • Proper calibration ensures accurate, repeatable measurements
  • Flight parameters significantly affect image quality
  • Multiple spectral indices provide different crop insights
