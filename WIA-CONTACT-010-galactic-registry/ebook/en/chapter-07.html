Chapter 7: Governance and Access Control

"Power over cosmic knowledge must be distributed, transparent, and accountable—for no single nation, institution, or individual can be trusted with the sole authority to shape humanity's relationship with the stars."

7.1 The Governance Challenge

Who decides what goes into the Galactic Registry? Who can access what information? Who has the authority to make policy decisions that might affect all humanity? The governance framework for the WIA-CONTACT-010 standard must balance multiple competing imperatives:

Governance Principle Requirements Tensions
Global Representation All nations and cultures have voice in decisions vs. Technical expertise concentration in wealthy nations
Scientific Merit Decisions guided by evidence and expertise vs. Democratic accountability to non-experts
Transparency Open processes, public access to information vs. Legitimate national security concerns
Agility Quick response to contact events vs. Deliberative consensus building
Long-term Stability Resilient across political changes vs. Adaptability to new circumstances

7.2 Access Control Architecture

Not all registry information should be accessible to all people. The challenge: implement robust access controls while minimizing secrecy and maximizing transparency.

7.2.1 Multi-Tier Classification System

Tier Classification Access Content Examples
1 Public Anyone, anywhere, anytime Confirmed peaceful civilizations (location obscured if nearby), scientific data with no security implications, cultural exchanges
2 Scientific Community Credentialed researchers, peer review required Raw observational data, candidate detections, detailed technosignature analysis, full location data for non-threatening civilizations
3 Government Restricted National security agencies, UN Security Council Threat assessments, Earth's precise location references, vulnerabilities analysis, strategic intelligence
4 Compartmented Specific need-to-know basis only Hostile contact details, weapons technology, Earth defense capabilities, active operations
5 Sealed Archive Time-locked, auto-declassifies after period Information too sensitive for current era but valuable to future generations, diplomatic negotiations

7.5 Crisis Governance

Crisis situations demand rapid decision-making with reduced normal deliberation.

7.5.1 Emergency Protocols

Crisis Response Framework: LEVEL 1: ROUTINE ANOMALY (Normal procedures) Timeline: Days to weeks for verification Authority: Scientific Committee Disclosure: After peer review (30-90 days) LEVEL 2: CONFIRMED DETECTION (Accelerated procedures) Timeline: 24-72 hours for strategic assessment Authority: Strategic Council convened Disclosure: 7 days after Strategic Council review LEVEL 3: ACTIVE CONTACT (Emergency procedures) Timeline: 12-24 hours for initial response decision Authority: Strategic Council + UNOEA Director Disclosure: 24 hours (unless actively harmful to reveal) LEVEL 4: IMMINENT THREAT (War footing) Timeline: 1-6 hours for emergency session Authority: UN Security Council + emergency coalition Disclosure: Immediate (public has right to know) LEVEL 5: EXISTENTIAL CRISIS (Survival mode) Timeline: Immediate action authority Authority: Pre-authorized response protocols activate Disclosure: Full transparency (survival overrides all secrecy)

7.6 Preventing Capture and Corruption

Over decades or centuries, powerful interests may attempt to capture registry governance. The standard includes anti-corruption mechanisms.

7.6.1 Term Limits and Rotation

Governance Body Term Length Rotation Policy Rationale
Strategic Council 5 years 1/3 rotates every 18 months Continuity + fresh perspectives
Scientific Committee 4 years Half rotates every 2 years Maintain expertise while preventing entrenchment
Ethics Board 6 years Staggered replacement Long-term moral consistency
Public Oversight 2 years Random sortition every 2 years Fresh citizen perspective, resist capture

Chapter Summary

Key Concepts:

  • Multi-stakeholder governance distributes authority among Strategic Council, Scientific Committee, Ethics Board, and Public Oversight
  • Decision-making protocols match authority level to decision significance (routine to existential)
  • Multi-tier classification system (Public, Scientific, Government, Compartmented, Sealed) with explicit justification requirements
  • Dynamic access control rules adapt to context while maintaining audit trails
  • Transparency as default principle: classify minimally, declassify quickly
  • International coordination through federated regional nodes
  • Crisis governance protocols enable rapid response while preventing abuse
  • Anti-corruption mechanisms include term limits, rotation, and conflict of interest policies
