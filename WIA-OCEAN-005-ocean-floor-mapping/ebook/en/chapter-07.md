# Chapter 7: Global Mapping Initiatives

## 7.1 The Quest to Map the Ocean Floor

Despite covering more than 70% of Earth's surface, the ocean floor remains largely unexplored. As of 2025, only approximately 25% of the seafloor has been mapped at high resolution, a stark contrast to our comprehensive maps of land surfaces, the Moon, and Mars. This knowledge gap represents both a scientific challenge and an opportunity—closing it will reveal unknown ecosystems, geological processes, resources, and hazards while demonstrating international cooperation at its finest.

Several ambitious initiatives are working to accelerate seafloor mapping, coordinating contributions from governments, research institutions, commercial entities, and citizen scientists. These efforts recognize that comprehensive ocean floor mapping serves humanity's collective interests, requiring collaboration that transcends national boundaries and institutional silos.

The WIA-OCEAN-005 standard supports these global initiatives by ensuring data compatibility, quality, and accessibility. Standardized formats and metadata enable integration of data from diverse sources into coherent global maps. This interoperability is essential for achieving the ambitious goal of mapping the entire ocean floor at high resolution.

## 7.2 Seabed 2030: The Definitive Initiative

The Nippon Foundation-GEBCO Seabed 2030 Project represents the most ambitious seafloor mapping initiative ever undertaken. Launched in 2017, Seabed 2030 aims to map the entire ocean floor at high resolution by the year 2030. This collaborative project coordinates data contributions from around the world, identifies priority areas for new surveys, and makes all data freely available to benefit humanity.

**Organizational Structure**: Seabed 2030 operates through four Regional Data Assembly and Coordination Centers (DACCs) covering the Atlantic and Indian Oceans, Pacific Ocean, Southern Ocean, and Arctic Ocean. Each center coordinates mapping activities in its region, assembles contributed data, and produces regional compilations. A Global Data Assembly and Coordination Center integrates regional products into a global grid.

**Data Requirements**: The project establishes clear data quality requirements based on IHO standards. Contributed data must include comprehensive metadata documenting acquisition parameters, processing methods, and uncertainty estimates. Quality control procedures ensure data meets minimum standards before inclusion in compilations.

**Progress Tracking**: As of 2025, the project has mapped approximately 25% of the seafloor at target resolution (100 meters or better), a significant increase from the 6% mapped when the project began. However, the remaining 75% presents substantial challenges, particularly in remote ocean regions far from established survey infrastructure.

**Crowdsourced Bathymetry**: Seabed 2030 actively encourages data contributions from all sources. Commercial vessels, research expeditions, fishing boats, and even recreational sailors can contribute bathymetric data. Standardized data formats and submission procedures make contributing easy, while quality control ensures data reliability.

## 7.3 National Mapping Programs

Individual nations conduct systematic mapping of their territorial waters, exclusive economic zones (EEZs), and extended continental shelves:

**United States**: The National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) leads U.S. seafloor mapping efforts through programs including the Office of Coast Survey, the Office of Ocean Exploration and Research, and partnerships with academic institutions. The National Bathymetric Source provides a single authoritative repository for U.S. bathymetric data.

**United Kingdom**: The UK Hydrographic Office (UKHO) has mapped British waters for navigation purposes for over 200 years. Modern programs use multibeam sonar and lidar bathymetry to create comprehensive coverage of UK territorial waters, supporting navigation, marine planning, and environmental management.

**Japan**: The Japan Coast Guard and Japan Agency for Marine-Earth Science and Technology (JAMSTEC) conduct extensive mapping around Japan and throughout the Pacific. Japan's island geography and vulnerability to tsunamis make seafloor mapping particularly important for hazard assessment and disaster preparedness.

**Norway**: Norway systematically maps its extensive coastline and continental shelf using multibeam sonar and other technologies. The MAREANO program integrates bathymetric mapping with biological, geological, and chemical sampling to support ecosystem-based management.

**Australia**: Geoscience Australia coordinates national seafloor mapping efforts, covering Australia's vast maritime jurisdiction—the third largest in the world. Mapping supports resource management, environmental protection, and maritime safety.

**Canada**: The Canadian Hydrographic Service maps Canadian waters, from the Atlantic to the Pacific to the Arctic. Canada's extensive coastline and northern waters present unique challenges, particularly ice-covered regions.

## 7.4 Korean Ocean Mapping Efforts

The Republic of Korea has emerged as a significant contributor to global ocean floor mapping efforts, leveraging advanced technology and strong commitment to marine science:

**Korea Hydrographic and Oceanographic Agency (KHOA)**: As Korea's primary hydrographic authority, KHOA conducts systematic surveys of Korean waters using state-of-the-art multibeam sonar systems. The agency produces nautical charts, supports maritime safety, and contributes data to international databases.

**Korea Institute of Ocean Science and Technology (KIOST)**: KIOST conducts research-focused mapping expeditions in Korean waters and internationally. The institute operates research vessels equipped with advanced mapping systems, including the R/V Isabu and R/V Onnuri, capable of deep-ocean surveys.

**Yellow Sea and East China Sea Surveys**: Korea has conducted extensive mapping in the Yellow Sea and East China Sea, regions of significant economic and environmental importance. These surveys support fisheries management, marine spatial planning, and understanding of sediment transport processes.

**Deep-Sea Exploration**: Korean research vessels have participated in mapping expeditions to the Pacific Ocean, Indian Ocean, and Southern Ocean, contributing to global seafloor knowledge. These efforts include surveys of polymetallic nodule fields and hydrothermal vent systems.

**Technology Development**: Korean institutions develop advanced ocean mapping technologies, including autonomous underwater vehicles (AUVs), multibeam sonar systems, and data processing software. These technological advances benefit the global ocean mapping community.

**International Collaboration**: Korea actively participates in international ocean mapping initiatives, contributing data to Seabed 2030 and collaborating with regional partners on joint surveys. This cooperation exemplifies the 弘益人間 philosophy—advancing knowledge that benefits all humanity.

**Korean Continental Shelf Project**: Korea has conducted extensive mapping to delineate its extended continental shelf under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS). This work demonstrates the importance of bathymetric data for establishing maritime boundaries and jurisdictions.

## 7.5 Commercial and Industry Contributions

Commercial entities contribute significantly to global seafloor mapping, often as byproducts of their primary operations:

**Offshore Energy**: Oil, gas, and offshore wind companies conduct detailed seafloor surveys for site characterization, route planning, and environmental assessment. These surveys often cover large areas at high resolution. Sharing this data with public repositories multiplies its value.

**Submarine Cable Industry**: Companies deploying submarine telecommunications and power cables conduct route surveys that produce valuable bathymetric data. Cable routes span ocean basins, providing data for previously unsurveyed areas.

**Deep-Sea Mining**: Companies exploring deep-sea mineral resources conduct extensive mapping of potential mining sites. These surveys reveal seafloor features and geological processes in remote ocean regions.

**Fisheries**: Fishing vessels equipped with modern echo sounders collect bathymetric data during normal operations. While individual fishing tracks provide limited spatial coverage, aggregating data from many vessels over time can fill gaps in global coverage.

**Marine Transportation**: Commercial shipping routes cross all ocean basins. Equipping vessels with systems that log depth measurements during transit could generate enormous quantities of bathymetric data, though quality control and standardization remain challenges.

## 7.6 Technological Innovations

Achieving comprehensive ocean floor mapping by 2030 requires technological innovations that improve efficiency and reduce costs:

**Autonomous Platforms**: Uncrewed surface vehicles (USVs) and autonomous underwater vehicles (AUVs) enable cost-effective surveys, operating without large crews or expensive ship time. These platforms can access shallow, confined, or hazardous areas unsuitable for traditional vessels.

**Multi-Platform Integration**: Combining data from satellites, aircraft, ships, AUVs, and USVs maximizes coverage and cost-effectiveness. Each platform's strengths compensate for others' limitations, creating comprehensive surveys more efficiently than any single platform could achieve.

**Improved Processing**: Automated data processing reduces the time and expertise required to transform raw sonar data into finished maps. Machine learning algorithms detect and remove artifacts, classify seafloor types, and identify features of interest.

**Cloud Computing**: Cloud-based processing and storage enable handling of massive datasets, distributed collaboration, and on-demand access to computing resources. This infrastructure supports the data-intensive workflows required for global mapping.

**Improved Sensors**: Advances in sonar technology provide better resolution, greater range, and higher reliability. New sensor types, including quantum gravimeters and advanced optical systems, complement acoustic mapping in specific applications.

interface DataSource {
  id: string;
  organization: string;
  country: string;
  platform: 'ship' | 'auv' | 'usv' | 'satellite';
  quality: 'IHO_Order_1a' | 'IHO_Order_1b' | 'IHO_Order_2' | 'reconnaissance';
}

interface BathymetricDataset {
  source: DataSource;
  acquisitionDate: Date;
  bounds: GeographicBounds;
  resolution: number;
  verticalUncertainty: number;
  horizontalUncertainty: number;
  dataUrl: string;
  metadata: Map<string, string>;
}

interface GlobalGrid {
  resolution: number;
  bounds: GeographicBounds;
  cells: Map<string, GridCellWithProvenance>;
}

interface GridCellWithProvenance {
  depth: number;
  uncertainty: number;
  sourceId: string;
  acquisitionDate: Date;
  quality: string;
}

/**
 * Integrate multiple bathymetric datasets into a global grid
 */
class GlobalBathymetryIntegrator {
  private datasets: Map<string, BathymetricDataset> = new Map();
  private grid: GlobalGrid;

  constructor(resolution: number) {
    this.grid = {
      resolution,
      bounds: {
        north: 90,
        south: -90,
        east: 180,
        west: -180
      },
      cells: new Map()
    };
  }

  /**
   * Register a new dataset
   */
  registerDataset(dataset: BathymetricDataset): void {
    this.datasets.set(dataset.source.id, dataset);
  }

  /**
   * Integrate dataset into global grid with quality-based prioritization
   */
  async integrateDataset(datasetId: string): Promise<void> {
    const dataset = this.datasets.get(datasetId);
    if (!dataset) {
      throw new Error(`Dataset ${datasetId} not found`);
    }

    // Load dataset (in practice, would fetch from URL)
    const data = await this.loadDataset(dataset);

    // Process each data point
    for (const point of data) {
      const cellKey = this.getCellKey(point.position);
      const existingCell = this.grid.cells.get(cellKey);

      // Decide whether to use new data based on quality and recency
      if (this.shouldReplaceCell(existingCell, dataset, point)) {
        this.grid.cells.set(cellKey, {
          depth: point.depth,
          uncertainty: point.uncertainty,
          sourceId: dataset.source.id,
          acquisitionDate: dataset.acquisitionDate,
          quality: dataset.source.quality
        });
      }
    }
  }

  /**
   * Determine if new data should replace existing cell value
   */
  private shouldReplaceCell(
    existingCell: GridCellWithProvenance | undefined,
    newDataset: BathymetricDataset,
    newPoint: BathymetricSounding
  ): boolean {
    if (!existingCell) return true;  // No existing data

    // Quality hierarchy
    const qualityRank = {
      'IHO_Order_1a': 4,
      'IHO_Order_1b': 3,
      'IHO_Order_2': 2,
      'reconnaissance': 1
    };

    const existingQuality = qualityRank[existingCell.quality as keyof typeof qualityRank] || 0;
    const newQuality = qualityRank[newDataset.source.quality] || 0;

    // Prefer higher quality
    if (newQuality > existingQuality) return true;
    if (newQuality < existingQuality) return false;

    // Same quality: prefer more recent
    if (newDataset.acquisitionDate > existingCell.acquisitionDate) return true;
    if (newDataset.acquisitionDate < existingCell.acquisitionDate) return false;

    // Same quality and date: prefer lower uncertainty
    return newPoint.uncertainty < existingCell.uncertainty;
  }

  /**
   * Get grid cell key for position
   */
  private getCellKey(position: Position): string {
    const x = Math.floor(position.longitude / this.grid.resolution);
    const y = Math.floor(position.latitude / this.grid.resolution);
    return `${x},${y}`;
  }

  /**
   * Calculate coverage statistics
   */
  calculateCoverageStats(): {
    totalCells: number;
    coveredCells: number;
    coveragePercent: number;
    byQuality: Map<string, number>;
    bySource: Map<string, number>;
  } {
    const latCells = Math.ceil(180 / this.grid.resolution);
    const lonCells = Math.ceil(360 / this.grid.resolution);
    const totalCells = latCells * lonCells;

    const byQuality = new Map<string, number>();
    const bySource = new Map<string, number>();

    for (const cell of this.grid.cells.values()) {
      byQuality.set(
        cell.quality,
        (byQuality.get(cell.quality) || 0) + 1
      );
      bySource.set(
        cell.sourceId,
        (bySource.get(cell.sourceId) || 0) + 1
      );
    }

    return {
      totalCells,
      coveredCells: this.grid.cells.size,
      coveragePercent: (this.grid.cells.size / totalCells) * 100,
      byQuality,
      bySource
    };
  }

  /**
   * Identify gaps in coverage
   */
  identifyGaps(minQuality: string): GeographicBounds[] {
    const gaps: GeographicBounds[] = [];
    const qualityRank = {
      'IHO_Order_1a': 4,
      'IHO_Order_1b': 3,
      'IHO_Order_2': 2,
      'reconnaissance': 1
    };

    const requiredRank = qualityRank[minQuality as keyof typeof qualityRank] || 0;

    // Simplified gap detection - find contiguous regions below quality threshold
    // Production implementation would use more sophisticated spatial analysis

    const gapCells: Set<string> = new Set();

    const latCells = Math.ceil(180 / this.grid.resolution);
    const lonCells = Math.ceil(360 / this.grid.resolution);

    for (let y = 0; y < latCells; y++) {
      for (let x = 0; x < lonCells; x++) {
        const cellKey = `${x},${y}`;
        const cell = this.grid.cells.get(cellKey);

        if (!cell || qualityRank[cell.quality as keyof typeof qualityRank] < requiredRank) {
          gapCells.add(cellKey);
        }
      }
    }

    // Convert gap cells to geographic bounds
    // (simplified - production would cluster into regions)
    for (const cellKey of gapCells) {
      const [x, y] = cellKey.split(',').map(Number);
      const west = x * this.grid.resolution - 180;
      const south = y * this.grid.resolution - 90;

      gaps.push({
        north: south + this.grid.resolution,
        south: south,
        east: west + this.grid.resolution,
        west: west
      });
    }

    return gaps;
  }

  /**
   * Generate coverage report
   */
  generateReport(): string {
    const stats = this.calculateCoverageStats();

    let report = '=== Global Bathymetric Coverage Report ===\n\n';
    report += `Total ocean area: ${stats.totalCells.toLocaleString()} cells\n`;
    report += `Mapped area: ${stats.coveredCells.toLocaleString()} cells\n`;
    report += `Coverage: ${stats.coveragePercent.toFixed(2)}%\n\n`;

    report += 'Coverage by Quality:\n';
    for (const [quality, count] of stats.byQuality) {
      const percent = (count / stats.coveredCells) * 100;
      report += `  ${quality}: ${count.toLocaleString()} cells (${percent.toFixed(2)}%)\n`;
    }

    report += '\nCoverage by Source:\n';
    for (const [source, count] of stats.bySource) {
      const dataset = this.datasets.get(source);
      const percent = (count / stats.coveredCells) * 100;
      report += `  ${dataset?.organization || source}: ${count.toLocaleString()} cells (${percent.toFixed(2)}%)\n`;
    }

    report += '\n弘益人間 (Benefit All Humanity)\n';
    report += 'Global ocean mapping serves all of humanity through international cooperation.\n';

    return report;
  }

  /**
   * Load dataset (placeholder for actual data loading)
   */
  private async loadDataset(dataset: BathymetricDataset): Promise<BathymetricSounding[]> {

  }

   */
