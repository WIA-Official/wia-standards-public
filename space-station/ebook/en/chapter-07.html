Station Operations

Resupply, EVA, and Maintenance

🛸 Cargo Spacecraft

Space stations cannot be self-sufficient. Food, water, oxygen, experiments, spare parts, and personal items must be regularly resupplied from Earth. Multiple nations and private companies operate cargo vehicles.

Russian Progress

Progress MS - Longest-serving cargo spacecraft (since 1978)

  • Payload capacity: up to 2,450 kg
  • Launch vehicle: Soyuz rocket
  • Docking: Automatic (Kurs system)
  • Station time: ~6 months
  • Propulsion: Can boost ISS orbit
  • Reentry: Burns up with trash over Pacific
  • Frequency: 3-4 missions annually

US Commercial Cargo

SpaceX Dragon (Cargo Dragon)

  • Upmass capacity: 6,000 kg
  • Return capacity: 3,000 kg (only cargo vehicle that returns safely)
  • Launch: Falcon 9
  • Docking: Automatic (IDSS)
  • Reusability: 5+ flights
  • Landing: Ocean splashdown (Pacific/Atlantic)
  • Frequency: 3-4 annually

Dragon's return capability is critical for bringing experiment samples back to Earth.

Northrop Grumman Cygnus

  • Payload: up to 3,500 kg
  • Launch: Antares rocket
  • Capture: Canadarm2 grapples, then berths
  • Station time: 3-6 months
  • Propulsion: Can boost ISS orbit
  • Reentry: Burns up with trash
  • Frequency: 2-3 annually

Each mission named after space pioneers (S.S. Kalpana Chawla, etc.)

Japan HTV / HTV-X

H-II Transfer Vehicle (Kounotori - "Stork")

  • Payload: up to 6,200 kg
  • Launch: H-IIB rocket (HTV-X uses H3)
  • Capture: Canadarm2 grapples
  • Pressurized/unpressurized: Can carry both
  • Large cargo: External equipment (batteries, experiments)
  • Frequency: 1-2 annually

Successfully completed 9 missions through 2020. Upgraded HTV-X began operations 2024.

🚀 Crew Vehicles

Russian Soyuz

Soyuz MS - Most reliable crew vehicle (since 1967)

  • Crew: 3 astronauts
  • Launch: Soyuz rocket
  • Docking time: ~3 hours (fast track)
  • Station time: Docked ~6 months
  • Landing: Parachute landing in Kazakhstan steppes
  • Structure: Orbital module, reentry capsule, service module
  • Reliability: Hundreds of successful flights

US Commercial Crew

SpaceX Crew Dragon

  • Crew: up to 7 (typically 4)
  • Launch: Falcon 9
  • Docking: Automatic
  • Station time: up to 210 days
  • Landing: Ocean splashdown
  • Reusability: 5+ flights
  • Features: Touchscreen controls, abort system

First crewed flight: Demo-2 mission May 2020

Boeing Starliner

  • Crew: up to 7 (typically 4-5)
  • Launch: Atlas V
  • Docking: Automatic
  • Station time: up to 210 days
  • Landing: Parachute land landing (western US)
  • Reusability: 10 flights
  • Features: Traditional controls + touchscreens

Successful uncrewed test 2022, first crewed mission 2024.

🌐 Mission Control Centers

ISS receives 24/7 ground support from multiple control centers:

🏢 Major Control Centers

  • Mission Control Houston (USA): NASA Johnson Space Center - controls entire US segment
  • Mission Control Moscow (Russia): Roscosmos - controls Russian segment
  • Columbus Control (Germany): DLR - European Columbus module
  • JEM Control (Japan): JAXA - Kibo module
  • ATV Control (France): CNES - ATV operations (past)

Controllers work in shifts, monitoring all systems and solving problems. CAPCOM (Capsule Communicator) directly communicates with astronauts.

🚶‍♂️ Spacewalks (EVA)

Extravehicular Activity (EVA) - working outside the station for repairs, installation, and experiments. Essential but dangerous and exhausting.

EVA Preparation

  1. Training (months ahead): Repeated practice in Neutral Buoyancy Lab underwater
  2. Procedure review (weeks ahead): Memorize detailed work procedures
  3. Pre-breathe (day before): Pure oxygen breathing prevents decompression sickness
  4. Suit checkout (day of): Verify all systems
  5. Airlock entry: Gradually reduce pressure
  6. Hatch opening: EVA begins

Spacesuits

EMU (Extravehicular Mobility Unit) - USA

  • Mass: ~127 kg
  • Pressure: 4.3 psi (~30% of Earth atmosphere)
  • Oxygen: ~8 hours supply
  • Temperature control: Water-cooled undergarment
  • Communications: Wireless system
  • Helmet: Gold-coated visor blocks sunlight
  • Emergency: SAFER jetpack attached

Orlan - Russia

  • Mass: ~112 kg
  • Pressure: 5.7 psi
  • Feature: Rear door makes donning easier
  • Oxygen: ~7 hours
  • Reusability: up to 15 times

EVA Work

Average EVA lasts 6-7 hours; longest was 8 hours 56 minutes. Astronauts connected by safety tethers, tool bags also tethered.

🔧 Major EVA Tasks

  • Module Installation: Connecting new modules to truss
  • Component Replacement: Pumps, batteries, antennas
  • Cable Connections: Power and data cable installation
  • Experiment Equipment: Installing and retrieving external experiments
  • Inspection and Photography: Checking damage
  • Cleaning: Removing contaminants

🛠️ Maintenance

ISS requires continuous maintenance. Failed components must be replaced, plus software updates, filter changes, and system checks.

Regular Maintenance

  • Daily: System checks, filter cleaning, disinfection
  • Weekly: Exercise equipment inspection, water quality testing
  • Monthly: Fire extinguisher checks, emergency equipment verification
  • Quarterly: Major system detailed inspections
  • Annual: Comprehensive inspection and upgrades

⚠️ Emergency Response

ISS prepares for multiple emergency scenarios:

🚨 Major Emergencies

  • Fire: Extinguishers, respirators, fire procedures
  • Depressurization (air leak): Locate leak, temporary seal, isolate module
  • Ammonia Leak: Immediately close hatches and isolate, don spacesuits
  • Medical Emergency: Medical kits, remote ground medical team support
  • Computer Failure: Switch to backup systems
  • Debris Collision: Shelter in advance, damage assessment

Evacuation and Escape

Worst case: Crew evacuates in docked Soyuz or Crew Dragon to return to Earth. Each spacecraft serves as a lifeboat, always maintained ready for use.
