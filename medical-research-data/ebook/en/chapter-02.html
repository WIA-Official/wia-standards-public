Chapter 2: FAIR Principles

Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, and Reusable Data for Maximum Research Value

2.1 Understanding FAIR

The FAIR principles, published in 2016 in Scientific Data, provide guidelines for making research data Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, and Reusable. Rather than a standard or specification, FAIR is a set of guiding principles that emphasize machine-actionability—the capacity of computational systems to find, access, and process data with minimal human intervention.

FAIR emerged from the recognition that research data's long-term value depends on more than just availability. Data must be discoverable, obtainable under clear conditions, combinable with other data, and structured for reuse. These principles apply to data itself, to metadata, and to the infrastructure enabling discovery and access.

2.2 Findable

The first step toward data reuse is discovery. Data and metadata must be easy for both humans and machines to find:

F

Findable

  • F1: (Meta)data are assigned a globally unique and persistent identifier (e.g., DOI)
  • F2: Data are described with rich metadata
  • F3: Metadata clearly and explicitly include the identifier of the data they describe
  • F4: (Meta)data are registered or indexed in a searchable resource

Implementing Findability

Requirement Implementation Examples
Persistent Identifiers Assign DOIs or similar PIDs DataCite DOI, ORCID for researchers
Rich Metadata Use community standards Dublin Core, DataCite Metadata Schema
Indexing Register in searchable catalogs re3data, Google Dataset Search
Keywords Use controlled vocabularies MeSH, SNOMED CT, ontologies

Digital Object Identifiers (DOIs)

DOIs provide globally unique, persistent identifiers for datasets. Unlike URLs that may break, DOIs are maintained by registries and continue to resolve even when data moves. DataCite manages DOIs for research data, providing metadata schemas and resolution infrastructure. Each dataset should have its own DOI, separate from any associated publication DOI.

2.3 Accessible

Once data is found, users need to know how to access it. Accessible means data can be retrieved, not necessarily that it must be open to everyone:

A

Accessible

  • A1: (Meta)data are retrievable by their identifier using a standardized communications protocol
  • A1.1: The protocol is open, free, and universally implementable
  • A1.2: The protocol allows for an authentication and authorization procedure, where necessary
  • A2: Metadata are accessible, even when the data are no longer available

Access Considerations

  • Standard Protocols: Use HTTP/HTTPS for data access; FTP and proprietary protocols reduce accessibility
  • Authentication: Implement standard authentication (OAuth, OIDC) when access restrictions are needed
  • Access Levels: Define clear access tiers (open, registered, controlled, closed)
  • Metadata Persistence: Keep metadata available even if data is removed for privacy reasons

⚠️ Accessible ≠ Open

FAIR does not require all data to be open. Sensitive medical data may require controlled access with data use agreements. The key is that access conditions are explicit and machine-readable. "As open as possible, as closed as necessary" is the guiding principle. Metadata describing the data should always be open, even when data access is restricted.

2.4 Interoperable

Data often needs to be integrated with other data or used with diverse applications and workflows:

I

Interoperable

  • I1: (Meta)data use a formal, accessible, shared, and broadly applicable language for knowledge representation
  • I2: (Meta)data use vocabularies that follow FAIR principles
  • I3: (Meta)data include qualified references to other (meta)data

Interoperability Standards

Domain Standards Purpose
Clinical HL7 FHIR, OMOP CDM, CDISC Clinical data exchange and analysis
Genomics GA4GH standards, VCF, FASTQ Genomic data formats and exchange
Imaging DICOM, NIfTI, OME-TIFF Medical and microscopy imaging
Ontologies OWL, SNOMED CT, MeSH, HPO Semantic annotation and linking

The Power of Ontologies

Ontologies provide formal, shared conceptualizations of domains. In medical research, ontologies like SNOMED CT (clinical terms), HPO (human phenotypes), and Gene Ontology enable precise annotation that computers can reason over. Using ontology terms rather than free text dramatically improves interoperability and enables discovery across datasets.

2.5 Reusable

The ultimate goal of FAIR is data reuse. Data should be well-described so it can be replicated or combined with new research:

R

Reusable

  • R1: (Meta)data are richly described with a plurality of accurate and relevant attributes
  • R1.1: (Meta)data are released with a clear and accessible data usage license
  • R1.2: (Meta)data are associated with detailed provenance
  • R1.3: (Meta)data meet domain-relevant community standards

Reusability Requirements

  • Clear Licensing: Use standard licenses (CC0, CC-BY, Data Use Agreements) and make license machine-readable
  • Provenance: Document how data was collected, processed, and transformed
  • Context: Provide sufficient information to understand what data means
  • Community Standards: Follow domain minimum information standards (MIAME, STROBE, etc.)

✓ Economic Impact of FAIR

The European Commission estimated that FAIR data practices could save €10.2 billion annually by reducing time spent searching for data, avoiding duplicate research, and enabling secondary analysis. Return on investment studies show that for every €1 invested in research data infrastructure, €4 in economic benefits are generated.

📌 Key Takeaways

  • FAIR principles (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable) emphasize machine-actionability of research data
  • Findable: Assign persistent identifiers (DOIs), provide rich metadata, register in searchable catalogs
  • Accessible: Use standard protocols (HTTPS), implement clear access conditions—FAIR doesn't mean open
  • Interoperable: Use community standards and ontologies for data and metadata representation
  • Reusable: Provide clear licensing, detailed provenance, and follow community standards
  • FAIR implementation generates significant economic returns—estimated €10.2B annually in Europe alone
