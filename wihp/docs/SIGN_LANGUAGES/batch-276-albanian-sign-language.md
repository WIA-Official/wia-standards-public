### Historical Development
- **Ottoman Era**: No dedicated facilities for deaf people
- **1912-1939**: Fragmented interwar kingdom; no deaf services
- **1963**: First residential school for deaf opened in Tirana
- **1963-1990**: Communist era; oralist methods enforced
- **1990**: Collapse of communism; deaf began congregating
- **Post-1990**: Fully-fledged sign language developed
- **2014**: Legal recognition by Albanian government

### Communist Era (1963-1990)
- Soviet-influenced educational model
- Oralist methods prioritized (lip-reading, speech)
- Signing actively suppressed and coercive measures used
- Teachers trained in Soviet Union and China
- Sign language viewed as impediment to integration
- Extensive fingerspelling and initialized signs used

### Post-Communist Development
- Deaf people invented new signs replacing fingerspelling
- Contact with International Sign and European SLs
- Numerous loan words incorporated
- Community workshops preserve core native lexicon
- ANAD promotes grassroots self-organization

### Linguistic Features
- Visual-spatial language independent of spoken Albanian
- Influence from Albanian gestures in hearing community
- Initialized signs from communist-era fingerspelling
- Regional variations across Albania
- Intergenerational transmission growing post-1991

### Organizations
- Albanian National Association of the Deaf (ANAD)
- Joint projects with international deaf federations
- Self-directed community bonding activities

### Legal Framework (2014)
- Recognized as natural way of communication
- Equal status to oral communication
- Right to request sign language interpreter
- Definition of language and deaf rights included
