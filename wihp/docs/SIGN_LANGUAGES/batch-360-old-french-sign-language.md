### Language Description
Old French Sign Language refers to the sign language varieties used in France before and during the early establishment of formal deaf education, particularly before the standardization that occurred through l'Abbé de l'Épée's work and the subsequent development of the Institut National de Jeunes Sourds de Paris.

This historical language represents the natural sign communication that existed among deaf French people before institutional education, and the early stages of what would become modern French Sign Language (Langue des Signes Française, LSF).

### The Pre-Épée Era
Before formal deaf education:
- Deaf communities existed throughout France
- Family and local sign systems developed
- Paris had deaf communities in certain quarters
- No written documentation of signs
- Church occasionally educated deaf individuals

### L'Abbé Charles-Michel de l'Épée
The "Father of the Deaf":
- Began teaching deaf children 1760
- Learned sign language FROM deaf community
- Systematized and expanded existing signs
- Created "methodical signs" for teaching
- Published first sign language descriptions
- Trained teachers who spread methods

### Key Insight
L'Épée's crucial contribution was recognizing that:
- Deaf people already had a language
- This language could be used for education
- He learned FROM deaf people, not invented signs
- His work documented and expanded existing signing

### What Made It "Old" French Sign Language
Differences from modern LSF:
- More regional variation
- Less standardization
- Different vocabulary in many areas
- Pre-education community language
- Less grammatical description

### Évolution to Modern LSF
| Era | Language Characteristics |
|-----|-------------------------|
| Pre-1760 | Natural, varied, undocumented |
| 1760-1800 | Documentation begins, methodical signs added |
| 1800-1880 | Institutional standardization |
| 1880-1960 | Oralist suppression, underground survival |
| 1960-present | LSF revival and recognition |

### The "Methodical Signs" Controversy
L'Épée created methodical signs:
- Artificial signs for French grammar
- Deaf community preferred natural signs
- Methodical signs largely abandoned later
- Natural sign language prevailed

### Loss and Continuity
What was lost:
- Regional varieties absorbed into standard LSF
- Pre-education community variations
- Rural isolated sign systems
- Documentation gaps

What continued:
- Core vocabulary and grammar into modern LSF
- Community-based signing tradition
- Natural language principles
- Global influence through spread

### Research Sources

### Significance for Sign Language Studies
Old French Sign Language demonstrates:
- Sign languages exist naturally in deaf communities
- Institutional education can document and standardize
- Language can spread globally through education
- Historical sign languages can be partially reconstructed
