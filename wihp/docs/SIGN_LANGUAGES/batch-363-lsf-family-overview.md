### Introduction
The French Sign Language family is one of the largest and most widespread sign language families in the world. Originating from the natural sign languages of French deaf communities and formalized through l'Abbé de l'Épée's educational work in the 18th century, it spread globally through deaf education networks.

The family includes American Sign Language (ASL), making it by far the most widely used sign language family due to ASL's status as the world's most-used sign language.

### Shared Features
Across the family:
- One-handed manual alphabet (most)
- SVO-flexible word order
- Non-manual grammatical markers
- Topic-comment structures
- Classifier systems

### Key Differences
What distinguishes members:
- Local vocabulary development
- Grammatical innovations
- Cultural adaptations
- Contact with other sign languages
- National identity development

### Laurent Clerc's Role
The crucial figure in spreading LSF:
- Deaf educator from Paris
- Brought to America by Thomas Gallaudet (1817)
- First deaf teacher in America
- LSF mixed with local signs → ASL
- Most influential single event in family spread

### Contemporary Spread
The LSF family continues to expand:
- ASL influence in deaf education worldwide
- International deaf communication
- Media and internet spread

### Significance
The LSF family demonstrates:
- Sign languages can spread like spoken languages
- Education drives language spread
- Colonial history affects deaf communities
- Sign languages develop independently after contact
- Families can span continents
