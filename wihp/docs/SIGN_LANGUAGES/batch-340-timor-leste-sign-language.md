### Language Description
Timor-Leste Sign Language is the emerging national sign language of Timor-Leste (East Timor), one of the world's youngest nations, having gained independence in 2002. The language is still in early stages of standardization, with significant variation across the country.

Due to Portuguese colonial history (until 1975) and subsequent Indonesian occupation (1975-1999), the sign language shows influences from both Portuguese Sign Language (LGP) and Indonesian Sign Language (BISINDO/SIBI). Since independence, there has been growing effort to develop a unified national sign language.

The linguistic situation reflects the country's spoken language complexity, where Tetun and Portuguese are official languages, and numerous indigenous languages are spoken.

### Key Characteristics
- **Mixed Heritage**: Portuguese and Indonesian sign influences
- **Regional Variation**: Significant differences between districts
- **Emerging Standard**: Efforts to create unified national language
- **Home Signs**: Many deaf still use family-based signs
- **Young Community**: Organized deaf community developing

### Deaf Community
Timor-Leste's deaf community faces significant challenges:
- Limited infrastructure in young nation
- Post-conflict recovery ongoing
- Rural deaf often isolated
- Urban centers (Dili) have more organized community
- International NGO support important

### Population Distribution
- **Dili (Capital)**: Largest deaf community concentration
- **District Capitals**: Small groups of signers
- **Rural Areas**: Often isolated deaf individuals
- **Institutions**: Schools and organizations mainly in Dili

### Education
- **Deaf School**: Escola Especializada for deaf students in Dili
- **NGO Programs**: International organizations support deaf education
- **Teacher Training**: Limited trained deaf educators
- **Materials**: Developing teaching resources

### Legal Framework
- **Constitution**: Guarantees rights for persons with disabilities
- **CRPD**: Timor-Leste ratified in 2022
- **Sign Language Recognition**: Not yet formally recognized
- **Developing Policy**: Disability rights legislation evolving

### Challenges
1. One of world's poorest nations
2. Limited educational infrastructure
3. Geographic isolation of rural deaf
4. No standardized sign language yet
5. Shortage of trained interpreters
6. Post-conflict trauma in community
7. Competition between Portuguese and Indonesian systems

### Development Priorities
