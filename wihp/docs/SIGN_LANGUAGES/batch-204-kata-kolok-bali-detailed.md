## The Deaf Village

### Desa Kolok
Bengkala is called Desa Kolok in Balinese meaning deaf village
- 1 in 50 babies born deaf (vs 2-3 per 1,000 globally)
- 15x higher deaf rate than world average
- Genetic cause: DFNB3 recessive gene (MYO15A mutation)

### Unique Community
- Every villager knows Kata Kolok
- Children taught sign as 2nd or 3rd language
- Deaf (kolok) fully integrated with hearing (enget)
- No stigma - deaf seen as strong and brave

## Cultural Significance

### Dewa Kolok - Deaf God
- Villagers believe in a deaf god
- Resides in local graveyard
- Protects deaf people
- Reflects spiritual acceptance of deafness

### Kolok Reputation
Deaf villagers known for being:
- Physically stronger
- Resilient
- Loyal
- Honest
- Brave (immune to spirits)

### Special Roles
Deaf often work as:
- Security guards
- Gravediggers (not afraid of spirits)
- Skilled craftspeople
- Dancers

## Linguistic Features

### Unique Grammar
- Uses cardinal directions (east/west vs left/right)
- Signers have internal compass awareness
- No fingerspelling
- No mouthing (common in contact situations)
- Unrelated to spoken Balinese

### Independence
- NOT related to BISINDO (Indonesian Sign Language)
- NOT related to SIBI (Indonesian Signed Language)
- Developed organically over generations
- Based on local gesture systems

### Sign-Spatiality
Connie de Vos research (2012):
- Unique spatial referencing
- Absolute frame of reference
- Different from most sign languages

## Cultural Expressions

### Janger Kolok (Deaf Dance)
- Traditional Balinese dance adapted for deaf
- Created specifically for kolok community
- Performed 3x monthly
- Pride of Bengkala
- Tourist attraction

### Craft Center (KEM)
- Kolok women employed
- Traditional handloom weaving
- Economic independence
- Cultural preservation

## Modern Challenges

### External Contact
- Deaf boarding school in Jimbaran teaches BISINDO
- Younger generation learning Indonesian Sign Language
- Smartphones and social media introducing new signs
- Only 5 deaf villagers currently literate

### Tourism
- Growing tourist attraction
- Deaf tourists visit from worldwide
- Can create romanticized expectations
- Risk of cultural commodification

### Economic Pressure
- Village is poor
- Limited education opportunities
- Difficult for deaf to find jobs outside village
- Daily wage approximately $5 USD

## Preservation Efforts

### Community Leadership
- I Ketut Kanta - community leader, free sign lessons
- Mayor Ida Mardana - advocacy since 2010
- Nyoman Santiya - head of kolok community

### Infrastructure
- Pertamina (oil company) donations
- Government support for community center
- Inclusive school accepting deaf students
- Kolok Villa Bengkala - deaf-run homestay

### Education
- Inclusive elementary school with sign-fluent teachers
- Children from nearby villages can attend
- Integration of deaf and hearing students

## Why Kata Kolok Survives Better

1. **Deaf god belief** - spiritual protection
2. **Tourism** - brings attention and resources
3. **Strong community identity**
4. **Geographic concentration** - not dispersed
5. **Integration** - deaf valued roles
6. **Documentation** - researchers involved
7. **Local champions** - community leaders active

## WIA Talk Approach

### Support Rather Than Document
Kata Kolok has good existing documentation.
WIA Talk should:
1. Support community-led initiatives
2. Create accessible learning materials
3. Help economic development projects
4. Connect with global deaf community
5. Avoid over-tourismfication

### Ethical Tourism
- Respect village customs
- Support deaf-owned businesses
- Learn basic signs before visiting
- Dont treat villagers as exhibits
