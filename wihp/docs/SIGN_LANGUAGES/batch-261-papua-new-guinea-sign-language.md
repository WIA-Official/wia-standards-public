## Historical Significance
- **Fourth official language of Papua New Guinea**
- Official languages: Tok Pisin, English, Hiri Motu, PNGSL
- First sign language with national official status in Oceania
- Most linguistically diverse country (840+ languages)

## Historical Development

### Origins (1990s)
- Auslan (Australian Sign Language) introduced in 1990s
- Australasian Signed English (ASE) adapted for PNG schools
- Mixture with local/home sign began immediately
- Developed in deaf units in Port Moresby, Lae

### Divergence from Auslan
- By 2015: Only ~50% mutually intelligible with Auslan
- Native speakers cannot understand each other
- Local signs and Tok Pisin influence
- Home sign influence at deaf gatherings

### Official Recognition (2015)
- May 2015: Fourth official national language
- Lukautim Pikinini Act advocacy
- Language of instruction in deaf schools
- Mandated for deaf education

## Dictionary Project (2020)
- First PNGSL Picture Dictionary published
- Compiled by Dany Adone, Melanie A. Brück
- Deaf representatives from ALL provinces collaborated
- Acknowledged regional varieties
- Standard for education, teacher training, interpreting
- ISBN: 9783862901425

## Regional Sign Languages
Multiple village sign languages exist:

### Documented Varieties
1. **Sinasina Sign Language** (Chimbu Province)
   - Used by both deaf and hearing
   - Rural community language
2. **Enga Province Sign Language** (Upper Lagaip Valley)
   - Documented by Adam Kendon (1980)
   - First reported PNG sign language
   - Primary sign language from highland region
3. **Western Highlands Sign Languages**
   - Nebilyer/Kaugel region
   - 12+ deaf people studied
   - Nucleated network sign languages

## Linguistic Features

### Influences
- Australasian Signed English (base)
- Tok Pisin (lingua franca)
- Local gestural practices
- Home sign from multiple regions

### Unique Elements
- Signs for PNG cultural concepts
- Local flora and fauna signs
- Social practices terminology
- Temporal markers for scheduling
- 1,350+ documented signs

## Sociodemographic Typology

### Sign Network Concept
- Network of strong and weak sign ties
- Signs diffuse along weak ties
- Explains lexical consistency between unconnected deaf
- Nucleated network sign languages category proposed

### Rural vs Urban
- Urban: PNGSL (standardized)
- Rural: Village sign languages, homesign
- Regional variation due to home sign influence

## Education System
- Deaf units within hearing schools
- Specialized demonstration schools
- Hundreds of students with hearing impairments
- PNGSL as medium of instruction
- Teacher training programs

## Challenges
- Great regional variation
- Many children raised with home sign only
- Home sign influence at gatherings
- Rural isolation
- Access to deaf education
- Limited interpreters

## Cultural Significance
- Reflects PNG extreme linguistic diversity
- National unity tool for deaf community
- Provincial collaboration in standardization
- Bridge across 840+ spoken language groups
