### Introduction
The Indo-Pakistani Sign Language family represents the sign languages of the Indian subcontinent, particularly those that share historical roots in the deaf education systems established during British colonial rule and continued after independence in 1947.

The term "Indo-Pakistani Sign Language" (IPSL) is sometimes used to describe a shared linguistic heritage, though the relationship between modern Indian Sign Language (ISL) and Pakistani Sign Language (PSL) remains debated.

### Post-Partition Development

#### India
- Multiple regional varieties developed
- No single national standard historically
- Recent government standardization efforts
- Indian Sign Language Research and Training Centre (ISLRTC)
- Growing recognition movement

#### Pakistan
- Developed separately from India
- Karachi, Lahore centers
- International (ASL, BSL) influences
- Limited documentation
- Advocacy growing

### Challenges
1. Huge deaf populations with limited services
2. Regional variation complicates standardization
3. Poverty affects deaf education access
4. Rural deaf often isolated
5. Limited interpreter training
6. Multiple varieties within countries
