### Introduction
Chinese Sign Language (CSL), known in Chinese as 中国手语 (Zhōngguó Shǒuyǔ), is the sign language used by the deaf community in mainland China. With China's population of 1.4 billion and an estimated 20+ million deaf individuals, CSL is likely one of the world's most-used sign languages by number of signers.

CSL is distinct from Hong Kong Sign Language and Taiwan Sign Language, which developed under different historical circumstances.

| Feature | Northern (Beijing) | Southern (Shanghai) |
|---------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Prestige | National standard base | Major regional variety |
| Vocabulary | ~60% overlap with South | ~60% overlap with North |
| Education | Promoted nationally | Strong local use |
| Media | TV standards | Local programming |

### Chinese Manual Alphabet
CSL uses a fingerspelling system for:
- Pinyin romanization
- Foreign words
- Names
- Technical terms
- 30+ handshapes

### Deaf Population Context
China's deaf community:
- One of world's largest
- Urban/rural divide significant
- Education access varies by region
- Cochlear implant policies
- Employment challenges

### Challenges
1. Regional variety vs. standardization
2. Signed Chinese vs. natural CSL tension
3. Cochlear implant emphasis
4. Rural deaf access to education
5. Interpreter shortage
6. Recognition as full language
