# Fiji Sign Language / FJSL

## Overview
| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Official Name** | Fiji Sign Language |
| **Abbreviation** | FJSL |
| **Region** | Republic of Fiji |
| **Deaf Population** | ~29,580 (3.3% estimate) |
| **Interpreters** | 38 (deaf and hearing) |
| **Status** | Developing, Constitutional recognition |

## Historical Development

### Origins
- Influenced by British/Australian Sign Language
- British colonial history (until 1970)
- Gospel School for the Deaf founded ~1999
- Viviane Harland - founder (profoundly deaf)

### Modern Development
- 2019: First FJSL Self-Learning Video Dictionary launched
- 2020: COVID-19 brought TV sign language interpretation
- April 2020: First regular TV news sign interpretation
- Research at Chinese University of Hong Kong

## Linguistic Research

### Key Findings
- FJSL has own word order and structure
- NOT equivalent to English word order
- Flexible noun placement
- Adjective and number ordering varies
- Strong English influence historically

## Education System

### Gospel School for the Deaf (Suva)
- Founded ~1999 by Viviane Harland
- Only school for deaf in Fiji
- Three-storey modern building
- Medium of instruction: Fijian BSL/Auslan variant

### Challenges
- Many deaf children not in school
- Island isolation prevents access
- Lack of early language access
- Many variations due to late exposure

## Constitutional Recognition
- Fijian Constitution promotes deaf rights
- Access to information guaranteed
- Sign language use recognized
- Rights of Persons with Disabilities Act enacted

## Government Initiatives
- National Inclusive Employment Policy
- 300% rebate for employers hiring disabled
- Deaf awareness TV programming
- Justice for All - 13-episode TV series

## COVID-19 Impact
- April 2020: Daily sign language news began
- First regular TV interpretation in Fiji
- Life-saving information made accessible:
  - Curfew announcements
  - COVID-19 case updates
  - Lockdown locations
  - Fever clinic information
- Tropical Cyclone Harold: Evacuation info signed

## Pacific Context
- Fiji Sign Language recognized across Pacific islands
- Part of Pacific Island sign language family
- Related to Australian Sign Language
- Distinct from other Pacific sign languages

## Challenges
- Not many sign language interpreters
- Difficulty training teachers/parents
- Island geography creates isolation
- Many families lack deafness knowledge
- Need for specialist training locally

## Cultural Significance
- Bridge between deaf and hearing communities
- National identity for Fijian deaf
- Growing media visibility
- Disability rights awareness increasing
