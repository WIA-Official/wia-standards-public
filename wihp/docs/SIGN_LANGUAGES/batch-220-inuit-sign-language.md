## Origins and History

### Hunting Origins
- Created during nomadic lifestyle
- Silent communication for hunting
- Avoid alerting wildlife
- Communication over distances
- Pre-dates deaf community use

### Historical Timeline
| Period | Development |
|--------|-------------|
| 18th century+ | Hearing Inuit used sign for trade |
| Pre-contact | Gestural communication established |
| 1930 | Earliest written account (Mallet) |
| Nomadic era | Spread across Arctic territories |
| Settlement era | Community-based development |
| 2008 | First use in Nunavut Legislature |

### Linguistic Origins
- Inuit gestural traditions as foundation
- Hunting/trapping manual signals
- Proto-signs not language-specific
- Indigenous development (not colonial)

## Social Integration

### Community Acceptance
> "Here in Nunavut, deaf people are accepted by their families and by their friends, and people can communicate with them. This is very special in all of Canada. We have something to teach Canada from Nunavut."
> — Jamie MacDougall, McGill University

### Key Features
- Little or no social stigma
- No apparent social exclusion
- Deaf fully participate in community
- Hunting, artistry, work roles
- Family transmission regardless of deafness

### Martha's Vineyard Parallel
- 2 hearing signers per deaf person
- Similar integration pattern
- High hereditary deafness
- Community-wide signing

---

## Dialectal Variation

### Within Nunavut
- Regional variations across territory
- Vast distances between communities
- Similar but with dialect differences
- Mutual intelligibility maintained

### Cross-Community Discovery
Deaf Inuit from different communities discovered:
> "They're very similar, although we have different dialects. That's the only difference... A lot of these Inuktitut signings are the same."
> — Geetee Maniapik

### Greenlandic Connection?
- Reports of related sign language in Greenland
- May have ASL and Danish SL loans
- Relationship unclear
- Research needed

## Linguistic Features

### Language Isolate Status
- No established genetic affiliation
- Not related to ASL or LSQ
- Unique phonology, morphology, syntax
- Independent development

### Verb Classification
Three-way distinction:
1. **Plain verbs** - No subject/object reference
2. **Agreement verbs** - Location/movement shifts
3. **Spatial verbs** - Space-based reference

### Non-Manual Features
- Eyebrow raising + eye widening = "yes"
- Brow furrowing + squinting + nose wrinkling = "no"
- May include smile or frown
- From Inuit cultural gestures

### Numeral System
- Counting starts from little finger
- Progresses toward thumb
- From Inuit oral culture tradition

---

## Current Threats

### ASL Encroachment
- No formal IUR education
- Children sent south for schooling
- ASL primary instruction language
- English-ASL interpreted curriculum

### Language Shift Pattern
| Education Level | Language Used |
|-----------------|---------------|
| No schooling | IUR only |
| Some schooling | IUR + some ASL |
| Southern schools | ASL dominant |

### Replacement Signs
- Some IUR signs replaced by ASL counterparts
- Code-mixing occurring
- Younger generation more ASL-influenced
