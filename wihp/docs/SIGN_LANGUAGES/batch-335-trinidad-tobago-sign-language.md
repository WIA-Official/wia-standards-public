# Trinidad and Tobago Sign Language (TTSL)
## Trinbago Sign Language / TSL

### Overview
| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Native Name** | Trinidad and Tobago Sign Language |
| **Other Names** | Trinidadian SL, Trinbago SL, TSL |
| **ISO 639-3** | lst |
| **Country** | Trinidad and Tobago |
| **Origin** | ~1943 (Cascade School for the Deaf) |
| **Language Type** | Deaf community sign language |
| **Status** | Stable but vulnerable |
| **Age** | ~80 years (one of worlds youngest languages) |

### Language Description
- Indigenous sign language of Trinidad and Tobago
- Full natural language with own grammar and syntax
- Distinct from ASL and BSL
- Contains Trinidad-specific vocabulary (DOUBLES, steelpan, etc.)
- Regional variations (North vs South Trinidad)
- Age-based variations (over 50 vs under 50)

### Historical Timeline
- **Pre-1943**: No deaf schools, home signing only
- **1943**: Cascade School for the Deaf founded (Edward Street, Port of Spain)
- **1943-1974**: Oralist education (signing prohibited in class)
- **1946**: First residential deaf school
- **1950s-60s**: TTSL emerges in dormitories
- **1974**: Total Communication replaces oralism, ASL introduced
- **2000s+**: Dictionary project, research initiatives

### Language Creation
- Children created TTSL in dormitories at Cascade School
- Signing prohibited in classrooms, punished
- After class, children communicated freely using signs
- No prior exposure to any sign language
- Language passed through generations of pupils
- Became language of Trinbagonian deaf community

### Linguistic Features

#### Vocabulary Examples
- **DOUBLES**: Sign based on local food
- **TRINIDAD**: Steelpan playing motion + T handshape
- **PORT OF SPAIN**: Based on tongue/town homophony

#### Variation
- **Age**: ARIMA signed differently by 50+ vs under 50
- **Region**: North vs South Trinidad differences
- **Register**: More TTSL in informal, more ASL in formal

### Current Status
| Aspect | Situation |
|--------|-----------|
| **Education** | ASL used, not TTSL |
| **Associations** | TTSL-ASL mixture |
| **Informal use** | Primarily TTSL |
| **Youth** | Learning ASL in schools |
| **Teachers** | Starting to switch to TTSL |

### Endangerment Factors
1. **ASL dominance**: Schools teach ASL since 1974
2. **Mainstreaming**: Deaf integrated into hearing schools
3. **Cochlear implants**: Reducing deaf population
4. **Declining enrollment**: Fewer students in deaf schools
5. **Interpreter shortage**: Few TTSL-skilled interpreters
6. **Youth language shift**: Young people know mainly ASL

### Intergenerational Transmission
- Less than 1% of deaf children have deaf signing parents
- Language acquired in schools, not homes
- Exposure to ASL and Signed English in education
- Institutional support crucial for transmission

### Caribbean Context
One of several Caribbean indigenous sign languages:
- **TTSL** (Trinidad and Tobago) - Vulnerable
- **Konchri Sain** (Jamaica) - Moribund
- **Providence Island SL** (Colombia) - Endangered
- **Kajana SL** (Suriname) - Unknown
- **Cuban SL** - Stable
- **Jamaican SL** - ASL-based

### Educational Challenges
- Deaf are multilingual: TTSL, ASL, Creole, English
- No access to spoken English outside school
- Very few pass CXC English exams
- Need curriculum in indigenous sign language

### Advocacy
