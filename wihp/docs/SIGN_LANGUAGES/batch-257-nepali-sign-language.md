# Nepali Sign Language / NSL

|-----------|---------|

| **ISO 639-3** | nsp |
| **Region** | Nepal |
| **Deaf Population** | 51,373 (Census 2021) |
| **NSL Users** | 1,784 as primary language |
| **Language Family** | South Asian Sign Language family |
| **Status** | Stable, Institutional, Government recognized |

## Historical Development

### Origins (1966)
- 1966: First school for the deaf established
  - Founded by ENT doctor
  - Initially at Bir Hospital, Kathmandu
  - Later moved to Naxal Childrens Home
- Oral education policy (speech therapy focus)
- Deaf students developed signs among themselves

### Early Development (1967-1989)
- 1967: First NSL manual published
- 1980: Kathmandu Association of the Deaf (KAD) founded
  - 13 deaf youths established organization
  - First disability organization run by disabled in Nepal
- 1985: Sign collection work began
  - Patricia Ross (American Peace Corps volunteer)
  - Documented existing signs (not creating new ones)
- 1988: Oral method abandoned
- 1989: First NSL dictionary published

### Modern Era (1990+)
- 1995: National Federation of the Deaf Nepal (NDFN) established
- 1996: Nepal National Federation of the Deaf dictionary
- Government recognition achieved
- Sign language news broadcasting began
- Multiple research projects conducted

## Village Sign Languages
Three distinct village sign languages identified:
1. **Jumla Sign Language** - different from NSL
2. **Ghandruk Sign Language** - different from NSL
3. **Jhankot Sign Language** - different from NSL

## Language Influences
- Indo-Pakistani Sign Language (IPSL)
- British Sign Language (via IPSL)
- American Sign Language
- International Sign
- European sign languages (via visitors/NGOs)
- Signed Nepali (used by hearing teachers)

## Manual Alphabet
- One-handed fingerspelling system
- Based on Devanagari script
- Developed by KAD with UNICEF support
- Some forms derived from American/International alphabets
- Most forms are unique to NSL

## Organizations
- Kathmandu Association of the Deaf (KAD) - 1980
- National Federation of the Deaf Nepal (NDFN) - 1995
  - 53 local deaf associations as members
- Gandaki Association of the Deaf (Pokhara)
- Nepal National Federation of the Deaf and Hard of Hearing

## Education System

### Schools
- 5 government-run deaf schools
- 7 deaf association-run schools
- At least 1 private school

### Challenges
- Overseas funding drying up
- Some schools send home older boarders
- Lack of teacher training
- Teachers use Signed Nepali, not NSL
- Teacher training: 10 days to 6 months only

## Linguistic Features

### Sign Variation
- LONG SIGN and SHORT SIGN variants documented
- Homesign systems among isolated deaf
- Natural sign - limited repertoire shared by deaf/hearing
- Regional variations across Nepal

### Cultural Elements
- Hindu religious influences
- Nepali month and date signs
- Festival-related signs
- Traditional food signs (sel roti, jomari, til ko laddu)

## Cultural Context

### Traditional Views
- Ritual pollution model (bad karma beliefs)
- Deafness as result of bad karma
- Competing with ethno-linguistic identity model

### Modern Developments
- Deaf community as linguistic minority
- Visual communication emphasis
- Cultural identity formation
- Advocacy for rights and equality

## Challenges
- Majority of deaf dont use NSL
- Late exposure to sign language
- Hearing families without signing members
- Teacher-student communication gaps
- Limited interpreter services
- Rural isolation

## Post-2015 Earthquake
- Natural disaster awareness programs
- Federation advocacy for deaf access
- COVID-19: Information access advocacy
- Right to information campaigns
