# Hawaii Sign Language (HSL)
## Hoailona Olelo o Hawaii

### Overview
| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Native Name** | Hoailona Olelo o Hawaii |
| **Other Names** | Hawaii Pidgin Sign Language, Old Hawaii Sign Language |
| **ISO 639-3** | hps |
| **Country** | USA (Hawaii) |
| **Language Family** | Pacific Sign Language Isolate |
| **Status** | Critically endangered / Nearly extinct |
| **Users** | ~30-40 (mostly 80+ years old) |

### Language Description
- Indigenous sign language native to Hawaii
- Unrelated to American Sign Language (ASL)
- 80% vocabulary different from ASL
- SOV word order (typologically distinct)
- Full-fledged natural language, not a pidgin
- Pacific Sign Language Isolate classification

### Historical Timeline
- **1820s**: Village sign use attested (deaf and hearing)
- **1821**: Rev. Hiram Bingham letter to Thomas Gallaudet documents deaf Hawaiians signing
- **1853**: Government census counted 106 deaf people across islands
- **1914**: School for the Deaf founded in Honolulu
- **1939-1940**: Edwin Inn taught HSL at Deaf Club
- **1941**: ASL introduced, began replacing HSL
- **1950s**: ASL became dominant
- **2013**: HSL formally recognized by linguists (March 1)
- **2014**: ISO 639-3 code assigned (hps)

### Discovery
- First new language discovered in USA since 1930s
- University of Hawaii at Manoa research team
- Funded by Hawaii Council for the Humanities
- 19 elderly deaf + 2 CODAs interviewed on 4 islands

### Key Figures
- **Linda Lambrecht**: Champion of HSL, ASL instructor at Kapiolani Community College
- **Barbara Earth**: Research Fellow, Gallaudet University
- **Dr. James Woodward**: Confirmed HSL uniqueness
- **Prof. William OGrady**: UH Manoa Linguistics, grant director
- **Edwin Inn**: Chinese-Hawaiian deaf man, HSL teacher and Deaf Club president (1939-1940)

### Geographic Distribution
Islands with HSL users:
- Oahu (Hawaii School for the Deaf and Blind)
- Maui
- Molokai
- Big Island of Hawaii
- Note: Kauai had its own sign language, made extinct by HSL

### Linguistic Features
- **Word Order**: SOV (Subject-Object-Verb)
- Typologically distinct from:
  - Hawaiian (VSO)
  - Pidgin English (SVO)
  - ASL (SVO)
- Full grammatical system
- Not a pidgin despite common name

### Population Context
- Total Hawaii deaf population: ~15,857 (1.9% of 833,610)
- HSL users: ~30-40, mostly elderly
- Creole Hawaii Sign Language (CHSL): ~40 users
- ASL now dominant among deaf Hawaiians

### Creole Hawaii Sign Language (CHSL)
- HSL-ASL creole variety
- ~40 users
- Bridge generation between HSL and ASL
- Likely to be lost in next 50 years

### Cultural Significance
- Embodies Hawaiian spirit and immigrant influences
- Connected to plantation camp history
- Possible Okinawan or Japanese sign influences
- Important for understanding deaf Hawaiian identity

### Endangerment Factors
- ASL introduction in 1941
- Oralist school philosophy
- Lack of intergenerational transmission
- Most users over 80 years old
- Limited preservation services

### Services for Deaf in Hawaii
- Aloha State Association of the Deaf
- American Sign Language Interpreter Education Program
- (HSL services nearly non-existent)

### Historical Context
- Oralist philosophy at deaf school opposed signing
- Children learned HSL informally among themselves
- ASL seen as progressive by some community members
- Generational disconnect between HSL and ASL users
