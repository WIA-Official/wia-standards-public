# Saint Lucia Sign Language
## Sign Language of Saint Lucia

### Overview
| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Primary Language** | American Sign Language (ASL) |
| **Country** | Saint Lucia |
| **Population** | ~184,100 total |
| **Official Language** | English |
| **CRPD Status** | Signed 2011, Ratified |
| **Status** | ASL-dominant, developing |

### Language Situation
- **American Sign Language (ASL)** is most common sign language
- Part of Windward Islands region
- Several sign language organizations provide services
- No documented indigenous Saint Lucian sign language

### Geographic Context
- Eastern Caribbean island
- Part of Lesser Antilles (Windward Islands)
- North/northeast of Saint Vincent
- South of Martinique
- Northwest of Barbados
- Independence from Britain: February 22, 1979

### Demographics
- Population: ~184,100 (2023)
- ~85% African descent
- English: Official language
- Saint Lucian French Creole (Kweyol): Widely spoken (~80%)
- 90% Roman Catholic

### Sign Language Organizations
- Various organizations provide services to deaf community
- Interpreter services available
- Sign language instruction offered
- Regional Caribbean partnerships

### Healthcare Challenges
- Limited early hearing detection screening
- Lacked EHDI programs or had little data (2020 study)
- Healthcare communication barriers
- Journal of Early Hearing Detection and Intervention noted gaps

### Legal Framework
- **CRPD**: Signed 2011, Ratified
- Optional protocol also ratified
- Part of UN Convention on Rights of Persons with Disabilities
- Member of Caribbean Community (CARICOM)
- Member of OECS (Organisation of Eastern Caribbean States)

### Regional Connections
- **CARPHA**: Caribbean Public Health Agency initiatives
- **Caribbean Sign Language Centre** (Trinidad-based services)
- **Caribbean Deaf News**: Regional information sharing
- Shared resources with other Windward Islands

### Cultural Context
- Strong French Creole heritage (Kweyol)
- British colonial history (official English)
- French influence (1649-1783 periods)
- Jounen Kweyol celebrated annually (October)
- Roman Catholic majority (early French influence)

### Education
- English primary language of instruction
- Various schools and educational programs
- Access to sign language training through regional organizations

### Linguistic Heritage
- English: Official language since British colonial period
- Kweyol: French Creole spoken by majority
- Indigenous Arawak/Carib languages extinct
- ASL introduced through Caribbean deaf education networks

### Challenges
- Limited deaf-specific services locally
- Need for more interpreter training
- Healthcare accessibility gaps
- Early intervention program development needed
