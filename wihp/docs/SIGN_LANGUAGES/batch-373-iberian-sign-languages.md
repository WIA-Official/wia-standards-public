### Introduction
The Iberian Peninsula hosts multiple distinct sign languages, reflecting the linguistic diversity of Spain and Portugal. Spain uniquely recognizes two sign languages (Spanish and Catalan), while Portugal has its own distinct Portuguese Sign Language.

These languages developed independently and represent separate linguistic traditions, though some historical contact has occurred.

### Franco Era Impact (Spain)
Under Franco's dictatorship (1939-1975):
- Oralism enforced
- Sign language suppressed
- Catalan language banned (affecting LSC)
- Deaf education strictly oral
- Underground signing continued
- Post-Franco: slow recovery

### Two-Handed Alphabet (Portugal)
LGP uniquely uses two-handed fingerspelling:
- Similar to British/Swedish type
- Different from Spain (one-handed)
- Historical Swedish deaf educator influence
- Distinguishes LGP from LSE/LSC

### Legal Framework

#### Spain (2007 Law)
- Recognizes both LSE and LSC
- Education rights in sign
- Interpreter access
- Media accessibility
- Research and teaching

#### Portugal (1997 Constitution)
- LGP "protected and valued"
- Fourth official expression alongside Portuguese, Mirandese, and Braille
- Education in LGP
- Strong constitutional protection
