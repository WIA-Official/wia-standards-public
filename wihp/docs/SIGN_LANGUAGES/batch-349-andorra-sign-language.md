### Language Description
Andorra, a small principality of 77,000 people nestled in the Pyrenees between France and Spain, does not have a distinct national sign language. The Deaf community primarily uses Catalan Sign Language (Llengua de Signes Catalana, LSC) due to Catalan being the official language and strong cultural ties to Catalonia.

Some deaf individuals may also use Spanish Sign Language (Lengua de Signos Española, LSE) or French Sign Language (Langue des Signes Française, LSF), reflecting the multilingual nature of Andorran society and the diverse origins of residents.

Andorra's population is highly international, with only about 33% being Andorran nationals, which creates a diverse signing environment.

### Key Characteristics
- **LSC Primary**: Catalan Sign Language dominant
- **Multilingual Context**: Spanish, French, Portuguese spoken
- **International Population**: Diverse signing backgrounds
- **Small Community**: Intimate deaf network
- **Cross-border Access**: Services in Spain and France

### Deaf Community
Andorra's deaf community characteristics:
- Very small, tight-knit group
- Many are immigrants from Spain, France, Portugal
- Access services across borders
- Mixed signing depending on background
- Youth increasingly connected via internet

### Education
- **Andorran System**: Three school systems exist (Andorran, Spanish, French)
- **Special Education**: Limited due to small population
- **Cross-border**: Many deaf students educated in Catalonia or France
- **Language of Instruction**: Varies by system

### Legal Status
- No specific sign language recognition in Andorra
- Disability rights protected constitutionally
- Spain's LSE recognition (2007) and Catalonia's LSC efforts affect access
- Cross-border healthcare agreements support service access

### Healthcare
Andorra provides:
- National healthcare system
- Newborn hearing screening
- Audiological services
- Referral to Spanish specialists when needed

### Challenges
1. Too small for distinct deaf infrastructure
2. Reliance on Spain for specialized services
3. Multilingual environment complicates standardization
4. International population creates language mixing
5. No local interpreter training
6. Young deaf often relocate

### Cultural Context

- Pyrenean cultural events
- Cross-border deaf gatherings
