# Providence Island Sign Language (PISL)
## Provisle / Lengua de Senas de Providencia

### Overview
| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Native Name** | Lengua de Senas de Providencia |
| **Other Names** | Provisle, Providence Sign Language (PSL) |
| **ISO 639-3** | prl |
| **Country** | Colombia |
| **Location** | Providence Island (Providencia), Caribbean Sea |
| **Language Type** | Village sign language (shared sign) |
| **Status** | Critically endangered |

### Demographics
| Period | Deaf Population | Total Population | Ratio |
|--------|-----------------|------------------|-------|
| **1970s-80s** | ~19-20 | 2,500-3,000 | 1:130 |
| **2018** | ~13 | ~4,500-5,000 | 1:350 |
| **Hearing signers** | ~1,500 (1986) | - | - |

### Geographic Context
- Part of Colombian department: San Andres, Providencia and Santa Catalina
- Located off Caribbean coast of Nicaragua
- Area: ~15-17 sq miles (39 km2)
- Isolated island community
- English-based Creole speaking (not Spanish)

### Language Emergence
- **Emerged**: Late 19th or early 20th century (~1900s)
- **Origin**: Sustained genetic deafness in isolated population
- **Development**: Without outside sign language influence
- **Transmission**: Deaf to hearing and deaf community

### Linguistic Features

#### Context Dependency
PISL described as "context-dependent language" (Washabaugh et al. 1978):
- Utterances interpretable only with physical/social context hints
- Grammatical code itself context dependent
- Lexical meanings tied to island context
- Grounded in cultural experience of Providence Islanders

#### Unique Characteristics
- Restricted handshape inventory
- Extensive use of non-manual markers
- High lexical variation
- Use of absolute spatial reference
- Simplistic compared to other sign languages
- No poetry, drama, or word play developed

### Social Context
- **Shared sign language**: Used by both deaf AND hearing
- Most deaf communication mediated by hearing population
- Little direct deaf-to-deaf communication
- Nearly all conversations deaf have are with hearing people
- Deaf regarded as "inferior in mental ability" (historical)
- Marginalized social position for deaf

### Research History
- **1970s-80s**: First documented by William Washabaugh, James Woodward, Susan DeSantis
- **1978**: "Providence Island Sign: A Context-Dependent Language" published
- **1980**: "The Organization and Use of Providence Island Sign Language" (Washabaugh)
- **2019**: Rehana Omardeen doctoral research, ELDP documentation project
- **2021**: "Initial person reference in Providence Island Sign Language" (Glossa journal)

### Endangerment Factors
- Colombian Sign Language (LSC) encroachment
- Falling deaf population (from 20 to 13)
- Increasing island population dilutes ratio
- Younger generation language shift
- Contact with mainland Colombia
- 2020 Hurricane Iota destroyed 98% of infrastructure

### Caribbean Context
One of several Caribbean village sign languages:
- Providence Island SL (Colombia)
- Konchri Sain / Jamaican Country Sign (Jamaica)
- Kajana Sign Language (Suriname)
- Grand Cayman Sign Language (extinct)

### Unique Study Value
- One of first village sign languages ever documented
- Shows emergence of sign without outside influence
- Demonstrates "context-dependent" language type
- Example of shared signing community
- Important for understanding language genesis

### Current Status
- 13 deaf signers remaining (ages from <10 to 80+)
- All born to hearing parents
- Most are only deaf person in family
- No deaf siblings (unlike historical pattern)
- Documentation ongoing but language threatened
