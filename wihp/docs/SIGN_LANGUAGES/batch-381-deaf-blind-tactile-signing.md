### Introduction
Deaf-blind communication methods are adaptations of sign languages and other systems for individuals who cannot see or hear. These tactile systems represent remarkable adaptations of visual-manual languages into tactile-manual communication.

The deaf-blind population is heterogeneous, and communication methods vary based on age of onset, degree of sensory loss, and individual preferences.

### How Tactile Signing Works
The receiver places hands on signer's hands:
| Position | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| Hands-over-hands | Feel sign production |
| Hand-under-hand | Feel finger movements |
| Adapted signs | Modified for tactile clarity |
| Space reduction | Smaller signing space |

### Modifications from Visual SL
| Aspect | Visual SL | Tactile SL |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| Space | Large | Reduced |
| Non-manual | Visual | Tactile indicators |
| Speed | Faster | Slower |
| Contact | Optional | Required |
| Location | In front | On hands |

### Usher Syndrome
Most common cause of deaf-blindness:
- Born deaf with progressive blindness
- Learn visual SL first
- Transition to tactile as vision loss progresses
- Tunnel vision adaptation
- Close-range signing
- Eventually full tactile

### Helen Keller
Most famous deaf-blind person:
- Born 1880, died 1968
- Deaf-blind from age 19 months
- Learned communication from Anne Sullivan
- Multiple methods used
- Global advocate
- Demonstrated deaf-blind potential

### Pro-Tactile Movement

- Emphasizes touch-based culture
- Deaf-blind ways of knowing
- Environmental information sharing
- Back-channel feedback
- Autonomous deaf-blind identity
