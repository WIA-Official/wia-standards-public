# Quebec Sign Language (LSQ)
## Langue des Signes Québécoise

### Overview
| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Native Name** | Langue des Signes Québécoise (LSQ) |
| **ISO 639-3** | fcs |
| **Country** | Canada (Quebec, Ontario, New Brunswick) |
| **Language Family** | French Sign Language family |
| **Users** | ~6,000-10,000 |

### Language Description
- Predominant sign language of francophone Deaf communities in Canada
- Primarily used in Quebec
- Also found in Ontario and New Brunswick
- Member of French Sign Language (LSF) family
- Mix of American Sign Language (ASL) and French Sign Language (LSF)

### Historical Development
- Developed around 1850
- Religious communities combined LSF and ASL
- **Clerics of Saint Viator**: Brought LSF from France
- **Sisters of Providence**: Brought ASL from US training
- Combination created LSQ
- Period of forced oralism followed
- Revival from 1960s onwards

### Legal Recognition
- **2019**: Canada federal legislation recognized ASL, LSQ, and Indigenous sign languages
- **2007**: Ontario - Only province recognizing LSQ (Bill 213)
  - Courts, education, Legislative Assembly
- Quebec: NOT officially recognized
- Charter of French Language complications
- Bills 104 (2002), 14 (2013) rejected

### Linguistic Features
- Same digital alphabet as ASL (one-handed)
- Same number system as ASL
- 116 handshapes documented
- Three-dimensional language
- High level of French borrowing:
  - Mouthing (silent lip movements)
  - Spelling
  - Initialization
- Different grammar from spoken French

### Related Languages
- **French Sign Language (LSF)** - Parent/related
- **American Sign Language (ASL)** - Partial origin
- **Maritime Sign Language (MSL)** - Regional dialect
- Not mutually intelligible with European LSF

### Education
Three types of teaching environments:
1. Regular classes (with/without interpreter)
2. Special classes in regular schools
3. Special schools

- Total communication official model
- Bilingual-bimodal approach (Montreal 2004)
- LSQ taught as subject
- Written French as second language

### Historical Deaf Schools

### Challenges
- Quebec: 90% deaf unemployment or social assistance
- Charter of French Language excludes LSQ
- Bill 96 concerns (French-only policy)
- Limited higher education access
- No specialized post-secondary institution
- Professional licensing restrictions

### Canadian Sign Language Context
Three legitimate sign languages in Canada:
1. **American Sign Language (ASL)** - Most widely used
2. **Quebec Sign Language (LSQ)** - Francophone communities
3. **Maritime Sign Language (MSL)** - Regional dialect

Plus Indigenous sign languages (Inuit Sign Language, etc.)

### Cultural Significance
- Heart of Deaf culture in francophone Canada
- Marker of identity
- Central to traditions, values, norms
- Community and artistic activities
- Advocacy for linguistic recognition
