### Introduction
Thailand is home to several village sign languages that have developed independently in communities with high rates of hereditary deafness. While Ban Khor Sign Language (documented in batch-127) is the most well-known, other village sign systems exist or have existed across Thailand.

This document surveys additional Thai village sign languages beyond Ban Khor.

### Known Village Sign Languages

#### Ban Khor Sign Language (Reference)
- Location: Ban Khor, Isan region
- Status: Well-documented
- Users: ~1,000+ (hearing and deaf)
- Research: Extensive (Nonaka, others)

### Why Thailand Has Village Sign Languages
Factors contributing to development:
1. **Genetic factors**: Hereditary deafness in isolated communities
2. **Rural isolation**: Limited contact with urban deaf community
3. **Community structure**: Close-knit villages
4. **Cultural acceptance**: Deaf inclusion traditions
5. **Endogamy**: Marriage within community

### Characteristics of Thai Village Signs
Common features observed:
- **Iconicity**: High visual representation
- **Community use**: Hearing people sign extensively
- **Local vocabulary**: Signs for local environment, agriculture
- **Buddhist concepts**: Religious vocabulary
- **Agricultural terms**: Farming, fishing, rice cultivation

### Challenges to Village Sign Languages
1. Thai Sign Language expansion
2. Deaf education draws children away
3. Community migration to cities
4. Reduced genetic isolation
5. Mainstream schooling
6. Limited documentation funding

### Conservation Concerns
Village sign languages face:
- **Language shift**: To Thai Sign Language
- **Population change**: Youth migration
- **Education policies**: Mainstreaming deaf children
- **Awareness gap**: Communities may not recognize language value

### Research Priorities
For undocumented Thai village sign languages:
1. Survey to identify communities
2. Community engagement and consent
3. Basic linguistic documentation
4. Video recording
