### Language Situation
- Primary sign language undetermined per PeopleGroups.org
- Part of SIL 24-location Caribbean survey
- British territory with likely ASL/BSL influence
- Very small deaf population (~60)
- Limited documentation available

### Deaf Community Size
- Estimated 60 deaf Montserratians
- One of smallest deaf populations in Caribbean
- Part of Deaf Peoples affinity bloc
- Classified as Unengaged and Unreached

### Geographic Context
- British Overseas Territory in Caribbean
- Part of Leeward Islands, Lesser Antilles
- 16 km long, 11 km wide
- ~40 km of coastline
- Nicknamed The Emerald Isle of the Caribbean
- Irish ancestry significant

### Historical Impact
- **1995 Soufriere Hills Eruption**: Destroyed Plymouth (capital)
- Caused significant population displacement
- Affected language use and community interactions
- Resettlement brought new linguistic interactions

### Official Language
- **English** - Official language
- Montserrat Creole - Widely spoken (~10,000 speakers)
- Creole: African, Irish, Caribbean influences
- Variety of Antiguan and Barbudan Creole
- Similar to Jamaican Creole

### Political Status
- British Overseas Territory
- Only non-fully sovereign full CARICOM member
- Only non-sovereign full OECS member
- British Leeward Islands (1871-1958)
- West Indies Federation (1958-1962)

### Caribbean Sign Language Resources
- **Caribbean Sign Language Centre (Trinidad)**
  - Sign language interpreting services
  - Sign language tuition (Levels 1, 2, 3)
  - Consulting services
  - Taught by deaf instructors

### Regional Sign Language Context
Nine sign languages in Caribbean region:
1. American Sign Language (ASL)
2. British Sign Language (BSL)
3. Danish Sign Language
4. Dutch Sign Language (NGT)
5. French Sign Language
6. Guyanese Sign Language
7. Kajana Sign Language
8. Quebec Sign Language
9. Surinamese Sign Language

### Cultural Significance
- Irish ancestry influence
- Creole cultural cornerstone
- African, Irish, Caribbean blend
- Resilient linguistic identity
- National dish: Goat water (stew)

### Deaf Community Challenges
- Very small population size
- Limited educational access
- Scripture access difficulties
- Limited interpreter availability
- Natural disaster impact

### Language and Culture
- Creole: Language of streets and homes
- English: Government, education, formal settings
- Dual identity: Global yet deeply local
- Music, storytelling in Creole
