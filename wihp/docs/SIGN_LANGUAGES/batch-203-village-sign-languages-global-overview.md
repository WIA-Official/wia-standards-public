# Batch 203: Village Sign Languages - Global Overview

## Definition

Village Sign Languages are:
- Natural sign languages emerging in small communities
- Typically linked to hereditary deafness
- Used by BOTH deaf AND hearing community members
- Developed independently of national sign languages
- Also called: Shared Sign Languages, Rural Sign Languages

## Common Characteristics

### Origin Pattern
1. High hereditary deafness in isolated community
2. Home signs develop in deaf families
3. Signs spread to hearing neighbors/relatives
4. Full language emerges over 2-3 generations
5. Entire community becomes bilingual (speech + sign)

### Social Features
- Deaf fully integrated in society
- Little or no stigma against deafness
- Deaf work alongside hearing
- Marriage between deaf and hearing common
- Deaf participate in all cultural activities

### Linguistic Features
- Independent of national sign languages
- Often grammatically distinct from urban SL
- May share features with local spoken language
- Smaller vocabulary than national SL
- Highly context-dependent

## Threats to Village Sign Languages

### External Contact
1. National sign language introduction through education
2. Deaf children sent to distant schools
3. Economic migration to cities
4. Media exposure to dominant sign languages

### Internal Changes
1. Marriage policies reducing deaf population
2. Genetic counseling discouraging deaf births
3. Cochlear implants
4. Younger generation language shift

### Structural Factors
1. No official recognition
2. No education in local sign
3. No written/recorded materials
4. No trained interpreters
5. National SL seen as prestigious

## The Double Minority Problem

Village sign languages are marginalized:
1. First: by spoken language (like all sign languages)
2. Second: by NATIONAL sign language

This makes them among the most endangered language types globally.

## Case Study Comparisons

### Successful Survival: Kata Kolok (Bali)
- Strong community identity
- Tourism supports awareness
- Some documentation
- Still transmitted to children

### Declining: Adamorobe (Ghana)
- GSL replacing AdaSL
- Marriage ban reducing population
- Active documentation underway
- Uncertain future

### Critical: Ban Khor (Thailand)
- TSL rapidly replacing BKSL
- Only elderly fluent users
- May disappear in one generation
- Urgent documentation needed

### Lost: Martha Vineyard (USA)
- Last speaker died 1952
- Only historical records remain
- Contributed to ASL development
- Model of what can happen

## Key Insight

"By the time linguists are aware of their presence, village sign languages may already be at risk of merging with the national sign language." - Nonaka (2004)

Urgency is critical. These languages can disappear within a single generation once contact with national sign languages begins.
