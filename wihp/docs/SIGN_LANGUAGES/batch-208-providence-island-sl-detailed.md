## Geographic Context

### Island Identity
- Politically: Part of Colombia
- Linguistically: English Creole speakers
- Culturally: Caribbean, distinct from mainland Colombia
- Location: Off Nicaragua's coast

### Isolation Factors
- Geographic isolation (Caribbean island)
- Cultural/linguistic isolation from Spanish Colombia
- Small, interrelated population
- Genetic isolation led to deafness

## Historical Emergence

### Origin
- Emerged late 19th/early 20th century
- Without outside sign language influence
- Genetic deafness from consanguineous marriages
- Interrelated island population

### First Documentation
**William Washabaugh** (1970s-1980s)
- One of first village sign languages ever documented
- Published landmark studies
- Identified unique typological features

## Deaf Population History

| Era | Deaf Population | Total Population |
|-----|-----------------|------------------|
| 1970s | ~50 | 2,500-3,000 |
| 1980s | ~20 | 3,000+ |
| 2019 | 13 | ~5,000 |

### Declining Numbers
- Deaf population falling dramatically
- Ratio of deaf to hearing changed significantly
- Today: few deaf people, scattered across island

## Unique Linguistic Features

### Context Dependency
PISL famously labeled "context-dependent language":
- Utterances ambiguous without context
- Physical/social context essential for interpretation
- Grammatical code itself context-dependent
- Expressions grounded in island cultural experience

### Typological Distinctiveness
- Restricted handshape inventory
- Extensive non-manual markers
- High lexical variation
- Absolute spatial reference (like Kata Kolok)

### Simplicity Debate
Some research noted PISL as "simplistic":
- Limited complexity compared to other SL
- Possible causes:
  - Social attitudes toward deaf
  - Hearing-mediated communication
  - Small deaf community size
  - Limited deaf-deaf interaction

## Social Status of Deaf

### Historical Marginalization
Washabaugh's research found:
- Deaf regarded as "inferior in mental ability"
- Hearing people don't discuss complex ideas with deaf
- Marginalized social position
- No poetry, drama, wordplay developed

### Consequences
- Limited language development
- Hearing people mediate most signing
- Few direct deaf-deaf conversations
- Ground-level, practical uses only

### Contrast with Other Villages
| Community | Deaf Status |
|-----------|-------------|
| Adamorobe | Integrated, valued |
| Kata Kolok | Respected, special roles |
| ABSL | No stigma |
| Providence | Marginalized |

### 2021 Findings
- Recent study on person reference
- Deaf signers use multiple strategies
- Draw heavily on shared context
- Optimize specificity in small community

## Current Threats

### Colombian Sign Language
- Increasing influence in archipelago
- Threatens PISL transmission
- Younger generation exposure

### Cultural/Economic Pressures
- Rapidly shifting island economy
- Linguistic pressure from mainland
- Tourism and development changes

### Community Fragmentation
- Most deaf are only deaf person in family
- No deaf siblings anymore (historically common)
- Limited deaf-deaf interaction opportunities

## Academic Debate

### Is PISL a Full Language?
Some 2020s research suggests:
- May still be home sign systems
- Groups only recently interacting
- Not fully schooled deaf population
- May not have reached "language" status

### Counterarguments
- 120+ year history
- Community-wide use by hearing
- Systematic features documented
- Shared sign language characteristics
