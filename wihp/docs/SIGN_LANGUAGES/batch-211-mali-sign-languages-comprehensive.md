# Batch 211: Mali Sign Languages - Comprehensive Overview

## Overview
Mali has an extraordinary concentration of sign languages:
- 4+ distinct sign languages documented
- Mix of village, family, and urban sign languages
- Dogon region particularly rich in signing communities
- ASL threatens all indigenous sign languages

---

## 1. Douentza Sign Language (Dogon Sign Language)

### Basic Info
| Item | Content |
|------|---------|
| **French** | Langue des signes de Douentza |
| **Location** | Douentza and neighboring communities |
| **Region** | Dogon country, Mopti Region |
| **Type** | Community Sign Language |
| **Status** | ENDANGERED |

### Geography
- Douentza: 145 km east-northeast of Mopti
- On RN16 road linking Mopti and Gao
- Administrative center of Douentza Cercle
- Below Bandiagara Escarpment

### Documentation
- Video corpus collected by Max Planck Institute
- Pre-contact form documented
- Nyst, Magassouba & Sylla (2013) study

### Threat
- No deaf school in area (as of 2013)
- School planned with ASL instruction
- ASL will likely replace local signs

### Relationship to Other Languages
- Unknown similarity to Tebul Sign Language
- May be unrelated to Berbey Sign Language

| **Location** | Uluban village |
| **Region** | Dogon region |
| **Spoken Language** | Tebul Dogon |
| **Type** | Village Sign Language |
| **Status** | ENDANGERED |

### Community Context
- High incidence of congenital deafness
- Village-wide sign language use
- Both deaf and hearing signers

### Tebul Dogon Context
- Tebul Dogon is linguistically divergent
- May constitute own branch of Dogon family
- Sign language developed due to deafness rates

## 3. Berbey Sign Language

### Basic Info
| Item | Content |
|------|---------|
| **Location** | Berbey village |
| **Region** | Hombori region |
| **Spoken Language** | Humburi Senni |
| **Type** | Family Sign Language |
| **Status** | CRITICALLY ENDANGERED |

### Current Users
- Two brothers and their families
- One brother deaf, one hearing
- Four deaf children
- Extremely small user base

### Historical Note
- In father's generation: all signers were deaf
- Now: hearing family members also sign
- Intergenerational transmission occurring

### Isolation
- May be unrelated to other Dogon sign languages
- Family-internal development

## 4. Bamako Sign Language (LaSiMa)

### Basic Info
| Item | Content |
|------|---------|
| **French** | Langue des signes de Bamako |
| **Also Known As** | Malian Sign Language, LaSiMa |
| **Location** | Bamako (capital city) |
| **Type** | Urban Sign Language |
| **Status** | ENDANGERED |

### Unique Origin
- Developed OUTSIDE educational system
- Emerged in urban "tea-circles"
- Deaf men gathered after work
- Spontaneous community formation

### Gender Pattern
- Used predominantly by MEN
- Tea-circle culture is male-dominated
- Women less represented

### Threat
- ASL used in deaf education
- Educated deaf learn ASL, not LaSiMa
- LaSiMa losing ground to ASL

## American Sign Language in Mali

### History
- Introduced by Andrew Foster
- "Father of the Deaf in Africa"
- Established 32 deaf schools across Africa
- ASL became language of instruction

### Current Status
- Most frequently used sign language
- Formal education medium
- Threatens all indigenous sign languages

---

## Dogon Region Sign Language Diversity

### Why So Many Sign Languages?
1. Geographic isolation (Bandiagara Escarpment)
2. Multiple distinct villages
3. High hereditary deafness rates
4. Limited inter-village contact historically
5. Each community developed independently

### Possible Relationships
| Languages | Relationship |
|-----------|--------------|
| Douentza ↔ Tebul | Unknown |
| Douentza ↔ Berbey | Possibly unrelated |
| Tebul ↔ Berbey | Unknown |
| All ↔ Bamako | Likely unrelated |

## Mali Deaf Education Context

### Schools
- Deaf schools use ASL
- No indigenous sign language instruction
- One school planned for Douentza area

### Impact
- Generational language shift
- Young deaf learn ASL
- Elderly use indigenous signs
- Communication gap emerging
