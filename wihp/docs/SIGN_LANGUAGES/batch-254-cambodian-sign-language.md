# Cambodian Sign Language / CBDSL

## Overview
| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Official Name** | Cambodian Sign Language |
| **Alternative Names** | Khmer Sign Language |
| **ISO 639-3** | csx |
| **Region** | Cambodia (Phnom Penh and surrounding areas) |
| **Users** | ~1,500-13,000 (estimates vary widely) |
| **Language Family** | Shared 40% vocabulary with Modern Thai SL |
| **Status** | Developing, post-conflict emergence |

## Historical Context

### Pre-1997 Situation
- No formal sign language existed before 1997
- No deaf community - only deaf population
- No assistive resources or deaf schools
- Khmer word for deaf associated with dumb/mentally disabled

### Khmer Rouge Impact (1975-1979)
- Devastating regime destroyed social structures
- Civil war until late 1990s
- Thousands in refugee camps on Thai border
- CSL beginnings traced to refugee camp interactions

## Development History

### 1997: Foundation
- Finnish Association of the Deaf field worker began work
- Gathered deaf people in Phnom Penh
- Encouraged natural communication development
- Maryknoll Deaf Development Programme (DDP) established

### Sign Language Creation Process
- Deaf people brought together to decide signs collectively
- Leader showed objects, asked What is the sign for this?
- Each person showed different signs, one was chosen
- Field workers visited provinces to include regional signs

### Parallel Development
- DDP: Natural sign language development approach
- Krousar Thmey (1997): Formal education using ASL base
- Two different sign systems emerged
- 2013: Committee formed to develop common CSL

## Organizations

### Maryknoll Deaf Development Programme (DDP)
- Founded with Finnish Association support
- Informal education for deaf adults
- Only organization using authentic CBDSL
- 2-year basic education program (16+ age)
- Six projects: CSL, Education, Job Training, Community Development, Social Service, Interpreting

### Krousar Thmey
- Formal deaf education (children)
- Used modified ASL following Khmer word order
- Now collaborating with DDP on unified CSL
- Completed curriculum translation for grades 1-3

## Key Developments

### 2013 Agreement
- National Technical Institute for the Deaf (USA) funding
- Committee with DDP and Krousar Thmey members
- Goal: Common Cambodian Sign Language
- Hun Sen called for one sign language for Cambodia

### Ongoing Work
- Translating national curriculum into CSL
- Creating new signs by committee
- Documenting signs from local communities
- Training specialized teachers and interpreters

## Linguistic Features

### Vocabulary Relationships
- 40% basic vocabulary shared with Modern Thai SL
- Some ASL influence from Krousar Thmey schools
- Limited intelligibility with ASL (iconic elements only)
- No noted connection to neighboring countries languages

### Unique Elements
- Fingerspelling based on Khmer alphabet
- Visual representations of Khmer sounds
- Local signs for Cambodian animals (rats, water buffalo, etc.)
- Market gestures (deaf discount system)

## Challenges

### Community Issues
- Many deaf people isolated in villages
- Many arrive at DDP without any language (age 16-30s)
- Dont know names, origins, or family locations
- Cannot read/write Khmer

### Social Barriers
- Deafness associated with mental disability
- Families dont understand deafness
- Village isolation prevents language acquisition
- Rural to urban migration creates repertoire gaps

## Education

### DDP Program
- 2-year basic education (literacy, math)
- Sign language instruction from zero knowledge
- Job training and placement
- 32+ students supported annually
- Students wear uniforms (self-chosen for connectedness)

### Interpreting Challenge
- CSL interpreters needed for deaf workers
- Training programs being developed

## Cultural Significance
- Post-Khmer Rouge deaf culture emergence
- First deaf community formation in Cambodian history
- Symbol of recovery and national development
- Ongoing documentation as research subject
