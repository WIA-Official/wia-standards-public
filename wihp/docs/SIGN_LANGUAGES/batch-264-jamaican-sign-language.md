## Sign Languages in Jamaica
1. **Jamaican Sign Language (JSL)** - Primary deaf community language
2. **Jamaican Country Sign Language (Konchri Sain)** - Indigenous village SL
3. **Signed English** - Used in some educational settings

## Historical Development

### Jamaica Association for the Deaf (JAD)
- **Founded: 1938** - Oldest deaf organization in Caribbean
- First Adult Deaf Club formed
- First Unit Class at Kingston Senior School

### Timeline

### Unique Characteristics
- Jamaican cultural signs

- Place names
- Cultural references unique to Jamaica
- "WGH" sign for sentence punctuation

## Jamaican Country Sign Language (Konchri Sain)

### Overview
- Indigenous village sign language
- Located in St. Elizabeth parish (rural south-west)
- Originated in Top Hill
- ~40 speakers remaining (highly endangered)

### History
- Used by both deaf and hearing Jamaicans historically
- 1975: American Mennonite missionaries introduced Signed English/ASL
- School officials discouraged Konchri Sain use
- By 1985: Primarily used by elderly monolingual deaf
- Now endangered - younger generation uses JSL

### Attitudes
- Country Sign users see their language as beautiful
- Positive attitudes toward unique language and community
- Now coexists with higher prestige signed systems

## Education
- Schools originally staffed by British educators
- North American missionaries introduced ASL/Signed English
- Mixing of JSL, ASL, and Signed English in classrooms
- Some schools moving toward JSL use
- Deaf schools are primary sites of sign language acquisition

## ASL Relationship
- JSL largely considered ASL dialect
- 75% overlap reported by JAD
- Historical educational connections with United States
- ASL intelligibility varies among Jamaican deaf
- Some embrace ASL for upward social mobility
- Others prefer distinctly Jamaican signing

## Accessibility Efforts
- JSL interpretation for public events
- News broadcast interpretation
- Government communications accessibility
- JAD advocacy for deaf rights and inclusion

## Cultural Integration
- JSL incorporates Jamaican cultural elements
- Food signs (local dishes)
- Music and entertainment references
- Patois influence on signing style
- Community events and gatherings
