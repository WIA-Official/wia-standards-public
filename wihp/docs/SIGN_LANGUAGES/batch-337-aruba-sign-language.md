### Language Description
Aruba Sign Language is the sign language used by the Deaf community on the island of Aruba, a constituent country of the Kingdom of the Netherlands located in the southern Caribbean Sea. Like its neighbor Curaçao, Aruba's sign language has developed within a multilingual context where Papiamento, Dutch, Spanish, and English are commonly used.

The language shares some features with sign languages of neighboring islands (Curaçao and Bonaire) but has developed its own distinct characteristics. Due to Aruba's smaller deaf population, the language community is more intimate, and there is significant variation between families and generations.

ASL has had substantial influence on Aruban signing due to educational connections and media exposure from the United States. Dutch Sign Language (NGT) has also influenced the language through the Netherlands connection.

### Key Characteristics
- **Small Community**: Intimate signing community with close relationships
- **Family Signs**: Many signs originate from home sign systems
- **Tourism Influence**: Exposure to tourists brings international sign contact
- **Papiamento Influence**: Mouth movements reflect local spoken language
- **Code-mixing**: Frequent mixing with ASL signs

### Deaf Community
Aruba's total population of approximately 107,000 includes a small but active Deaf community. Due to the island's size, most deaf individuals know each other personally. The community maintains connections through:
- Social gatherings and events
- Church services with interpretation
- Sports activities
- Social media groups

### Education
- **School**: Centro di Enseñansa Especial provides services
- **Approach**: Mix of local signs, ASL, and spoken language support
- **Challenges**: Limited specialized teachers
- **Tertiary**: Deaf students typically must leave island for higher education

### Cultural Integration
The Deaf community participates in Aruba's vibrant cultural life:
- **Carnival**: Deaf participation in annual celebrations
- **Sports**: Deaf sports programs
- **Arts**: Deaf artists contribute to island culture

### Challenges
1. Very small language community
2. Risk of language shift to ASL
3. Limited documentation
4. No formal interpreter training
5. Educated deaf often emigrate

### Relationship with Other ABC Island Sign Languages
The ABC islands (Aruba, Bonaire, Curaçao) share historical and cultural connections, and their sign languages show similarities while maintaining distinct identities. Research is needed to determine the degree of mutual intelligibility.
