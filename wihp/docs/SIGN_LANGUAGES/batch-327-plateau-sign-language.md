# Plateau Sign Language
## Old Plateau Sign Language

### Overview
| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Region** | Northwest Plateau (BC, Montana, Washington) |
| **Country** | Canada (British Columbia), USA (Montana, Washington) |
| **Language Family** | Possibly related to Hand Talk, possibly distinct |

### Language Description
- Indigenous sign language of Northwest Plateau region
- Poorly attested due to limited documentation
- Mother tongue to deaf Plateau peoples historically
- Lingua franca for inter-tribal communication
- Distinct from Plains Indian Sign Language
- Eventually replaced by Plains Sign Talk and Chinook Jargon

### Geographic Distribution
Historical range (Columbian Plateau):
- **Central British Columbia**
- **Southeastern British Columbia**
- **Montana** (Bitterroot Valley area)
- **Washington State**
- **Idaho** (parts)

### User Nations
- **Salish (Séliš)** peoples
- **Sahaptian** peoples
- **Ktunaxa (Kootenay)** peoples
- Other Plateau nations

### Historical Documentation
- **Pre-1879**: Canadian government reports describe use among Ktunaxa and Salish
- **Lewis & Clark Expedition** (1805): Encountered Salish using signs in Bitterroot Valley
- Limited formal linguistic documentation
- Replaced before systematic study

### Language Replacement
1. **Plains Sign Talk**: Introduced by Crow Tribe, replaced Plateau SL among eastern nations
2. **Chinook Jargon**: Spoken lingua franca replaced sign language on Pacific coast
3. **American Sign Language**: Modern replacement for deaf individuals

### Relationship to Plains Sign Language
- Debate on whether related to Hand Talk or distinct
- McKay-Cody: May or may not be related to Hand Talk
- Davis (2015): Describes as counterpart to Plains SL
- Separate evolutionary path from Plains lingua franca

### Current Status
- **Extinct** or nearly extinct
- Only partially known by 1-2 hearing elders
- **Francis Auld** (Ksanka/Ktunaxa): One of last known partial speakers in Montana
- No comprehensive documentation exists
- Cannot be effectively revived due to lack of records

### Research Context
Three distinct Indigenous sign languages in Canada:
1. **Plains Sign Language**: Still known by Dakota, Cree, Blackfoot
2. **Plateau Sign Language**: Nearly extinct
3. **Inuit Sign Language**: Used in Canadian Arctic

### Lewis & Clark Encounter (September 10, 1805)
Meriwether Lewis journal entry on meeting Salish:
> our guide could not speake the language of these people but soon engaged them in conversation by signs or jesticulation

Note: Salish familiar with sign language due to hunting forays into buffalo country east of Continental Divide

### Chinook Jargon Replacement
- Spoken trade language of Pacific Northwest
- Also called Chinuk Wawa
- Eventually replaced Plateau Sign Language for inter-tribal communication
- Coastal Indians did not understand Plains/Plateau signs

### Documentation Gap
- Darin Flynn (University of Calgary) notes failure to document Plateau SL
- Means effective revival impossible
- Lost linguistic heritage
- Similar pattern to many unrecorded languages
