# Haitian Sign Language / Langue des Signes Haïtienne (LSH)

## Overview
| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Official Name** | Haitian Sign Language |
| **Native Name** | Langue des Signes Haïtienne (LSH) |
| **Alternative** | HSL (English abbreviation) |
| **Region** | Haiti |
| **Deaf Population** | ~72,000 (2003 WHO estimate) |
| **Status** | Deaf-community sign language |
| **Influence** | Similar to ASL but distinct |

## First Documentation
- **2016**: First formal description published
- Authors: Julie Hochgesang and Kate McAuliff
- Published: Sign Language Studies Vol. 16, No. 2
- Collaboration between Deaf Haitians and Gallaudet University linguists

## Historical Context
- Limited information on LSH emergence
- Believed influenced by or derived from ASL
- Foreign aid programs used ASL in Haiti
- Deaf Haitians assert LSH is distinct language
- Relationship to ASL topic of keen discussion in community

## Education

### Deaf Schools
- 5 government-recognized schools
- Additional missionary-run schools
- Schools are primary sites of LSH transmission
- Children socialize and use LSH regardless of instruction language

### Language Acquisition
- Some children have Deaf parents/siblings (early acquisition)
- Others learn from peers at deaf schools
- LSH used alongside French, Haitian Creole, English
- Fluency in majority languages varies widely

## Cultural Challenges

### Stigma
- Deafness seen as punishment for sin
- Viewed as curse from higher power
- Deaf people considered below disabled community
- Many fear using sign language around hearing people

### Discovery of Deafness
- No early hearing detection screening
- Families discover deafness from infancy to age 6-8
- Some seek hospital medications
- Others attend voodoo ceremonies
- No government-sponsored family support programs

## Legal and Rights Issues

### Barriers
- Courts and police lack resources for deaf communication
- No interpreting services or language specialists
- 2016: Three Deaf Haitian women murdered (lack of justice)
- 2018: Act on Inclusion of Persons with Disabilities provision
- Provisions not consistently observed

### Voting Rights
- Deaf Haitians have right to vote
- Information transmitted via radio (inaccessible)
- Political candidate info unavailable in accessible format

## Healthcare
- Limited audiological services
- No medical records for many
- More access to hearing technology than sign language resources
- Comcare International: solar-powered hearing aid clinic
- NGOs focus on medical needs over language support

## Linguistic Features
- Distinct from ASL despite similarities
- Used in Port-au-Prince and rural areas
- Becoming more conventionalized as community concentrates
- Community asserts its distinctiveness from ASL

## Community Context
- Haitian Creole and French commonly used
- English presence (minimal)
- Spanish, Portuguese, Taino influences
- Post-earthquake challenges to infrastructure

## Organizations
