# Levantine Arabic Sign Language / LASL

## Overview
| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Official Name** | Levantine Arabic Sign Language |
| **Alternative Names** | Syro-Palestinian Sign Language |
| **Region** | Jordan, Lebanon, Palestine, Syria (Bilad al-Sham) |
| **Users** | 15,000-20,000+ in Jordan; 12,000+ in Lebanon |
| **Language Family** | Arab sign-language family |
| **Status** | High mutual intelligibility across Levant |

## Language Variants

### National Names
- **Jordanian SL**: Lughat il-Ishara il-Urduniyyah (LIU)
- **Lebanese SL**: Lughat al-Isharat al-Lubnaniyyah (LIL)
- **Palestinian SL**: Lughat al-Isharat al-Filistiniyyah (LIF)
- **Syrian SL**: Lughat il-Ishara il-Suriyyah (LIS)

## Linguistic Features

### Unity and Diversity
- Significant vocabulary differences between four states
- NOT greater than regional differences WITHIN states
- Grammar quite uniform across region
- High mutual intelligibility - indicates single language with dialects
- 60-61% lexical similarity between Jordanian and Syrian varieties
- Amman and Salt varieties: 74% similarity

### Historical Development
- Emerged organically in isolated deaf households
- Expanded through interpersonal networks
- NOT derived from spoken Levantine Arabic
- Some gestural influences from local Arabic communication
- Signing accepted in Arab world as early as 16th century (Scalenghe 2014)

## Organizations and Education

### Jordan
- Holy Land Institute of the Deaf (HLID) in Salt
- Considered model school for deaf in Arab world
- Leading nation in Middle East for deaf education
- 50% deaf children receive primary education
- Only 0.2% finish secondary education

### Lebanon
- Learning Center for the Deaf (LCD)
- Educational programs and sign language classes
- Challenges in widespread recognition and standardization
- ~12,000 deaf population

## Challenges
- No official recognition as minority languages in Arab countries
- Limited interpreter services
- Educational access limited
- Lack of research and documentation
- Unified Arabic Sign Language threatens local varieties

## Cultural Significance
- Emerged from 16th century or earlier
- Unique grammatical structures distinct from spoken Arabic
- Emphasizes simultaneous morphology
- Regional identity despite political borders
