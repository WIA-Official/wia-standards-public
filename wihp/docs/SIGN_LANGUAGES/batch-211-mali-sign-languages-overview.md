## Sign Languages in Mali

Mali has at least **4 distinct sign language types**:

| Sign Language | Type | Location | Status |
|---------------|------|----------|--------|
| ASL | Imported | Nationwide (schools) | Dominant in education |
| Bamako SL (LaSiMa) | Urban | Bamako | Endangered |
| Douentza SL | Village | Dogon region | Documented |
| Tebul SL | Village | Uluban village | Endangered |

## 1. American Sign Language (ASL) in Mali

### Introduction
- Brought by **Andrew Foster** (American missionary)
- Known as "Father of Deaf Africa"
- Established 32 deaf schools across Africa
- Mali school: École Enfants Déficients Auditifs (EDA) Bamako

### Current Status
- Used in formal deaf education
- Threatening local sign languages
- Most recognized sign language
- Not indigenous to Mali

## 2. Bamako Sign Language (LaSiMa)

### Overview
| Item | Content |
|------|---------|
| **French Name** | Langue des Signes de Bamako |
| **Also Known As** | Malian Sign Language, LaSiMa |
| **Origin** | Urban tea-circles of Bamako |
| **Users** | Predominantly men |
| **Status** | ENDANGERED |

### Origin Story
- Developed OUTSIDE the educational system
- Urban deaf men gathered after work
- Tea-circles became signing spaces
- Spontaneous community development

### Unique Characteristics
- Male-dominated user base
- Urban origin (vs village sign languages)
- Independent of ASL/school influence
- Community-generated vocabulary

### Threats
- ASL is language of instruction in schools
- Younger deaf children learn ASL
- LaSiMa declining among youth
- No formal institutional support

### Documentation
- Pinsonneault (1999) - Lexicon of signs in Mali
- Nyst, Magassouba & Sylla (2008, 2011, 2012) - Corpus projects
- CLaSiMa - Corpus de Langue des Signes Malienne

## 3. Douentza Sign Language (Dogon Sign Language)

### Overview
| Item | Content |
|------|---------|
| **Location** | Douentza and neighboring communities |
| **Region** | Dogon country, Mopti Region |
| **Distance** | 145 km east-northeast of Mopti |
| **Status** | VULNERABLE |

### Geographic Context
- Bandiagara Escarpment area
- UNESCO World Heritage region
- Traditional Dogon culture area
- Remote rural location

### Documentation
- MPI (Max Planck Institute) video corpus
- Nyst, Sylla & Magassouba (2012) study
- Pre-contact form documented

### Relationship to Other Languages
- May or may not be related to Tebul SL
- Possibly unrelated to Berbey SL (also Dogon region)
- Independent development uncertain

### Future Threats
- Deaf school planned for area
- ASL will be language of instruction
- May dramatically affect Douentza SL
- Documentation urgent before school opens

## 4. Tebul Sign Language

### Overview
| Item | Content |
|------|---------|
| **French Name** | Langue des signes de Tebul |
| **Location** | Uluban village |
| **Region** | Dogon region |
| **Spoken Language** | Tebul Dogon |
| **Type** | Village Sign Language |
| **Status** | ENDANGERED |

### Characteristics
- High incidence of congenital deafness
- Village-wide sign language
- Used by deaf and hearing
- Isolated development

### Tebul Language Context
- Tebul Ure (spoken) is Dogon language
- Also called "Oru Yille" (error - means "two words")
- First reported by Roger Blench

## Dogon Region Significance

### UNESCO Heritage
- Bandiagara Escarpment: World Heritage Site
- Ancient cliff dwellings
- Traditional culture preserved
- Geographic isolation

### Why Multiple Sign Languages?
- Remote villages separated by escarpment
- Different hereditary deafness sources
- Minimal inter-village contact historically
- Each village may have developed independently
