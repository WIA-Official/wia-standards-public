### Introduction
The Japanese Sign Language family is centered on Japanese Sign Language (JSL), known in Japanese as 日本手話 (Nihon Shuwa). JSL developed independently in Japan and, through the Japanese colonial period (1895-1945), influenced sign languages in Taiwan and Korea.

This family represents East Asian sign language development distinct from both the BSL and LSF families of Western origin.

### Colonial Period Impact
Japanese deaf education in colonies:
- Japanese deaf educators sent to Taiwan, Korea
- JSL used as instruction medium
- Local deaf learned Japanese-based signs
- Post-independence: languages diverged
- Legacy vocabulary remains

### Language Relationships

### Post-Colonial Development

#### Taiwan
- TSL developed independently after 1945
- Chinese influence increased
- Maintained JSL core vocabulary
- Added Taiwanese cultural signs
- Now distinct language

#### Korea
- KSL diverged significantly
- Korean cultural adaptation
- Hangul-based fingerspelling developed
- North Korea developed separate language
- Strong deaf identity in South Korea

| Aspect | JSL | Signed Japanese |

| Structure | Natural sign language | Manual Japanese |
| Use | Deaf community | Education, media |
| Recognition | Advocates promote | Government prefers |
