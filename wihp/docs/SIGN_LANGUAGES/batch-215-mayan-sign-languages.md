# Batch 215: Mayan Sign Languages (Mexico & Guatemala)

## Overview

The Maya world has developed **multiple indigenous sign languages** independent of national sign languages:

| Language | Region | Country |
|----------|--------|---------|
| Yucatec Maya SL (YMSL) | Yucatán Peninsula | Mexico |
| Highland Maya SL | Western Highlands | Guatemala |
| Chatino Emerging SL | Oaxaca | Mexico |
| Tzotzil Maya Emerging SL | Chiapas | Mexico |

---

## 1. Yucatec Maya Sign Language (YMSL)

### Basic Information
| Item | Content |
|------|---------|
| **Spanish Name** | Lengua de Señas Maya Yucateca (LSMY) |
| **Also Known As** | Nohya Sign Language |
| **ISO 639-3** | msd |
| **Location** | Yucatán Peninsula, Mexico |
| **Type** | Village Sign Language |
| **Status** | VULNERABLE |

### Discovery History
| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1976 | SIL researchers first encounter deaf signers |
| 1977 | Malcolm Shuman collects 150-sign glossary |
| 1978 | John Du Bois presents at AAA meeting |
| 1980 | Shuman publishes preliminary account |
| 1987 | Johnson & Erting (Gallaudet) visit |
| 1991 | Johnson publishes landmark paper |

### Original Village Statistics
When first studied (1976):
- Population: ~450 villagers
- Deaf inhabitants: 13 (3% of population)
- All deaf people signed
- All hearing villagers could sign

### Key Features

#### Full Integration
- Deaf villagers "almost fully integrated"
- Access to economic benefits
- Deafness "relatively depoliticized"
- Full lives as Maya agriculturalists

#### Why Integration Works
- ALL hearing villagers competent in sign
- Three levels of signing skill observed
- Communication barriers minimal
- Shared cultural identity

### Multiple Villages
Recent research identifies YMSL variants in different villages:
- **Chicán Village** - one YMSL variant
- **Nohkop Village** - different YMSL variant
- Multiple other communities

### Linguistic Features

#### Language Isolate
- NOT related to Mexican Sign Language (LSM)
- NOT related to spoken Yucatec Maya
- NOT related to Spanish
- May have similarities with Guatemala sign languages

#### Documentation
- Extensive written vocabulary
- Video-taped vocabulary
- Swadesh word list on film
- Contains natural signed language features

### Sign Names in YMSL
Research on sign name construction shows:
- Based on appearance and behavior (common)
- Extensive use of generic names (unusual)
- Names by kinship association (unusual)
- Nonmanual sign names (unusual)
- Follows Yucatec Maya cultural naming rules

### Social Changes

#### Marriage Patterns (1987 vs later)
- 1987: No deaf had married
- Later: All eligible deaf men married
- Only 1 of 5 eligible deaf women married
- One deaf-deaf marriage occurred

#### Modern Changes
- Economic patterns shifting
- Young people seek city employment
- Increased contact with Mexican Deaf community
- Social media and technology access
- Deaf and indigenous identities in flux

| Researcher | Institution | Contribution |
|------------|-------------|--------------|

| Carol Erting | Gallaudet University | 1987 field visits |
| Malcolm K. Shuman | - | First glossary (1977) |

## 2. Highland Maya Sign Language (Guatemala)

### K'iche' Names
- **Meemul Ch'aab'al** = "mute language"
- **Meemul Tziij** = "mute language"

### Distribution
- Western Highlands of Guatemala
- Used by deaf rural Maya
- Some traders use it
- Traditional storytellers use it

### Researcher
**Erich Fox Tree** (2004)
- Society for Linguistic Anthropology
- Reports "sign language complex"
- Region-wide use

### Concerning Aspect
Unlike Yucatec Maya SL:
- May be used for social segregation
- Reports of oppression via signing
- Deaf may be marginalized
- Complex social dynamics

### Connection to Yucatec Maya SL
- Possible similarities exist
- Historical connections debated
- Fox Tree's claim of pre-Hispanic pan-Mayan SL
- No solid proof from documents or memories

## Relationship to Mexican Sign Language (LSM)

### LSM Overview
- National sign language of Mexico
- ~300,000+ users estimated
- Historical link to Old French Sign Language
- Similar to ASL in some ways

### Independence of YMSL
All Mayan sign languages are:
- Completely unrelated to LSM
- Developed independently
- Language isolates
- Indigenous to their communities

## Translanguaging in Maya Communities

### Multimodal Communication
Maya communities use spectrum of resources:
- Signing (various fluency levels)
- Yucatec Maya speech
- Spanish speech
- Gesture
- Drawing on ground
- Pointing to objects
- Environmental manipulation

### Why Maya Context is Special
1. Rich conventional co-speech gestures in Yucatec Maya
2. Positive attitudes toward deafness and sign
3. Critical shared cultural knowledge
4. Small face-to-face communities
5. "Local experiential knowledge" (Kisch 2008)

### Gestural Foundation
Yucatec Maya speakers have rich gesture system that:
- Scaffolds sign language emergence
- Provides shared manual communication base
- Facilitates deaf-hearing communication
- May explain similarities between village varieties
