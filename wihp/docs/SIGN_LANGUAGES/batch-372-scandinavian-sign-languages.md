### Introduction
The Scandinavian (Nordic) sign languages represent a group of historically connected but now distinct sign languages used across Northern Europe. While sharing some historical roots through educational contacts and geographic proximity, each has developed as an independent national language.

The Nordic countries have been leaders in sign language recognition and deaf rights, making this region important for global sign language policy.

### Historical Connections
Early Nordic deaf education had contact:
- Danish teachers in early Swedish school
- Some vocabulary shared historically
- Educational exchanges
- Geographic proximity enabled contact
- Now largely independent

### Finland-Swedish SL: Endangered
Unique endangered language:
- Used by Swedish-speaking deaf in Finland
- ~90-150 signers remaining
- Distinct from both FinSL and Swedish SL
- Constitutional recognition (2015)
- Active preservation efforts

### Linguistic Features
Common Nordic SL features:

### Challenges
1. Small community sizes (especially Iceland)
2. Finland-Swedish SL critically endangered
3. Mainstreaming reducing deaf schools
4. Rural deaf isolation
5. Immigrant deaf integration
