# Keresan Sign Language (KPISL)
## Keresan Pueblo Indian Sign Language / Keresign

### Overview
| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Common Names** | KPISL, Keresign |
| **Country** | USA (New Mexico) |
| **Community** | Keresan Pueblo (likely Zia Pueblo) |
| **Language Family** | Village sign language (unrelated to PISL) |
| **Status** | Endangered, slipping into extinction |
| **Deaf Population** | 15 out of 650 tribal members |

### Language Description
- Village sign language of Keresan-speaking pueblo
- Developed locally within community
- Unrelated to Plains Indian Sign Language (PISL)
- Used by both deaf and hearing tribal members
- Functions as:
  - Alternative to spoken language for hearing members
  - Primary/first language for deaf members

### Geographic Location
- Central New Mexico
- One small Keresan-speaking pueblo
- Likely **Zia Pueblo** based on population data
- Part of New Mexico Pueblo cultural area

### Deaf Population Statistics
- 15 out of 650 tribal members have severe to profound hearing loss
- Rate: ~2.3% (twice the national average)
- High incidence of congenital deafness
- Hereditary and otitis media factors

### Health Context
American Indians have elevated rates of hearing issues:
- Almost 3x hospitalization rate for ear conditions
- 20-70% have middle ear problems (otitis media)
- High rates continue among Southwestern tribes
- Leading to hearing loss, especially in children

### Historical Development
- Developed by family members to communicate with deaf relatives
- Transmitted from eldest siblings to younger family members
- Extended to non-family pueblo members
- Distinct origin from PISL trade language

### Language Functions
1. **For hearing members**: Alternative to spoken language
2. **For deaf members**: Primary/first language
3. **Community-wide**: Communication across pueblo
4. **Cultural transmission**: Feast days, holidays, ceremonies

### Cultural Signs Documented
Meaning-symbol relationships widely used:
- Corn
- Dancing
- Eagle
- Singing
(Important during feast days and holidays)

### Linguistic Comparison
Kelley & McGregor research compared signs with:
- Plains Indian Sign Language (PISL)
- Cheyenne signs
- American Sign Language (ASL)
- Linguistic differences documented

### Endangerment Factors
- English dominance among Pueblo Indians
- Younger generation learning:
  - School English
  - American Sign Language (ASL)
  - Manually coded English
- Dominant culture influence
- Before 1990s, deaf members sent to residential schools
- Deaf World vs home community decisions

- **Walter P. Kelley**: Primary researcher

### Preservation Recommendations
- Develop illustrated sign dictionary
- Place in pueblo library and museum
- Document signs before extinction
- Record usage patterns

### Keres People & Language
- **Keres** (Keresan): Native American language isolate
- Spoken by Keres Pueblo people in New Mexico
- Pueblos include: Acoma, Laguna, Cochiti, Zia, others
- Eastern and Western Keres dialects

### Relationship to Other Sign Languages
**Unrelated to**:
- Plains Indian Sign Language (different origin/purpose)
- Southwest Hand Talk
- American Sign Language

**Context**:
- One of two documented Southwest indigenous sign languages
- Other: Navajo Family Sign (Davis & Supalla 1995)
- Historical reports of Apache and Hopi sign languages

### Southwest Indigenous Sign Languages
McKay-Cody classification includes:
- **Southwest Hand Talk**: Navajo, Hopi, Apache, Pueblo
- **Keresan Sign Language**: Unrelated village sign
- **Navajo Family Sign**: Unrelated family sign
