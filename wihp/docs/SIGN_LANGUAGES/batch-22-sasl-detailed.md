# WIA Talk Sign Language Mapping - SASL (South African Sign Language)
## Batch 22: 20 Signs with Full WIA Talk Component Analysis

**Language:** SASL - South African Sign Language (Afrikaans: Suid-Afrikaanse Gebaretaal)
**Region:** South Africa
**Estimated Users:** 12,400 (official) to 255,000 (2011 estimate)
**Language Family:** Influenced by British Sign Language, Irish Sign Language, ASL
**Recognition:** **12th OFFICIAL LANGUAGE** - July 19, 2023 (Constitutional Amendment!)

---

## SASL Characteristics

### HISTORIC MILESTONE: 12th Official Language!

**Timeline of Recognition:**
- **May 2, 2023:** National Assembly approved Constitutional amendment
- **May 3, 2023:** SASL officially declared 12th official language
- **July 19, 2023:** President Cyril Ramaphosa signed into law at Union Buildings
- South Africa now has **12 official languages** including SASL!

### Historical Background
- **1863:** Irish nuns involved in training programmes for Deaf
- **1874:** First institution for Deaf - Grimley Institute (Cape Town) by Bridget Lynne (Irish Deaf woman)
- **1881:** De La Bat school for Deaf (Worcester)
- **1884:** Sister Stephanie Hanshuber (Germany) introduced oral method
- **1888:** King William's Town Convent School formally opened
- **1995:** SANCD transformed to Deaf Federation of South Africa (DeafSA)
- **1996:** Sign language mentioned in Constitution
- **2001:** National Language Unit for SASL established
- **2008:** SASL Policy Implementation Conference
- **2018:** SASL as official home language in education; first matriculation subject
- **2023:** 12th official language!

### Language Family & Influences
- **Irish Sign Language** - heavy historical influence via Irish nuns
- **British Sign Language (BSL)** - due to historical ties
- **American Sign Language (ASL)** - some influence
- Indigenous signing practices from local communities
- Evolved into independent language with own grammar

### Key Features
- **Visual-gestural language** 
- Own grammar and structure (different from spoken languages)
- Handshapes, facial expressions, body movements
- Non-manual signs (facial expressions, body posture) essential
- Grammar differs from Afrikaans, Zulu, English

### Regional Variation
- Not entirely uniform
- Localized dialects exist
- Different schools used different sign systems historically
- Signs with many variants
- Geographic spread affects variation

### Organizations & Resources
- **DeafSA** (Deaf Federation of South Africa) - main advocacy organization
- **Pan South African Language Board (PanSALB)**
- ~40 schools for the Deaf (38 using SASL)
- SASL interpreters: 84 on DeafSA register (2011)
  - 43 without training
  - 31 with 240 study hours
  - 10 with 3 years experience + 480 hours
  - Only 7 SATI/DeafSA accredited

### SASL Manual Alphabet
- 31 static gestures (26 letters + 5 numeric digits for 0-5)
- Recognition apps developed by University of Cape Town

### Challenges
- Different schools still use different sign systems
- Interpreter shortages and quality variation
- TV news uses SASL but interpreters use different signs for same concepts
- Many SASL teachers lack formal qualification

## WIA Talk 5-Component System Reference

| Code | Component | Description |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| HS | Hand Shape | Configuration of fingers and palm |
| LC | Location | Where the sign is performed |
| MV | Movement | Direction and type of motion |
| OR | Orientation | Which way palm/fingers face |
| FE | Facial Expression | Required non-manual markers |

### Unique SASL Features
- **12th OFFICIAL LANGUAGE** (July 19, 2023!)
- First African country to constitutionally recognize sign language
- Multi-cultural context (English, Afrikaans, Zulu, Xhosa users)
- Influenced by **Irish SL, BSL, ASL**
- **40 schools for Deaf** (38 using SASL)
- Regional dialects exist
- **31 static gestures** in manual alphabet
- First matriculation subject (2018)
- **Miss South Africa 2024** has hearing impairment - milestone!

### Constitutional Amendment
Section 6 of South African Constitution now includes SASL as official language
- Promotes rights of deaf and hard of hearing
- Equal protection and benefit of law
- Inclusive and substantive equality
- Prevents discrimination on grounds of disability
