### Language Description
- Sign language of Canadas Atlantic provinces
- Descended from British Sign Language (BSL)
- Created through convergence of deaf communities:
  - Northeastern United States immigrants
  - United Kingdom immigrants
  - 18th-19th century migration
- Being supplanted by American Sign Language (ASL)
- Regional dialect recognized by Canadian Association of the Deaf

### Geographic Distribution
- **Nova Scotia** - Primary region
- **New Brunswick** - Secondary region
- **Prince Edward Island** - Secondary region
- **Newfoundland and Labrador** - Some users

### Historical Context
- **1857**: Halifax School for the Deaf established
- MSL was language of instruction until 1961
- **1961-1995**: Atlantic Provinces Special Education Authority (Amherst, NS)
- Schools for Deaf no longer exist in NS and NB
- ASL dominance increased after school closures

### Language Endangerment
- **Critically endangered**
- Fewer than 100 native MSL users remaining
- Largely restricted to older Deaf people
- Younger generations educated in ASL
- No intergenerational transmission
- No formal MSL classes until recent efforts

### Cultural Significance
- Contains history and perspectives of community
- Stories and culture embedded in language
- Loss would mean losing cultural heritage
- Identity marker for Maritime Deaf community
- Community placemaking function

### Relationship to Other Languages
- **British Sign Language (BSL)**: Ancestor
- **American Sign Language (ASL)**: Now dominant, supplanting MSL
- Atlantic regional ASL shows lexical MSL influence
- MSL users often multilingual (MSL/ASL/English)

### Causes of Decline
- ASL brought by American interpreters
- Deaf people from other parts of Canada
- Maritimers trained at Gallaudet University (Washington, D.C.)
- Television and interpretation in ASL
- School closures
- Lack of resources (education, interpretation)

### Preservation Challenges
- VHS tapes need digitization
- No standardized curriculum
- Lack of formal instruction
- Ethical duty for interpreters to incorporate MSL
- Need for intergenerational transmission

### Canadian Sign Language Context
Three sign languages recognized in Canada:
1. **American Sign Language (ASL)** - Dominant
2. **Quebec Sign Language (LSQ)** - Francophone
3. **Maritime Sign Language (MSL)** - Regional, endangered

Plus Indigenous sign languages
