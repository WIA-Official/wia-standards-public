# Trinidad and Tobago Sign Language (TTSL)
## Trinbago Sign Language

### Overview
| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Official Name** | Trinidad and Tobago Sign Language |
| **Native Name** | TTSL / Trinbago Sign Language (TSL) |
| **ISO 639-3** | lst |
| **Country** | Trinidad and Tobago |
| **Users** | Tens of thousands |
| **Status** | Vulnerable / Potentially Endangered |
| **Language Family** | Indigenous Caribbean sign language |

### Historical Origin
- **1943**: Cascade School for the Deaf opened in Port of Spain
- First opportunity for deaf children to form community
- Children created language in dormitories after class
- Signing prohibited in classrooms (Oralist approach)
- Language emerged spontaneously over ~80 years

### Language Development
- Similar emergence pattern to Nicaraguan Sign Language
- Children developed signs from home sign systems
- Passed through successive generations of pupils
- One of the youngest languages in the world (~80 years)
- Has own grammar, syntax, and vocabulary

### ASL Influence
| Period | Development |
|--------|-------------|
| Early years | Some British Sign Language influence |
| 1974 | Total Communication replaced Oralist approach |
| 1970s-present | ASL introduced by US missionaries/educators |
| Current | TTSL-ASL bilingualism common |

### Current Language Situation
- TTSL used in informal deaf community interactions
- ASL used in formal education
- Many signers code-switch between TTSL and ASL
- Teachers starting to switch back to TTSL
- Younger generation primarily knows ASL

### Distinctive Features
- Reflects Trini dialect color and vibrancy
- Signs for local concepts (DOUBLES, steelpan)
- Regional variations (North vs South Trinidad)
- Age-related phonological variations
- Manual alphabet borrowed from ASL

### Endangerment Concerns
- Declining enrollment in deaf schools
- Mainstreaming of deaf education
- Cochlear implantation increasing
- Shrinking deaf community
- Intergenerational transmission threatened
