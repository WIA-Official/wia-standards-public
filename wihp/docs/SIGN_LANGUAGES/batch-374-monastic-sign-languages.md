# Monastic Sign Languages
## Sign Systems of Religious Communities

### Overview
| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Type** | Alternate sign systems |
| **Users** | Monks, nuns (hearing) |
| **Purpose** | Communication during silence vows |
| **History** | Medieval to present |
| **Status** | Various, some documented |

### Introduction
Monastic sign languages are sign systems developed in religious communities where vows of silence restrict spoken communication. Unlike deaf sign languages, these are "alternate" sign systems used by hearing people for specific purposes.

These systems have existed for over 1,000 years and provide valuable insight into how manual communication develops in different contexts. They also have historical significance for deaf sign language development.

### Historical Significance
Monastic signs are important because:
- Predate formal deaf education
- Show humans naturally develop signs
- May have influenced early deaf education
- Document manual communication history
- Cross-cultural phenomenon

### Cistercian Signs: Best Documented
The Trappist/Cistercian tradition:
- Most systematic documentation
- Used for essential communication
- Covers daily life vocabulary
- Religious terminology
- Food, work, time

### Vocabulary Categories

### Sample Cistercian Signs
| Concept | Sign Description |
|---------|------------------|
| Bread | Hands form bread-kneading motion |
| Book | Hands open like pages |
| Prayer | Hands clasped |
| Water | Fingers wave like water |
| Fish | Hand moves like swimming |

### Why Sign Use Declined
1. Relaxation of silence rules (Vatican II)
2. Smaller communities
3. Modern communication needs
4. Less strict interpretation of Rule
5. Practical considerations

- Universal human capacity for manual communication
- Iconicity in sign development
- Limited vs. full language systems
- Cultural transmission of signs
- Historical depth of signing
