# Dominican Sign Language / Lengua de Señas Dominicana (LSD)

## Overview
| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Official Name** | Dominican Sign Language |
| **Native Name** | Lengua de Señas Dominicana (LSD) |
| **ISO 639-3** | doq |
| **Region** | Dominican Republic |
| **Deaf Population** | ~18,000-357,980 (estimates vary) |
| **Overlap with ASL** | ~70% |
| **Status** | Official recognition (2023), Stable, Institutional |

## Historic Milestone
⭐ **2023: LSD officially recognized as language of Dominican Deaf community**
- Approved after 8 previous denials (2012-2023)
- World Federation of the Deaf (WFD) support
- Discovering Deaf Worlds (DDW) collaboration
- Awaiting final government signature for law status

## Historical Development

### ASL Influence
- ASL traditionally used in deaf schools
- American missionaries active since 1980s
- French Sign Language (LSF) original influence
- Combined with local gesture traditions

### Move Toward Independence
- Community wants LSD separate from ASL
- Indigenous signs sometimes called wrong - now shifting
- Emerging LSD dictionary development
- Spanish initialization incorporated (e.g., WATER with A for agua)

## Organizations
- Asociación Nacional de Sordos de la República Dominicana (ANSORDO)
- Two National Deaf Associations in Santo Domingo
- 2015: New board of members elected

## Key Figures
- Pablo Taveras - President, ANSORDO
- Alan Jesurum - Secretary, ANSORDO
- Darling Trinidad - Member, ANSORDO

## Geographic Distribution
- Santo Domingo - major hub for Deaf activity
- Most deaf Dominicans live Santo Domingo to Puerto Plata
- Capital is center of deaf community life

## Community Life
- Deaf schools as sites of sign language acquisition
- Deaf people spend more time with other Deaf than hearing families
- Deaf clubs and associations
- Some Deaf Dominicans travel to nearby countries to evangelize

## Linguistic Features

### Relationship to ASL
- Local variant of ASL
- ~70% similarity (lower than Jamaican 75%)
- ASL intelligibility varies among users
- Some easily understand ASL, others cannot

### Unique Characteristics
- Spanish initialization
- Vocabulary from Spanish-speaking countries
- Some grammatical differences from ASL
- Indigenous signs being documented

### Languages in Schools
- Mixing of LSD, ASL, and Signed English
- Signed English: word-for-word English grammar
- Initialized signs common

## Research

### SIL Surveys (2008-2009)
- Rapid appraisal sociolinguistic surveys
- Data from 7 Dominican Republic cities
- Participant observation and questionnaires
- ASL intelligibility testing

### Key Findings
- Deaf Dominicans recognize distinct ethnolinguistic identity
- Mixed attitudes toward ASL
- Moving toward distinguishing LSD from ASL
- Desire for LSD development

## Community Desires
- Development of LSD
- Increased Spanish literacy
- More government support
- Better educational opportunities
- Better employment opportunities
- Increased access to interpreter services
- Support from hearing parents

## Challenges
- Low Spanish literacy rate among deaf
- ASL has more prestige than indigenous signs
- Government officials lacked knowledge
- Need for patience and collaboration (key lessons)

## Cultural Identity
- Strong ethnolinguistic community identity
- Country sign: República Dominicana (not English Dominican Republic)
- Pastoral roles for some Deaf Dominicans
- Evangelical outreach to other Caribbean deaf communities
