# Batch 214: Nepal Village Sign Languages

## Overview

Nepal has at least **4 documented village sign languages** in addition to Nepali Sign Language:

| Language | Location | District | Status |
|----------|----------|----------|--------|
| Jhankot SL | Jhankot Village | Dolpo | Endangered |
| Jumla SL | Jumla District | Jumla | Endangered |
| Ghandruk SL | Ghandruk VDC | Kaski | Endangered |
| Maunabudhuk-Bodhe SL | Maunabudhuk & Bodhe | Far East | Endangered |

## Why So Many Village Sign Languages?

### Geographic Factors
- Rugged Himalayan terrain
- Isolated mountain villages
- Difficult travel between communities
- Limited medical care access

### High Deafness Rates
- Genetic deafness in isolated communities
- Lack of medical intervention
- Consanguineous marriages in remote areas
- Environmental factors

### Quote from SIL Survey
> "Given the rugged conditions in much of Nepal, and the lack of medical care due to the difficulties of travel, it is probable that there are other village sign languages tucked away up in the mountains."

## 1. Jhankot Sign Language

### Location
| Item | Content |
|------|---------|
| **Nepali** | झनकोट साङ्केतिक भाषा |
| **Village** | Jhankot |
| **District** | Dolpo District |
| **Region** | Remote northwestern Nepal |

### Discovery
- Discovered by **Irene Taylor** (American CODA)
- Taylor was trekking in Dolpo
- Met deaf people she couldn't communicate with
- Documented in her book (1997)

### Key Facts
- 10% of villagers completely deaf (as reported)
- Independent sign language
- Unintelligible to Nepali Sign Language users
- Isolated development

### Irene Taylor
- Child of Deaf Adults (CODA) from America
- Learned ASL as small child
- Traveled to Nepal for trekking
- Could NOT communicate with Jhankot deaf
- Included pages about encounter in her book

| **Region** | Northwestern Nepal |

## 3. Ghandruk Sign Language

### Location
| Item | Content |
|------|---------|
| **Nepali** | घान्द्रुक साङ्केतिक भाषा |
| **Village** | Ghandruk VDC |
| **District** | Kaski District |
| **Region** | Central Nepal (Annapurna region) |

### Discovery
- Travelers noticed many people signing
- SIL researchers investigated
- Confirmed separate sign language

### Research Findings
- Some contact with Nepali Sign Language
- 53% lexical similarity with Pokhara NSL
- Still distinct language
- "Not assimilated a lot of Nepali Sign Language yet"

### Location Note
- Ghandruk is popular trekking destination
- Near Annapurna base camp
- More accessible than other villages
- More contact with outside world

## National Context: Nepali Sign Language

### Overview
- Main sign language of Nepal
- Based on Kathmandu variety
- Influenced by ISL, BSL, ASL
- 68-71% similar to Indian Sign Language

### Legal Status
- 2015 Constitution mentions deaf education rights
- 2017 Disability Rights Act includes sign language
- Recognized by Ministry of Education
- Used in deaf schools (in pidginized form)

### History
- First deaf school: 1966 in Kathmandu
- Oral approach initially
- Total Communication introduced 1985
- Kathmandu Association of the Deaf: 1980

### Limitation
Most deaf Nepalese do NOT know Nepali Sign Language:
- Few deaf schools
- Most born to hearing families
- Home signs more common
- Village sign languages separate

---

## Relationship Between Languages

### Mutual Unintelligibility
All four village sign languages:
- Unintelligible to each other
- Unintelligible to Nepali Sign Language
- Developed independently
- Qualify as separate languages

### Language Isolates
Nepal village sign languages appear to be:
- Language isolates
- Not related to Indo-Pakistani SL family
- Not derived from any known sign language
- Unique developments

- Confirmed multiple sign languages

- Recommended further research
