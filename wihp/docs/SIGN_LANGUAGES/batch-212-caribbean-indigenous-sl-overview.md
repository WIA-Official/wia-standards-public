# Batch 212: Caribbean Indigenous Sign Languages - Overview

## Overview
The Caribbean has remarkable indigenous sign language diversity:
- Village sign languages in isolated communities
- School-based sign languages (post-1900s)
- Many undocumented or disappeared
- ASL influence threatens all

---

## Village/Rural Sign Languages

### 1. Providence Island Sign Language (PISL)
- **Location**: Providence Island, Colombia
- **Status**: Critically Endangered
- **See**: Batch 208 for detailed entry

### 2. Kajana Sign Language
| Item | Content |
|------|---------|
| **Dutch** | Kajana Gebarentaal |
| **Location** | Kajana village, Suriname |
| **Community** | Saramaka Maroon village |
| **Size** | Just 3 families |
| **Status** | CRITICALLY ENDANGERED |

#### Maroon Context
- Saramaka are descendants of escaped African slaves (17th century)
- Settled in Suriname interior along rivers
- Highly isolated communities
- Strong African cultural retention

#### Language Emergence
- High hereditary deafness in village
- Sign language developed locally
- Both deaf and hearing use signs
- Similar pattern to other village SL

### 3. Jamaican Country Sign (Konchri Sain)
| Item | Content |
|------|---------|
| **Local Name** | Konchri Sain (KS) |
| **Location** | St. Elizabeth Parish, Jamaica |
| **Type** | Rural Sign Language |
| **Status** | ENDANGERED |

#### Features
- Used in rural parish
- High hereditary deafness
- Community-wide signing
- Distinct from Jamaican Sign Language

### 4. Grand Cayman Sign Language (EXTINCT)
| Item | Content |
|------|---------|
| **Location** | Grand Cayman, Cayman Islands |
| **Status** | EXTINCT |
| **Last Documentation** | Washabaugh (1981) |

#### History
- Village sign language existed
- Washabaugh "glimpsed" it in 1981
- Now completely gone
- No documentation preserved

## School-Based Sign Languages

### 5. Trinidad and Tobago Sign Language (TTSL)
| Item | Content |
|------|---------|
| **Location** | Trinidad and Tobago |
| **Origin** | First deaf school 1946 |
| **Status** | Developing |

#### History
- First residential deaf school: 1946
- Oralist methods for ~30 years
- Sign language emerged among students
- Now recognized as TTSL

### 6. Cuban Sign Language (Lengua de Signos Cubana)
- Emerged from deaf school establishment
- Indigenous development
- Now recognized nationally

### 7. Puerto Rican Sign Language (PRSL)
- May have existed before first deaf school
- Now very close to ASL after 100+ years contact
- Significant ASL influence

## Suriname Sign Language Situation

### Surinamese Sign Language (SSL)
- Primary deaf community language
- Developed naturally in community and schools
- Recently standardized
- Official language for deaf community

### Kajana Sign Language
- Separate from SSL
- Maroon village language
- Critically endangered
- Only 3 families

### NGT Influence
- Dutch Sign Language present
- Colonial heritage
- Some deaf educated in Netherlands

---

## Threats to Caribbean Indigenous Sign Languages

### 1. ASL Dominance
- Used in most deaf education
- International prestige
- Media exposure
- Mission school influence

### 2. Medical Advances
- Rubella vaccination (reduced congenital deafness)
- Cochlear implants spreading
- Better hearing aids

### 3. Community Changes
- Isolated communities opening up
- Intermarriage reducing genetic deafness
- Youth migration to cities

### 4. Educational Policies
- Mainstreaming deaf students
- Preference for international sign languages
- Limited indigenous SL instruction

## The Caribbean Paradox

> "The quickest route to accessible education may be to focus on ASL-based variety. With limited resources, this may increase likelihood that older, less widely used languages disappear without documentation." - Caribbean Sign Language Documentation Project

**Dilemma**:
- Deaf rights require accessible education NOW
- ASL provides immediate access
- BUT indigenous languages are lost
- No easy solution
