| Attribute | Details |

| **Status** | Stable, Indigenous |

## Important Note
There is NO single Laotian Sign Language. Multiple sign languages and systems are in use across Laos.

## Sign Languages Used in Laos
1. **Lao Sign Language** - derived from French Sign Language (FSL)
2. **French Sign Language** - colonial influence
3. **American Sign Language** - international NGO influence
4. **Thai Sign Language** - geographic proximity influence
5. **Home sign** - isolated family communication systems
6. **Indigenous regional sign languages** - undocumented varieties

## Historical Context

### Colonial Period
- French colonial rule until 1953
- French Sign Language introduced through schools
- FSL influence remains in formal education

### Geographic Connections
- Possible historical connections with Vietnam sign languages
- Possible connections with Thailand sign languages
- Relationship between regional varieties unknown

## Organizations and Schools

### Schools for the Deaf
- Only 3 deaf schools in entire country
- Luang Prabang Deaf and Mute School (founded 2006)
  - Sisters of Charity of St. Jeanne-Antide Thouret
  - ~60 pupils from age 8
  - Education up to middle school (3rd grade)
  - Vocational training included

### Key Organizations
- Hands of Hope Centre (founded by Sophaphone Heuanglit)
- World Education Laos
- Center for Communication on Education and Health (CCEH)
- Ministry of Health
- Children of the Mekong (international charity)

## Key Figures
- **Sophaphone Heuanglit** - pioneering sign language interpreter
  - Founded Hands of Hope Centre
  - COVID-19 daily briefing interpreter
  - Became household name during pandemic
- **Ms. Chanlot Somngam** - deaf community advocate

## COVID-19 Impact
- Daily Facebook live-stream updates (2020-2021)
- Sign language interpretation provided by Sophaphone
- Demonstrated critical need for accessible information
- Raised public awareness of deaf community needs

## Linguistic Features

### FSL-Derived Lao Sign
- Based on French Sign Language structure
- Adapted to Lao context and culture
- Used in formal education settings

### No Training Curriculum
- No standardized Lao sign interpretation curriculum exists
- Training programs urgently needed
- Each school may use different sign systems

## Challenges

### Social Barriers
- Deaf children often hidden by families
- Isolation or sent to work in fields
- Buddhist/Animist societies sometimes exclude deaf
- Karma beliefs may affect family attitudes

### Resource Limitations
- Only 3 deaf schools for entire country
- Less than 10 qualified interpreters nationally
- No standardized curriculum
- Rural isolation prevents language acquisition

### Awareness Issues
- Need for more interpreters
- Sign language interpretation not readily available
- Only provided during emergencies (COVID demonstrated gap)

## Education Programs

### Luang Prabang School Model
- Ages 8-12+ admission
- Some arrive later from extreme poverty families
- Sign language instruction
- Basic education (literacy, numeracy)
- Vocational training:
  - Weaving
  - Agriculture/farming
  - Handicrafts

### Residential Program
- Girls age 13+ live with Sisters
- Foster home environment
- Small farm management training
- Near self-sufficient food production

## Demographics
- ~80,000 deaf and hard of hearing people
- Communist country with limited NGO presence
- Landlocked nation between Thailand, Vietnam, China, Cambodia, Myanmar
- 49+ ethnic groups with diverse languages

## Cultural Significance
- Access to information is critical (COVID proved this)
- Language enables social integration
- Deaf community gaining visibility
- International Day of Sign Languages celebrated (Sept 23)
