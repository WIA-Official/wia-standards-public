# Bahamian Sign Language
## Sign Language of The Bahamas

### Overview
| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Official Name** | Bahamian Sign Language |
| **Country** | Commonwealth of The Bahamas |
| **Users** | ~12,900 Deaf and Hard of Hearing |
| **Status** | Developing, ASL-influenced |
| **Language Family** | ASL variety |

### Population Statistics
- Estimated 12,900 Deaf and Hard of Hearing
- ~3.3% of general population (rough estimate)
- Majority reside on New Providence (Nassau)
- Second largest population on Grand Bahama
- Small groups or individuals on other inhabited islands

### Historical Background
- First documented hearing impairment cases: Measles/Rubella outbreak mid-late 1900s
- 3-4 generations of deaf community in Nassau
- **1964**: Centre for the Deaf established (Bahamas Red Cross + Ministry of Education)
- **1991**: First Bahamian audiologist (Dr. Deborah NuBirth)

### Language Characteristics
- Strongly influenced by American Sign Language (ASL)
- ASL used as primary sign language
- Home Signs used by isolated families on outer islands
- Unique Bahamian signs exist (e.g., signs for Eleuthera, Junkanoo)

### Deaf Community Culture
- Rich social experience
- Frequent meetings in clubs, homes, recreation centers
- At least 3 churches with active deaf ministries in New Providence
- **Talking Hands Society**: Local deaf club

### Organizations
- **Centre for the Deaf** (Nassau) - First deaf school and audiology clinic
- **Talking Hands Society** - Deaf club
- **National Commission for Persons with Disabilities** - Statutory board
- Various NGOs and deaf ministries

### Legal Framework
- **Persons with Disabilities (Equal Opportunities) Act, 2014**
- Focus on ending discrimination
- Equal opportunities advocacy
- AccessAbility Bahamas App (2020s) - Sign language interpretation feature

### Challenges
- No established newborn hearing screening in public sector
- No Closed Caption technology on television
- No standards for interpreter training
- Language not yet documented for education/training
- Limited services on outer islands (Family Islands)

### Education
- Centre for the Deaf in Nassau provides education
- Deaf students from outer islands transferred to Nassau
- Some students sent to US for specialized education
- Center for Exceptional Learners (Eleuthera) - Special needs school

### Geographic Distribution
| Island | Deaf Community Status |
|--------|----------------------|
| New Providence (Nassau) | Largest community, organized |
| Grand Bahama | Second largest |
| Eleuthera | Very limited, no resident ASL users |
| Other islands | Isolated individuals, home signs |

### Healthcare Services
- Audiology services primarily private practice
- No government-mandated early intervention programs
- Newborn screening only at private facilities
- Cochlear implants available (often abroad)

### Key Figures
- **Dr. Deborah NuBirth**: First Bahamian audiologist
- **Marvin Finlayson** (late): Visionary for deaf rights organization

### Preservation Efforts
- Documentation of Bahamian Sign Language valued
- Aims to eradicate discrimination
- Goals include gainful employment and educational reform
