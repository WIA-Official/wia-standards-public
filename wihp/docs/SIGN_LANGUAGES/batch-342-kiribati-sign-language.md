### Language Description
Kiribati Sign Language is the sign language developing among the Deaf community of Kiribati, a nation of 33 atolls and reef islands spread across 3.5 million square kilometers of the Pacific Ocean. With a population of approximately 120,000 spread across this vast area, the deaf community faces unique challenges of geographic dispersion.

The language appears to be developing organically within the I-Kiribati (Kiribati people) deaf community, with possible influences from Auslan through Australian connections and from home sign systems across the islands.

The extreme geographic spread of the nation—spanning both sides of the International Date Line—creates significant challenges for deaf community cohesion and language standardization.

### Key Characteristics
- **Geographic Spread**: Signs may vary across distant islands
- **Austronesian Context**: Cultural connections with Pacific signing
- **Home Sign Base**: Many families develop their own signs
- **Limited Contact**: Deaf individuals often isolated on outer islands
- **Emerging Standardization**: Centralization in South Tarawa

### Geographic Challenge
Kiribati's unique geography:
- **33 atolls** across three island groups
- **Line Islands** and **Phoenix Islands** extremely remote
- **South Tarawa**: Capital with highest population density
- Travel between islands expensive and difficult
- Deaf individuals on outer islands may never meet others

### Deaf Community
- **South Tarawa**: Largest concentration of deaf individuals
- **Outer Islands**: Isolated deaf, often using home signs only
- **Community Formation**: Gradual through schools and churches
- **Youth**: Increasingly connected through limited technology

### Education
- **Te Toa Matoa School**: Primary special education facility
- **Inclusive Education**: Government policy promotes inclusion
- **Teacher Training**: Supported by Australian and NZ aid
- **Resources**: Very limited specialized materials

### Climate Change Impact
Kiribati faces existential climate threat:
- Rising seas threaten all islands
- Potential national relocation discussed
- Deaf community vulnerable in emergencies
- Communication challenges in disasters
- Future of language community uncertain

### Challenges
1. Extreme geographic dispersion
2. No standardized sign language
3. Outer island isolation
4. Climate change existential threat
5. Very limited resources
6. No interpreter training
7. Economic constraints

### Development Priorities
1. Sign language documentation
2. Connecting dispersed deaf community
3. Training deaf teachers from local community
4. Developing climate emergency communication
