# Bhutanese Sign Language / BhSL

## Overview
| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Official Name** | Bhutanese Sign Language |
| **Native Name** | Drukgi Lagdai Khakay (Dzongkha) |
| **Region** | Kingdom of Bhutan |
| **Users** | ~6,000 deaf individuals |
| **Lexicon Size** | 2,800+ signs documented |
| **Status** | Emerging, Government recognized |

## Historical Development

### Early Development (2000-2002)
- ca. 2000: First program for deaf in hearing school (Thimphu)
- 2002: Research and documentation began
- Home-grown sign language already in use

### Wangsel Institute (2003-2013)
- 2003: School for the Deaf established
- 2010: Video Lexicon of 2,800 Signs (CD Sets A-F)
- 2013: First dedicated deaf school approved (Paro)
- Currently known as Wangsel Institute for the Deaf

### Modern Recognition
- Legal recognition through National Policy for Persons with Disabilities
- Government shall recognize and adopt standardized BhSL
- Deaf community recognized as cultural/linguistic minority
- 2023: First Bhutanese Sign Language App launched

## Linguistic Features

### Characteristics
- Visual-gestural language with own grammar
- No written form (3-D aspects)
- Mother tongue/first language (L1) of Bhutanese Deaf
- Uses hands, eyes, face, mouth, body
- Distinct from spoken Dzongkha and English
- Different lexicon and syntax from spoken languages

### Development Process
- Up to one week to agree on single sign
- Recorded on film (not paper - 2D limitation)
- Regional signs documented for standardization
- Technical vocabulary being developed

## Education System

### Wangsel Institute for the Deaf
- Location: Drukgyel Lower Secondary School, Paro district
- Bilingual education approach:
  - L1: Bhutanese Sign Language
  - L2: English (official language)
  - Later: Dzongkha (national language)
- Classes: Preparatory to Grade 12
- National Certificate II (vocational)

### Special Education Needs (SEN) Program
- Inclusive education for various disabilities
- 21%+ of children aged 2-9 born with disabilities
- Government commitment to inclusive education

## Technology

### BhSL App (2023)
- Launched: International Day for Persons with Disabilities
- Available on iOS and Android
- Bridge communication gaps
- Tool for learning sign language

### Documentation
- YouTube channel for dissemination
- Video calls and social media communication
- Smartphone-connected deaf community

## Government Policy

### National Policy for Persons with Disabilities
- Page 40: Recognize and adopt standardized BhSL
- Page 29: Recognize Deaf as cultural/linguistic minority
- Page 11: Access to sign language from diagnosis
- Signed version available on YouTube

## Challenges

### Current Issues
- Shortage of deaf teachers
- Need for more sign language interpreters
- Limited deaf schools
- Lack of deafness knowledge among teachers
- Deaf students difficulty mixing with hearing

### Bhutanese Refugees
- Ethnic Nepalis expelled 1990s
- ~5% of refugees have disabilities
- Half are hearing/speech impairments
- Refugee camps in Nepal
- Diaspora communities (Vermont, USA)

## Geographic Distribution
- Main use: Wangsel Institute (Paro)
- Communication via social media across:
  - Thimphu (capital)
  - Paro (west)
  - Tashigang (far east)
- Individuals connected nationwide via smartphones

## Cultural Significance
- Only accessible language for deaf adults
- Used by both hearing and deaf at Wangsel
- Reflects Bhutan history, culture, political system
- Growing community via digital connections
