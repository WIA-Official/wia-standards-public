# Batch 192: Yucatec Maya Sign Language(s) (YMSL)

## Overview
| Item | Content |
|------|---------|
| **ISO Code** | msd |
| **Region** | Yucatan Peninsula, Mexico |
| **Native Name** | Lengua de Señas Maya Yucateca |
| **Type** | Village Sign Language Complex |
| **Status** | Endangered / Developing |
| **Relation** | Unrelated to Mexican Sign Language (LSM) |

## Geographic Distribution

### Primary Communities
1. **Chicán** - Most studied village, 13+ deaf in ~400 population
2. **Nohya** - Early documentation (1980)
3. **Multiple villages** across Yucatan Peninsula

### Characteristics
- High percentage deaf population (3%+ in some villages)
- Shared sign language by both deaf AND hearing
- Deaf fully integrated into community life
- Deafness as **positive** community identity feature

## Linguistic Features

### Unique Characteristics
1. **Shared Language**: Both deaf and hearing fluent
2. **Multiple Varieties**: Different villages, families, generations
3. **Translanguaging**: Deaf-hearing space overlap
4. **Early Agreement Emergence**: Unlike many new sign languages
5. **Spatial Reference**: Complex spatial grammar

### Relation to Other Languages
- Unrelated to Mexican Sign Language (LSM)
- Possible similarities with Guatemalan Mayan sign languages
- No proven link to Highland Maya Sign Language
- Distinct from Spanish and Yucatec Maya spoken language

## Sociolinguistic Situation

### Community Attitudes
- **Non-discriminatory**: Positive view of deafness
- **Full Integration**: Deaf participate in all activities
- **Cultural Identity**: Sign language part of Maya heritage

### Variation
- Inter-village differences
- Generational variation
- Family-specific signs
- Limited contact between some deaf groups

## Documentation Status

### Resources
- ELDP archived materials
- Smithsonian Institution corpus
- Academic publications
- Video documentation

### Gaps
- No comprehensive dictionary
- Limited grammatical description
- No writing system
- No formal education materials

## Threats

1. **Mexican Sign Language (LSM)** expansion
2. **Urban migration** of young deaf
3. **Educational policies** favoring LSM
4. **Limited documentation**
5. **No formal recognition**

## WIA Talk Approach

### Priority Actions
1. Partner with existing researchers (Le Guen, Safar)
2. Document all village varieties
3. Create community-controlled dictionary
4. Develop bilingual education materials
5. Support deaf-led language planning

|---------|-----------------|------------------|

| Attitude | Positive | Sometimes oppressive |

| Documentation | Moderate | Limited |

## Cultural Context

### Maya Deaf Experience
- Traditional acceptance predates contact
- Hunting/farming activities fully accessible
- Marriage and family life normal
- Community ceremonies include deaf

### State vs Community Views
Mexican government: Deafness as disability
Local community: Deafness as variation
