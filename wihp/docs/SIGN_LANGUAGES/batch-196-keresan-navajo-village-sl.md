# Batch 196: Southwest US Indigenous Village Sign Languages

## 1. Keresan Sign Language (KPISL / Keresign)

### Overview
| Item | Content |
|------|---------|
| **Other Names** | Keresan Pueblo Indian Sign Language |
| **Location** | Keresan Pueblo, New Mexico (likely Zia Pueblo) |
| **Type** | Village Sign Language |
| **Status** | Endangered |

### Background
- Developed locally in Keresan-speaking pueblo
- High incidence of congenital deafness in community
- UNRELATED to Plains Indian Sign Language
- UNRELATED to American Sign Language
- Used by entire community (deaf and hearing)

### Keresan People
The Keres are one of the Pueblo peoples of New Mexico:
- Cochiti Pueblo (Kotyit)
- San Felipe Pueblo (Katishtya)
- Santo Domingo Pueblo (Kewa)
- Santa Ana Pueblo (Tamaya)
- Zia Pueblo (Tsia)
- Acoma Pueblo (Haaku)
- Laguna Pueblo (Kawaika)

### Documentation Status
- Very limited academic study
- Community maintains language internally
- No published dictionary or grammar
- Mentioned in PISL literature as distinct

## 2. Navajo Family Sign

### Overview
| Item | Content |
|------|---------|
| **Location** | Navajo Nation |
| **Type** | Family Sign Language |
| **Status** | Unknown |

### Background
- Used by a Navajo clan with high deaf membership
- Developed independently within the family/clan
- UNRELATED to Plains Indian Sign Language
- UNRELATED to Southwest Hand Talk
- UNRELATED to American Sign Language

### Characteristics
- Family/clan-based transmission
- Small community of users
- Integrated into Navajo cultural life
- No formal documentation

---

## 3. Southwest Hand Talk (Different Category)

### Overview
- Part of Plains Sign Language family
- NOT a village sign language
- Regional variety of Hand Talk

### Users
- Navajo
- Hopi
- Apache
- Pueblo peoples

### Distinction
Southwest Hand Talk is a TRADE language
Keresan SL and Navajo Family Sign are VILLAGE/FAMILY languages
These are separate linguistic systems

## WIA Talk Notes

### Research Gaps
1. No systematic documentation of either village sign language
2. Community access required
3. Tribal permission essential
4. Cultural sensitivity paramount

### Ethical Considerations
- Pueblo communities have strong privacy traditions
- Research must be community-initiated
- External documentation may not be welcomed
- Respect tribal sovereignty absolutely
