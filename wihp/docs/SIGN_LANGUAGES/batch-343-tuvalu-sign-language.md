### Language Description
Tuvalu Sign Language refers to sign communication among deaf individuals in Tuvalu, the world's fourth smallest country with a population of approximately 11,000 people spread across nine islands (only eight permanently inhabited).

With such a tiny population, the deaf community is extremely small and likely relies primarily on home signs and family-based communication systems. No formal documentation of Tuvaluan sign language exists, making this one of the world's least studied deaf linguistic situations.

Given historical British colonial connections and current Commonwealth ties, any external sign language influence would most likely come from Auslan or New Zealand Sign Language through regional connections.

### Key Characteristics
- **Micro-State Context**: Among world's smallest nations
- **Home Sign Predominance**: No standardized system documented
- **Island Isolation**: Each island may have unique signs
- **Undocumented**: No linguistic research conducted
- **Polynesian Culture**: Traditional community inclusion values

### Deaf Community
In a nation of ~11,000 people:
- Estimated deaf population: 30-80 individuals
- Spread across 8 inhabited islands
- Funafuti (capital) has highest concentration
- No formal deaf organization documented
- Extended family systems provide support

### Climate Change Crisis
Tuvalu faces imminent existential threat:
- Highest point only 4.6 meters above sea level
- King tides already flood islands regularly
- National survival uncertain beyond 2100
- Potential full evacuation to Australia/NZ discussed
- Deaf community particularly vulnerable

### Challenges
1. Smallest national deaf community context
2. No documentation whatsoever
3. Multi-island isolation
4. Climate change existential threat
5. Extreme resource limitations
6. No interpreters or trained teachers
7. Future of nation itself uncertain

### Urgent Research Needs
- Basic survey of deaf population
- Documentation of any existing signs
- Recording of home sign systems
- Oral history from elderly deaf
- Emergency communication planning

### Cultural Context
Tuvaluan culture traditionally:
- Values community inclusion
- Extended family provides support
- Small community knows everyone
- Traditional structures may support deaf individuals
- Migration for education/work common
