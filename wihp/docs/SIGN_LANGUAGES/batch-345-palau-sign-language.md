### Language Description
Palau Sign Language is the sign language developing among the Deaf community of Palau, an archipelago of over 500 islands in the Western Pacific. With a population of approximately 18,000, Palau has a small but organized deaf community.

Due to Palau's Compact of Free Association with the United States, American Sign Language (ASL) has significant influence on Palauan signing. However, local signs reflecting Palauan culture and environment are also present, creating a unique variety.

Palau's strong traditional culture and clan-based social structure provide a supportive environment for deaf individuals within extended family networks.

### Key Characteristics
- **ASL Base**: Strong American influence
- **Palauan Elements**: Local signs for cultural concepts
- **Small Community**: Intimate signing network
- **US Connection**: Free movement to US brings ASL contact
- **Traditional Support**: Clan systems include deaf members

### Palauan Culture and Deafness
Traditional Palauan society:
- **Clan System**: Extended family provides support
- **Bai** (traditional meeting houses): Community inclusion
- **Ocheraol** (customary exchanges): Deaf participate
- **Storied Society**: Oral traditions adapted for deaf
- **Strong Identity**: Palauan deaf identity developing

### Deaf Community
- **Koror**: Capital, majority of deaf population
- **Babeldaob**: Main island, some deaf residents
- **Outer Islands**: Isolated individuals
- **US Diaspora**: Palauan deaf in Hawaii, Guam, mainland US

### Education
- **Palau Ministry of Education**: Oversees special education
- **Special Education Program**: Established with US support
- **Palau Community College**: Some deaf students attend
- **Overseas Education**: Some study in US, Guam

### Compact of Free Association
The Compact provides:
- Free entry to United States
- Access to US federal programs
- Educational opportunities
- Healthcare referrals
- Creates transnational community

### Environmental Signs
Unique signs likely exist for:
- Traditional foods and practices
- Marine life (jellyfish lake, fish species)
- Rock Islands geography
- Clan and family names
- Traditional ceremonies

### Challenges
1. Very small language community
2. Risk of complete ASL shift
3. Limited documentation
4. Outer island isolation
5. No local interpreter training
6. Language preservation concerns

### Development Priorities
1. Document unique Palauan signs
2. Develop bilingual ASL-Palauan approach
3. Train local deaf educators
4. Connect outer island deaf
5. Preserve traditional cultural signs
