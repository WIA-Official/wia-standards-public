# Inuit Sign Language (IUR)
## Inuit Uukturausingit (ᐃᓄᐃᑦ ᐆᒃᑐᕋᐅᓯᖏᑦ)

### Overview
| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Native Name** | Inuit Uukturausingit (ᐃᓄᐃᑦ ᐆᒃᑐᕋᐅᓯᖏᑦ) |
| **Abbreviation** | IUR, ISL |
| **Country** | Canada (Nunavut) |
| **Language Family** | Language isolate |
| **Status** | Severely endangered |
| **Users** | Fewer than 40-50 native signers |

### Language Description
- Indigenous sign language of Inuit people
- Language isolate (unrelated to other sign languages)
- Native to Canadian Arctic communities
- Evolved from hunting/gathering gestural systems
- Used by both deaf and hearing Inuit
- Mutually intelligible across Nunavut despite dialectal variation

### Geographic Distribution
Documented communities in Nunavut:
- **Rankin Inlet** (62°48N, 92°05W)
- **Baker Lake** (64°19N, 96°01W)
- **Taloyoak** (69°32N, 93°32W)

Potential range:
- Northern Quebec
- Greenland (possible connection)
- Northwest Territories
- Alaska (unconfirmed)

### Historical Origins
- Used since at least 18th century
- Originally for hunting communication (silent)
- Trade language between Inuit groups
- Similar function to Plains Indian Sign Language
- Earliest documented account: Mallet (1930)
- Adapted for deaf community needs over time

### Deaf Population Context
- Nunavut deafness rate: 0.6% (6x southern Canada)
- High incidence of hereditary/congenital deafness
- ~155 deaf residents in Nunavut (c. 2000)
- ~47 used IUR as native language (~1/3)
- ~2/3 use ASL or Manually Coded English

### Unique Social Context
- Deaf well integrated in Inuit communities
- Less stigma than southern Canada
- ~2 hearing users for every deaf signer
- Similar to Martha's Vineyard historically
- **Tunnganarniq**: Inuit value of being welcoming/inclusive
- Deaf participate in hunting, artistry, labor

### Language Endangerment
**Severely endangered** due to:
- ASL encroachment from southern schools
- No formal IUR education
- Children sent to residential schools (Ottawa)
- Loss of intergenerational transmission
- No deaf children acquiring IUR as L1
- Estimated extinction in 2 generations without intervention

### Legal Status
- **2019**: Accessible Canada Act recognized Indigenous sign languages
- No provincial/territorial official language status
- **2008**: IUR interpretation in Nunavut Legislative Assembly
- **R. v. Suwarak (1999)**: Court case highlighted interpreter need

### Linguistic Features
- **33 handshapes** identified
- Limited non-manual simultaneity
- High manual simultaneity
- All verb types present (plain, agreement, spatial)
- Classifier constructions
- Spatial grammar adapted to Arctic environment
- No formal alphabet

### Relationship to Other Languages
- Possible connection to Greenlandic Sign Language
- ASL borrowing increasing
- Lexical items from ASL incorporated
- Not related to Plains Indian Sign Language (different origin)

### Research Communities
Three communities documented:
1. Rankin Inlet
2. Baker Lake
3. Taloyoak

### Educational Challenges
- Deaf children sent to southern Canada schools
- ASL primary instructional language
- Communication problems upon return
- Family members don't know ASL
- Children's IUR knowledge limited
- Relatives often switch to learning ASL

### Revitalization Efforts
- CLEY funded teaching materials development
- Support services for deaf individuals
- Video documentation projects
- Life story recordings
- Interpreter training program expansion
- Academic documentation (Schuit, MacDougall)

### Cultural Significance
- Part of Inuit cultural heritage
- Embodies Inuit Qaujimajatuqangit (societal values)
- Regional dialectal variations reflect local culture
- Used in storytelling and daily life
- Important for Nunavummiut identity
