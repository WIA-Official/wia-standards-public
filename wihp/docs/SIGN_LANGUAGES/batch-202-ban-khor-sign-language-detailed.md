# Batch 202: Ban Khor Sign Language (BKSL)

## Overview
| Item | Content |
|------|---------|
| **Local Name** | Pasa kidd (language of the mute) |
| **Location** | Ban Khor village, Issarn region, NE Thailand |
| **Type** | Village Sign Language Isolate |
| **Origin** | ~1930s (about 90 years ago) |
| **Status** | CRITICALLY ENDANGERED |

## Emergence Story

### Before 1930s
- No deaf people in Ban Khor
- No sign language existed
- Normal rural Thai village

### 1930s Sudden Change
- Hereditary deafness suddenly appeared
- Home sign systems developed
- Rapidly evolved into full language

### Development
Home sign → Family sign → Village sign language
- Natural human language capacity
- Social interaction and creative repetition
- Full-fledged language within decades

## Demographics

### Deaf Population
- Deafness rate: 1 in 100 (1-1.5% of village)
- Some sectors: 5-10x normal rates
- Genetic/hereditary cause

### Speech/Sign Community
- Both deaf AND hearing villagers signed
- Widespread BKSL acquisition by hearing
- True bimodal bilingual community

## Key Researcher

## Linguistic Features

### Unique Phonology
Signs where arm CONCEALS face of signer:
- THERE
- FOREIGN
- Rare cross-linguistically

### Language Isolate
- Unrelated to Thai Sign Language (TSL)
- Developed in complete isolation
- No external sign language influence (originally)

## The Endangerment Crisis

### Before Contact
- Isolated village
- BKSL only sign language known
- Full integration of deaf residents
- Deaf perceived positively

### What Changed

#### Education
- External deaf schools opened
- Thai Sign Language (TSL) introduced
- Children sent away to learn TSL

#### Mobility
- Economic development
- Work opportunities outside village
- Contact with national deaf community

#### Status Shift
- TSL promoted by government
- TSL seen as prestigious
- BKSL seen as local, inferior

### Evidence of Shift
1. Code-switching BKSL ↔ TSL
2. Lexical borrowing from TSL
3. Core vocabulary replacement
4. Younger generation prefers TSL
5. BKSL reduced to older generation

## Martha Vineyard Parallel

### Similar Pattern
Martha Vineyard Sign Language (USA):
- Island with hereditary deafness
- Shared signing community
- External school contact (1817)
- Gradual decline
- Last speaker died 1952

### BKSL Following Same Path
Ban Khor may experience same extinction trajectory.

## Cultural Impact

### Traditional Integration
- Deaf fully included in daily life
- No disability stigma
- Communicative bridge between deaf-hearing
- Economic participation

### Nostalgic Loss
- Village elders express sadness
- Sense of cultural loss beginning
- Recognition that something precious is disappearing

## WIA Talk Priority

### Critical Actions
1. URGENT documentation of remaining fluent signers
2. Record elderly signers before extinction
3. Create intergenerational transmission programs
4. Work with Thai authorities on recognition
5. Digital archive for preservation

### Timeline Concern
- May disappear within current generation
- 10-20 year window for documentation
- Some vocabulary already lost

## Lessons for Sign Language Preservation

### Nonaka Warning (2004)
Village sign languages are:
- Doubly minoritized (by spoken language AND national SL)
- Overlooked in language rights discussions
- Forgotten endangered languages

### Key Factors for Survival
1. Community isolation (now broken)
2. Positive deaf identity (challenged by external contact)
3. Hearing signer population (declining)
4. Intergenerational transmission (disrupted)
