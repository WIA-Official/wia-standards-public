# Plains Indian Sign Language (PISL)
## Hand Talk / Plains Sign Talk

### Overview
| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Common Names** | Hand Talk, Plains Sign Talk, First Nation Sign Language |
| **Abbreviation** | PISL, NASL |
| **Region** | Central Canada, Central/Western USA, Northern Mexico |
| **Language Family** | Language isolate (possibly family of related languages) |
| **Status** | Critically endangered |
| **Historical Users** | 110,000+ (1885 estimate) |

### Language Description
- Indigenous sign language of North America
- Oldest documented sign language in North America
- Lingua franca for international relations, trade, diplomacy
- Used by both deaf and hearing Indigenous people
- Pre-contact origins (before European arrival)
- Universal across two-thirds of US tribal nations

### Geographic Distribution
Historical range:
- **Gulf of Mexico to Calgary, Canada**
- Great Plains (primary)
- Northeast Woodlands
- Great Basin
- Northern Mexico

Notable tribal users:
- Blackfoot, Cheyenne, Sioux, Kiowa, Arapaho
- Crow, Dakota, Cree
- Hidatsa, Atsina
- Potawatomi, Algonquian nations

### Regional Variations (McKay-Cody Framework)
- **Northeast Hand Talk** (including Oneida Sign Language)
- **Plains Sign Talk** (most documented)
- **Great Basin Sign Language** (Ute)
- **Southwest Hand Talk** (Navajo, Hopi, Apache, Pueblo)
- **West Coast** (Chumash)
- **Coast Salish Sign Language** (Pacific Northwest)

### Historical Origins
- Earliest European records: Cabeza de Vaca (1527), Coronado (1541)
- May have originated in southern North America (Texas/Mexico)
- Possible connection to Maya sign languages (Meemul Ch'aab'al)
- Lewis and Clark Expedition interpreters (George Drouillard)
- September 1930: Largest filmed gathering of PISL users

### Contemporary Status
- **Critically endangered** (UNESCO)
- Very small number of fluent signers
- Primarily used by Elders and Deaf citizens
- Siouan and Algonquian Nations maintain some users
- Revitalization efforts increasing in 21st century

### Key Historical Figures
- **Hugh L. Scott**: 1930 council organizer, film documentation
- **William Tomkins**: Indian Sign Language book (1926)
- **George Drouillard**: Lewis & Clark interpreter
- **Charles Russell**: Artist who incorporated PISL in paintings
- **White Swan (Strikes Enemy)**: Deaf Crow elder who taught PISL

### Contemporary Researchers & Advocates
- **Dr. Melanie McKay-Cody**: Cherokee Deaf scholar, linguist
  - First deaf researcher specializing in PISL
  - Works with tribes on preservation
- **Jeffrey E. Davis**: Leading documentation linguist
- **Willie LeClair**: Eastern Shoshone elder (d. 2023), Wyoming Humanities project
- **Lanny Real Bird**: Crow, Arikara, Hidatsa heritage
- **Charles Scott**: Citizen Potawatomi Nation revitalization
- **Ron Garritson**: Choctaw heritage, Billings MT

### Linguistic Features
Five components of communication:
1. **Hand shape** (e.g., J-shape for yes)
2. **Hand movement**
3. **Hand location**
4. **Hand orientation**
5. **Facial expression**

Prosodic structure (West):
- Syllable-like packages
- Word-like individual signs
- Sentence-like phrases
- Paragraph-like utterances

### Functions
- Trade and economics
- International diplomacy
- Hunting (silent communication)
- Storytelling and oratory
- Ceremonies and rituals
- Daily communication with deaf members
- Conflict resolution

### Decline Factors
- **Boarding schools**: Forced English-only policies
- Government assimilation policies (late 1800s)
- Alexander Graham Bell's anti-sign movement
- Loss of intergenerational transmission
- ASL dominance in deaf education

### Relationship to ASL
- Some researchers hypothesize ASL partially descends from PISL
- Contact with Martha's Vineyard Sign Language
- Indigenous children at American School for the Deaf (Hartford)
- 19th century influence documented

### Preservation Efforts
- **Wyoming Humanities**: Willie LeClair video project
- **1930 Council films**: Rediscovered National Archives footage
- **TravelStorys GPS Audio Tour**: Wind River Reservation
- Sign language conferences for standardization
- Tribal revitalization programs
- Incorporation into tribal language learning

### Cultural Significance
- Three-dimensional visual language
- Integral to Native American heritage
- Used in Charles Russell's artwork
- Connected to petroglyphs
- Part of storytelling traditions
- Universal despite linguistic diversity

### Limitations
- Cannot be used in darkness
- Regional variations cause some confusion
- Complex abstract topics challenging
- Two-dimensional representation difficult (art)
