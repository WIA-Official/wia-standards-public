### Introduction
Russian Sign Language (RSL) and its related languages form a family that spans the territory of the former Soviet Union. Through the centralized Soviet deaf education system, RSL influenced sign language development across 15 republics, creating a family of related but increasingly distinct sign languages.

Since the dissolution of the USSR in 1991, these languages have diverged as national deaf communities assert their own linguistic identities.

### Soviet Era Influence
The USSR's deaf education system:
- Centralized curriculum
- RSL as primary language
- Teachers trained in Moscow/Leningrad
- Deaf schools in all republics
- RSL vocabulary spread across USSR

### Fingerspelling
The Russian manual alphabet:
- One-handed (unlike old LSF influence)
- 33 letters (Cyrillic)
- Spread across Soviet space
- National adaptations for local alphabets

### All-Russian Society of the Deaf (VOG)
Founded 1926, VOG:
- Largest deaf organization in Russia
- Owns businesses, cultural centers
- Provides services nationwide
- Political representation
- International connections

### Post-Soviet Divergence
Since 1991:
- National languages asserting independence
- Local vocabulary development
- Reduced RSL contact
- National identity in signing
- Some languages distancing from RSL

### War Impact (2022+)
The Russia-Ukraine war has:
- Displaced Ukrainian deaf
- Disrupted deaf services
- Created humanitarian needs
- Affected cross-border deaf contact
- Accelerated Ukrainian SL distinction from RSL
