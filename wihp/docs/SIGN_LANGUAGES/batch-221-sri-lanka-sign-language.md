## Linguistic Classification

### Language Isolate Status
**Wittmann (1991)** posits:
- SLSL is a "language isolate"
- "Prototype" sign language
- Developed through stimulus diffusion
- From existing sign language (unclear which)

### Variants
Three variants based on:
1. **Sinhala-based** variant
2. **Tamil-based** variant  
3. **English-based** variant

### Regional Variations
- 25 deaf schools create regional variations
- School-based dialect development
- Not clear if related to each other
- Unknown how many distinct varieties exist

### BSL Family Connection
- Related to British Sign Language (BSL)
- Part of BANZSL family influence
- Similar to Indian Sign Language
- Through colonial-era connections

## Current Challenges

### From 2024 Research Study

#### Major Issues Identified
1. **No fully developed common sign language**
2. **Severe shortage of interpreters**
3. **Few resources for teachers**
4. **DHH community marginalized**
5. **Under-supported education system**
6. **Urgent attention needed**

#### Study Scope
- All nine provinces surveyed
- Interviews with Special Education Directors
- Focus groups with principals and teachers
- Deaf school and mainstream unit teachers

### Interpreter Crisis
- Lack of SLSL interpreters
- Scarcity of digital translation tools
- National Institute of Education publishes materials
- Sri Lanka Central Federation of Deaf (SLCFD) helps

## Deaf Education

### School System
- 25+ deaf schools throughout the island
- 16-18 Deaf primary schools (formal)
- Residential hostel system
- Sign language as primary instruction
- Some mainstream integration units

### Educational Challenges
- Sign language not recognized as language of instruction
- Special education not in "formal" category
- Limited Sinhala literacy pedagogy adaptation
- Significant teacher training gaps
- Lack of parental support

### Historical Approach
- Traditional methods (sign-based)
- Social marginalization of deaf children
- "Deaf and dumb" stigma
- Segregated institutions
- 1980s: Shift toward integration attempted

## Language Recognition Status

### Current Situation
- SLSL NOT officially recognized
- Not acknowledged as language of instruction
- Constitutional silence on minority languages
- Need for formal recognition urgent

### Needed Reforms
1. Official recognition of SLSL
2. Embedding as instruction language
3. Including special education in formal system
4. Teacher training programs
5. Interpreter certification
6. Standardization across regions
