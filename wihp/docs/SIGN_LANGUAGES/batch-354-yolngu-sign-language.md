### Language Description
Yolngu Sign Language (YSL) is an indigenous sign language used by the Yolngu people of northeast Arnhem Land in Australia's Northern Territory. Unlike most sign languages which are primary languages of deaf communities, YSL is an "alternate sign language" used by both hearing and deaf Yolngu people in various social contexts.

YSL is deeply integrated into Yolngu culture and serves multiple functions beyond communication with deaf individuals. It is used during mourning periods when speaking is restricted, for communication during hunting to maintain silence, across long distances, and in ceremonial contexts.

This makes YSL one of the world's most well-documented examples of an alternate sign language in an indigenous Australian context.

### Key Characteristics
- **Alternate Language**: Used by hearing and deaf
- **Cultural Integration**: Part of broader Yolngu tradition
- **Taboo Contexts**: Used when speech is forbidden
- **Hunting Use**: Silent communication in bush
- **Ceremonial Role**: Part of religious practice

#### Social Contexts
- Widows may not speak for extended periods
- Men's ceremonial restrictions on speech
- Across-group communication
- Inter-generational knowledge transfer

### Deaf Community
Within Yolngu society:
- Deaf individuals fully included
- YSL primary language for deaf
- Hearing family members sign
- No social stigma around deafness
- Deaf participate in all cultural activities

### Linguistic Features
- **Iconicity**: Many signs visually represent meanings
- **Pointing**: Extensive use of directional pointing
- **Mouth Patterns**: Different from Auslan
- **Kinship Signs**: Elaborate relationship terminology
- **Country Signs**: Rich geographic vocabulary

### Cultural Significance
YSL embedded in Yolngu worldview:
- **Kinship System**: Complex relationship signs

- **Law**: Part of traditional knowledge

### Challenges
1. Younger generations may learn less
2. Influence from Auslan in education
3. Colonial disruption to traditions
4. Mining and development pressures
5. Documentation gaps remain

### Relationship with Auslan
Important distinctions:
- YSL is indigenous, Auslan is derived from BSL
- YSL used by hearing people extensively
- Different grammatical structures
- Deaf Yolngu may learn both
- Education system may use Auslan

### Preservation Efforts
Current initiatives:
- Elder-youth transmission programs
- Video documentation projects
- Community language planning
- Integration with bilingual education
- Academic-community partnerships

### Significance for Sign Language Studies
YSL demonstrates:
- Sign languages develop independently worldwide
- Alternate sign languages exist cross-culturally
- Indigenous sign languages predate colonial contact
- Integration of sign in oral cultures possible
