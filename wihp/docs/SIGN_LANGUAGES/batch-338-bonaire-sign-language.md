### Language Description
Bonaire Sign Language is the indigenous sign language of Bonaire, the smallest of the ABC islands (Aruba, Bonaire, Curaçao) and a special municipality of the Netherlands since 2010. With a total population of approximately 21,000, Bonaire has the smallest deaf community of the three islands.

Due to its small size, the sign language is primarily maintained within families and close community networks. The language shows characteristics of home sign systems that have been shared across generations and among deaf individuals on the island.

The language has received influence from both neighboring island sign languages (particularly Curaçao) and from Dutch Sign Language (NGT) and ASL through education and media.

### Key Characteristics
- **Home Sign Origins**: Many signs from family systems
- **Island Variation**: Different families may use different signs
- **Dutch Influence**: Increasing NGT presence since 2010
- **Small Network**: All deaf signers typically know each other
- **Oral Influence**: Many deaf individuals also use spoken language

### Deaf Community
Bonaire's tiny deaf community faces unique challenges:
- Limited peer interaction opportunities
- Many deaf individuals may be the only signer in their family
- Strong integration with hearing community due to size
- Close relationships across the small island

### Education
- **Limited Local Services**: Small population limits specialized education
- **Travel for Education**: Some students go to Curaçao or Netherlands
- **Mainstreaming**: Most deaf students in regular schools
- **NGT Introduction**: Dutch Sign Language taught in some contexts

### Legal Status
Since becoming a special municipality of the Netherlands in 2010, Bonaire falls under Dutch law. The Netherlands has recognized Dutch Sign Language (NGT), which affects language policy in Bonaire, potentially threatening the indigenous sign variety.

### Challenges
1. Extremely small user base
2. No formal documentation
3. Pressure from NGT and ASL
4. Limited educational resources
5. Geographic isolation
6. Risk of complete language shift

### Conservation Needs
- Urgent documentation required
- Recording of elderly signers
- Comparison with Curaçao and Aruba varieties
- Development of local teaching materials
