# Marthas Vineyard Sign Language (MVSL)
## Chilmark Sign Language

### Overview
| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Other Names** | Chilmark Sign Language |
| **Country** | USA (Massachusetts) |
| **Location** | Marthas Vineyard Island |
| **Language Family** | Possibly Old Kent Sign Language descendant |
| **Status** | Extinct (1952) |
| **Peak Period** | Early 18th century to 1952 |

### Language Description
- Village sign language unique to Marthas Vineyard
- Used by both deaf AND hearing residents
- Influenced development of American Sign Language (ASL)
- Full integration of deaf people into community
- Two-handed manual alphabet (unlike ASL one-handed)
- No grammatical markers for gender or plural
- Facial expressions, lip movements, head tilts key features
- Island-specific vocabulary (scallop dredging, codfish, locations)

### Historical Timeline
- **1640**: Island founded
- **1694**: Jonathan Lambert (first known deaf settler) arrives
- **1710**: Migration virtually ceased, endogamous community formed
- **Early 1800s**: Distinct Chilmark Sign Language emerged
- **1817**: American School for the Deaf founded in Hartford
- **1825**: Marthas Vineyard students begin enrolling at ASD
- **1854**: Deaf population peaked (1 in 155 on island)
- **1950**: Last fluent MVSL speaker passed away
- **1952**: Katie West (last deaf person born into tradition) died
- **1980s**: Researchers began documenting with elderly residents
- **2013**: James Woodward, linguistics team continued research

### Origins
- Ancestry traced to Weald region in Kent, England
- Possibly descended from hypothesized Old Kent Sign Language (16th c.)
- Puritan families emigrated to Massachusetts Bay Colony
- Descendants settled on Marthas Vineyard
- Possible influence from Eastern Woodlands Algonquian sign language
- Wampanoag interactions may have contributed

### Key Figures
- **Jonathan Lambert**: First deaf settler (1694)
- **Katie West**: Last deaf person born into tradition (d. 1952)
- **Jared Mayhew**: Deaf landowner, founded islands first bank
- **Nora Ellen Groce**: Author of "Everyone Here Spoke Sign Language"
- **Joan Poole Nash**: Compiled ~500 MVSL signs
- **Lynn Thorp**: Modern ASL revival advocate on island

### Community Integration (Deaf Utopia)
- Deafness NOT considered handicap or disability
- Deaf people seen as "people first"
- Full participation in civic, social, religious life
- Town meetings conducted in MVSL (Chilmark)
- Deaf residents were fishermen, farmers, business owners
- Children learned MVSL as natural life skill
- Signing used even when no deaf present
- Nearly 100% of Chilmark could sign

### Influence on ASL
- Marthas Vineyard students attended American School for the Deaf
- MVSL blended with French Sign Language and other regional signs
- Contributed vocabulary and grammatical structures to ASL
- Considered direct ancestor of modern ASL
- ASL now used by over 10 million Americans

### Decline Factors
- American School for the Deaf drew students to mainland
- Improved transportation to island (19th century)
- Tourism replaced fishing/farming as main economy
- Tourism jobs less deaf-friendly
- Intermarriage with mainlanders
- Migration diluted deaf gene pool
- Students returned from ASD with ASL

### Research and Documentation
- **1980s**: Researchers interviewed elderly residents
- Limited video recordings exist
- Joan Poole Nash collected ~500 signs from grandmother
- Two-handed alphabet documented
- Chilmark Free Public Library maintains archives
- James Woodward (linguistics expert) contributed

### Key Publication
**Nora Ellen Groce**: "Everyone Here Spoke Sign Language: Hereditary Deafness on Marthas Vineyard" (1985)
- Groundbreaking anthropological study
- First identified MVSL as village sign language
- Documented community life and language use

### Modern Revival Efforts
- **Lynn Thorp**: Teaching ASL on island since early 2000s
- Edgartown Elementary incorporated ASL into curriculum
- Martha Vineyard Museum preserves some history
- Limited MVSL reconstruction possible due to extinction

### Notable Visitors/Interest
- **Judge Samuel Sewall** (Salem witch trials): Met early deaf residents
- **John Adams**: Met deaf islanders
- **Alexander Graham Bell**: Visited 1885, interviewed Chilmark family
- **Thomas Hart Benton**: Painted two portraits of deaf islanders

### Linguistic Features
- Two-handed manual alphabet
- No fingerspelling in traditional form
- Strong facial expression component
- Island-specific vocabulary
- Distinct from ASL grammatically

### Legacy
- Foundation for American Sign Language
- Model for village sign language studies
- Example of successful deaf community integration
- Inspired research on other village sign languages globally
