# Moroccan Sign Language / Langue des signes marocaine (LSM)

## Overview
| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Official Name** | Moroccan Sign Language |
| **Native Name** | Langue des signes du Maroc / LSM |
| **ISO 639-3** | xms |
| **Region** | Morocco |
| **Users** | 10,000 - 1,000,000 (estimate) |
| **Language Family** | ASL-derived (with indigenous influence) |
| **Legal Status** | Recognized in 1994 (national legislation) |

## Historical Development

### Pre-ASL Era
- Indigenous sign systems existed in local communities
- Home signs used by deaf individuals and families

### Peace Corps Introduction (1987)
- American Peace Corps volunteers arrived in Tetouan
- Introduced American Sign Language
- Mixed with existing local signs
- Less than 50% lexical similarity with ASL

### Modern Development
- Influenced by French Sign Language (LSF)
- Regional variations across cities
- USAID MITTA program (since 2021) improving deaf education

## Regional Variations
- Different dialects in different cities (like spoken Moroccan Arabic)
- Oujda region influenced by Algerian Sign Language
- Tetouan region has strong ASL influence

## Organizations and Education

### Key Institutions
- al-Fath Association for Deaf Children (Meknes, 1948)
- Lalla Asmaa Foundation (Rabat, 1972)
- Achourouk School (Ouarzazate, 1979)

### Current Challenges
- 85% of deaf children do not attend school
- Limited resources and trained staff
- Deaf community largely self-teaches via videos/internet

## Documentation
- Signs of Morocco (Wismann and Walsh, 1987)
- Digital Moroccan Sign Language STEM Thesaurus (2016)
- USAID MITTA training materials (2021-present)

## Cultural Significance
- MSL Master Trainers program developed
- Various influences from France, USA, Saudi Arabia, Qatar
- Ongoing standardization efforts
