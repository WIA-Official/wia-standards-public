# Navajo Family Sign
## Indigenous Deaf Sign Language of Navajo Clan

### Overview
| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Language Type** | Village/family sign language |
| **Country** | USA (Arizona, New Mexico, Utah) |
| **Community** | Navajo Nation (Diné) |
| **Language Family** | Unrelated to Plains Indian Sign Language |
| **Status** | Small community use |

### Language Description
- Sign language used by small deaf community of Navajo People
- Developed within a Navajo clan with large number of deaf members
- More linguistically enriched than typical home sign systems
- Natural development within family/clan structure
- Used by both deaf and hearing clan members
- Functions as primary sign language for some users

### Geographic Context
**Navajo Nation** (Dinétah):
- Largest Native American reservation in USA
- Spans Arizona, New Mexico, Utah
- Population: ~170,000 Navajo speakers (2011)
- Four Sacred Mountains define traditional boundaries:
  - Blanca Peak (Sisnaajiní) - Colorado
  - Mount Taylor (Tsoodził) - New Mexico
  - San Francisco Peaks (Dookʼoʼoosłííd) - Arizona
  - Hesperus Mountain (Dibé Nitsaa) - Colorado

### Research Documentation
**Davis & Supalla (1995)**:
- Primary research study
- A Sociolinguistic Description of Sign Language Use in a Navajo Family
- Found language more enriched than general home sign systems
- Documented varying proficiency levels among clan members

**Key Findings**:
- Signing used with or without speech
- Functions as primary sign language for some users
- Range from basic to fluent proficiency
- Unique compared to typical isolated deaf home signs

### Family Structure
Research documented one Navajo family:
- 11 siblings total
- 6 out of 11 siblings deaf or hard of hearing
- Natural sign language acquisition
- Both deaf and hearing members sign

### Relationship to Other Sign Languages
**Unrelated to**:
- Plains Indian Sign Language (Hand Talk)
- Southwest Hand Talk
- American Sign Language

**Context**:
- Southwest region also has Southwest Hand Talk (Navajo, Hopi, Apache, Pueblo)
- Keresan Sign Language also exists separately among Keres people
- Multiple sign language traditions in Southwest

### Navajo Deaf Community Context
**Colin Denny** (Notable Navajo Deaf person):
- Born in Shiprock, New Mexico
- Grew up in Pinon, Arizona
- Parents both teach Navajo language
- 2023 Super Bowl sign language performer
- Research assistant, University of Arizona
- Works with Dr. Melanie McKay-Cody

**Challenges on Navajo Nation**:
- Limited certified sign language interpreters
- Public schools lack accommodations
- Geographic isolation of deaf individuals
- Arizona State Schools for the Deaf alternative

### Southwest Indigenous Sign Languages
Dr. Melanie McKay-Cody's classification includes:
- **Southwest Hand Talk**: Navajo, Hopi, Apache, Pueblo peoples
- **Navajo Family Sign**: Unrelated family sign
- **Keresan Sign Language**: Keres people, also unrelated

### Navajo Cultural Context
- **Diné**: The People (Navajo self-designation)
- **Spoken Language**: Navajo (Diné bizaad) - Southern Athabascan
- Strong language preservation efforts
- Navajo code talkers in WWII
- Traditional clan system important
