# Batch 216: Ghana Sign Languages - Complete Overview

## Sign Languages in Ghana

Ghana has **three distinct sign languages**:

| Language | Type | Location | Users |
|----------|------|----------|-------|
| Ghanaian SL (GhSL) | National | Nationwide | ~6,000+ |
| Adamorobe SL (AdaSL) | Village | Adamorobe, Eastern Region | ~1,400 community |
| Nanabin SL (NanaSL) | Family | Nanabin, Central Region | ~26 family members |

---

## 1. Ghanaian Sign Language (GhSL)

### Overview
| Item | Content |
|------|---------|
| **Status** | Developing national sign language |
| **Users** | ~6,000 (Ethnologue 2019) |
| **Education** | Used in deaf schools |
| **Recognition** | Not yet official language |

### History
- Andrew Foster established deaf education in Ghana
- Foster was deaf African-American missionary
- Established 31 schools across Africa
- ASL influence introduced through schools

### Characteristics
- Strong resemblance to American Sign Language
- Influence of Signed English
- Regional variations exist
- Ongoing ASL influence continues

### Institutional Use
- Deaf education (schools)
- Ghana National Association of the Deaf (GNAD, est. 1968)
- University programs (University of Ghana, UEW)
- Religious services
- Some TV interpretation

### Legal Status
- Persons with Disability Act (Act 715, 2006)
- Recognizes sign language teaching in colleges
- GhSL NOT officially recognized as national language
- No specific legislation for interpreting

## 2. Adamorobe Sign Language (AdaSL)

*(Already documented in Batch 201, summary here)*

### Overview
| Item | Content |
|------|---------|
| **Local Name** | Mumu kasa (deaf language) |
| **Location** | Adamorobe, Eastern Region |
| **Age** | 200+ years |
| **Users** | ~30 deaf, ~1,370 hearing |
| **Status** | ENDANGERED |

### Key Features
- One of oldest documented village SLs
- Both deaf and hearing use it
- Independent of Ghanaian SL
- Influenced by Akan culture

### Current Situation
- Bilingualism with GhSL increasing
- Children attend Mampong school (GhSL)
- Weekly church service in GhSL
- Gradual language shift occurring

### Documentation
- Victoria Nyst (2007) PhD dissertation
- MPI corpus (36+ hours)
- Well-researched linguistically

## 3. Nanabin Sign Language (NanaSL)

### Overview
| Item | Content |
|------|---------|
| **Type** | Family Sign Language |
| **Location** | Ekumfi Nanabin, Central Region |
| **Distance** | ~8 km east of Mankessim |
| **Users** | ~26 family members |
| **Generations** | 3 generations |
| **Status** | CRITICALLY ENDANGERED |

### Community Profile
- Coastal Fante village
- Single family with hereditary deafness
- Family "mostly deaf"
- Small user population

### Linguistic Features
- Similar to AdaSL in some signs
- Shared Akan hearing culture conventions
- Lax handshapes (like AdaSL)
- Character perspective (not observer)

### Bilingualism
- Second generation bilingual in GhSL
- First generation monolingual NanaSL
- Third generation learning both

### Documentation Status
- ~2 hours of spontaneous signing recorded
- Part of AdaSL corpus
- Victoria Nyst (2010) documentation
- Limited research otherwise

### Classification Debate
- Too few users for "village sign"?
- Too many for "home sign"
- Classified as village sign language
- Used by "group of Deaf people"

## Similarities: AdaSL and NanaSL

Both village/family sign languages share:
1. Akan cultural influence
2. Lax handshapes
3. Character perspective in signing
4. Independence from GhSL
5. Hearing community members sign
6. Endangered by GhSL contact

### Why Similar?
- Same cultural region (Akan)
- Shared gestural heritage
- Independent development from same base
- NOT descended from each other

## Language Contact Situation

### GhSL Influence on Local SLs
- Schools use GhSL
- Religious services in GhSL
- External deaf community contact
- Media and social media

### Results of Contact
- Younger generation bilingual
- Code-switching observed
- Lexical borrowing from GhSL
- Gradual shift toward GhSL

### Preservation Challenges
- No official protection
- School-based GhSL dominance
- Small user populations
- Aging native signers

## Deaf Education in Ghana

### History
- First school: Osu, Accra (1957) by Andrew Foster
- 1959: Relocated to Mampong Akwapim
- ~20 schools for deaf (public and private)

### Current System
- University of Education, Winneba (UEW) admits deaf students
- University of Ghana has sign language program
- Interpreters available but limited
- Late diagnosis common (~20% unidentified)
