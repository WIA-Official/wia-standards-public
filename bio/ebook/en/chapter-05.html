Chapter 05

Quality Control

Comprehensive quality control frameworks, testing procedures, and validation protocols for biotechnology operations.

5.1 Quality Management System Overview

A robust Quality Management System (QMS) is essential for ensuring consistent quality in biotechnology operations. The WIA-BIO standard establishes QMS requirements that integrate with international quality standards including ISO 9001, ISO 17025, and GMP guidelines.

Quality Management Principles

The WIA-BIO quality framework is built on fundamental principles: customer focus, leadership commitment, process approach, evidence-based decision making, and continuous improvement. These principles guide all quality-related activities.

QMS Components

  • Quality Policy: Organizational commitment to quality objectives
  • Quality Manual: Documentation of the QMS structure and procedures
  • Standard Operating Procedures: Detailed procedures for all critical activities
  • Work Instructions: Step-by-step guides for specific tasks
  • Records: Evidence of activities performed and results achieved
  • Training Program: Ensuring personnel competency
  • Audit Program: Internal and external quality assessments

5.2 Testing Procedures

Biotechnology products require comprehensive testing to verify identity, purity, potency, and safety. Testing procedures must be validated and performed according to established protocols.

Identity Testing

Identity Verification Methods

Identity testing confirms that the product is what it is claimed to be. Methods include molecular, biochemical, and immunological techniques.

  • DNA sequencing and fingerprinting
  • Restriction enzyme analysis
  • PCR-based identification
  • Protein electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE)
  • Mass spectrometry
  • Immunological assays (Western blot, ELISA)

Purity Testing

Purity Assessment Methods

Purity testing ensures that the product is free from contaminants and impurities within acceptable limits.

  • Endotoxin testing (LAL assay)
  • Sterility testing
  • Mycoplasma testing
  • Host cell protein (HCP) assays
  • Residual DNA testing
  • Aggregation analysis
Test Category Method Specification Frequency
Sterility USP <71> No growth Every batch
Endotoxin LAL (kinetic) <5 EU/mg Every batch
Mycoplasma PCR or culture Negative Cell banks, final product
Host Cell Protein ELISA <100 ppm Every batch
Residual DNA qPCR <10 ng/dose Every batch

5.3 Potency Assays

Potency assays measure the biological activity of biotechnology products. These assays are critical for ensuring that products will perform their intended function.

Potency Testing Importance

Potency is the specific ability of a product to achieve a defined biological effect. For biological products, potency cannot be adequately assessed by physico-chemical methods alone and requires functional biological assays.

Potency Assay Development Framework
===================================

Phase 1: Assay Design
├── Define biological activity to measure
├── Select appropriate assay format
│   ├── Cell-based assays
│   ├── Binding assays
│   ├── Enzymatic assays
│   └── Animal models (if necessary)
├── Establish reference standard
└── Define acceptance criteria

Phase 2: Assay Optimization
├── Identify critical parameters
├── Optimize assay conditions
├── Develop standard curve approach
├── Establish dilution scheme
└── Define sample preparation method

Phase 3: Assay Qualification
├── Accuracy (% recovery)
├── Precision (repeatability, intermediate)
├── Specificity (matrix effects)
├── Linearity and range
├── Robustness
└── Stability indicating capability

Phase 4: Assay Validation
├── Full validation protocol
├── Pre-defined acceptance criteria
├── Formal validation report
├── Transfer protocol if applicable
└── Ongoing stability monitoring

Phase 5: Routine Use
├── Qualified personnel
├── Qualified equipment
├── Reference standard management
├── Trend analysis
└── Periodic revalidation

5.4 Validation Protocols

Validation demonstrates that processes, methods, and systems consistently produce results meeting predetermined specifications. WIA-BIO requires validation of all critical processes and analytical methods.

Process Validation

  • Stage 1 - Process Design: Developing process understanding based on knowledge gained through development and scale-up activities
  • Stage 2 - Process Qualification: Confirming that the process design is capable of reproducible commercial manufacturing
  • Stage 3 - Continued Process Verification: Ongoing assurance that the process remains in a state of control

Analytical Method Validation

Parameter Definition Acceptance Criteria
Accuracy Closeness of measured value to true value 80-120% recovery
Precision Degree of agreement between measurements %CV ≤15% (≤20% at LLOQ)
Specificity Ability to measure analyte in presence of other components No interference
Linearity Proportional relationship within range R² ≥0.99
Range Interval of suitable accuracy and precision Defined per method
Robustness Capacity to remain unaffected by small variations No significant change

5.5 Stability Testing

Stability testing evaluates how the quality of a product changes over time under the influence of environmental factors. Results are used to establish shelf life and storage conditions.

Stability Study Design
======================

Study Types:
├── Accelerated Stability
│   ├── Purpose: Predict long-term stability
│   ├── Conditions: Elevated stress (40°C/75% RH)
│   ├── Duration: 6 months minimum
│   └── Sampling: 0, 1, 2, 3, 6 months
│
├── Long-term Stability
│   ├── Purpose: Confirm shelf life
│   ├── Conditions: Intended storage (e.g., 5°C, 25°C)
│   ├── Duration: Through expiration + 1 year
│   └── Sampling: 0, 3, 6, 9, 12, 18, 24... months
│
├── Photostability
│   ├── Purpose: Evaluate light sensitivity
│   ├── Conditions: ICH Q1B guidelines
│   └── Samples: Drug substance and product
│
└── In-use Stability
    ├── Purpose: Evaluate after opening/reconstitution
    ├── Conditions: Simulated use conditions
    └── Duration: Based on use instructions

Stability Indicating Tests:
├── Appearance
├── Identity
├── Purity/Impurities
├── Potency
├── pH
├── Particulates
└── Container closure integrity

5.6 Equipment Qualification

All equipment used in critical operations must be qualified to demonstrate fitness for intended use. Qualification follows a structured approach from design through ongoing performance verification.

Qualification Phases

  • Design Qualification (DQ): Documented verification that the proposed design is suitable for intended purpose
  • Installation Qualification (IQ): Documented verification that equipment is installed correctly according to specifications
  • Operational Qualification (OQ): Documented verification that equipment operates correctly throughout all anticipated operating ranges
  • Performance Qualification (PQ): Documented verification that equipment consistently performs according to specifications under routine conditions
Equipment Qualification Checklist
=================================

Installation Qualification (IQ):
□ Equipment received per purchase specification
□ All components present and undamaged
□ Installation per manufacturer instructions
□ Utilities verified (power, water, air)
□ Environmental conditions verified
□ Software/firmware version documented
□ Calibration status of measuring devices
□ Documentation package complete

Operational Qualification (OQ):
□ All operating parameters tested
□ Safety features verified
□ Alarm functions tested
□ Control system functions verified
□ Performance within specifications
□ Calibration verified across range
□ User training documented

Performance Qualification (PQ):
□ Process-specific testing completed
□ Reproducibility demonstrated
□ Acceptance criteria met
□ Documentation reviewed and approved
□ Change control system implemented
□ Preventive maintenance scheduled

5.7 CAPA System

The Corrective and Preventive Action (CAPA) system is essential for identifying, investigating, and resolving quality issues. An effective CAPA system drives continuous improvement.

CAPA Process

CAPA involves systematic investigation of root causes, implementation of corrective actions to address existing problems, and preventive actions to prevent potential problems from occurring.

CAPA Workflow
=============

1. Issue Identification
   └── Sources: Deviations, complaints, audits, trends

2. Initial Assessment
   ├── Document the issue
   ├── Assess severity and impact
   ├── Determine if CAPA warranted
   └── Assign ownership

3. Investigation
   ├── Gather all relevant data
   ├── Conduct root cause analysis
   │   ├── 5 Whys
   │   ├── Fishbone diagram
   │   └── Failure mode analysis
   └── Document findings

4. Action Planning
   ├── Identify corrective actions
   ├── Identify preventive actions
   ├── Define responsibilities
   └── Set completion dates

5. Implementation
   ├── Execute planned actions
   ├── Document completion
   └── Train personnel as needed

6. Effectiveness Verification
   ├── Define verification criteria
   ├── Allow sufficient time
   ├── Verify issue resolved
   └── Confirm no recurrence

7. Closure
   ├── Review all documentation
   ├── Approve closure
   └── Update relevant procedures
