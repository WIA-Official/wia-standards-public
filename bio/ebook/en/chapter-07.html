Chapter 07

Ethics and Governance

Ethical frameworks, institutional oversight mechanisms, and responsible governance principles for biotechnology research and applications.

7.1 Ethical Foundations

Biotechnology raises profound ethical questions that require careful consideration. The WIA-BIO standard establishes ethical principles to guide responsible biotechnology practice while promoting innovation that benefits humanity.

WIA-BIO Core Ethical Principle

弘益人間 (Hongik Ingan) - "Benefit All Humanity"

This ancient Korean philosophy guides all WIA-BIO ethical considerations. Biotechnology must serve the greater good of humanity while respecting individual rights, protecting vulnerable populations, and preserving environmental integrity for future generations.

Foundational Ethical Principles

Respect for Persons

Individuals must be treated as autonomous agents with the right to make informed decisions about their participation in research. Those with diminished autonomy deserve additional protections.

Beneficence

Research must maximize potential benefits while minimizing risks of harm. This requires careful risk-benefit analysis and ongoing safety monitoring throughout the research process.

Justice

The benefits and burdens of research must be distributed fairly. No group should bear disproportionate risks, and the benefits of biotechnology should be accessible to all who might benefit.

Non-Maleficence

Above all, do no harm. This principle requires proactive identification and mitigation of potential harms before they occur, not merely responding to harms after the fact.

7.2 Informed Consent

Informed consent is the cornerstone of ethical research involving human subjects. It ensures that participants understand and voluntarily agree to participate in research activities.

Informed Consent Requirements
=============================

Essential Elements of Consent:

1. Disclosure
   ├── Purpose of the research
   ├── Expected duration of participation
   ├── Description of procedures
   ├── Identification of experimental procedures
   ├── Reasonably foreseeable risks
   ├── Potential benefits
   ├── Alternative treatments available
   └── Extent of confidentiality protections

2. Comprehension
   ├── Information presented in understandable language
   ├── Opportunity to ask questions
   ├── Assessment of understanding
   ├── Use of appropriate reading level
   └── Translation if needed

3. Voluntariness
   ├── No coercion or undue influence
   ├── Right to withdraw without penalty
   ├── No conditioning of treatment/benefits
   └── Adequate time for decision

4. Documentation
   ├── Written consent form
   ├── Copy provided to participant
   ├── Signature of participant
   ├── Signature of person obtaining consent
   └── Date of consent

Special Populations:
├── Children: Parent/guardian permission + assent
├── Cognitively impaired: Legally authorized representative
├── Prisoners: Additional IRB review, no undue influence
└── Pregnant women: Both maternal and fetal considerations

7.3 Institutional Review Boards

Institutional Review Boards (IRBs) or Ethics Committees provide independent oversight of research involving human subjects. IRBs ensure that research protocols protect participant rights and welfare.

IRB Functions

IRBs review research protocols before initiation and monitor ongoing research. They have authority to approve, require modifications to, or disapprove research activities based on ethical considerations.

IRB Review Categories

Category Criteria Review Process
Exempt Minimal risk, specific categories Administrative review
Expedited Minimal risk, approved categories Chair or designee review
Full Board Greater than minimal risk Convened meeting, majority vote

IRB Membership Requirements

  • Minimum of five members with varying backgrounds
  • At least one scientist and one non-scientist
  • At least one member unaffiliated with institution
  • Diversity in gender, race, and cultural backgrounds
  • Expertise relevant to research being reviewed

7.4 Animal Research Ethics

Research involving animals must adhere to ethical principles that minimize harm and ensure humane treatment. The 3Rs principle (Replace, Reduce, Refine) guides ethical animal research.

The 3Rs Framework
=================

Replace:
├── Use non-animal alternatives when possible
│   ├── In vitro models
│   ├── Computer simulations
│   ├── Organoids and tissue models
│   └── Human volunteer studies
└── Justify necessity of animal use

Reduce:
├── Use minimum number of animals needed
│   ├── Appropriate statistical design
│   ├── Sharing of animals/tissues
│   └── Pilot studies before larger experiments
└── Document sample size calculations

Refine:
├── Minimize pain and distress
│   ├── Appropriate anesthesia/analgesia
│   ├── Humane endpoints
│   ├── Improved husbandry
│   └── Training in gentle handling
└── Enhance animal welfare

IACUC Oversight:
├── Protocol review before initiation
├── Regular inspections of animal facilities
├── Training verification for personnel
├── Monitoring of ongoing research
└── Authority to suspend activities

7.5 Genetic Research Ethics

Genetic research raises unique ethical considerations including genetic privacy, discrimination risks, and implications for family members. Special protections are required for genetic information.

Genetic Information Protection

Genetic information is uniquely sensitive because it is predictive, familial, and permanent. Special safeguards are required to protect privacy and prevent discrimination based on genetic characteristics.

Key Ethical Considerations

  • Privacy: Genetic information must be protected from unauthorized access
  • Non-discrimination: Genetic information should not be used for discriminatory purposes
  • Family implications: Genetic findings may have implications for relatives who did not consent
  • Right not to know: Individuals may choose not to learn certain genetic information
  • Incidental findings: Policies needed for handling unexpected findings
  • Data sharing: Balance research benefits against privacy concerns

Germline Modification Ethics

Germline genetic modification raises profound ethical questions due to its heritable nature. The WIA-BIO standard requires enhanced ethical review for any research involving heritable genetic changes.

Germline Modification Considerations
====================================

Ethical Concerns:
├── Changes are heritable (affect future generations)
├── Consent cannot be obtained from those affected
├── Long-term consequences uncertain
├── Potential for enhancement vs. therapy
└── Equitable access concerns

Current WIA-BIO Position:
├── Basic research: Permitted with oversight
├── Clinical application: Moratorium recommended
├── International consensus needed
└── Ongoing ethical dialogue required

Required Oversight:
├── Enhanced IRB/ethics committee review
├── National regulatory approval
├── Institutional biosafety review
├── Public engagement and transparency
└── International consultation

7.6 Dual Use Research

Some biotechnology research has potential for both beneficial and harmful applications. Dual Use Research of Concern (DURC) requires special oversight to prevent misuse while enabling legitimate scientific progress.

DURC Category Examples Required Oversight
Enhanced pathogenicity Gain-of-function studies Institutional and national review
Transmissibility Aerosol transmission studies Enhanced biosafety, restricted access
Immune evasion Vaccine resistance studies Risk-benefit analysis required
Toxin production Enhanced toxin expression Strict containment requirements

7.7 Governance Structures

Effective governance ensures that biotechnology activities are conducted responsibly. Governance structures should include institutional, national, and international oversight mechanisms.

Biotechnology Governance Framework
==================================

Institutional Level:
├── Institutional Biosafety Committee (IBC)
│   ├── Review of recombinant DNA research
│   ├── Biosafety protocol approval
│   └── Compliance monitoring
├── Institutional Review Board (IRB)
│   ├── Human subjects protection
│   ├── Protocol approval
│   └── Ongoing oversight
├── Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC)
│   ├── Animal welfare oversight
│   └── Protocol review and monitoring
└── Research Integrity Officer
    ├── Misconduct investigation
    └── Training and compliance

National Level:
├── Regulatory agencies (FDA, EMA, etc.)
├── Biosafety advisory committees
├── Ethics commissions
└── Funding agency oversight

International Level:
├── WHO advisory committees
├── International conventions (Cartagena Protocol)
├── Professional organization guidelines
└── Scientific community self-governance

WIA-BIO Governance:
├── Standards development committee
├── Certification body
├── Appeals process
└── Continuous improvement mechanism
