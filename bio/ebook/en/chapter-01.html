Chapter 01

Introduction to Biotechnology

Understanding the foundational principles, historical context, and modern applications of biotechnology within the WIA-BIO framework.

1.1 What is Biotechnology?

Biotechnology is the broad area of science involving living systems and organisms to develop or make products, or any technological application that uses biological systems, living organisms, or derivatives thereof to make or modify products or processes for specific use. The WIA-BIO standard provides comprehensive guidelines for the safe and effective practice of biotechnology across all sectors.

WIA-BIO Definition

Biotechnology encompasses all techniques using living organisms or their components to produce industrial, agricultural, medical, and environmental applications that benefit humanity while maintaining strict safety and ethical standards.

The field of biotechnology extends across multiple disciplines including molecular biology, genetics, biochemistry, microbiology, and bioengineering. Each discipline contributes unique methodologies and perspectives that together form the comprehensive approach required for modern biotechnology practices.

Core Disciplines within Biotechnology

  • Red Biotechnology: Medical and pharmaceutical applications including drug development, gene therapy, and diagnostic tools
  • Green Biotechnology: Agricultural applications including crop improvement, biopesticides, and sustainable farming practices
  • White Biotechnology: Industrial applications including biofuels, enzymes, and biomaterials production
  • Blue Biotechnology: Marine and aquatic applications including aquaculture and ocean-derived compounds
  • Yellow Biotechnology: Environmental applications including bioremediation and waste treatment

1.2 Historical Development

The history of biotechnology spans thousands of years, from ancient fermentation practices to modern genetic engineering. Understanding this evolution provides context for current standards and helps anticipate future developments.

6000 BCE

Early humans use fermentation to produce beer and wine, representing the first practical application of biotechnology in human history.

1865

Gregor Mendel establishes the fundamental laws of heredity through his work with pea plants, laying the groundwork for modern genetics.

1953

Watson and Crick discover the double helix structure of DNA, revolutionizing our understanding of genetic information storage and transmission.

1973

Cohen and Boyer develop recombinant DNA technology, enabling the precise manipulation of genetic material across species boundaries.

1982

FDA approves first recombinant DNA drug (human insulin), marking the beginning of the modern biotechnology pharmaceutical industry.

2012

CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing technology is developed, providing unprecedented precision in genetic modification capabilities.

2020

mRNA vaccines demonstrate biotechnology's potential during the global health crisis, with development timelines compressed from years to months.

1.4 Key Principles

The WIA-BIO standard is built upon fundamental principles that guide all biotechnology activities. These principles ensure that biotechnology advances benefit humanity while minimizing risks.

Principle 1: Safety First

All biotechnology activities must prioritize the safety of personnel, the public, and the environment. Risk assessments must be conducted before initiating any work, and appropriate containment measures must be implemented.

Principle 2: Scientific Integrity

Research and development activities must adhere to the highest standards of scientific rigor. Results must be reproducible, and data must be accurately recorded and reported.

Principle 3: Ethical Responsibility

Biotechnology practitioners have an ethical obligation to consider the societal implications of their work. Research must be conducted with respect for human dignity and animal welfare.

Principle 4: Environmental Stewardship

Activities must be conducted with consideration for environmental impact. Containment of biological materials and proper waste disposal are essential requirements.

WIA Philosophy in Biotechnology

弘益人間 (Hongik Ingan) - The principle of "Benefit All Humanity" is central to WIA-BIO. Every standard and guideline is developed with the goal of ensuring that biotechnology advances serve the greater good of humanity while protecting individual rights and environmental health.

1.5 Biotechnology Applications

Modern biotechnology has transformed numerous industries and continues to expand into new application areas. Understanding these applications helps organizations identify relevant WIA-BIO requirements for their specific activities.

Healthcare Applications

  • Recombinant protein therapeutics including insulin, growth hormones, and blood factors
  • Monoclonal antibodies for cancer treatment and autoimmune diseases
  • Gene therapies for inherited genetic disorders
  • Cell-based therapies including CAR-T cells and stem cell treatments
  • Diagnostic tools including PCR-based tests and biomarker assays
  • Vaccine development using recombinant and mRNA technologies

Agricultural Applications

  • Genetically modified crops with improved nutritional profiles
  • Disease-resistant and drought-tolerant plant varieties
  • Biological pest control agents and biopesticides
  • Enhanced livestock breeding through genetic selection
  • Microbial soil enhancers for sustainable agriculture

1.6 Regulatory Landscape

Biotechnology operates within a complex regulatory framework that varies by jurisdiction and application area. WIA-BIO is designed to harmonize with major international regulatory systems while providing additional guidance for areas not fully addressed by existing regulations.

International Regulatory Alignment

WIA-BIO standards are developed in consultation with major regulatory bodies and align with guidelines from WHO, FDA, EMA, and other international health and safety organizations. Compliance with WIA-BIO facilitates regulatory approval processes in multiple jurisdictions.

Key Regulatory Frameworks

  • Cartagena Protocol: International agreement on biosafety for living modified organisms
  • FDA Regulations: US requirements for biologics, drugs, and medical devices
  • EMA Guidelines: European standards for biotechnology products
  • ICH Guidelines: Harmonized pharmaceutical development standards
  • WHO Biosafety Manual: International laboratory biosafety standards
