CHAPTER 06

Crop Production

6.1 Crop Selection for Hydroponic Systems

Not all crops are equally suited to hydroponic production. The most successful hydroponic crops share characteristics: short production cycles (rapid turnover increases profitability), high value per unit area (justifies infrastructure investment), consistent market demand (ensures sales), and adaptability to controlled environments. The WIA-AGRI-027 standard provides crop-specific guidelines for production parameters, system selection, and quality optimization.

6.2 Leafy Greens and Herbs

Leafy greens—lettuce, spinach, kale, arugula—and culinary herbs—basil, cilantro, parsley, mint—are ideal for hydroponic production and dominate the commercial industry.

Why Leafy Greens Excel in Hydroponics

Short production cycles 4-6 weeks from seed to harvest for lettuce, 6-8 weeks for herbs, enabling rapid turnover and cash flow. High density production 20-30 plants per square meter in NFT systems, 15-20 in DWC rafts. Year-round production constant demand and short cycles enable continuous weekly harvests. Low light requirements 200-400 μmol/m²/s PPFD compared to 600+ for fruiting crops, reducing energy costs. Simple system requirements shallow-rooted crops work well in NFT, DWC, and other simple systems.

Lettuce Production

Lettuce is the most widely grown hydroponic crop globally. Butterhead (Bibb, Boston) varieties dominate due to tender texture, mild flavor, and attractive appearance. Romaine offers good yields and shelf life. Oakleaf provides visual variety. System recommendations NFT systems for maximum space efficiency and yield or DWC rafts for commercial scale and reliability. Environmental conditions Temperature 18-24°C (avoid heat stress and tip burn), Humidity 60-70% (prevents tip burn caused by calcium deficiency), Light 250-350 μmol/m²/s PPFD for 14-16 hours daily.

Basil Production

Sweet basil is the most economically important hydroponic herb—high value, strong demand from restaurants and consumers, excellent flavor in hydroponic production. Production cycle 6-8 weeks from seed to first harvest, multiple harvests possible with cut-and-come-again technique. Environmental conditions Temperature 22-26°C (warm-season crop), Light 300-400 μmol/m²/s for optimal yield and essential oil production, Humidity 60-65% to prevent disease while supporting growth. System recommendations NFT for commercial production efficiency or Dutch bucket drip systems for larger plants and extended production cycles.

6.3 Fruiting Vegetables

Tomatoes, peppers, cucumbers, and strawberries represent the high-value segment of hydroponic production. These crops require longer production cycles, larger systems, more sophisticated environmental control, but command premium prices and strong market demand.

Tomato Production

Tomatoes are the second most valuable hydroponic crop after lettuce, particularly in greenhouse production. Indeterminate varieties (vining, continuous production) preferred for extended harvest seasons and maximum yield potential. Popular varieties include Beefsteak types for large fruit size, Cluster tomatoes for retail appeal and flavor, Cherry tomatoes for specialty markets and high price points, and Heirloom varieties for premium markets demanding unique flavors and colors.

System requirements High-wire systems growing plants vertically to 2-4 meters height, maximizing space utilization. Dutch bucket or NFT slab systems providing adequate root volume and stability. Support structures (strings, wires, clips) for vine support. Environmental conditions Temperature 22-26°C day, 18-20°C night for balanced vegetative and reproductive growth. Higher night temperatures promote vegetative growth (useful for young plants); lower temperatures favor fruit set and quality. Light 600-800 μmol/m²/s minimum, supplemental lighting often necessary in winter greenhouse production. CO2 enrichment to 800-1000 ppm significantly increases yield in well-lit conditions.

Pepper Production

Bell peppers and specialty peppers (jalapeño, habanero, sweet mini peppers) offer excellent returns in hydroponic systems. Production cycles 12-16 weeks from transplant to first harvest, extended harvests over 9-12 months with proper management. System selection Dutch buckets preferred for long production cycle and large plant size, allowing adequate root volume. NFT slabs or drip systems on rockwool slabs also successful. Environmental conditions Temperature 22-28°C day, 18-22°C night. Peppers tolerate warmer conditions than tomatoes. Light 500-700 μmol/m²/s for good fruit set and color development. Nutrient management Higher potassium during fruiting for fruit quality and color. Calcium essential for preventing blossom-end rot.

6.4 Growth Stages and Management

All crops progress through distinct growth stages requiring specific environmental conditions, nutrient formulations, and management practices. The WIA-AGRI-027 standard details stage-specific protocols optimizing development at each phase.

Seedling Stage

Germination and early development require gentle conditions: Lower light intensity 100-200 μmol/m²/s prevents stretching without stressing young seedlings. Higher humidity 70-80% RH reduces transpiration stress. Lower EC 0.8-1.2 mS/cm prevents salt stress in developing roots. Moderate temperature 20-24°C for most crops promotes healthy, compact growth. Duration 7-14 days until first true leaves develop and plants are ready for transplanting.

Vegetative Stage

Rapid leaf and stem development builds plant structure for future production: Increased light 200-400 μmol/m²/s for leafy greens, 400-600 for fruiting crops. Moderate humidity 60-70% RH balances growth and disease prevention. Moderate EC 1.2-2.0 mS/cm depending on crop supports vigorous growth. Nitrogen-rich nutrition promotes leaf and stem development. Duration Variable—2-4 weeks for greens before harvest, 4-8 weeks for fruiting crops before transitioning to reproductive stage.

Reproductive/Fruiting Stage

Flowering and fruit development require adjusted conditions favoring fruit production over vegetative growth: Full light intensity 600-800+ μmol/m²/s maximizes photosynthesis and fruit quality. Lower humidity 50-60% RH reduces disease pressure during fruit development. Higher EC 2.0-3.5 mS/cm (crop dependent) increases fruit quality and flavor. Adjusted nutrition higher potassium and phosphorus for fruit development, adequate calcium for quality. Temperature management slightly cooler nights promote fruit set in many crops. Duration 8-20 weeks for fruiting crops depending on variety and management strategy.

6.5 Integrated Pest Management (IPM)

While controlled-environment hydroponics significantly reduces pest pressure compared to field agriculture, vigilant monitoring and proactive management are essential for preventing problems before they impact production.

Prevention Strategies

Exclusion physical barriers (screens, positive air pressure, airlocks) prevent pest entry. Sanitation cleaning between crops, removing plant debris, disinfecting systems and tools eliminates pathogen reservoirs. Environmental control proper temperature, humidity, and air circulation reduces conditions favoring pests and diseases. Plant health vigorous, well-nourished plants resist pests better than stressed plants.

Monitoring and Early Detection

Regular scouting weekly inspection of plants for pest presence, damage symptoms, or disease signs. Sticky traps yellow or blue cards coated with adhesive capture flying insects for monitoring population trends. Predictive models using environmental data to forecast disease pressure and time preventive treatments.

Biological Control

Beneficial insects predatory mites, parasitic wasps, ladybugs control aphids, whiteflies, thrips, spider mites. Microbial biocontrols Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt) for caterpillars, Beauveria bassiana for various insects, Trichoderma fungi for root disease suppression. The WIA-AGRI-027 standard recommends biological controls as first-line defense, reserving chemical pesticides for situations where biocontrols are insufficient.

6.6 Harvesting and Post-Harvest Handling

Proper harvest timing and handling preserve quality, extend shelf life, and command premium prices.

Harvest Timing

Leafy greens harvest when plants reach marketable size, typically 4-6 weeks after seeding. Morning harvest preferred when plants are most turgid. Herbs cut when plants have adequate foliage but before flowering for best flavor. Fruiting crops harvest at optimal maturity—color development, firmness, sugar content vary by crop and variety.

Post-Harvest Handling

Rapid cooling immediately reduce temperature to slow respiration and water loss. Target 1-4°C for most crops. High humidity maintain 90-95% RH during storage to prevent wilting. Gentle handling minimize bruising and damage that accelerates deterioration. Proper packaging protect product during transport while allowing gas exchange. Traceability lot codes and harvest dates enable tracking for food safety and quality management. The WIA-AGRI-027 standard specifies food safety protocols including water quality testing, pathogen monitoring, and traceability documentation ensuring products meet regulatory requirements and consumer expectations.

6.7 Conclusion

Successful hydroponic crop production combines crop selection suited to system capabilities and market demand, stage-appropriate environmental and nutritional management, proactive pest and disease prevention, and careful harvesting and post-harvest handling. The WIA-AGRI-027 standard provides comprehensive guidance enabling growers to produce consistent, high-quality crops efficiently and profitably.
