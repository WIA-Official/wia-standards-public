Chapter 05

Photoresists and Patterning Materials

EUV photoresists, metal oxide resists, dry resist technology, DSA materials, and advanced patterning chemistry

5.1 Introduction to Lithographic Patterning

Lithography is the process by which circuit patterns are transferred from a photomask to the wafer surface. Photoresists are light-sensitive polymer films that form the cornerstone of this patterning process. When exposed to the appropriate wavelength of light, photoresists undergo chemical changes that alter their solubility, enabling the formation of patterns after development. As semiconductor features have shrunk from micrometers to nanometers, photoresist technology has evolved dramatically to meet increasingly stringent requirements.

Modern lithography represents one of the most demanding applications of precision materials science. At the 2nm technology node, lithography must reliably produce features as small as 20nm pitch (10nm half-pitch), requiring photoresists that can resolve atomic-scale differences while maintaining process stability across thousands of wafers. This chapter explores the materials that enable cutting-edge lithographic patterning.

Resolution Fundamentals

Lithographic resolution is governed by the Rayleigh equation: R = k1 × λ/NA, where R is minimum feature size, λ is exposure wavelength, NA is numerical aperture, and k1 is a process factor. Reducing wavelength from 193nm (ArF) to 13.5nm (EUV) fundamentally improves resolution capability.

5.1.1 Photoresist Classification

Photoresists are classified by their response to light exposure:

  • Positive-tone: Exposed areas become soluble in developer, pattern matches mask
  • Negative-tone: Exposed areas become insoluble, pattern is inverse of mask
  • Chemically amplified (CAR): Acid generated by exposure catalyzes chemical changes
  • Non-chemically amplified: Direct photochemical reaction (traditional resists)

5.2 ArF Immersion Lithography

ArF (argon fluoride excimer laser) immersion lithography at 193nm wavelength has been the workhorse of advanced semiconductor manufacturing since 2006. By using water between the projection lens and wafer, the effective numerical aperture increases to 1.35 (vs 0.93 dry), enabling resolution down to approximately 38nm half-pitch with single exposure.

5.2.1 ArF Photoresist Chemistry

ArF photoresists are chemically amplified, using photoacid generators (PAGs) to initiate acid-catalyzed deprotection reactions. Key components include:

  • Polymer backbone: Methacrylate or cycloolefin-based polymers with acid-labile protecting groups
  • Photoacid generator (PAG): Onium salts (triphenylsulfonium triflate) that generate strong acid upon exposure
  • Quencher: Weak base that controls acid diffusion and improves resolution
  • Solvent: PGMEA, ethyl lactate, or similar for coating

5.2.2 Immersion-Specific Requirements

Immersion lithography places unique demands on photoresist materials:

  • Topcoat/hydrophobic layer: Prevents water from contacting resist, eliminates defects
  • Leaching control: Components must not leach into immersion fluid
  • Contact angle: High receding angle (>70°) prevents water marks
  • Water absorption: Minimal swelling to prevent pattern distortion

5.2.3 Multiple Patterning

Single-exposure ArF immersion cannot resolve features below ~38nm pitch. Multiple patterning techniques extend resolution:

  • LELE (Litho-Etch-Litho-Etch): Two separate exposures and etch steps
  • SADP (Self-Aligned Double Patterning): Sidewall spacer doubles feature density
  • SAQP (Self-Aligned Quadruple Patterning): Two spacer steps for 4x density

Multiple Patterning Complexity

SAQP requires up to 60 process steps for a single metal layer, compared to ~20 for single exposure. This complexity drives significant cost increase and underscores the value of EUV lithography which can achieve similar resolution in a single exposure.

5.3 EUV Photoresists

Extreme Ultraviolet (EUV) lithography at 13.5nm wavelength represents the most significant advance in lithography technology since the introduction of 193nm ArF. EUV enables single-exposure patterning at sub-40nm pitch, dramatically simplifying process flows compared to multiple patterning. However, EUV photoresists face unique challenges that require fundamentally new materials approaches.

5.3.1 EUV Exposure Mechanism

At 13.5nm wavelength, photon energy is 92 eV, far exceeding molecular bond energies. EUV exposure proceeds through a different mechanism than DUV:

  1. Photon absorption: High-energy photon absorbed by any atom in resist
  2. Photoelectron generation: Primary electron ejected with ~80 eV energy
  3. Secondary electrons: Cascade of low-energy secondary electrons (2-30 eV)
  4. Chemical events: Secondary electrons trigger chemical changes (acid generation, crosslinking)

5.3.2 The EUV Resist Trilemma

EUV photoresists face a fundamental three-way tradeoff between resolution, sensitivity, and line edge roughness (LER). Improving any one parameter typically degrades the others:

  • Resolution: Smaller features require less acid diffusion, thinner films
  • Sensitivity: Higher sensitivity (lower dose) means more shot noise, worse LER
  • LER: Smoother edges require higher dose, larger polymer aggregates

5.3.3 EUV CAR (Chemically Amplified Resists)

First-generation EUV resists are based on chemically amplified platforms similar to ArF, but optimized for EUV absorption and secondary electron chemistry:

  • High-absorption PAGs: Iodine-containing PAGs for increased EUV absorbance
  • Bulky quenchers: Larger quencher molecules to control acid blur
  • Optimized polymer: Smaller aggregates, controlled molecular weight distribution
  • Metal-containing additives: Increase EUV absorption while maintaining chemistry

5.4 Metal Oxide Resists (MOR)

Metal oxide resists (also called inorganic resists or metal-containing resists) represent a revolutionary approach to EUV lithography. By incorporating metal atoms into the resist structure, MORs achieve dramatically higher EUV absorption while potentially breaking the traditional resolution-sensitivity-LER trilemma.

5.4.1 MOR Chemistry and Mechanism

Metal oxide resists consist of metal-organic molecules or clusters that form dense oxide networks upon exposure. Common systems include:

  • Tin-based: Organotin compounds (Sn-C bonds) that form SnO2 network upon exposure
  • Zirconium-based: Zr-oxo clusters with organic ligands
  • Hafnium-based: Hf-oxo clusters, similar to Zr chemistry
  • Metal oxide nanoparticles: Pre-formed MO nanoparticles with organic coating

5.4.2 MOR Advantages

  • High EUV absorption: Metal atoms absorb EUV 3-10x more efficiently than organic materials
  • High etch resistance: Metal oxide provides excellent selectivity to underlying layers
  • Small "pixel" size: Molecular cluster reaction unit ~1nm, smaller than polymer aggregates
  • Lower sensitivity requirement: Higher absorption means fewer photons needed per feature

Industry Adoption

Metal oxide resists, particularly tin-based formulations from Inpria (now part of JSR), have been adopted for leading-edge EUV layers by major chipmakers. MOR enables sub-30nm pitch patterning with improved defectivity and process window compared to CAR.

5.4.4 MOR Challenges

  • Defectivity: Metal contamination concerns if resist not fully removed
  • Outgassing: Organic byproducts must be compatible with EUV vacuum
  • Line collapse: Thin films prone to pattern collapse at high aspect ratio
  • Developer chemistry: Often requires non-standard developers

5.5 Dry Resist Technology

Dry resist technology represents a paradigm shift in photoresist processing. Instead of spin-coating from liquid solution, dry resists are deposited in vacuum using chemical vapor deposition (CVD) or atomic layer deposition (ALD). This approach offers potential advantages in uniformity, defectivity, and resist performance.

5.5.1 Dry Resist Deposition

Dry resist deposition methods:

  • CVD: Vapor-phase precursors react on wafer surface to form resist film
  • ALD: Self-limiting surface reactions for atomic-level thickness control
  • Physical vapor deposition: Evaporation of organic materials in vacuum

5.5.2 Advantages of Dry Resist

  • Zero defects from coating: Eliminates particles, comets, bubbles from spin coating
  • Uniform thickness: Conformal deposition independent of underlying topography
  • Thin film capability: <20nm films feasible without uniformity issues
  • Reduced chemical waste: No resist solution waste, cleaner process
  • Novel chemistries: Enables materials not compatible with spin coating

5.5.3 Dry Resist Materials

Dry resist materials being developed include:

  • Organotin compounds: Alkyl tin halides (e.g., trimethyltin chloride) for MOR-like films
  • Metal-organic frameworks: Porous coordination polymers with controlled structure
  • Organometallic precursors: Volatile compounds deposited by thermal or plasma CVD

Lam Research Dry Resist

Lam Research has developed dry resist technology using tin-based precursors deposited by CVD. The process produces uniform, defect-free films with demonstrated resolution to sub-20nm pitch, representing a promising alternative to conventional spin-on resists.

5.6 Directed Self-Assembly (DSA)

Directed self-assembly uses the inherent tendency of block copolymers to phase-separate into regular nanoscale patterns. When guided by lithographically defined templates, DSA can produce features smaller than the lithographic resolution limit, offering a path to sub-10nm features.

5.6.1 Block Copolymer Self-Assembly

Block copolymers consist of two or more chemically distinct polymer blocks covalently linked together. When heated, the blocks microphase-separate into regular morphologies with characteristic dimensions determined by molecular weight:

  • Lamellar: Alternating layers, useful for line/space patterns
  • Cylindrical: One block forms cylinders in matrix of other block
  • Spherical: One block forms spheres in matrix
  • Gyroid: Complex bicontinuous network structure

5.6.2 DSA Guiding Methods

  • Chemoepitaxy: Chemical patterns on substrate direct BCP orientation
  • Graphoepitaxy: Physical topography (trenches) guides BCP alignment
  • Hybrid: Combination of chemical and topographical guidance

DSA challenges include:

  • Defectivity: DSA defects (dislocations, bridges) remain too high for HVM
  • Pattern placement: Registration to lithographic guides must be extremely precise
  • Pattern flexibility: DSA produces only periodic patterns, limiting design options
  • Integration: Additional process steps required for pattern transfer

5.7 Ancillary Patterning Materials

Beyond photoresists, numerous other materials are critical to the patterning process.

5.7.1 Bottom Anti-Reflective Coatings (BARC)

BARCs suppress reflections from underlying layers that would otherwise cause standing waves and notching in the photoresist. Key properties:

  • Optical properties: n and k tuned to minimize reflectivity at exposure wavelength
  • Etch selectivity: Must be removable after resist development
  • Chemical compatibility: No intermixing with photoresist

5.7.2 Hardmasks

Hardmasks provide etch selectivity that thin photoresists cannot. Common materials include:

  • Spin-on carbon (SOC): Planarizing layer, high etch selectivity vs oxide
  • Silicon-containing hardmasks: Si-BARC, SiON for oxygen etch selectivity
  • Metal hardmasks: TiN, WC for most demanding etch applications

5.7.3 Developers

Developers selectively dissolve exposed (positive tone) or unexposed (negative tone) resist:

  • TMAH (2.38%): Standard aqueous base developer for positive-tone CAR
  • Organic solvents: n-butyl acetate, 2-heptanone for negative-tone development
  • Metal-ion free: Critical to prevent metal contamination of devices

5.7.4 Edge Bead Removal (EBR) and Backside Rinse

Solvents used to remove resist from wafer edge and backside to prevent contamination and handling issues. Must be compatible with resist chemistry without affecting patterned areas.

5.8 Stochastic Effects in EUV

At EUV wavelengths, the statistical nature of photon absorption and subsequent chemical reactions becomes significant at small feature sizes. These "stochastic effects" represent fundamental challenges for EUV patterning.

5.8.1 Sources of Stochastics

  • Shot noise: Random variation in number of photons absorbed per feature
  • Secondary electron blur: Random range of secondary electrons from absorption site
  • Chemical stochastics: Random location of PAGs, quenchers, reactive sites
  • Polymer aggregation: Finite polymer/cluster size creates "granularity"

5.8.2 Stochastic Defect Types

  • Line bridges: Unexposed region fails to clear, connecting adjacent lines
  • Line breaks: Exposed region overdevelops, breaking line continuity
  • Missing contacts: Contact hole fails to open due to under-exposure
  • Merged contacts: Adjacent contacts connect due to over-exposure

Defect Requirements

For a 100mm² chip with 10 billion features, random defect probability must be below 10^-11 per feature to achieve acceptable yield. This places extreme demands on process control and stochastic management in EUV lithography.

5.8.3 Mitigating Stochastics

  • Higher dose: More photons reduce statistical fluctuation (but slower throughput)
  • Higher absorption materials: MORs generate more chemistry per photon
  • Smaller reaction units: Molecular resists vs polymer aggregates
  • Pattern optimization: OPC, assist features tuned for stochastic performance

5.9 Summary and Key Takeaways

Photoresists and patterning materials continue to evolve rapidly to meet the demands of advanced semiconductor manufacturing. Key points from this chapter:

  • ArF immersion lithography with chemically amplified resists remains a workhorse technology, extended through multiple patterning techniques
  • EUV lithography at 13.5nm enables single-exposure patterning at sub-40nm pitch, but faces the resolution-sensitivity-LER trilemma
  • Metal oxide resists offer dramatically higher EUV absorption and are entering volume production for leading-edge layers
  • Dry resist technology eliminates spin-coating defects and enables novel resist chemistries
  • Directed self-assembly can produce sub-lithographic features but faces defectivity and pattern flexibility challenges
  • Stochastic effects at EUV wavelengths represent fundamental challenges requiring materials innovation
