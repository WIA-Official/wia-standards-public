Chapter 03

High-k Dielectrics and Gate Materials

Hafnium-based oxides, ferroelectric materials, gate stack engineering, and interface optimization for advanced transistors

3.1 The Gate Stack Challenge

The transistor gate stack is arguably the most critical component of modern CMOS devices. It controls current flow through the channel by applying an electric field through a thin dielectric layer. As transistor dimensions shrink, the gate stack must provide sufficient capacitance to maintain electrostatic control while minimizing leakage current and interface defects. This chapter explores the materials and technologies that enable high-performance gate stacks at advanced technology nodes.

For decades, silicon dioxide (SiO2) served as the ideal gate dielectric due to its excellent interface with silicon, high breakdown strength, and simple thermal oxidation process. However, as SiO2 thickness approached 1nm at the 90nm node, direct tunneling current through the oxide became unacceptably high, necessitating a fundamental materials change. The introduction of high-k (high dielectric constant) materials allowed thicker physical films while maintaining equivalent oxide capacitance, dramatically reducing gate leakage.

Equivalent Oxide Thickness (EOT)

EOT is the thickness of SiO2 that would provide the same capacitance as the actual high-k gate dielectric. For a high-k material with dielectric constant k and physical thickness t: EOT = t × (3.9/k). A 2nm HfO2 film (k~20) has an EOT of approximately 0.4nm.

3.1.1 Dielectric Constant Requirements

The dielectric constant (k) of a material describes its ability to store electrical energy in an electric field. Higher k values allow thicker films for the same capacitance, reducing tunneling leakage. However, very high k materials often suffer from poor interface quality with silicon and reduced bandgap, leading to increased leakage through other mechanisms.

3.1.2 Band Alignment Considerations

For a gate dielectric to effectively block leakage current, it must have sufficient band offsets to both the conduction and valence bands of silicon. The conduction band offset (CBO) and valence band offset (VBO) should each exceed approximately 1 eV to prevent significant electron and hole injection. Hafnium dioxide (HfO2) represents an optimal balance between high dielectric constant and adequate band offsets.

3.2 Hafnium-Based High-k Dielectrics

Hafnium dioxide (HfO2) has become the standard high-k gate dielectric for advanced CMOS manufacturing. First introduced in production at the 45nm node in 2007 by Intel, HfO2-based gate stacks now enable continued scaling to the most advanced technology nodes.

3.2.1 HfO2 Properties and Structure

Hafnium dioxide exhibits several crystalline phases, with the monoclinic phase being thermodynamically stable at room temperature. During thin film deposition, the as-deposited films are typically amorphous, crystallizing to monoclinic or tetragonal phases during subsequent high-temperature annealing. The crystalline phase affects both the dielectric constant and leakage characteristics.

Key properties of HfO2:

  • Dielectric constant: 20-25 (depends on crystallinity and deposition conditions)
  • Bandgap: 5.3-5.7 eV (phase dependent)
  • Thermal stability: Stable in contact with Si up to ~800°C
  • Breakdown field: 3-5 MV/cm
  • Density: 9.68 g/cm3 (bulk), lower for thin films

3.2.2 HfO2 Deposition Methods

Atomic layer deposition (ALD) is the preferred method for depositing HfO2 gate dielectrics due to its exceptional thickness control, uniformity, and conformality. ALD proceeds through self-limiting surface reactions, depositing material one atomic layer at a time.

ALD Process Steps

  1. Precursor pulse: Hafnium precursor (e.g., HfCl4, TEMAH, TDMAH) adsorbs on surface
  2. Purge: Excess precursor and byproducts removed by inert gas
  3. Oxidant pulse: H2O, O3, or O2 plasma reacts with adsorbed precursor
  4. Purge: Reaction byproducts removed
  5. Repeat: Cycle repeated until desired thickness achieved

3.2.3 Doped and Alloyed HfO2

Pure HfO2 can be modified through doping or alloying to tune its properties for specific applications:

  • HfSiO (Hafnium silicate): Improved thermal stability, higher crystallization temperature, better interface quality. Used in some early high-k implementations.
  • HfLaO (La-doped HfO2): Shifts threshold voltage for NMOS devices through dipole formation at interface. Critical for work function engineering.
  • HfAlO (Al-doped HfO2): Shifts threshold voltage for PMOS devices. Provides complementary threshold voltage tuning to HfLaO.
  • HfZrO: Enables ferroelectric properties when properly doped and processed. Used in emerging ferroelectric memories (FeFET).

Threshold Voltage Engineering

Precise threshold voltage (Vt) control is essential for modern CMOS. The use of La and Al dopants in the high-k stack creates interface dipoles that shift Vt by 100-300mV, enabling the multiple Vt options (LVT, SVT, HVT) required for optimal power/performance tradeoffs.

3.3 Interface Engineering

The interface between the high-k dielectric and the silicon channel is critical for device performance. Interface defects cause carrier scattering, reducing mobility, and create charge trapping states that cause threshold voltage instability. Careful interface engineering is essential to realize the benefits of high-k dielectrics.

3.3.1 Interface Layer (IL) Requirements

A thin interface layer between the silicon channel and the high-k dielectric is essential for good electrical properties. This layer, typically 0.5-1nm of SiO2 or SiON, provides:

  • Low interface trap density (Dit): The Si/SiO2 interface has the lowest Dit of any semiconductor/insulator system (~10^10 cm-2eV-1)
  • Carrier mobility preservation: Prevents direct high-k/Si contact that would degrade mobility
  • Chemical stability: Prevents intermixing between high-k and silicon
  • EOT penalty: The low-k IL increases overall EOT, limiting scaling

3.3.2 IL Formation Methods

Several methods are used to form the interface layer:

  • Chemical oxide: Formed during wet chemical cleaning (SC-1/SC-2), provides ~0.5nm SiO2
  • Thermal oxidation: Controlled low-temperature oxidation for precise thickness control
  • Ozone oxidation: Higher quality oxide with better uniformity
  • Plasma oxidation: Enables lowest temperature IL formation
  • Scavenging: Post-deposition thinning using metal scavenging effects

3.3.3 EOT Scaling Challenges

Reducing EOT below 0.7nm while maintaining acceptable mobility and reliability remains challenging. The IL contributes a significant portion of the total EOT, and thinning it increases interface traps and degrades mobility. Advanced approaches to EOT scaling include:

  • IL scavenging: Using reactive metals (Ti, La) to reduce IL thickness post-deposition
  • Dipole engineering: Optimizing interface dipoles to maintain capacitance without IL scaling
  • Alternative channels: SiGe or Ge channels may tolerate thinner IL or direct high-k contact
  • Higher-k materials: Research into k>30 materials for future nodes

Mobility-EOT Tradeoff

Aggressive EOT scaling typically degrades channel mobility due to increased remote phonon scattering from the high-k and/or increased interface trap density. At the 2nm node, maintaining acceptable mobility with EOT below 0.7nm requires careful optimization of the entire gate stack.

3.4 Metal Gate Electrodes

The transition from polysilicon to metal gates occurred simultaneously with the introduction of high-k dielectrics. Metal gates provide several advantages: elimination of polysilicon depletion effects, lower gate resistance, and work function engineering through metal selection rather than doping.

3.4.1 Work Function Requirements

The metal gate work function directly affects the transistor threshold voltage. For planar CMOS with undoped channels, the ideal work functions are:

  • NMOS: Work function near Si conduction band edge (~4.1 eV)
  • PMOS: Work function near Si valence band edge (~5.2 eV)

Achieving these work functions with practical, process-compatible metals requires careful materials selection and integration.

3.4.3 Gate Stack Integration Schemes

Two primary integration approaches have been used for high-k/metal gate (HKMG):

Gate-First HKMG

The high-k and metal gate are deposited before source/drain formation and must withstand subsequent high-temperature processing. This approach is simpler but limits metal selection due to thermal stability requirements.

Gate-Last (Replacement Metal Gate) HKMG

A dummy polysilicon gate is used during source/drain processing, then removed and replaced with the final high-k/metal gate stack. This approach allows use of lower thermal stability metals and provides better work function control, but adds process complexity.

3.4.4 Work Function Tuning Techniques

Multiple threshold voltage options are required for modern SoC designs, achieved through work function engineering:

  • Metal thickness modulation: Different TiN thicknesses shift work function
  • Interface dipoles: La and Al doping in high-k create dipoles
  • Metal alloy composition: TiAl composition tuning for NMOS
  • Capping layers: Different cap materials between high-k and metal

3.5 Ferroelectric HfO2

The discovery of ferroelectricity in doped HfO2 thin films has opened new possibilities for non-volatile memory applications. Unlike conventional ferroelectric materials (PZT, SBT), ferroelectric HfO2 is CMOS-compatible and can be scaled to nanometer dimensions.

3.5.1 Origin of Ferroelectricity

Ferroelectricity in HfO2 arises from the metastable orthorhombic phase (space group Pca21), which has a non-centrosymmetric crystal structure allowing spontaneous polarization. This phase can be stabilized through:

  • Dopants: Si, Al, Y, Gd, La, Zr stabilize the ferroelectric phase
  • Film thickness: Surface energy effects favor orthorhombic phase in thin films
  • Electrode material: TiN electrodes help stabilize ferroelectric phase
  • Annealing conditions: Rapid thermal processing promotes ferroelectric crystallization

3.5.2 Ferroelectric Memory Applications

Ferroelectric HfO2 enables several memory technologies:

Device Type Structure Operation Advantages
FeRAM MFM capacitor Polarization switching Fast, low power
FeFET MFIS transistor Vt modulation Non-destructive read, high density
FTJ MFM tunnel junction TER effect Analog operation, neuromorphic

FeFET Advantages

Ferroelectric FETs using HfO2-based ferroelectric gate stacks offer CMOS-compatible non-volatile memory with fast programming, low power, and potential for extremely high density. FeFET is considered a leading candidate for embedded non-volatile memory at advanced nodes.

3.5.3 Reliability Challenges

Ferroelectric HfO2 faces several reliability challenges that must be addressed for commercial deployment:

  • Wake-up effect: Initial cycling required to reach stable ferroelectric properties
  • Fatigue: Degradation of polarization after repeated cycling (10^9-10^12 cycles)
  • Imprint: Preference for one polarization state after prolonged DC bias
  • Retention: Long-term stability of polarization state (10 years required)

3.6 Gate Stack for GAA Transistors

Gate-all-around (GAA) nanosheet transistors present unique challenges for gate stack formation. The gate must wrap around multiple thin silicon channels, requiring highly conformal deposition and careful work function engineering.

3.6.1 Inner Spacer and Gate Formation

The GAA gate stack process involves several unique steps:

  1. Channel release: Selective SiGe removal to create suspended Si nanosheets
  2. Interface treatment: Careful cleaning and IL formation on all surfaces
  3. High-k ALD: Highly conformal HfO2 coating all channel surfaces
  4. Work function metal: Conformal TiN/TiAl filling between sheets
  5. Gate fill: Complete metal fill of remaining gate cavity

3.6.2 Conformality Requirements

Gate stack deposition for GAA devices requires exceptional conformality (ratio of film thickness in confined spaces to open areas). ALD provides the best conformality, but even ALD faces challenges in the narrow gaps between nanosheets:

  • Step coverage: > 95% required for uniform gate stack
  • Aspect ratio: Effective AR of 10-20:1 in nanosheet gaps
  • Precursor diffusion: Limited by narrow spacing and long diffusion path
  • Purge efficiency: Critical to prevent CVD-mode growth and non-uniformity

3.6.3 Work Function Engineering for GAA

Multiple threshold voltages in GAA devices require careful work function engineering, made more complex by the wrapped gate geometry:

  • Dipole layers: Must coat all channel surfaces uniformly
  • Metal thickness effects: Thin metal in narrow gaps may shift work function
  • Process integration: Selective treatments for different device types

3.7 Future Gate Stack Directions

As CMOS technology continues to advance, gate stack materials and structures will continue to evolve. Key areas of research and development include:

3.7.1 Beyond HfO2

Higher dielectric constant materials are being explored for future nodes:

  • HfO2-based ferroelectric: Combining logic and memory functions
  • Rutile TiO2: Very high k (~80-100) but significant integration challenges
  • SrTiO3: Extremely high k (>200) for advanced capacitors
  • 2D dielectrics: Hexagonal boron nitride for 2D channel materials

3.7.2 Alternative Channel Integration

High-mobility channel materials require different gate stack approaches:

  • Ge channels: Different interface passivation, GeO2 unstable
  • III-V channels: Complex oxides, different interface chemistry
  • 2D channels: Van der Waals interface, no dangling bonds

CFET Gate Stack

Complementary FET (CFET) architectures with stacked NMOS and PMOS present extreme gate stack challenges. Independent work function control for each device type in a vertically integrated structure requires innovative materials and process solutions.

3.8 Summary and Key Takeaways

High-k dielectrics and metal gates have been essential enablers of CMOS scaling over the past 15+ years. Key points from this chapter:

  • Hafnium dioxide (HfO2) has become the standard high-k gate dielectric, offering excellent balance of dielectric constant, bandgap, and integration compatibility
  • Interface engineering, including the SiO2 interface layer, is critical for maintaining channel mobility and device reliability
  • Metal gates provide work function engineering capability and eliminate polysilicon depletion, with TiN and TiAl as key materials
  • Ferroelectric HfO2 enables new non-volatile memory devices compatible with advanced CMOS processes
  • GAA transistors require exceptionally conformal gate stack deposition to wrap around multiple nanosheet channels
  • Future developments include higher-k materials, alternative channel integration, and increasingly complex 3D gate structures
