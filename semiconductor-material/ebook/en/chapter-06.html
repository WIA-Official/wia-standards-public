Chapter 06

Specialty Gases and Chemicals

Precursor chemicals, etch gases, deposition materials, CMP slurries, and ultra-pure chemicals for semiconductor manufacturing

6.1 Introduction to Semiconductor Chemicals

Semiconductor manufacturing relies on an extensive array of specialty gases and chemicals that must meet exacting purity specifications. From the gases used in plasma etching to the slurries for chemical-mechanical polishing, each material plays a critical role in achieving the nanometer-scale precision required for advanced devices.

The global semiconductor chemicals market exceeds $60 billion annually and continues to grow with increasing process complexity. Key material categories include bulk gases, specialty gases, wet chemicals, CMP consumables, and precursors for thin film deposition. Material purity requirements have increased dramatically, with many critical chemicals now specified at parts-per-trillion (ppt) impurity levels.

Purity Evolution

At the 350nm technology node, parts-per-million (ppm) purity was typically sufficient. By the 14nm node, parts-per-billion (ppb) specifications became standard. For 3nm and beyond, many critical materials require parts-per-trillion (ppt) purity levels - equivalent to finding one drop of impurity in a million Olympic swimming pools.

6.2 Etch Gases

Plasma etching uses reactive gases to selectively remove materials with nanometer-scale precision. The choice of etch chemistry determines selectivity (ratio of etch rates between target and mask materials), profile control, and process damage characteristics.

6.2.1 Fluorine-Based Etch Gases

Fluorine-containing gases are workhorses for etching silicon-based materials:

Gas Formula Applications Characteristics
Carbon tetrafluoride CF4 Silicon, SiO2, SiN etch High etch rate, low selectivity
Octafluorocyclobutane C4F8 High AR oxide etch Passivation, high selectivity
Hexafluoroethane C2F6 Chamber clean, oxide etch Moderate selectivity
Sulfur hexafluoride SF6 Silicon etch, DRIE Very high Si etch rate
Nitrogen trifluoride NF3 Chamber cleaning Efficient remote plasma clean
Fluoromethane CH3F SiN selective etch High SiN:SiO2 selectivity

6.2.2 Chlorine and Bromine Gases

Halogen gases provide excellent selectivity for metal and silicon etching:

  • Cl2 (Chlorine): Metal etch (Al, W, Ti), silicon etch, high selectivity to oxide
  • BCl3 (Boron trichloride): Aluminum etch (removes native oxide), improves sidewall profile
  • HBr (Hydrogen bromide): Silicon etch with excellent selectivity to SiO2 masks
  • HCl (Hydrogen chloride): Selective epitaxial clean, metal etch

6.2.4 Atomic Layer Etching (ALE)

Atomic layer etching provides atomic-scale precision through self-limiting sequential reactions:

  1. Surface modification: Reactive species (e.g., Cl2) modifies top atomic layer
  2. Purge: Remove excess reactants
  3. Removal: Noble gas ions (Ar+) or thermal energy removes modified layer
  4. Purge: Remove etch products

ALE enables sub-nanometer etch depth control, essential for GAA nanosheet formation and gate recess processes.

6.3 CVD and ALD Precursors

Chemical vapor deposition (CVD) and atomic layer deposition (ALD) require volatile precursor chemicals that react to form thin films. The choice of precursor determines deposition temperature, film quality, conformality, and growth rate.

Silane Safety

Silane (SiH4) is pyrophoric - it spontaneously ignites upon contact with air. Semiconductor fabs employ extensive safety systems including gas cabinets with purge capabilities, point-of-use abatement, and sophisticated gas detection and interlock systems to manage this hazard.

6.3.3 Precursor Delivery Systems

Liquid and solid precursors require specialized delivery systems:

  • Bubblers: Carrier gas (N2, Ar) bubbles through liquid precursor
  • Direct liquid injection (DLI): Liquid precursor atomized into vaporization zone
  • Solid source sublimation: Heated container sublimates solid precursor
  • Ampoules: Heated vessels for low vapor pressure materials

Precursor Purity Requirements

Metal-organic precursors for advanced nodes require 99.9999% (6N) purity or higher. Key impurities controlled include metallic contaminants (< 10 ppb each), moisture (< 1 ppm), and oxygen (< 1 ppm). Impurities in precursors directly translate to defects in deposited films.

6.4.2 Hydrofluoric Acid (HF)

HF is uniquely capable of dissolving silicon dioxide and remains irreplaceable for many critical processes:

  • Native oxide removal: Pre-clean before epitaxy and gate oxidation
  • Oxide etching: Pattern transfer, spacer formation
  • Particle removal: Undercuts particles by etching underlying oxide
  • Selective etching: High selectivity to Si, SiN in certain conditions

HF Hazard Warning

Hydrofluoric acid is extremely hazardous. Unlike other acids, HF penetrates skin and attacks underlying tissues, including bone. Even small exposures can be fatal. Strict protocols, specialized PPE, and calcium gluconate antidote availability are mandatory in all areas handling HF.

6.5 CMP Slurries and Consumables

Chemical-mechanical polishing (CMP) uses a combination of chemical reactions and mechanical abrasion to planarize wafer surfaces. Slurry formulations are complex, containing abrasive particles, oxidizers, complexing agents, and surfactants optimized for specific materials.

6.5.1 Slurry Components

  • Abrasive particles: Silica (SiO2), ceria (CeO2), alumina (Al2O3)
  • Oxidizers: H2O2, Fe(NO3)3, KIO3 (for metal CMP)
  • Complexing agents: Organic acids (citric, malonic) to chelate removed metals
  • Corrosion inhibitors: BTA (benzotriazole) for copper protection
  • Surfactants: Control particle dispersion and surface wetting
  • pH adjusters: Control chemical reactions and selectivity

6.5.2 Slurry Types

Application Abrasive pH Key Additives

6.5.3 Polishing Pads

Polishing pads provide the mechanical action for material removal:

  • Polyurethane pads: Dominant material with engineered pore structure
  • Hard pads: Higher removal rate, better planarity
  • Soft pads: Lower defectivity, better for final polish
  • Pad conditioning: Diamond disk maintains pad surface texture

6.5.4 Advanced CMP Challenges

Sub-3nm nodes present significant CMP challenges:

  • Dishing: Copper recesses below adjacent dielectric in wide features
  • Erosion: Excessive oxide removal in dense metal areas
  • Scratching: Large particles cause fatal defects in fine features
  • Corrosion: Post-CMP corrosion attacks copper surfaces
  • Selectivity: Stopping precisely on thin films (etch stop layers)

6.7 Environmental and Sustainability Considerations

Semiconductor chemicals present environmental challenges that the industry actively addresses:

6.7.1 Perfluorinated Compounds (PFCs)

Many etch and clean gases are potent greenhouse gases with long atmospheric lifetimes:

  • CF4: Global warming potential (GWP) = 6,500, lifetime = 50,000 years
  • C2F6: GWP = 9,200, lifetime = 10,000 years
  • SF6: GWP = 22,800, lifetime = 3,200 years
  • NF3: GWP = 17,200, lifetime = 740 years

Industry mitigation strategies include:

  • Point-of-use abatement systems (plasma burn, thermal decomposition)
  • Process optimization to reduce PFC consumption
  • Substitution with lower GWP alternatives where possible
  • Capture and recycle systems for high-value gases

6.7.2 Water and Chemical Conservation

Advanced fabs implement extensive conservation measures:

  • Water recycling: Reuse of rinse water for non-critical applications
  • Chemical recycling: Regeneration and reuse of expensive chemicals
  • Waste minimization: Optimized dispense volumes and process times
  • On-site treatment: Wastewater treatment before discharge

WIA-SEMI-MAT-001 Sustainability Requirements

The WIA standard includes sustainability specifications for semiconductor chemicals, including PFC emission limits, water consumption targets, hazardous waste reduction goals, and supply chain transparency requirements for environmental impact assessment.
