Chapter 07

Advanced Materials for GAA/CFET

Nanosheet channel materials, selective epitaxy, inner spacer materials, and CFET integration materials

7.1 Evolution to Gate-All-Around Architecture

The gate-all-around (GAA) nanosheet transistor represents the next major evolution in transistor architecture following FinFET. By completely surrounding the channel with the gate electrode, GAA devices achieve superior electrostatic control, enabling continued scaling to the 3nm node and beyond while maintaining acceptable power and performance characteristics.

The transition from FinFET to GAA architecture is driven by fundamental scaling limits. As FinFET fin widths decrease below approximately 5nm, quantum confinement effects reduce carrier mobility and increase variability. GAA nanosheets, with their wider (40-60nm) but thin (~5nm) sheets, avoid these problems while gaining density through vertical stacking.

GAA Timeline

Samsung introduced the first commercial GAA transistors at the 3nm node (2022). TSMC plans GAA adoption at the 2nm node (2025). Intel's GAA implementation ("RibbonFET") debuts at Intel 20A (2024). Each company's approach differs in nanosheet dimensions, stacking, and process details.

7.1.2 Key Material Challenges

GAA fabrication requires materials innovation in several areas:

  • Superlattice epitaxy: Alternating Si/SiGe layers with precise thickness and composition
  • Selective removal: Chemicals and processes to remove SiGe without attacking Si
  • Inner spacers: Low-k materials to fill the gaps between nanosheets
  • Gate stack: Conformal high-k and metal deposition in confined spaces
  • Source/drain epitaxy: Selective growth connecting to all channel layers

7.2 Si/SiGe Superlattice Epitaxy

The foundation of GAA nanosheet fabrication is the epitaxial growth of alternating silicon and silicon-germanium layers. This superlattice structure provides the template from which nanosheets are formed through selective removal of the sacrificial SiGe layers.

7.2.2 Epitaxial Growth Techniques

Reduced Pressure CVD (RPCVD)

RPCVD is the workhorse technique for Si/SiGe epitaxy, using silane (SiH4) or dichlorosilane (DCS) with germane (GeH4) at pressures of 10-100 Torr and temperatures of 500-700°C.

Ultra-High Vacuum CVD (UHVCVD)

Lower growth temperatures possible with UHVCVD enable sharper interfaces but with reduced throughput. Pressures below 10^-3 Torr minimize gas-phase reactions.

Molecular Beam Epitaxy (MBE)

MBE provides ultimate control for research but is too slow for manufacturing. Used for fundamental materials development.

7.2.3 Strain Management

SiGe layers are compressively strained when grown on silicon due to the larger lattice constant of SiGe. Managing this strain is critical:

  • Critical thickness: SiGe must stay below the strain relaxation threshold (~10-20nm for 30% Ge)
  • Strain symmetry: Alternating layers balance strain to prevent wafer bow
  • Defect density: Excessive strain causes threading dislocations and stacking faults

Germanium Content Tradeoff

Higher Ge concentration improves etch selectivity during nanosheet release but increases lattice mismatch and strain. The optimal 25-30% Ge range balances these competing requirements for manufacturable processes.

7.3 Selective SiGe Removal

The defining process step in GAA fabrication is the selective removal of SiGe sacrificial layers to release the silicon nanosheets. This process must achieve extremely high selectivity (>100:1 SiGe:Si) while minimizing damage to the delicate nanosheet structures.

7.3.1 Wet Etch Approaches

Chemistry Selectivity Mechanism Challenges
HCl vapor >100:1 Chlorination of Ge Temperature control
H2O2/HF 50-100:1 Oxidation + oxide removal Si oxidation
Carboxylic acids >100:1 Selective Ge dissolution Rate uniformity
NH4OH/H2O2 30-50:1 Oxidation-based Si surface damage

7.3.2 Dry Etch Approaches

Plasma-based SiGe removal using chlorine or HCl-based chemistries offers:

  • Better uniformity: Gas-phase delivery more uniform than liquid
  • Reduced stiction: No liquid surface tension to cause nanosheet collapse
  • Process integration: Compatible with cluster tool processing

Key dry etch chemistries include:

  • HCl/Cl2: High selectivity at elevated temperature (300-500°C)
  • CF4/O2/Cl2: Modified fluorine chemistry with chlorine addition
  • Remote plasma: Radical-based etching for damage-free removal

7.3.3 Atomic Layer Etching (ALE) for Nanosheet Release

ALE provides ultimate control for SiGe removal through self-limiting reactions:

  1. Surface modification: Chlorine adsorption on SiGe surface
  2. Purge: Remove excess chlorine
  3. Removal: Gentle ion bombardment or thermal desorption of chlorides
  4. Purge: Remove etch products

Each ALE cycle removes approximately one monolayer of SiGe (~0.3nm), enabling angstrom-level control.

7.4 Inner Spacer Materials

After selective SiGe removal, the spaces between nanosheets must be partially filled with dielectric material to electrically isolate the gate from the source/drain regions. These "inner spacers" present unique materials challenges due to the confined geometry and subsequent high-temperature processing.

7.4.1 Inner Spacer Requirements

  • Low dielectric constant: k < 5 to minimize parasitic capacitance
  • Conformal deposition: Uniform coverage in 8-10nm gaps
  • Thermal stability: Withstand ~1000°C source/drain activation
  • Etch selectivity: Different etch rate than surrounding materials
  • Adhesion: Strong bonding to Si nanosheet surfaces

7.5 Source/Drain Epitaxy for GAA

Source/drain regions in GAA transistors must make contact to all nanosheet layers while providing strain and dopant activation. Selective epitaxial growth (SEG) fills the source/drain cavities with highly doped material.

7.5.1 NMOS Source/Drain

NMOS devices use phosphorus-doped silicon or silicon-carbon:

  • Si:P: Phosphorus concentrations up to 3×10²¹ cm⁻³ achievable
  • Si:C:P: Carbon addition (~1-2%) provides tensile strain for electron mobility
  • Epitaxial profile: Must merge and connect all nanosheet layers

7.5.2 PMOS Source/Drain

PMOS devices use boron-doped silicon-germanium:

  • SiGe:B: 30-50% Ge for compressive channel strain
  • Boron concentration: Up to 5×10²⁰ cm⁻³
  • Graded Ge profile: Optimizes strain distribution and contact resistance

7.5.3 Wrap-Around Contact

Advanced GAA designs feature wrap-around contacts (WAC) where the contact metal surrounds the source/drain epitaxy on multiple sides, reducing contact resistance:

  • Reduced contact area: Compensates for smaller S/D region in scaled devices
  • Contact silicide: Ti or other metals form silicide with S/D epitaxy
  • Fill metal: Tungsten, cobalt, or ruthenium fills contact trench

7.6 Gate Stack Materials for GAA

Depositing the high-k/metal gate stack in the confined spaces between nanosheets presents extreme challenges for conformality and thickness control.

7.6.1 Interface Layer in GAA

The interface layer must uniformly coat all nanosheet surfaces:

  • Chemical oxide: Ozone or peroxide-based treatments
  • Thickness: 0.5-0.8nm for optimal mobility vs. EOT tradeoff
  • Uniformity: Critical on (100), (110), and (111) crystal faces exposed

7.6.2 High-k Deposition Challenges

Challenge Requirement Solution Approach
Conformality >98% step coverage Optimized ALD pulse/purge
Thickness control ±0.1nm uniformity In-situ metrology
Limited space Total stack < 8nm Thin layers, no barrier
Void-free fill No seams in center Super-conformal ALD

7.6.3 Work Function Metal Stack

The extremely confined space between nanosheets limits work function metal options:

  • Total thickness budget: ~4-6nm available for work function metals
  • Single dipole layer approach: La or Al oxide dipoles tune Vt
  • Simplified stack: Fewer individual layers than FinFET

GAA Gate Fill Challenge

For a 3nm GAA transistor with 8nm nanosheet spacing, after depositing 0.8nm IL, 1.5nm HfO2, and 2nm work function metals, only ~4nm remains for the fill metal. Achieving void-free tungsten or molybdenum fill in this space requires advanced ALD processes.

7.7 Complementary FET (CFET) Architecture

The complementary FET (CFET) represents the ultimate scaling architecture, vertically stacking NMOS and PMOS devices to essentially double transistor density without increasing footprint. CFET is projected for introduction at the 1nm node (approximately 2030).

7.7.2 CFET Material Challenges

CFET fabrication introduces additional material requirements beyond GAA:

  • Inter-device isolation: Thin, high-quality dielectric between N and P devices
  • Thermal budget: Bottom device must survive top device processing
  • Contact routing: Independent contacts to stacked S/D and gates
  • Channel materials: Potentially different optimal materials for N and P

7.7.3 CFET Fabrication Approaches

Monolithic CFET

Both NMOS and PMOS formed sequentially on same wafer:

  • Extended superlattice with both Si (NMOS) and SiGe (PMOS) channels
  • Complex selective processes for independent device formation
  • Thermal budget challenges for bottom device

Sequential CFET (Wafer Bonding)

Separate wafers bonded together:

  • PMOS fabricated on one wafer, NMOS on another
  • Wafer bonding and layer transfer
  • Relaxed thermal constraints
  • Alignment challenges for bonded layers

7.8 Future Materials Research

Research continues on materials that may enable devices beyond CFET:

7.8.1 2D Channel Materials

  • MoS2: Promising n-type channel with atomic thickness
  • WSe2: Ambipolar material for both n and p devices
  • Graphene nanoribbons: High mobility but bandgap engineering needed

7.8.2 III-V Materials Integration

  • InGaAs: Very high electron mobility for NMOS
  • GaSb: High hole mobility for PMOS
  • Integration challenges: Lattice mismatch with silicon, defect density

7.8.3 Novel Device Concepts

  • Negative capacitance FET: Ferroelectric materials for sub-60mV/dec
  • Tunnel FET: Band-to-band tunneling for ultra-low power
  • Spintronics: Magnetic materials for non-volatile logic

WIA-SEMI-MAT-001 GAA/CFET Specifications

The WIA standard defines material specifications for GAA and CFET fabrication including superlattice epitaxy uniformity, SiGe removal selectivity, inner spacer dielectric properties, gate stack conformality requirements, and reliability criteria for stacked architectures.
