Chapter 04

Interconnect Materials (Cu, Co, Ru, Mo)

Copper damascene, cobalt contacts, ruthenium liners, and molybdenum interconnects for next-generation BEOL integration

4.1 Introduction to Interconnects

The interconnect system is the network of metal wires and vertical contacts that connect billions of transistors in a modern integrated circuit. As transistors have shrunk, the interconnect system has become increasingly critical to chip performance, accounting for a significant portion of signal delay and power consumption. This chapter explores the materials that enable interconnect technology at advanced nodes, from copper damascene to emerging alternatives like cobalt, ruthenium, and molybdenum.

Modern processors contain 10-15 metal layers, with the finest (local) interconnects at the transistor level and progressively larger (intermediate and global) interconnects for longer-distance routing and power distribution. The total interconnect length in a modern chip exceeds several kilometers, making efficient, low-resistance metallization essential for performance.

Interconnect Hierarchy

BEOL interconnects are organized hierarchically: Local (M1-M2) connects transistors within standard cells (~20-30nm pitch), Intermediate (M3-M6) routes signals within functional blocks (~40-100nm pitch), Semi-global (M7-M10) connects blocks within the chip (~100-400nm pitch), and Global (M11+) provides power distribution and long-distance routing (>400nm pitch).

4.1.1 Interconnect Delay Challenge

While transistor switching speed continues to improve with scaling, interconnect delay (RC delay) increases as wire dimensions shrink. The resistance-capacitance product determines signal propagation delay:

  • Resistance (R): Increases as cross-sectional area decreases and length increases
  • Capacitance (C): Determined by dielectric constant and spacing between wires
  • RC delay: Scales unfavorably as R × C = ρL²/twd² where t is thickness, w is width, d is spacing

At sub-10nm line widths, additional phenomena exacerbate resistance increase:

  • Surface scattering: Electrons scatter at metal surfaces, increasing resistivity
  • Grain boundary scattering: Grain boundaries in polycrystalline films impede electron flow
  • Barrier layer overhead: Barrier/liner layers consume a larger fraction of the wire volume

4.2 Copper Interconnect Technology

Copper replaced aluminum as the primary interconnect metal starting with IBM's 0.22μm technology in 1997. Copper offers 40% lower bulk resistivity than aluminum (1.7 vs 2.7 μΩ-cm) and significantly better electromigration resistance, enabling smaller wires and higher current densities.

4.2.1 Damascene Process Flow

Unlike aluminum, copper cannot be effectively etched using plasma processes. Instead, copper interconnects are formed using the damascene process, where trenches are etched in the dielectric, lined with barrier metals, filled with copper, and planarized by CMP.

4.2.2 Barrier and Liner Materials

Copper diffuses rapidly through silicon and dielectrics, causing device failure. A diffusion barrier is essential between copper and surrounding materials. Traditional barrier stacks consist of:

  • TaN: Primary diffusion barrier, excellent adhesion to dielectric (2-3nm)
  • Ta: Adhesion layer for copper seed, improves electromigration (2-3nm)
  • Cu seed: Electroplating nucleation layer deposited by PVD (5-15nm)
Barrier Material Resistivity (μΩ-cm) Barrier Effectiveness Cu Adhesion Deposition Method
TaN 200-400 Excellent Good PVD, ALD
Ta 15-30 Good Excellent PVD
TiN 100-200 Moderate Good PVD, ALD, CVD
Ru 7-10 Good (with barrier) Excellent ALD, CVD
Co 6-10 Moderate Very good ALD, CVD

4.2.3 Copper Electroplating

Copper fill is accomplished primarily by electrochemical deposition (ECD), or electroplating. The process uses a copper sulfate-based electrolyte with organic additives that control plating behavior:

  • Accelerator: Sulfur-containing compounds (e.g., SPS) that increase plating rate at feature bottom
  • Suppressor: Polyethylene glycol (PEG) derivatives that reduce plating rate on field areas
  • Leveler: Nitrogen-containing compounds that prevent overplating at feature tops

The combination of these additives produces "superfilling" or "bottom-up fill" behavior essential for void-free metallization of high aspect ratio features.

Superfilling Mechanism

Superfilling occurs because accelerator molecules become concentrated at the bottom of features as the plating surface rises, while suppressor molecules dominate on field areas. This creates a differential plating rate that fills features from the bottom up, preventing void formation.

4.2.4 Copper CMP

After electroplating, excess copper is removed by chemical mechanical polishing (CMP). Copper CMP is a multi-step process:

  1. Bulk copper removal: High rate removal of overburden copper
  2. Barrier removal: Selective removal of barrier metals
  3. Buffing: Final surface cleaning and defect removal

Copper CMP slurries typically contain oxidizers (H2O2), complexing agents (glycine, BTA), abrasive particles (colloidal silica or alumina), and pH adjusters. Benzotriazole (BTA) forms a protective film on copper surfaces, controlling the etch rate and preventing corrosion.

4.3 Cobalt for Contacts and Local Interconnects

At the 10nm node and below, cobalt has been introduced for contact-level metallization (M0) and local interconnects (M1). While copper remains superior at larger dimensions, cobalt offers advantages at the smallest pitches where barrier scaling and void-free fill become critical.

4.3.1 Why Cobalt?

Cobalt offers several advantages at sub-20nm line widths:

  • No barrier required: Cobalt can be deposited directly on TiN liner, eliminating thick TaN/Ta barriers
  • Better gap fill: CVD cobalt provides excellent conformality and void-free fill
  • Electromigration resistance: Higher melting point provides better EM performance
  • Lower effective resistivity: At small dimensions, Co without barrier beats Cu with barrier

4.3.3 Cobalt Integration Challenges

Despite its advantages, cobalt integration presents challenges:

  • Higher bulk resistivity: Co (6 μΩ-cm) vs Cu (1.7 μΩ-cm) limits use to smallest features
  • Oxidation sensitivity: Cobalt oxide is insulating and must be prevented
  • CMP complexity: Different chemistry required than copper CMP
  • Selective deposition: Bottom-up fill requires careful process tuning

Resistivity Crossover Point

There is a crossover point (~15-20nm line width) below which cobalt (barrierless) has lower effective resistivity than copper (with barrier). Above this width, copper remains superior. Modern BEOL uses cobalt at M0/M1 and copper for larger metal layers.

4.4 Ruthenium Liners and Beyond

Ruthenium is emerging as a critical material for advanced interconnects, serving as both a liner material and potentially as a bulk conductor for the smallest features.

4.4.1 Ruthenium as Liner

Ruthenium offers unique advantages as a liner/barrier material:

  • Low resistivity: 7 μΩ-cm bulk, significantly lower than TaN
  • Cu direct plating: Cu can be electroplated directly on Ru without PVD seed
  • Thin effective barrier: 1-2nm Ru on thin TaN provides adequate barrier
  • Adhesion promotion: Excellent adhesion to both Cu and dielectric

4.4.2 Ruthenium Deposition

Ruthenium is typically deposited by ALD or CVD using organometallic precursors:

  • Ru(EtCp)2: Bis(ethylcyclopentadienyl)ruthenium, common CVD precursor
  • RuO4: Ruthenium tetroxide, used with reducing co-reactant
  • DMBD-Ru: (1,3-dimethyl-1,3-butadiene)(ethylcyclopentadienyl)Ru

4.4.3 Ruthenium as Conductor

For the smallest dimensions (sub-10nm), ruthenium is being explored as the primary conductor:

  • Mean free path: Ru has shorter electron mean free path (6nm) than Cu (40nm)
  • Size effect: Ru resistivity increases less dramatically with scaling than Cu
  • Barrierless: Ru can be deposited directly without barrier, maximizing conductor volume
  • Air-stable: RuO2 is conductive, eliminating oxidation concerns

4.5 Molybdenum: The Next-Generation Interconnect

Molybdenum is emerging as a leading candidate for interconnect metal at the 2nm node and beyond. While its bulk resistivity (5.3 μΩ-cm) is higher than copper's, molybdenum offers compelling advantages at atomic-scale dimensions.

4.5.1 Why Molybdenum?

Molybdenum's advantages for advanced interconnects include:

  • Short mean free path: 11nm, compared to 40nm for Cu - less size-effect resistivity increase
  • Barrierless integration: Mo does not require diffusion barrier, maximizing conductor volume
  • High melting point: 2623°C provides excellent electromigration resistance
  • Etch compatibility: Mo can be patterned by reactive ion etching (subtractive process)
  • Low-temperature fill: CVD at 300-400°C enables gap fill without substrate damage

4.5.2 Molybdenum Deposition

Method Precursor Temperature Resistivity Application
PVD Mo target RT-400°C 8-12 μΩ-cm Seed, thin films
CVD MoF6/H2 300-400°C 8-15 μΩ-cm Conformal fill
CVD MoCl5/H2 400-600°C 7-10 μΩ-cm Low-impurity films
ALD Mo(NtBu)2(NMe2)2 200-350°C 15-25 μΩ-cm Conformal liner

4.5.3 Subtractive vs Damascene

Unlike copper, molybdenum can be patterned using subtractive etch processes, similar to traditional aluminum metallization. This opens new integration possibilities:

  • Subtractive Mo: Blanket deposition, litho, etch - simpler process, tighter pitch capability
  • Semi-damascene: Hybrid approach with subtractive lines and damascene vias
  • Full damascene: Traditional approach, leveraging CVD gap fill capability

Industry Adoption

Intel announced the use of molybdenum interconnects for the Intel 18A (1.8nm) process node, marking the first high-volume production use of Mo in advanced logic. This transition represents a fundamental shift in interconnect materials for the most advanced manufacturing.

4.5.4 Molybdenum Integration Challenges

Challenges for molybdenum adoption include:

  • Oxidation: MoO3 is volatile, requiring careful process control during high-temperature steps
  • Fluorine residues: MoF6 precursor can leave F contamination affecting device performance
  • CMP development: New slurry chemistries required for Mo planarization
  • Supply chain: Limited high-purity precursor availability compared to established materials

4.6 Low-k Dielectrics

While interconnect metals determine resistance, the surrounding dielectric determines capacitance. Reducing dielectric constant (k) is essential for minimizing RC delay and power consumption in the interconnect system.

4.6.1 Low-k Material Evolution

Material Dielectric Constant Technology Node Key Features

FSG 3.5-3.8 180-130nm Fluorine-doped oxide
SiCOH (dense) 2.7-3.0 90-65nm Carbon-doped oxide

4.6.2 Porous Low-k Challenges

Reducing k below 2.5 requires introducing porosity, which creates significant integration challenges:

  • Mechanical weakness: Porous films have lower elastic modulus, affecting CMP and packaging
  • Plasma damage: Reactive species penetrate pores, damaging the material
  • Moisture absorption: Pores can absorb moisture, increasing k and causing reliability issues
  • Line edge roughness: Pores at surfaces create roughness affecting patterning

4.6.3 Air Gap Technology

The ultimate low-k material is air (k=1). Air gaps between metal lines can be created through selective removal of sacrificial dielectric or by exploiting non-conformal deposition. Air gap integration is used at advanced nodes to achieve effective k values below 2.0 in critical interconnect layers.

4.7 Electromigration and Reliability

Electromigration (EM) is the gradual movement of metal atoms caused by momentum transfer from conducting electrons. This phenomenon can cause interconnect failure through void formation or hillock growth, making EM resistance a critical reliability concern.

4.7.1 Electromigration Fundamentals

The atomic flux due to electromigration is proportional to current density and can be described by:

  • Driving force: Electron wind force proportional to current density (j)
  • Activation energy (Ea): Energy barrier for atomic diffusion, higher is better
  • Dominant path: Grain boundaries (Cu) or interfaces (advanced nodes)
  • Black's equation: MTTF = A × j^(-n) × exp(Ea/kT), predicts lifetime

4.7.2 EM Improvement Strategies

  • Metal capping: CoWP or CuSiN caps on copper prevent surface diffusion
  • Alloy additions: Cu(Al), Cu(Mn) improve EM through interface modification
  • Bamboo structure: Large grain size with spanning boundaries blocks flux divergence
  • Alternative metals: Co, Ru, Mo offer intrinsically higher EM resistance

Current Density Limits

Typical maximum current density specifications for 10-year lifetime at 105°C: Copper with capping: 1-2 MA/cm², Cobalt: 5-10 MA/cm², Molybdenum: >10 MA/cm². Higher allowed current density enables smaller wire cross-sections.

4.8 Summary and Key Takeaways

Interconnect technology continues to evolve rapidly as traditional copper scaling reaches its limits. Key points from this chapter:

  • Copper damascene technology has been the industry standard since 1997, but faces increasing challenges at sub-20nm dimensions due to resistivity size effects and barrier overhead
  • Cobalt has been adopted for contacts and local interconnects where its barrierless integration outweighs its higher bulk resistivity
  • Ruthenium enables thinner barrier/liner stacks and direct copper plating, improving conductor volume at advanced nodes
  • Molybdenum is emerging as the leading candidate for interconnects at 2nm and beyond, offering better scaling behavior and etch compatibility
  • Low-k dielectrics, including porous materials and air gaps, are essential for minimizing interconnect capacitance and RC delay
  • Electromigration reliability requirements drive metal selection and processing choices at each technology node
