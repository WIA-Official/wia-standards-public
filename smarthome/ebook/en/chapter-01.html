1.7 Why a smart-home standard exists at all

A smart-home product category that began with internet-connected lightbulbs in the early 2010s now spans door locks, fall detectors, hospital-grade oxygen concentrators, autonomous wheelchairs that route through a hallway, and panic buttons worn under sleeves. The industry has solved the easy cases — a healthy adult turning on a light from a phone — and stalled on the hard ones: a wheelchair user who cannot reach a wall switch, a Deaf resident who cannot hear a doorbell, a partially-sighted older parent whose voice assistant routinely misroutes commands.

The standard exists because the second category is where lock-in hurts most. A vendor that orphans an accessibility feature in v1.4 of its firmware can leave a household in a state where a door lock no longer responds to the resident's adapted input device, and the resident has no practical recourse. The standard's role is to fix the wire format and the protocol so a household's accessibility configuration survives any single vendor exit.

1.9 Audience

Three audiences are addressed in different sections. Engineers building a hub or device read chapters 4–8 closely. Procurement and accessibility consultants read chapter 3 for the conformance levels and the bridges to existing programs (WCAG, EN 301 549, KS X). Households and resident advocates read chapter 2 to understand the current state and chapter 8 to understand what to ask for.

1.10 What this book deliberately omits

Topics adjacent to but outside the smart-home accessibility standard are deferred to companion specifications. Healthcare-grade vital signs from a wearable belong to WIA-MED-020 (wearable health) and WIA Vital Sign Streaming. Building-level access control across multi-tenant residences belongs to WIA-BUILDING and is out of scope here. Vehicle integration (a paired EV that begins charging when the resident's "arrived home" routine fires) is referenced through WIA-V2X and not specified in detail in this volume.

Note on iteration order

Implementations encountering large device lists should iterate by commissioning timestamp rather than alphabetic identifier so that recently-added devices appear first in the resident's preferred scanning order. This preserves the muscle-memory effect that long- time residents rely on while keeping new device discovery immediate.
