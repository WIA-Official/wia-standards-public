2.7 The interoperability landscape today

Five major fabrics share the consumer market: Apple Home, Google Home, Samsung SmartThings, Amazon Alexa, and the open-source Home Assistant. Matter (the Connectivity Standards Alliance specification) has reduced the per-device commissioning burden across these fabrics, but Matter explicitly does not standardise the accessibility extensions that make a device usable by a person with a disability. A Matter-certified door lock interoperates across fabrics for the basic lock/unlock verb but offers no standard contract for the haptic-feedback signal that a Deafblind resident relies on to confirm the lock state.

2.8 The accessibility gap

The accessibility gap is the gap between "the device works" and "every body who lives here can use the device." Concretely, the gap shows up as:

  • Voice assistant bias. Off-the-shelf NLU models misroute commands from voices outside the training distribution (atypical pronunciation, post-stroke speech, AAC voice synthesis).
  • Single-modality alerts. A doorbell that only chimes is invisible to a Deaf resident; a vibrating pager that only vibrates is invisible to a resident with reduced tactile sensitivity.
  • Switch-input poverty. A user who can only reliably actuate a single mechanical switch (sip-and-puff, head-mounted) needs the entire smart-home surface to be reachable through that single input, with predictable scanning and no surprise menus.
  • Fall and inactivity blind spots. Fall detectors exist as point products but rarely participate in the household event stream that other devices and human caregivers can react to.

2.9 Why earlier standardisation attempts under-delivered

The CTA-2089 / IEEE 2089 family attempted accessibility-aware home automation a decade ago and stalled on encyclopaedic scope. Continua Health Alliance pursued the medical adjacent of this problem and succeeded narrowly for vital-sign devices. Both efforts predated the modern phone-as-hub assumption and treated streaming events as an afterthought. The current standard is positioned as a thin overlay on Matter rather than a wholesale replacement.

2.10 Why this is solvable now

Three trends make a standardised accessibility-aware smart-home possible today that were not feasible five years ago. Matter has established a baseline cross-fabric commissioning protocol so the accessibility extensions ride on rather than replace existing wire formats. Local-NLU models small enough to run on a household hub with reasonable latency now exist (sub-3-billion-parameter models with sub-second response under quantisation). Federated identity primitives (DID + WebAuthn + the WIA-OMNI-API trust fabric) make per-adapter consent practical without re-asking the resident at every device.
