================================================================================
Chapter 2: Ingestible Sensor Technology
================================================================================

[Table of Contents]

This chapter covers sensor technologies, power systems, data collection
mechanisms, and biosensing techniques that enable smart pills to monitor
physiological parameters inside the gastrointestinal tract.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.1 Sensor Types and Functions
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Chemical Sensors:
  pH Sensor (Ion-Selective Field Effect Transistor):
    - Range: pH 0.5 - 10.0
    - Accuracy: ± 0.05 pH units
    - Response time: < 30 seconds
    - Applications: GERD diagnosis, transit tracking, drug release

  Glucose Sensor (Enzymatic Electrochemical):
    - Technology: Glucose oxidase with ferrocene mediator
    - Range: 0 - 500 mg/dL
    - Response: < 60 seconds
    - Applications: Diabetes management, absorption studies

  Oxygen Sensor (Clark Electrode Variant):
    - Range: 0 - 21% O₂
    - Applications: Intestinal ischemia, bacterial metabolism

  Electrolyte Sensors (Ion-Selective Electrodes):
    - Ions: Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻
    - Applications: Dehydration, renal function monitoring

Physical Sensors:
  Temperature Sensor (NTC Thermistor):
    - Range: 30°C - 45°C
    - Accuracy: ± 0.05°C
    - Applications: Fever monitoring, inflammation detection

  Pressure Sensor (MEMS Capacitive):
    - Range: 0 - 400 mmHg
    - Applications: GI contractions, transit time, motility disorders

  Accelerometer (3-Axis MEMS):
    - Applications: Capsule orientation, motion analysis, activity tracking

Optical Sensors:
  Imaging Sensor (CMOS):
    - Resolution: 256x256 to 1280x1024 pixels
    - Frame rate: 2-35 fps
    - Applications: Capsule endoscopy, lesion visualization

  Spectroscopic Sensor (NIR):
    - Applications: Tissue oxygenation, composition analysis

Biosensors:
  Enzymatic Biosensors:
    - Lactate oxidase for ischemia detection
    - Uricase for gout and renal function

  Immunosensors:
    - Inflammatory markers (CRP, IL-6, calprotectin)
    - Tumor markers (CEA, PSA)

  Nucleic Acid Sensors:
    - Pathogen DNA/RNA detection
    - MicroRNA disease biomarkers

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.2 Power Systems
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Battery Technologies:
  Silver-Oxide (Ag₂O/Zn):
    - Voltage: 1.55V nominal
    - Energy density: 130 Wh/kg
    - Advantages: Stable voltage, low self-discharge
    - Applications: Long-term monitoring capsules

  Lithium (Li/MnO₂):
    - Voltage: 3.0V nominal
    - Energy density: 200-300 Wh/kg
    - Advantages: High voltage, long shelf life
    - Applications: Imaging capsules, high-power devices

  Zinc-Air (Zn/O₂):
    - Voltage: 1.4V nominal
    - Energy density: 400+ Wh/kg
    - Advantages: Very high energy density, low cost
    - Applications: Short-term diagnostic capsules

Energy Harvesting:
  Gastric Acid Power (Galvanic Cell):
    - Electrodes: Magnesium anode, copper/platinum cathode
    - Voltage: 0.5 - 1.0V
    - Power: 10 - 100 μW
    - Example: Proteus Digital Health sensor

  Thermoelectric Generation (Seebeck Effect):
    - Materials: Bismuth telluride
    - Gradient: Body temperature vs. capsule internal
    - Power: 1-10 μW

  Piezoelectric Generation:
    - Source: GI contractions
    - Materials: PZT, PVDF
    - Power: 1-50 μW average

Power Management Strategies:
  - Duty cycling: 80-90% power reduction
  - Adaptive sampling: 50-70% savings
  - Power gating: 30-50% reduction
  - Voltage scaling: 20-40% savings
  - Data compression: 40-60% transmission energy reduction

Typical Power Budget (10-hour capsule):
  Battery: 100 mAh @ 3V = 300 mWh
  Sensors (continuous): 10 mWh
  Microcontroller (10% duty): 5.9 mWh
  RF transmitter (1% duty): 2 mWh
  Memory: 5 mWh
  Total average: 2.79 mW (27.9 mWh with 10x safety margin)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.3 Data Collection Mechanisms
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Signal Conditioning:
  Amplification: Instrumentation amplifiers, 10x - 1000x gain
  Filtering: Low-pass (0.1-10 Hz), high-pass (0.01-0.1 Hz), notch (50/60 Hz)
  ADC Conversion: 12-16 bit resolution, 10 Hz - 10 kHz sampling

Data Processing:
  Preprocessing:
    - Noise reduction: Moving average, median filter, Kalman filter
    - Baseline correction: High-pass filtering, polynomial subtraction
    - Artifact rejection: Amplitude/rate thresholds, statistical outliers

  Feature Extraction:
    - Statistical: Mean, median, std dev, min/max, percentiles
    - Temporal: Rate of change, acceleration, trend, periodicity
    - Domain-specific: pH transitions, contraction patterns, fever duration

  Compression:
    - Lossless: Huffman, LZW, arithmetic coding (2:1 - 5:1 ratio)
    - Lossy: Delta encoding, downsampling (5:1 - 50:1 ratio)
    - Adaptive: Choose method based on signal characteristics

  Event Detection:
    - Threshold crossing: pH < 4 (reflux)
    - Pattern matching: Contraction waveform identification
    - Machine learning: SVM, CNN for anomaly detection
    - Change-point detection: Gastric-duodenal transition

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.4 Biosensing Technologies
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Enzymatic Biosensors:
  Glucose Oxidase System:
    Reaction: Glucose + O₂ → Gluconic acid + H₂O₂
    Detection: O₂ consumption (Clark electrode) or H₂O₂ oxidation
    Performance: 0-30 mM range, 10-50 nA/mM sensitivity

  Enzyme Immobilization:
    - Physical adsorption: Simple but weak binding
    - Entrapment: Gel matrix protection
    - Covalent binding: Strong, stable (EDC/NHS chemistry)
    - Cross-linking: High enzyme loading (glutaraldehyde)

  Stabilization Strategies:
    - Enzyme crosslinking (CLEC)
    - Protective additives (BSA, trehalose)
    - Nanoparticle encapsulation
    - Genetic stabilization (mutations)

Immunosensors:
  Antibody-Antigen Binding (Kd ~ 10⁻⁹ - 10⁻¹² M):
    - Direct format: Electrode - antibody - antigen
    - Sandwich format: Capture Ab - antigen - detection Ab
    - Competitive format: For small molecule antigens

  Detection Methods:
    - Electrochemical: HRP/AP enzyme labels (pg/mL sensitivity)
    - Optical fluorescence: FITC, Cy5 labels (pg/mL)
    - Chemiluminescence: Luminol + H₂O₂ (fg/mL)
    - Mass-based: Piezoelectric crystal (ng/cm²)

  Clinical Targets:
    - Inflammatory: CRP, interleukins, TNF-α, calprotectin
    - Hormones: Insulin, cortisol, thyroid hormones
    - Tumor markers: CEA, PSA, CA 19-9

Nucleic Acid Sensors:
  Hybridization Detection:
    - Electrochemical: Redox intercalators (methylene blue)
    - Fluorescence: Molecular beacons
    - Impedance: Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy

  Amplification:
    - PCR: Requires thermal cycling (challenging)
    - LAMP: Isothermal at 60-65°C (feasible)
    - RCA: Isothermal, high specificity

Whole-Cell Biosensors:
  Genetically Modified Bacteria:
    - Reporter genes: Fluorescence, luminescence
    - Triggers: Specific molecules (toxins, heavy metals)
    - Advantages: High specificity, amplified signal
    - Challenges: Cell viability, GMO regulations

Microbiome Sensing:
  - 16S rRNA detection: Bacterial species identification
  - Metabolite sensors: Short-chain fatty acids
  - Impedance microbiology: Bacterial growth monitoring

Key Consideration: Biosensors must maintain stability and performance in
harsh GI conditions (pH 1-8, enzymes, microbes). Rigorous validation and
quality control are essential.
