Chapter 7: Tracking Networks and Data Sharing

No single tracking facility provides complete global coverage of all orbital regimes. Effective space situational awareness requires networks of sensors distributed geographically and integrated through data sharing protocols. The WIA-SPACE-026 standard facilitates this integration by defining common data formats, exchange protocols, and quality metrics that enable seamless combination of observations from diverse tracking systems. This chapter examines major international tracking networks, data sharing agreements, standardization frameworks, and the evolving landscape of commercial SSA data providers.

US Space Surveillance Network Architecture

The United States Space Surveillance Network comprises radar and optical sensors distributed globally to provide comprehensive tracking coverage. The network architecture divides responsibilities: surveillance radars perform wide-area searches detecting objects and monitoring orbital regions, tracking radars conduct precision measurements of known objects, optical systems monitor deep space where radar range performance degrades, and space-based sensors provide continuous GEO monitoring unaffected by weather or daylight.

This distributed architecture provides resilient tracking—no single sensor failure disrupts operations. Geographic diversity ensures objects in LEO pass within view of multiple sensors each day, providing the observation geometry essential for accurate orbit determination. The network generates over 500 million observations annually, processed at centralized facilities into the authoritative space object catalog maintained by US Space Command and distributed publicly via Space-Track.org.

European SST Support Framework

The European Space Surveillance and Tracking (SST) Support Framework coordinates space surveillance capabilities across European Union member states. Rather than building a centralized European network, the SST framework federates existing national sensors—French radar and optical systems, German tracking facilities, Spanish telescopes, and others—into a cooperative network serving collective European interests.

The SST framework provides free collision avoidance services to European satellite operators, fragmentation event monitoring, and re-entry predictions for high-risk objects. This service-oriented approach demonstrates how resource sharing and cooperation enable effective SSA capabilities without duplicating expensive infrastructure. The framework employs standardized data formats aligned with CCSDS specifications—the same standards incorporated into WIA-SPACE-026—enabling interoperability with US and other international tracking networks.

Commercial SSA Data Providers

The past decade has witnessed explosive growth in commercial space surveillance services. Companies like LeoLabs, ExoAnalytic Solutions, and NorthStar Earth & Space deploy proprietary sensor networks and offer tracking data, conjunction warnings, and characterization services through subscription models. These commercial providers complement government networks by offering additional observation capacity, redundant data sources, and specialized services tailored to commercial operators' needs.

LeoLabs operates dedicated phased-array radars optimized for LEO small debris detection, providing tracking updates multiple times daily for objects as small as 2 cm. ExoAnalytic's global optical network offers persistent GEO monitoring with rapid maneuver detection. These capabilities address gaps in freely available government data—more frequent updates, smaller object detection, and customer-specific analysis. Commercial providers democratize access to sophisticated SSA services, enabling operators without government tracking agreements to obtain critical collision avoidance data.

Bilateral Data Sharing Agreements

Space-faring nations maintain bilateral and multilateral data sharing agreements governing exchange of tracking observations and orbital catalogs. These agreements balance transparency and security concerns—sharing sufficient data to enable global collision avoidance while protecting sensitive information about military satellites and surveillance capabilities. The US shares catalog data publicly via Space-Track.org while maintaining closer cooperation with allied nations through enhanced data sharing.

Data sharing benefits all participants: combining observations from multiple networks improves orbit accuracy through diverse observation geometries, provides redundancy against sensor outages, and enables faster detection of fragmentation events through wider coverage. The WIA-SPACE-026 standard facilitates these agreements by providing neutral data formats and protocols that participants can adopt without revealing proprietary sensor characteristics or processing techniques.

Inter-Agency Space Debris Coordination Committee (IADC)

The Inter-Agency Space Debris Coordination Committee brings together space agencies from 13 nations plus ESA to coordinate debris research, develop mitigation guidelines, and promote information sharing. IADC provides a forum for technical collaboration on debris modeling, measurement techniques, and mitigation technologies. Annual meetings enable members to share research results, coordinate measurement campaigns, and develop consensus technical approaches.

IADC's technical working groups address specific challenges: reentry prediction, hypervelocity impact testing, debris modeling, mitigation measures, and protection systems. The committee develops technical reports and recommendations that inform national regulations and international guidelines. While IADC lacks regulatory authority, its technical consensus significantly influences space operations worldwide. The WIA-SPACE-026 standard incorporates IADC technical recommendations, providing implementation specifications for the committee's high-level guidelines.

CCSDS: Consultative Committee for Space Data Systems

The Consultative Committee for Space Data Systems develops technical standards for space data and information systems through international cooperation. CCSDS standards address data formats, communication protocols, and information architectures for space missions. For space surveillance, CCSDS specifications define Orbit Data Messages (ODM) covering orbit ephemeris, conjunction data, and tracking observations.

CCSDS standards enable interoperability between agencies using different internal systems. A satellite operator can receive conjunction warnings formatted as CCSDS CDMs from multiple sources—US Space Command, ESA SST, commercial providers—and process them with common tools despite different tracking network architectures. The WIA-SPACE-026 standard builds upon CCSDS foundations while extending coverage to emerging commercial applications and modern web-based data distribution.

Space-Track.org: Public Catalog Distribution

Space-Track.org, operated by the US military's 18th Space Defense Squadron, provides free public access to the authoritative space object catalog. Registered users—satellite operators, researchers, educators, hobbyists—can download TLEs for all publicly trackable objects, updated multiple times daily. The service distributes approximately 25,000 TLE sets representing all objects larger than ~10 cm that the SSN tracks.

Space-Track.org democratizes access to space situational awareness by providing data freely that would cost millions to generate independently. Universities worldwide use Space-Track data for research and education. Small satellite operators without tracking agreements depend on these TLEs for mission planning and collision avoidance. The service exemplifies 弘益人間—benefiting all humanity by sharing essential safety information freely while maintaining necessary security controls on sensitive observations.

Crowdsourced Observation Networks

Amateur astronomers and satellite tracking hobbyists contribute increasingly to space surveillance through crowdsourced observation networks. Projects like COSPAR's Amateur SSA Working Group coordinate observations from backyard telescopes worldwide, particularly targeting objects in GEO and highly elliptical orbits. While individual amateur observations lack the accuracy and completeness of professional systems, aggregate data from distributed observers provides valuable supplementary information.

Crowdsourced tracking demonstrates SSA democratization—participants using modest equipment contribute to the global understanding of the space environment. These networks discover classified satellites, detect maneuvers, and monitor orbit evolution. Integration of crowdsourced data into professional catalogs requires careful quality validation, but the WIA-SPACE-026 standard accommodates diverse data sources through flexible quality metadata enabling appropriate weighting of observations from different provider classes.

Data Classification and Security: Not all tracking data can be publicly shared. Observations revealing precise capabilities of surveillance systems or detailed information about classified military satellites require protection. Balancing transparency for collision avoidance against security concerns challenges international data sharing. The WIA-SPACE-026 standard includes access control and data classification frameworks enabling protected exchange while maintaining security requirements.
弘益人間 Through International Cooperation: Space debris threatens all space users equally regardless of nationality. International cooperation in tracking and data sharing embodies 弘益人間 by transcending national interests to protect the shared orbital environment. When nations share tracking data, coordinate conjunction warnings, and jointly develop standards like WIA-SPACE-026, they serve humanity's collective interest in sustainable space operations.

Key Takeaways

  • Global SSA requires distributed sensor networks—no single facility provides complete coverage, necessitating international cooperation and data sharing
  • The US SSN generates 500M+ observations annually from 30+ sensors, maintaining the authoritative catalog distributed via Space-Track.org
  • European SST framework demonstrates successful federation of national capabilities through standardized interfaces and coordinated services
  • Commercial providers (LeoLabs, ExoAnalytic, NorthStar) complement government networks with specialized capabilities and customer-oriented service models
  • Bilateral agreements and multilateral forums (IADC, CCSDS) enable data sharing while balancing transparency against security concerns
  • CCSDS standards (OEM, CDM, TDM) provide interoperable data formats essential for multi-source integration and international cooperation
  • Crowdsourced amateur observations demonstrate SSA democratization, with distributed observers contributing supplementary tracking data
