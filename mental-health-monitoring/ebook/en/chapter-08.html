Chapter 8: Privacy & Ethics

"Mental-health data is among the most sensitive data a person ever creates. Our standard is not 'what is legal' — it is 'what a loved one would say we should do'."

— WIA-MED-020, Ethics Charter

8.1 Regulatory baseline

WIA-MED-020 complies with, but does not limit itself to, the major privacy regimes:

  • HIPAA (United States) — when operating as a covered entity or business associate, technical safeguards follow 45 CFR §164.312, and the US HHS "Health Apps" guidance is applied for consumer deployments.
  • GDPR (European Union) — lawful basis is explicit consent under Article 9 (special-category data), data minimisation is enforced end-to-end, and users can exercise Article 15–22 rights through the in-app DSR flow.
  • PIPA (Korea) — sensitive-information handling under Article 23, with notice and consent in Korean, and local data-residency when required.
  • PIPEDA / LGPD / APP Act and regional equivalents — specific clauses for Canada, Brazil, Australia and similar jurisdictions are spelled out in the platform's Annex B.

All compliance matrices are versioned alongside the software; every release ships with a signed privacy-impact assessment (PIA) linked from the release notes.

8.2 Consent model

Consent is granular along three axes (what, with whom, until when) and always reversible. Consent state changes are FHIR Consent resources signed by the user's device key; the clinical and research systems never accept uploaded data whose Consent signature does not verify. Withdrawal is honoured immediately and triggers:

  1. Stopping any pending upload.
  2. Flagging previously uploaded records in the recipient's system with a "withdrawn" marker.
  3. Offering the user a data-erasure receipt (hash of deleted rows, timestamp, recipient ID).

8.3 Minimisation and on-device processing

The default is "never upload what you can compute locally." Feature extraction (Chapter 2) and mood-model inference (Chapter 3) run on the device. Only derived features, with explicit consent, are eligible to leave. When a cloud model is needed for scale, the platform uses differential-privacy mechanisms (Chapter 2 § 2.3) and secure aggregation so the server never sees individual observations.

8.4 Secondary use and research

Research access is always conditional on a named IRB / ethics-committee protocol. The governance flow is:

  • Research team submits protocol + purpose + retention.
  • Users are invited (never auto-enrolled), and each user decides independently.
  • Only de-identified, consented data enters the research pipeline.
  • Publications list the protocol ID; retractions propagate back to the originating records.

Commercial resale of mental-health data — for advertising, underwriting, employment screening, or any similar purpose — is prohibited. The platform's licence explicitly forbids it, and enforcement is via both contractual and technical means (access keys are scoped to specific IRB protocols and cannot be used outside them).

8.5 Bias, fairness, and cultural competence

Models built on digital phenotyping and language analysis can encode cultural and demographic bias. WIA-MED-020 requires:

  • Training data demographics to be published.
  • Performance to be reported separately by age band, sex, language, and region.
  • Clinically-relevant subgroup differences to be disclosed; when gaps exceed a published threshold, the feature is withheld for affected groups until remediation.
  • External, independent audits at least annually, with public reports.

8.6 Autonomy, coercion, and power

Mental-health monitoring can be weaponised. A worried parent, a controlling partner, an employer, a carceral setting — each is a plausible source of coercion. The platform must not be an instrument of that coercion. Concrete protections include:

  • No silent monitoring. Every data flow is visible to the primary account holder and is not installable without their active participation.
  • Private mode. A one-tap "private mode" pauses collection and hides uploads; the fact of having enabled private mode is itself never uploaded.
  • Safety-by-default messaging. If a contact pattern suggests intimate-partner surveillance, the platform surfaces the "signs of coercion" resource in the user's own language.

8.7 Governance

A standing ethics board — including people with lived experience, clinicians, ethicists, and patient advocates — reviews significant feature launches and adjudicates disputes. Board minutes and votes are public. The board has a binding veto over any feature that fails its criteria; this constraint is in the platform's governance documents and cannot be changed by the engineering team unilaterally.

8.8 A final principle

Mental-health data is, for many users, the most revealing information they will ever share. The governing question at every design review is not "Is this legal?" but "If this were my sibling's data, would I be proud of how we handle it?" The rest of this standard — the schemas, the APIs, the statistics — is in service of that question.
