Chapter 6: Therapist Integration & Collaborative Care

"The patient is always in the room, even when they are not. Our data presents their week honestly, so the clinician's minutes are spent where they matter most."

— WIA-MED-020, Clinician-Workflow Principles

6.1 Why collaborative care

Collaborative Care (CoCM) is an AHRQ-recognised, evidence-based model in which a primary-care clinician, a behavioural-health care manager, and a consulting psychiatrist share responsibility for a patient's mental-health outcomes. Multiple meta-analyses (Archer 2012; Unützer 2013) show meaningful improvements versus usual care. WIA-MED-020 is designed to be the data spine of a CoCM programme — the thing that makes the model affordable to run and sustainable over time.

6.2 Clinician dashboard

The clinician view is ruthlessly opinionated: it shows what matters in the first ten seconds, and hides what doesn't.

  • Header stripe. Risk level, last PHQ-9 and GAD-7 scores, change vs. last visit, adherence to plan.
  • Weekly timeline. Mood trend (with confidence interval), sleep hours, step count, screen unlock bursts, notable EMA entries.
  • Homework. CBT exercises assigned, completed, skipped; with quick re-assign and difficulty adjust.
  • Medications and therapy history. From the integrated FHIR MedicationStatement, never typed twice.
  • Notes and next steps. Auto-seeded SOAP note from the session; clinician edits and signs.

Every widget is built from a simple FHIR resource plus a few derived metrics. There is no proprietary middle layer; dashboards built on top of WIA-MED-020 can be re-implemented in weeks on any EHR.

6.3 Data-sharing consent

Consent in WIA-MED-020 is explicit, granular, and bidirectional. The user controls three layers:

  1. What is shared — instrument scores only, or include digital phenotyping, or include journal text.
  2. With whom — a specific clinician, a care-manager pool, or a research IRB cohort.
  3. Until when — time-boxed (e.g., 90 days), auto-renewed only with a confirmation prompt.

A consent state transition is itself a FHIR Consent resource, cryptographically signed (ES256) by the user's passkey, and stored on an append-only log anchored to the WIA Chain. Clinicians can attest they have seen the consent; they cannot fabricate it.

6.4 Care-manager workflow

The care manager is typically a masters-level clinician whose caseload is measured in hundreds. WIA-MED-020 gives them a triage view that sorts the caseload by a combination of clinical urgency (latest risk score, trend), schedule (last contact, next appointment), and engagement (instrument completion, homework adherence). The care manager can register an outreach directly from the triage view — a short click-path matters at 200 patients/week.

6.5 Psychiatrist consultation

Consulting psychiatrists in CoCM rarely meet patients in person; their value is decision support and medication guidance. WIA-MED-020 ensures their case review receives:

  • A longitudinal symptom plot (PHQ-9 / GAD-7 time-series).
  • Side-effect and adherence notes from the care manager.
  • Any active safety plan, pinned to the top.
  • A structured question from the care manager ("diagnosis?", "dose?", "step-up?"), with a written response field.

The consult is modelled as a FHIR CommunicationRequest + Communication pair so the whole interaction is visible to the patient's primary clinician without email archaeology.

6.6 Research and outcomes measurement

Practices running CoCM typically report registry-level outcomes: proportion of patients reaching PHQ-9 < 10, time-to-remission, re-hospitalisation rate. WIA-MED-020 computes these automatically over de-identified cohort data and exposes them as Prometheus metrics (wia_mhm_cohort_remission_rate) and as FHIR MeasureReport resources keyed to the registry's measure IDs. Quality-improvement cycles and federal reporting share a single source of truth — clinicians spend their time on patients, not on forms.
