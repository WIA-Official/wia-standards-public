Chapter 7: Voice & Text Sentiment Analysis

"The voice changes before the face does. Words shift before the mood is named. These signals are quiet; our job is to listen for them — with permission, with restraint, and with humility."

— WIA-MED-020, Language-Signal Charter

7.1 What voice signals can show

Twenty years of research in speech pathology have established that a set of acoustic features covaries with mood and cognitive state in clinically useful ways:

  • Prosody. Fundamental frequency (F0) variability, pitch range, intonation contours.
  • Tempo. Speech rate (words/second, syllables/second), articulation rate.
  • Pauses. Pause frequency, mean pause duration, pause:speech ratio.
  • Voice quality. Jitter, shimmer, harmonics-to-noise ratio.
  • Cognitive load. Disfluencies, repairs, mean length of utterance.

Across depression studies, reduced F0 variability, slower articulation, and increased pause ratio are the most reliable features. Models trained on these acoustic descriptors have reported AUROC ≈ 0.80 for depression severity in narrow clinical populations (Cummins 2015), but performance degrades substantially in the wild, with different microphones, languages, and accents.

7.2 Text signals

Journal entries, diary text, and CBT thought records (with the user's explicit consent) provide complementary signals:

  • Lexical. First-person singular pronoun frequency, absolutist words ("always", "never", "nothing"), negations (Al-Mosaiwi 2018).
  • Semantic. Topic concentration on themes of loss, helplessness, or self-criticism.
  • Sentiment. Multi-dimensional affect (valence, arousal, dominance) scored via validated lexicons.
  • Temporal. Shift from future-oriented to past-oriented language, reduced reference to other people.

LLM-based sentiment can add nuance, but the platform never relies on a model it cannot audit. Every text signal surfaced to the clinician carries a rule-based explanation that a human can inspect and challenge.

7.3 Privacy architecture

Voice and text analysis is disabled by default. If the user opts in, the platform applies three non-negotiable constraints:

  1. All acoustic processing runs on-device. Voice recordings never leave the phone.
  2. Only aggregated feature vectors (no audio, no transcripts) are shown to clinicians or researchers.
  3. A highly visible "mic indicator" is active whenever voice features are being computed, and the user can disable it with a single tap that persists across app restarts.

Text analysis similarly runs on-device; any journal upload is explicit per entry, never blanket.

7.4 Linguistic equity

Most published speech-pathology research uses English-language data. A platform that serves Korean, Japanese, Vietnamese, or Arabic speakers cannot simply port English-language models. WIA-MED-020 requires locale-specific validation before enabling voice analysis for a language. When validation is not yet available, the feature is disabled with a clear in-app notice pointing to the open research question — again, a disabled feature honestly labelled is always preferable to a feature silently degraded.

7.5 Integration surface

Derived features are emitted as FHIR Observation resources with custom LOINC answer codes where standard codes do not exist, accompanied by a free-text interpretation ("slowed speech, reduced pitch variability"). Clinicians can pin a specific session's voice metrics alongside the PHQ-9 trend in the dashboard described in Chapter 6.

7.6 Responsible limits

Voice and text sentiment analysis is seductive — it feels predictive. The discipline of this chapter is to refuse to use these signals alone for clinical decisions. They contextualise, they alert, they generate hypotheses. They do not diagnose. And because they are so intimate — the person's own voice, their own words — the consent, reversibility, and transparency demanded of them are the strongest in the entire standard.
