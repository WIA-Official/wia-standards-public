Chapter 3: Mood Tracking & Screening Tools

"A number on a screen is not a diagnosis. It is a conversation starter — with yourself, with your clinician, and with the future you are trying to become."

— WIA-MED-020, Instrument Use Guidance

3.1 Validated screening instruments

Digital mood tracking only delivers value when the underlying questionnaire is itself validated and used as its authors intended. WIA-MED-020 therefore ships a first-class implementation of the following public-domain instruments, maintained in lockstep with their reference versions:

  • PHQ-9 (9 items) — depression severity, two-week window. Score ≥ 10 is the standard major-depression threshold; item 9 (self-harm thoughts) always triggers the Chapter 5 safety-net workflow.
  • GAD-7 (7 items) — generalised anxiety; score ≥ 10 is a clinical cut-off.
  • PCL-5 (20 items) — PTSD checklist keyed to DSM-5.
  • AUDIT-C (3 items) — alcohol-use screen, with graceful escalation to the full AUDIT-10 on a positive screen.
  • WHO-5 — well-being index, validated for positive-state tracking when reverse-scoring depression inventories is undesirable.
  • EPDS — Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale; perinatal and postpartum care.
  • C-SSRS — Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale; always paired with the Chapter 5 protocol.

Each instrument ships with its official scoring algorithm, the original authors' attribution line, and the peer-reviewed citations baked into the data file so audit-trails do not depend on a live internet connection.

3.2 Ecological momentary assessment (EMA)

Long questionnaires are valuable but episodic. EMA complements them by asking short, contextual questions at semi-random times across the day:

  • Prompt budget. No more than 5 prompts per day by default; user-configurable down to zero.
  • Adaptive windows. Prompts avoid sleep, commute driving, meetings detected via calendar integration, and active phone-call windows.
  • Item pool. Slider mood (0–100), energy, anxiety, sleep-quality, loneliness; optional free-text with on-device tokenisation.
  • Compliance metric. 70 % response rate over seven days is the research-grade floor; below that, trends are marked as low-confidence in the clinician view.

3.3 Visualisation and interpretation

The patient dashboard presents mood as a temporal plot, not a single number. Three invariants ensure the plot is read correctly:

  • A 14-day rolling window of the underlying instrument is always visible.
  • Clinical cut-offs are drawn as translucent horizontal bands, labelled in the user's native language, with a pointer to the authors' guidance.
  • Confidence intervals around smoothed trends are always drawn — users must see uncertainty, not false precision.

The clinician view additionally overlays digital-phenotype context: a PHQ-9 uptick alongside a 30 % drop in step count and a 90-minute sleep compression is interpreted differently from a PHQ-9 uptick in an unchanged behavioral baseline.

3.4 Interoperability

Each completed questionnaire becomes a HL7 FHIR R5 QuestionnaireResponse resource, linked to the canonical Questionnaire definition published at https://terminology.hl7.org/CodeSystem/phq-9 (and equivalents for each instrument). Scores are additionally emitted as Observation resources with LOINC codes:

  • PHQ-9 total: LOINC 44249-1
  • GAD-7 total: LOINC 70274-6
  • PCL-5 total: LOINC 89204-2
  • C-SSRS lifetime ideation: LOINC 93373-8

This mapping is what enables a SMART-on-FHIR clinician app to render a patient's history without any proprietary bridge, and for integrated research cohorts to pool data safely under an IRB protocol.

3.5 Reliability and psychometrics

A screening result is only as good as its measurement properties. WIA-MED-020 implementations must preserve the ordinality and scoring fidelity of the source instrument — adaptive skip logic is permitted only where the authors explicitly support it, and total scores are never rescaled without an explicit crosswalk citation. Internal reliability (Cronbach's α), test-retest reliability, and minimal clinically important difference (MCID) values are displayed in the clinician documentation view for every instrument in the platform.

Finally, translations are validated, not machine-generated. When no validated translation exists for a locale, the instrument is disabled for that locale and the user is told why — a missing translation is never silently substituted by a literal one.
