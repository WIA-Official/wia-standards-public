Chapter 2: Digital Phenotyping

"Your phone knows when you are slipping before you do. The question is whether it asks for consent, and whether it tells you in time to matter."

— WIA-MED-020, Digital Phenotyping Ethics Statement

2.1 What is digital phenotyping?

Digital phenotyping is the moment-by-moment quantification of individual human behavior using data already generated by smartphones, wearables, and ambient sensors. The term was popularised by Jukka-Pekka Onnela (Harvard) around 2015 to describe how passive sensor streams — GPS, accelerometry, keystroke dynamics, voice, screen use — can be converted into continuous indicators of mental state, complementing the episodic snapshot that a clinical interview provides.

For WIA-MED-020 the operational definition is narrower: a digital phenotype is a continuous, consented, and clinically-interpretable signal derived from data captured on the individual's own device, processed on-device when feasible, and always ultimately owned by the individual.

2.2 Core digital biomarkers

Seven biomarker families account for the vast majority of validated research literature and form the minimum set a conformant platform should support:

  • Mobility and routine (GPS). Radius of gyration, number of significant places visited, entropy of location distribution, circadian stability. Low entropy + small radius is predictive of depressive relapse (Saeb 2015, Barnett 2018).
  • Sleep and activity (accelerometry). Time-in-bed, sleep onset latency, fragmentation index, daily step count, sedentary bouts. Disrupted sleep is both a risk factor and an early warning for bipolar and major depressive episodes.
  • Communication (metadata only). Count and duration of calls, SMS/messaging frequency — never content — as proxies for social engagement.
  • Device interaction. Screen-on duration, unlock count, app-category composition (news vs. social vs. tool), doomscroll patterns.
  • Typing and touch dynamics. Inter-key interval variability, backspace rate, swipe fluency; slowed typing correlates with cognitive load and low mood.
  • Voice prosody. Pitch variability, speech rate, pause ratio, jitter, shimmer; useful when the user opts into ambient recording during calls they explicitly mark as shareable.
  • Physiology (wearable). Heart-rate variability (HRV / RMSSD), resting heart rate, skin temperature, electrodermal activity, respiration.

2.3 Technical implementation

A WIA conformant collector runs as a foreground service on Android (JobScheduler + WorkManager) and a BGAppRefreshTask on iOS, backed by the HealthKit / HealthConnect APIs. Raw samples are written into an on-device SQLite database encrypted with SQLCipher (AES-256). Feature extraction windows are 5, 15, 60, and 1440 minutes; only derived features — never raw samples — are eligible for upload.

// Example feature record (5-minute window)
{
  "user_id":  "pseudonymous-uuidv7",
  "window":   "2026-04-25T14:30:00Z/PT5M",
  "mobility": { "radius_m": 82, "places_visited": 2, "entropy": 0.31 },
  "activity": { "steps": 12, "mets_mean": 1.1 },
  "device":   { "screen_on_s": 220, "unlocks": 6 },
  "typing":   { "median_ikd_ms": 420, "backspace_rate": 0.18 },
  "voice":    null,
  "physio":   { "rmssd_ms": 34, "rhr_bpm": 72 }
}

Feature extraction MUST be deterministic: rerunning the extractor over the same raw buffer MUST produce bit-identical output, so that clinical audits can reproduce any flagged alert.

2.4 Clinical use cases

Four categories dominate current validated practice:

  1. Relapse prediction. In bipolar II, a fall in step count, loss of routine, and shortening of sleep each stood up as independent predictors (Mood Rhythm Instrument; NIH RDoC initiative). A 72-hour lead time is typically achievable.
  2. Depression monitoring. PHQ-9 levels can be tracked continuously with digital phenotyping proxies at Pearson r ≈ 0.55 in the published Beiwe and Mindstrong datasets.
  3. Schizophrenia spectrum. Social isolation and deviation from baseline routine precede prodromal episodes; early intervention significantly reduces hospital admissions (Torous 2018).
  4. Adolescent risk surveillance. Screen-time patterns, sleep loss, and keyword-tagged self-harm ideation (opt-in) enable school counsellors to reach out with consent-preserving protocols.

2.5 Limitations and cautions

Digital phenotyping is emphatically not a diagnostic tool. The strongest-performing models are subject-specific, require weeks of personalised baseline, and carry substantial false-positive risk if repurposed for cross-sectional screening. WIA-MED-020 therefore enforces five guard-rails:

  • Algorithms never fire a clinical alert without a human-in-the-loop clinician review.
  • A drop below the personal baseline, not an absolute threshold, is the alert trigger.
  • Every feature has a confidence field; clinicians see both the score and its interval.
  • Users may pause collection instantly via a shortcut, without consequence to their care.
  • Raw data is deleted on-device after feature extraction and never leaves the phone.

These constraints are covered in detail in Chapter 8 (Privacy & Ethics) and mirrored in the FHIR R5 Consent resources that gate every upload.
