Chapter 02: Desalination Technologies: SWRO, MED, MSF

2.1 Seawater Reverse Osmosis (SWRO)

SWRO is the dominant modern desalination technology. Seawater is pumped at high pressure (~60-80 bar for typical Mediterranean seawater) through semi-permeable membranes that reject dissolved salts. The membrane stack produces freshwater permeate at one outlet and concentrated brine at another. SWRO is energy-intensive but far more efficient than thermal alternatives; energy recovery devices (pressure exchangers) recapture energy from the brine stream.

2.2 Multi-Effect Distillation (MED)

MED uses multiple stages of evaporation under decreasing pressure, with vapour from each stage heating the next. It tolerates lower-quality feedwater than SWRO but requires more thermal energy. Common in cogeneration plants that pair desalination with power generation.

2.3 Multi-Stage Flash (MSF)

MSF flashes seawater into vapour through multiple low-pressure stages, condensing the vapour as freshwater. Older technology, declining market share but still operational at large Gulf state installations.

2.5 Brine discharge characteristics

Brine is concentrated seawater with salinity 1.5-2× ambient. Discharge methods include surface outfall (cheap but high local impact), submerged diffuser (higher cost, better mixing), and post-treatment dilution. Environmental regulators set permit limits on discharge salinity, temperature differential, and dispersion zone radius. The standard\x27s Phase 1 envelope preserves all parameters needed for permit auditing.

2.6 Pre-treatment requirements

SWRO membranes are sensitive to fouling. Pre-treatment removes suspended solids, biological matter, and dissolved organic carbon via coagulation, sand filtration, ultrafiltration, and chemical dosing. Pre-treatment failures cascade quickly into membrane fouling that reduces plant output and increases membrane replacement frequency. The standard\x27s plant operations envelope tracks pre-treatment performance metrics.

Implementer Notes

Three operational concerns recur in desalination plant infrastructure and are baked into the standard. First, brine discharge audit latency: environmental regulators historically receive monthly compliance reports; the standard's real-time brine_discharge stream collapses this latency to minutes, but plants MUST provision the regulator-feed endpoint with sufficient throughput to handle aggregated jurisdiction-wide subscriptions. Second, energy consumption volatility: SWRO plants modulate production within minutes for grid demand response; the energy_consumption envelope must be published frequently enough (typically 1-minute cadence) for grid operators to use the data for balancing. Third, membrane fouling cascade: pre-treatment failures cascade quickly into membrane fouling; the standard's plant_operations envelope MUST include pre_treatment_dp_kpa so downstream consumers can detect early warning of cascade failures.
