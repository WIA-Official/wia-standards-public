Chapter 6

Privacy and Data Protection

Protecting vulnerable populations from surveillance and data exploitation while enabling necessary verification

Privacy as a Humanitarian Imperative

For refugee populations, privacy is not merely a legal compliance matter—it is a humanitarian imperative with life-or-death implications. The same identity data that enables access to services could, in the wrong hands, enable persecution, discrimination, or violence against refugees. Countries of origin may seek to identify and target dissidents or members of persecuted groups. Criminal organizations may target refugees for extortion or trafficking. Even well-intentioned data sharing can expose refugees to risks they never consented to bear.

The WIA-REFUGEE-CREDENTIAL standard embeds privacy protection as a core requirement, not an afterthought. Privacy-by-design principles guide every aspect of the standard from data collection through credential verification. Technical mechanisms including selective disclosure, zero-knowledge proofs, and data minimization limit what information is exposed in any transaction. Governance frameworks establish accountability for data handling throughout the credential lifecycle.

This chapter examines the privacy requirements of the WIA standard, the technical mechanisms that implement those requirements, and the operational practices that ensure privacy protection in humanitarian contexts. Understanding these elements is essential for implementing credential systems that protect the people they are meant to serve.

"The protection of refugee data is not merely an administrative requirement—it is a fundamental aspect of refugee protection itself. Data breaches can cost lives."
— UNHCR Data Protection Guidelines

Privacy-by-Design Principles

Privacy-by-Design (PbD) is a framework that embeds privacy protection into the design and operation of systems from the outset. Rather than treating privacy as a constraint to be addressed through add-on controls, PbD makes privacy a fundamental design requirement. The WIA standard adopts PbD principles adapted for humanitarian identity contexts.

1

Proactive not Reactive

Anticipate and prevent privacy-invasive events before they happen. Design systems to avoid risks rather than remediate them after occurrence.

2

Privacy as the Default

Personal data is automatically protected in any system. No action is required by the individual to protect their privacy—it is built into the system by default.

3

Privacy Embedded into Design

Privacy is embedded into the design and architecture of IT systems and business practices. It is not bolted on as an add-on after the fact.

4

Full Functionality

Avoid false dichotomies such as privacy vs. security. It is possible and necessary to have both. WIA credentials provide verification capability while minimizing data exposure.

5

End-to-End Security

Strong security measures are essential for privacy. Data must be protected throughout its entire lifecycle, from collection to deletion.

6

Visibility and Transparency

All stakeholders are assured that whatever the business practice or technology involved, it is operating according to stated promises and objectives.

7

Respect for User Privacy

Keep the interests of the individual uppermost by offering strong privacy defaults, appropriate notice, and empowering user-friendly options.

Data Minimization

Data minimization is the principle that only the minimum necessary data should be collected and retained for a given purpose. For refugee credentials, this means collecting only the information necessary for the credential's function and sharing only what is needed for verification. The less data collected and shared, the smaller the attack surface and the lower the risk of misuse.

Collection Minimization

At the point of registration, only data required for the specific credentials being issued should be collected. A basic identity credential needs biographical data and biometrics. A health credential needs medical information. Collecting comprehensive data "in case it's needed later" creates unnecessary risk. The WIA standard specifies minimum data sets for each credential type. 

// Minimum Data Set for RefugeeIdentityCredential
{
  "requiredClaims": [
    "givenName",
    "familyName",
    "birthDate"
  ],
  "optionalClaims": [
    "birthPlace",
    "nationality",
    "gender",
    "photo"
  ],
  "prohibitedCollection": [
    "religion",
    "ethnicity",
    "politicalAffiliation",
    "sexualOrientation"
  ],
  "biometricMinimum": {
    "modalities": 2,
    "retention": "template_only_after_enrollment"
  }
}

Disclosure Minimization

When presenting credentials for verification, only the claims necessary for the specific transaction should be disclosed. A refugee presenting credentials to access healthcare does not need to reveal their country of origin. Someone proving they are over 18 does not need to reveal their exact birthdate. Selective disclosure enables this minimization.

Retention Minimization

Data should be retained only as long as necessary for its purpose. Verification transactions should not store personal data beyond what is needed for immediate validation. Audit logs should retain minimum identifiers necessary for accountability. Backup systems should implement the same retention limits as primary systems.

Selective Disclosure

Selective disclosure is a privacy-preserving technique that allows credential holders to reveal only specific claims from a credential while proving that the disclosed claims come from a validly signed credential. The holder can prove their age without revealing their name, or prove their refugee status without revealing their country of origin.

Technical Implementation

The WIA standard supports multiple selective disclosure mechanisms. BBS+ signatures enable unlimited selective disclosure from signed credentials. SD-JWT (Selective Disclosure JSON Web Tokens) provides selective disclosure with simpler cryptography. ZKP-based approaches enable proving facts about claims without revealing the claims themselves. 

// Selective Disclosure Example with SD-JWT
// Original Credential Claims
{
  "givenName": "Ahmad",
  "familyName": "Hassan",
  "birthDate": "1985-03-15",
  "nationality": "SY",
  "refugeeStatus": "recognized",
  "registrationNumber": "UNHCR-JOR-2025-78432"
}

// Selective Disclosure for Age Verification
// Only reveals birthDate, other claims remain hidden
{
  "presented_claims": {
    "birthDate": "1985-03-15"
  },
  "disclosed_digests": ["sha256_of_birthDate"],
  "hidden_digests": [
    "sha256_of_givenName",
    "sha256_of_familyName",
    "sha256_of_nationality",
    "sha256_of_refugeeStatus",
    "sha256_of_registrationNumber"
  ],
  "proof": "signature_over_all_digests"
}

Zero-Knowledge Proofs

Zero-Knowledge Proofs (ZKPs) take privacy protection further by enabling proofs about data without revealing the data itself. Rather than revealing their birthdate to prove they are over 18, a holder can present a ZKP that proves the birthdate in their credential indicates age over 18 without revealing what that birthdate is.

ZKPs are particularly valuable for protecting sensitive information. A refugee could prove they have valid refugee status without revealing their country of origin—information that could identify them as a target for persecution. The WIA standard specifies ZKP capabilities for credentials containing sensitive claims. 

// Zero-Knowledge Proof for Age Verification
{
  "proofType": "BulletproofRangeProof",
  "claim": "birthDate",
  "predicate": {
    "type": "greaterThan",
    "threshold": "18_years_before_today"
  },
  "proof": {
    "commitment": "pedersen_commitment_to_birthdate",
    "rangeProof": "bulletproof_range_proof_bytes",
    "credentialBinding": "link_to_issuer_signature"
  },
  "metadata": {
    "proofGeneratedAt": "2025-01-15T10:30:00Z",
    "validForTransaction": "uuid_of_verification_request"
  }
}
// Verifier learns: "holder is over 18" - True/False
// Verifier does NOT learn: actual birthdate, name, or any other information

Consent Frameworks

Informed consent is foundational to ethical data handling, but traditional consent models face challenges in refugee contexts. Power imbalances may pressure refugees to consent to data uses they would otherwise refuse. Language barriers may prevent true understanding of consent terms. The urgency of humanitarian situations may not allow for considered consent decisions.

Meaningful Consent

The WIA standard requires meaningful consent that goes beyond pro forma checkbox acknowledgment. Consent information must be provided in languages the refugee understands. The consequences of consent and non-consent must be clearly explained. Consent must be freely given without conditioning essential services on acceptance of unnecessary data uses.

WIA Consent Requirements

  • Informed: Clear explanation of what data is collected and how it will be used
  • Specific: Separate consent for different purposes; no blanket consent forms
  • Freely Given: No conditioning essential services on unnecessary data sharing
  • Documented: Record of what was consented to and when
  • Revocable: Ability to withdraw consent for future processing
  • Accessible: Information in appropriate languages and formats

Consent Management

The WIA standard specifies consent management capabilities that enable holders to understand and control how their data is used. Consent dashboards show what data is held and who has accessed it. Consent records provide audit trails of what was agreed. Consent withdrawal mechanisms enable holders to revoke permissions for future use.

Data Protection Compliance

Refugee credential systems must comply with applicable data protection laws, which may include regulations from multiple jurisdictions. The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) applies when processing data of individuals in the European Union or by EU-based organizations. National data protection laws apply in host countries. The WIA standard provides frameworks for compliance across these varied requirements.

GDPR Alignment

The GDPR establishes comprehensive data protection requirements that serve as a global benchmark. The WIA standard aligns with GDPR principles including lawfulness, fairness, and transparency; purpose limitation; data minimization; accuracy; storage limitation; integrity and confidentiality; and accountability. WIA-compliant systems can demonstrate GDPR compliance through built-in documentation and controls.

GDPR Principle WIA Implementation
Lawfulness, Fairness, Transparency Consent frameworks, clear privacy notices, audit trails
Purpose Limitation Credential-specific data collection, use restrictions
Data Minimization Minimum data sets, selective disclosure, ZKPs
Accuracy Verification workflows, update mechanisms
Storage Limitation Retention policies, automated deletion
Integrity and Confidentiality Encryption, access controls, security standards
Accountability Audit logging, compliance documentation, DPO requirements

Cross-Border Data Transfers

Refugee credential systems inherently involve cross-border data flows. A credential issued in Jordan may be verified in Germany. Biometric data collected in Bangladesh may be matched against databases in Switzerland. These transfers must comply with legal requirements governing international data flows.

The WIA standard addresses cross-border transfers through multiple mechanisms. Technical measures including encryption protect data in transit. Legal mechanisms including Standard Contractual Clauses provide lawful bases for transfers. Architectural approaches minimize unnecessary transfers by enabling local verification where possible.

Protection Against Misuse

The greatest privacy risk for refugee credentials is misuse of data by parties who should not have access or who use data for purposes other than those consented to. The WIA standard includes safeguards against various misuse scenarios.

Surveillance Protection

Refugee credential data could enable surveillance of vulnerable populations by governments, corporations, or malicious actors. The WIA standard protects against surveillance through multiple mechanisms: unlinkability prevents tracking individuals across transactions, selective disclosure limits data exposure, and purpose limitation prevents repurposing of verification data.

Surveillance Threat Scenarios

  • Origin Country Surveillance: Persecuting government attempts to identify and track refugees
  • Host Country Surveillance: Excessive monitoring of refugee populations
  • Commercial Exploitation: Data harvesting for marketing or discrimination
  • Criminal Targeting: Identification of vulnerable individuals for exploitation
  • Cross-Reference Attacks: Combining data from multiple sources to re-identify individuals

Data Sharing Restrictions

The WIA standard places strict limits on data sharing. Credential data must not be shared with origin countries except with explicit consent and risk assessment. Biometric data must not be shared beyond minimum necessary for verification. Purpose limitation prevents data collected for one purpose from being used for another without fresh consent.

Rights of Data Subjects

Refugees retain rights over their personal data regardless of their legal status. The WIA standard ensures these rights can be exercised in practice, adapting traditional data subject rights for humanitarian contexts.

Right of Access

Refugees have the right to know what data is held about them and how it is being used. The WIA standard requires credential issuers to provide accessible mechanisms for refugees to view their data, in languages they understand and through channels they can access.

Right to Rectification

Inaccurate data must be correctable. Name transliterations may need adjustment. Biographical details may have been recorded incorrectly. The WIA standard provides amendment mechanisms that maintain credential integrity while enabling corrections.

Right to Erasure

In certain circumstances, refugees have the right to have their data deleted. However, erasure must be balanced against legitimate retention needs for credential validity and audit trails. The WIA standard specifies when erasure applies and how it is implemented while maintaining system integrity.

Right to Data Portability

Refugees should be able to obtain their data in portable formats and transfer it to other systems. The WIA standard's use of open, standardized credential formats inherently supports portability. Export mechanisms enable refugees to obtain copies of their credentials in standard formats.

Implementation Requirements

Organizations implementing WIA-compliant credential systems must meet specific privacy and data protection requirements. These requirements are verified through the certification process and ongoing audits.

Implementation Checklist

  • Privacy Impact Assessment completed for credential system
  • Data Protection Officer designated where required
  • Processing activities documented in data processing register
  • Privacy notices developed in relevant languages
  • Consent mechanisms implemented per WIA requirements
  • Selective disclosure capabilities enabled
  • Data subject rights request procedures established
  • Breach notification procedures documented and tested
  • Staff trained on privacy requirements and procedures
  • Third-party processors vetted and contractually bound

Chapter Summary

Privacy protection is a humanitarian imperative for refugee credential systems. The same data that enables service access could enable persecution if misused. The WIA standard embeds privacy protection through Privacy-by-Design principles, data minimization, selective disclosure, zero-knowledge proofs, and robust consent frameworks. Compliance with data protection laws including GDPR is built into the standard. Safeguards against surveillance and data misuse protect vulnerable populations from exploitation.

Key Takeaways

  • Privacy is a humanitarian imperative, not just a compliance requirement
  • Privacy-by-Design embeds protection into system architecture
  • Data minimization limits collection, disclosure, and retention
  • Selective disclosure and ZKPs enable verification without full data exposure
  • Meaningful consent requires clear information in accessible languages
  • GDPR alignment ensures compliance with global data protection standards
  • Safeguards prevent surveillance and data misuse against refugees
  • Data subject rights enable refugees to control their personal information
