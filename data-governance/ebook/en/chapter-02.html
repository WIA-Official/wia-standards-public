CHAPTER 2

Data Governance Framework

Building a comprehensive framework with organizational structures, roles, and decision-making processes.

Framework Components

A robust data governance framework consists of multiple interconnected components that work together to ensure effective data management. The framework provides the structure and blueprint for how data governance operates within an organization.

Core Framework Elements

  • Vision and Strategy: Overall direction and objectives for data governance
  • Organizational Structure: Committees, councils, and reporting relationships
  • Roles and Responsibilities: Clear accountability for data-related decisions
  • Policies and Standards: Rules and guidelines for data management
  • Processes and Workflows: How governance activities are executed
  • Metrics and KPIs: Measuring governance effectiveness
  • Tools and Technology: Enabling infrastructure and platforms

Governance Operating Models

Organizations can choose from several governance operating models based on their structure, culture, and needs:

Centralized Model

A single central team manages all data governance activities across the organization.

  • Advantages: Consistent standards, clear authority, efficient decision-making
  • Disadvantages: May be slow to respond to local needs, potential bottleneck
  • Best For: Smaller organizations, highly regulated industries

Federated Model

Governance responsibilities are distributed across business units with central coordination.

  • Advantages: Local ownership, faster decisions, business alignment
  • Disadvantages: Risk of inconsistency, coordination challenges
  • Best For: Large, decentralized organizations

Hybrid Model

Combines central oversight with distributed execution.

  • Advantages: Balance of consistency and flexibility
  • Disadvantages: More complex to implement and manage
  • Best For: Most medium to large organizations

Organizational Structure

Data Governance Council

The highest governing body for data-related decisions:

  • Membership: Executive leadership, business unit heads, IT leadership
  • Responsibilities: Strategic direction, policy approval, resource allocation
  • Meeting Frequency: Quarterly or bi-annually
  • Key Activities: Review governance performance, approve major initiatives, resolve escalations

Data Governance Office

Central coordination and execution team:

  • Leadership: Led by Chief Data Officer or Data Governance Director
  • Staff: Data governance analysts, program managers, policy specialists
  • Responsibilities: Day-to-day program management, policy development, training

Working Groups and Committees

Specialized teams focused on specific governance domains:

  • Data Quality Working Group
  • Privacy and Security Committee
  • Metadata Management Team
  • Data Architecture Board

Roles and Responsibilities

Role Key Responsibilities Typical Profile
Chief Data Officer Overall data strategy, governance leadership, executive alignment C-level executive with data and business expertise
Data Governance Lead Program management, policy development, stakeholder coordination Senior manager with governance and project management skills
Data Owner Business accountability for data domain, decision authority Business unit leader with domain expertise
Data Steward Data quality, metadata management, policy enforcement Business or technical professional with data expertise
Data Custodian Technical implementation, system administration, security IT professional with technical and security skills
Data Privacy Officer Privacy compliance, risk assessment, incident response Legal or compliance professional with privacy expertise

RACI Matrix

A RACI (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed) matrix clarifies who does what:

Example RACI Matrix for Data Governance Activities

Activity CDO Data Owner Data Steward IT
Policy Development A C R C
Data Quality Monitoring I A R R
Access Approval I A C R
Metadata Management I A R C

R = Responsible, A = Accountable, C = Consulted, I = Informed

Decision-Making Framework

Decision Rights

Clear decision rights prevent confusion and delays:

  • Strategic Decisions: Data Governance Council (e.g., major policy changes)
  • Tactical Decisions: Data Owners (e.g., data access approvals)
  • Operational Decisions: Data Stewards (e.g., quality issue resolution)

Escalation Process

Define clear escalation paths for issues and conflicts:

  1. Level 1: Data Steward attempts resolution
  2. Level 2: Data Owner makes decision
  3. Level 3: Data Governance Lead mediates
  4. Level 4: Data Governance Council decides

Governance Maturity Model

Assess and plan your governance maturity progression:

Level 1: Initial

  • Ad hoc data management practices
  • No formal governance structure
  • Limited data standards or policies

Level 2: Developing

  • Basic governance structure established
  • Initial policies and standards created
  • Some data stewards assigned

Level 3: Defined

  • Comprehensive governance framework in place
  • Documented policies and procedures
  • Active stewardship program
  • Regular governance meetings and reporting

Level 4: Managed

  • Proactive monitoring and enforcement
  • Metrics-driven improvement
  • Integrated with business processes
  • Strong executive support and funding

Level 5: Optimized

  • Continuous improvement culture
  • Data governance as business advantage
  • Innovation in data management practices
  • Industry leadership and recognition

Framework Implementation Checklist

  • ✓ Define governance vision and objectives
  • ✓ Select appropriate operating model
  • ✓ Establish organizational structure
  • ✓ Define and assign roles and responsibilities
  • ✓ Create RACI matrix for key activities
  • ✓ Document decision rights and escalation process
  • ✓ Develop governance charter
  • ✓ Secure executive sponsorship and budget
  • ✓ Communicate framework to organization
  • ✓ Launch pilot and iterate

Success Factors

  • Executive Sponsorship: Visible support from C-level leadership
  • Clear Accountability: No ambiguity about who owns what
  • Business Alignment: Framework supports business objectives
  • Practical and Pragmatic: Avoid overly complex structures
  • Flexibility: Framework can evolve with organizational needs
  • Communication: Regular updates and stakeholder engagement
