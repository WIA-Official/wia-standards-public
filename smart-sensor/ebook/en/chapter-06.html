Chapter 6

IoT Communication Protocols

BLE 5.x, LoRaWAN, NB-IoT, Wi-Fi integration, protocol selection criteria, range vs. power trade-offs, and mesh networking

Overview

Wireless connectivity transforms isolated sensors into networked IoT systems capable of data aggregation, remote monitoring, and cloud integration. The WIA-SEMI-015 standard requires support for at least one wireless protocol with encrypted communication. Selecting the appropriate protocol involves balancing range requirements, power budget constraints, data rate needs, infrastructure dependencies, and cost considerations.

This chapter explores the dominant wireless technologies for smart sensors: Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) for short-range, low-power connectivity; LoRaWAN for long-range, ultra-low-power wide-area networks; NB-IoT for cellular-based IoT; Wi-Fi for high-data-rate applications; and Zigbee/Thread for mesh networking. Each protocol occupies a distinct niche in the IoT connectivity landscape.

Key Takeaways

  • BLE 5.x: 50-100m range, 8-15 mA TX, ideal for wearables and beacons
  • LoRaWAN: 2-15 km range, 30-120 mA peak TX, years of battery life
  • NB-IoT: 1-10 km cellular, 100-220 mA TX, infrastructure-based
  • Wi-Fi: 50-100m, 120-200 mA TX, high data rate, infrastructure required
  • Protocol selection depends on range, power, data rate, and deployment constraints

Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) 5.x

BLE has become the dominant protocol for consumer and medical wearables, proximity-based applications, and smartphone-connected sensors. BLE 5.x introduced substantial improvements over BLE 4.2, including 2× speed (2 Mbps PHY), 4× range (Coded PHY with forward error correction), 8× broadcast capacity (advertising extensions), and improved coexistence with Wi-Fi and LTE.

Technical Characteristics

BLE operates in the 2.4 GHz ISM band with 40 channels (2 MHz spacing), including 3 advertising channels and 37 data channels. Physical layer options include 1 Mbps (legacy, best compatibility), 2 Mbps (higher throughput, shorter range), and Coded PHY (125/500 kbps, 4× range extension). Typical performance shows 50-100m range (1 Mbps), 200-400m range (Coded PHY in open air), 8-15 mA transmit current, and 8-12 mA receive current with 5-10 µA sleep current.

BLE Roles and Topologies

BLE supports multiple roles: Broadcaster (non-connectable advertising, beacons), Observer (scans for advertisers, passive listening), Peripheral (accepts connections, typically sensor), and Central (initiates connections, typically smartphone/gateway). Connection topologies include point-to-point (1 peripheral to 1 central, most common), multi-peripheral (1 central to multiple peripherals, up to 8-20 simultaneous), and mesh (BLE Mesh specification, many-to-many).

Power Optimization

BLE power consumption depends heavily on connection parameters. Advertising interval affects discovery latency and power (100ms advertising: ~50 µA average, 1000ms advertising: ~10 µA average). Connection interval balances latency and power (7.5ms interval: low latency, higher power consumption; 500ms interval: high latency, minimal power). Slave latency allows peripheral to skip connection events (latency=10 enables skipping up to 10 events, reducing power by 90%). 

// Example: BLE connection parameters for low power
ble_conn_params_t conn_params = {
    .min_conn_interval = MSEC_TO_UNITS(500, UNIT_1_25_MS),  // 500ms
    .max_conn_interval = MSEC_TO_UNITS(1000, UNIT_1_25_MS), // 1000ms
    .slave_latency     = 10,        // Skip up to 10 events
    .conn_sup_timeout  = MSEC_TO_UNITS(4000, UNIT_10_MS)    // 4s supervision
};

// Result: Connection event every 500-1000ms, but peripheral can
// skip 10 events if no data to send, waking every ~5-10 seconds
// Average current: ~15-25 µA including sleep

LoRaWAN (Long Range Wide Area Network)

LoRaWAN provides ultra-long-range, low-power connectivity ideal for battery-operated sensors in agriculture, smart cities, and industrial monitoring. Unlike BLE and Wi-Fi operating in 2.4 GHz, LoRa uses sub-GHz ISM bands (433/868/915 MHz) with superior propagation characteristics enabling multi-kilometer range.

LoRa Modulation and Spreading Factors

LoRa employs Chirp Spread Spectrum (CSS) modulation, trading data rate for range and robustness. Spreading Factor (SF) ranges from SF7 (fastest, shortest range) to SF12 (slowest, longest range). Each step doubles airtime and range while halving data rate. Typical performance shows SF7: 5-10 kbps, 2-5 km range, and SF12: 250-500 bps, 10-15 km range. Bandwidth options (125/250/500 kHz) trade range for data rate.

LoRaWAN Device Classes

LoRaWAN defines three device classes optimized for different use cases. Class A (lowest power) opens receive windows after each uplink with downlink possible only after uplink, minimal power consumption (<20 µA average), suitable for sensors with infrequent uplink. Class B (scheduled reception) synchronizes with network beacons, opens receive windows at scheduled intervals, enables downlink at predictable times with moderate power consumption (50-200 µA average). Class C (continuous reception) maintains receive window open except during transmission, lowest latency for downlink but highest power consumption (10-15 mA average), suitable for mains-powered applications.

Power Budget Example

A typical LoRaWAN sensor transmitting 50-byte payload once per hour achieves impressive battery life. With uplink transmission taking 1-2 seconds at 30-120 mA depending on SF, sleep current at 1-5 µA (deep sleep plus LoRa radio), and receive windows at 10-15 mA for 5-10ms, the average current calculates to 2-8 µA. This enables 5-10 year battery life on 2×AA batteries (5000 mAh capacity).

NB-IoT and LTE-M (Cellular IoT)

NB-IoT (Narrowband IoT) and LTE-M (LTE Cat-M1) represent 3GPP-standardized cellular technologies optimized for IoT applications. They leverage existing cellular infrastructure, providing global coverage in regions with cellular networks.

NB-IoT Characteristics

NB-IoT operates in licensed spectrum with carrier bandwidth of 180 kHz (single resource block), downlink speeds up to 250 kbps and uplink to 20-250 kbps, and excellent penetration (20 dB coupling loss improvement over GSM). Coverage reaches 1-10 km (cell tower dependent), transmit power peaks at 100-220 mA (23 dBm), and power-saving modes achieve <5 µA sleep current. Typical use cases include smart metering, asset tracking, environmental monitoring, and agricultural sensors.

LTE-M Characteristics

LTE-M provides higher data rates (1 Mbps downlink, 375 kbps uplink), supports voice (VoLTE), enables mobility and handover (unlike NB-IoT), and uses wider bandwidth (1.4 MHz, 6 resource blocks). Power consumption is moderate (80-150 mA transmit), range extends 1-10 km (cell tower dependent), and latency is lower than NB-IoT (10-100ms vs. 1-10s). Applications include wearables with voice, fleet tracking (mobile), and medical devices requiring real-time alerts.

Power-Saving Modes (PSM & eDRX)

Cellular IoT protocols incorporate sophisticated power-saving mechanisms. Power Saving Mode (PSM) allows the device to sleep for hours/days with radio off, no paging or downlink possible during sleep, wake-up for scheduled uplink, and <5 µA current consumption. Extended Discontinuous Reception (eDRX) extends paging intervals from seconds to minutes/hours (2.56s - 43 minutes), enables downlink at extended intervals, and provides moderate power savings (10-50 µA average). Combined PSM+eDRX enables years of battery life for infrequent reporting applications.

Wi-Fi for Smart Sensors

While Wi-Fi consumes more power than BLE or LoRaWAN, it offers high data rates, ubiquitous infrastructure, and direct internet connectivity. ESP32 and similar SoCs integrate Wi-Fi, BLE, and capable MCU in cost-effective packages.

Wi-Fi Power Optimization

Modern Wi-Fi chipsets support aggressive power saving. Modem sleep stops Wi-Fi radio between DTIM beacon intervals with the CPU remaining active, achieving 15-30 mA average. Light sleep adds CPU sleep between Wi-Fi activity for 5-15 mA average. Deep sleep disables Wi-Fi completely with periodic wake-up for data transmission, consuming <100 µA average. Typical usage patterns include wake every 5-60 minutes, connect to AP (1-3 seconds, 120-180 mA), transmit data (100-500ms, 120-150 mA), and disconnect and deep sleep (50-100 µA).

Protocol Selection Criteria

Choosing the optimal wireless protocol requires analyzing application requirements across multiple dimensions.

Range Requirements

Short-range (<100m) applications suit BLE, Wi-Fi, or Zigbee, ideal for wearables, smart home, and building automation. Medium-range (100m-1km) may use long-range BLE Coded PHY, Zigbee mesh, or LoRaWAN in urban areas. Long-range (>1km) requires LoRaWAN, NB-IoT, or LTE-M for agriculture, smart city, asset tracking, and remote monitoring.

Power Budget

Ultra-low-power (<20 µA average) suits LoRaWAN Class A or NB-IoT with PSM for multi-year coin cell operation. Low power (20-100 µA average) works with BLE optimized parameters or NB-IoT with eDRX for 1-5 year AA battery life. Medium power (100 µA - 1 mA average) enables Wi-Fi with deep sleep or BLE with frequent connections for weeks to months on rechargeable batteries.

Data Rate and Latency

Low data rate (<1 kbps) applications include periodic sensor readings and alarms. Medium data rate (1-100 kbps) suits sensor data with small images or audio snippets. High data rate (>100 kbps) requires Wi-Fi for video streaming, firmware updates, and high-resolution images. Latency-critical (<100ms) needs Wi-Fi, BLE, or LTE-M, while latency-tolerant (>1s) works with LoRaWAN or NB-IoT.

Mesh Networking

Mesh networks extend coverage through multi-hop routing, with each node relaying messages for others. BLE Mesh, Zigbee, and Thread represent the primary mesh protocols for smart sensors.

BLE Mesh

BLE Mesh uses managed flooding where messages propagate through the network with all nodes relaying broadcasts. It features many-to-many topology, up to 32,767 nodes (theoretical), provisioning and security built-in, and suitability for lighting control and building automation. However, it has higher power consumption than point-to-point BLE and requires careful network design to prevent congestion.

Zigbee and Thread

Both protocols use IEEE 802.15.4 PHY (2.4 GHz) with mesh routing. Zigbee offers mature ecosystem and profile support (Home Automation, Smart Energy), while Thread provides IP connectivity (IPv6), Matter compatibility, and Apple/Google support. Power consumption ranges from 25-35 mA transmit and 20-30 mA receive with 5-10 µA sleep.

This chapter explored IoT communication protocols for smart sensors, including BLE 5.x (short-range, 50-400m, low power), LoRaWAN (long-range, 2-15km, ultra-low power enabling years of battery life), NB-IoT/LTE-M (cellular infrastructure, 1-10km, PSM enabling multi-year operation), and Wi-Fi (high data rate, infrastructure-based, moderate power). Protocol selection depends on range requirements, power budget, data rate needs, infrastructure availability, and latency constraints. Chapter 6

Sensor Fusion Techniques

Sensor fusion combines data from multiple sensors to achieve more accurate, reliable, and comprehensive measurements than any single sensor could provide. This chapter explores fusion architectures, algorithms, and practical implementation considerations for smart sensor systems.

Fundamentals of Sensor Fusion

Sensor fusion addresses the fundamental limitations of individual sensors by combining complementary information sources. Each sensor type has characteristic strengths and weaknesses—accelerometers provide good short-term accuracy but drift over time, while GPS provides absolute position but with limited update rate and poor indoor coverage. By intelligently combining these sensors, fusion systems achieve performance exceeding any individual sensor.

The benefits of sensor fusion extend beyond accuracy improvement. Redundancy provides fault tolerance when sensors fail or produce erroneous readings. Complementary sensors cover different aspects of the measurand, creating a more complete picture. Cooperative sensors with overlapping coverage enable cross-validation and uncertainty reduction.

Fusion Architectures

Kalman Filtering

The Kalman filter is the foundational algorithm for sensor fusion in systems with linear dynamics and Gaussian noise. It provides optimal state estimation by recursively combining predictions from a system model with observations from sensors, weighting each by their respective uncertainties.

Standard Kalman Filter

// Kalman Filter Implementation for 1D Position/Velocity typedef struct { float x[2]; // State: [position, velocity] float P[2][2]; // State covariance float Q[2][2]; // Process noise covariance float R; // Measurement noise variance float dt; // Time step } KalmanFilter1D; void kalman_predict(KalmanFilter1D* kf) { // State prediction: x = F * x float pos_pred = kf->x[0] + kf->x[1] * kf->dt; float vel_pred = kf->x[1]; // Covariance prediction: P = F * P * F' + Q float P00 = kf->P[0][0] + kf->dt * (kf->P[1][0] + kf->P[0][1]) + kf->dt * kf->dt * kf->P[1][1] + kf->Q[0][0]; float P01 = kf->P[0][1] + kf->dt * kf->P[1][1] + kf->Q[0][1]; float P11 = kf->P[1][1] + kf->Q[1][1]; kf->x[0] = pos_pred; kf->x[1] = vel_pred; kf->P[0][0] = P00; kf->P[0][1] = P01; kf->P[1][0] = P01; kf->P[1][1] = P11; } void kalman_update(KalmanFilter1D* kf, float measurement) { // Innovation: y = z - H * x float y = measurement - kf->x[0]; // Innovation covariance: S = H * P * H' + R float S = kf->P[0][0] + kf->R; // Kalman gain: K = P * H' * S^-1 float K0 = kf->P[0][0] / S; float K1 = kf->P[1][0] / S; // State update: x = x + K * y kf->x[0] += K0 * y; kf->x[1] += K1 * y; // Covariance update: P = (I - K * H) * P kf->P[0][0] -= K0 * kf->P[0][0]; kf->P[0][1] -= K0 * kf->P[0][1]; kf->P[1][0] -= K1 * kf->P[0][0]; kf->P[1][1] -= K1 * kf->P[0][1]; }

Extended Kalman Filter (EKF)

Real-world systems often have nonlinear dynamics or measurement models. The Extended Kalman Filter handles nonlinearity by linearizing the system around the current state estimate using Jacobian matrices. While not optimal for highly nonlinear systems, EKF is widely used due to its computational efficiency and reasonable performance.

Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF)

The Unscented Kalman Filter provides better handling of nonlinearity by using sigma points to capture the mean and covariance of the state distribution through the nonlinear transformation. It avoids the need for Jacobian calculation and typically provides better accuracy than EKF for strongly nonlinear systems.

Complementary Filtering

Complementary filters provide a simpler alternative to Kalman filtering when sensors measure the same quantity with different frequency characteristics. By applying complementary high-pass and low-pass filters to different sensor signals, the strengths of each sensor are combined while their weaknesses are attenuated.

IMU Complementary Filter:
  • Accelerometer: Good long-term accuracy (gravity reference), noisy short-term → Low-pass filter
  • Gyroscope: Good short-term accuracy, drifts long-term → High-pass filter
  • Combined: angle = α * (angle + gyro * dt) + (1-α) * accel_angle
  • α: Typically 0.96-0.98, balances responsiveness and noise rejection
// Complementary Filter for Orientation typedef struct { float alpha; // Filter coefficient (0.96-0.98) float angle_roll; // Current roll estimate float angle_pitch; // Current pitch estimate } ComplementaryFilter; void complementary_update(ComplementaryFilter* cf, float gyro_x, float gyro_y, float gyro_z, float accel_x, float accel_y, float accel_z, float dt) { // Calculate angles from accelerometer float accel_roll = atan2f(accel_y, accel_z) * 180.0f / M_PI; float accel_pitch = atan2f(-accel_x, sqrtf(accel_y*accel_y + accel_z*accel_z)) * 180.0f / M_PI; // Integrate gyroscope (high-pass component) float gyro_roll = cf->angle_roll + gyro_x * dt; float gyro_pitch = cf->angle_pitch + gyro_y * dt; // Complementary filter fusion cf->angle_roll = cf->alpha * gyro_roll + (1.0f - cf->alpha) * accel_roll; cf->angle_pitch = cf->alpha * gyro_pitch + (1.0f - cf->alpha) * accel_pitch; }

Particle Filters

Particle filters (Sequential Monte Carlo methods) represent the state distribution using a set of weighted samples (particles). They can handle arbitrary nonlinearities and non-Gaussian distributions, making them suitable for challenging estimation problems where Kalman variants fail.

Multi-Sensor Fusion Applications

9-DOF IMU Fusion

Combining accelerometer, gyroscope, and magnetometer data provides full orientation estimation. The accelerometer provides roll and pitch reference through gravity, the magnetometer provides heading reference, and the gyroscope provides smooth high-frequency updates. Popular fusion algorithms include Madgwick and Mahony filters.

Indoor Positioning

Indoor positioning systems fuse multiple technologies to overcome individual limitations. WiFi fingerprinting provides room-level accuracy, BLE beacons improve local accuracy, IMU provides smooth tracking between updates, and barometers enable floor detection. The fusion system must handle intermittent availability and varying accuracy of each source.

Environmental Monitoring

Multi-modal environmental sensors combine temperature, humidity, pressure, air quality, and light sensors for comprehensive environmental characterization. Fusion algorithms can detect sensor faults through cross-validation, improve accuracy through redundancy, and derive higher-level information like thermal comfort indices.

Implementation Considerations

Sensor Calibration

Accurate fusion requires well-calibrated sensors. Calibration corrects for bias, scale factor errors, axis misalignment, and temperature dependencies. For IMUs, calibration involves characterizing the 3x3 misalignment matrix, scale factors, and biases for each sensor. Cross-axis coupling must also be considered.

Time Synchronization

Sensor fusion assumes synchronized measurements. In practice, sensors have different sampling rates, latencies, and timestamps. Interpolation, extrapolation, or explicit time-delay compensation may be needed. Hardware timestamping provides the most accurate synchronization.

Summary

Key Takeaways

  • Fusion Benefits: Improved accuracy, reliability, and completeness over single sensors
  • Architecture Levels: Data, feature, and decision-level fusion offer different trade-offs
  • Kalman Filtering: Optimal for linear Gaussian systems; EKF/UKF for nonlinear
  • Complementary Filters: Simple, effective for sensors with complementary frequency characteristics
  • Calibration Essential: Accurate fusion requires well-calibrated, synchronized sensors
  • Application-Specific: Fusion approach depends on sensors, dynamics, and requirements
