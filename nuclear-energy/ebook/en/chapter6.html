6.1 The SMR Revolution

Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) represent a paradigm shift in nuclear power deployment. Defined as reactors with electrical output of 300 megawatts or less, SMRs promise to address many challenges that have limited nuclear energy expansion. Through factory fabrication, enhanced safety, economic scalability, and deployment flexibility, SMRs could enable nuclear energy to serve diverse applications and markets beyond traditional large utility-scale power plants.

The SMR concept isn't entirely new—early nuclear reactors were relatively small, and nuclear submarines have successfully operated compact reactors for over 60 years. What's new is applying modern technology, manufacturing techniques, and licensing approaches to develop SMRs specifically designed for commercial deployment. This convergence of proven nuclear technology with advanced manufacturing and innovative design creates unprecedented opportunities.

Key SMR Advantages

SMRs offer multiple advantages that address traditional nuclear power limitations. Factory fabrication allows construction of major reactor components or entire reactor modules in controlled factory environments rather than on-site. This promises higher quality through better quality control, faster construction by eliminating weather delays and site constraints, lower costs through economies of serial production, and reduced construction risk by proving designs through multiple units before widespread deployment.

Enhanced passive safety features become practical at smaller scales. Passive systems rely on natural forces—gravity, natural circulation, evaporation—requiring no pumps, electrical power, or operator action. While challenging to implement in large reactors due to scaling physics, passive safety is natural for SMRs. Most advanced SMR designs can maintain safe conditions indefinitely following accidents without any active systems or external power.

Economic scalability addresses one of large nuclear plants' biggest challenges—enormous upfront capital requirements. An SMR can be deployed individually to match local demand, with additional units added incrementally as electricity demand grows. This modularity reduces financial risk, enables financing for smaller utilities or developing countries, and provides flexibility matching capacity expansion to actual demand growth.

Deployment flexibility opens markets previously inaccessible to nuclear power. SMRs can serve smaller electrical grids unable to absorb large plant output, provide process heat for industrial applications, support remote communities with limited grid connection, replace retiring coal plants using existing infrastructure, and integrate with renewable energy systems providing baseload stability.

6.2 Leading SMR Designs

Dozens of SMR concepts are under development worldwide, representing diverse approaches to reactor technology. Understanding leading designs illustrates the breadth of innovation occurring in advanced nuclear technology.

Light Water SMRs

The NuScale Power Module, developed in the United States, is among the most advanced SMRs approaching commercial deployment. Each NuScale module produces 77 MWe using a pressurized water reactor design with the reactor, steam generators, pressurizer, and containment integrated into a single compact vessel. This integrated design eliminates large-diameter primary loop piping, removing potential for large-break loss-of-coolant accidents entirely.

NuScale's defining feature is its passive safety system. Each reactor module sits in a pool of water within the containment. In any accident scenario—loss of power, loss of coolant, complete failure of all active systems—natural circulation removes heat from the reactor while water from the containment pool provides cooling. Heat is ultimately rejected to the atmosphere through simple evaporation from a large pool outside the containment. This system can function indefinitely without electrical power, diesel generators, or operator action.

A NuScale plant consists of up to 12 modules in a common pool, providing combined output up to 924 MWe with flexibility to operate any number of modules as needed. Each module operates independently—one can be shut down for refueling while others continue operation, maximizing plant availability. NuScale received design certification from the US NRC in 2020, the first SMR to achieve this milestone. The first NuScale plant is planned for deployment at Idaho National Laboratory in the late 2020s.

Russia's KLT-40S represents the only currently operating SMR design. Based on decades of icebreaker reactor experience, the KLT-40S powers floating nuclear plants—the Akademik Lomonosov provides electricity and heat to the remote Arctic port of Pevek, Russia. Each floating plant contains two 35 MWe reactors that can also provide 50 MW of thermal power for district heating or desalination. While unique in its floating deployment, the KLT-40S demonstrates that small reactors can operate reliably in challenging environments with limited infrastructure.

Advanced Reactor SMRs

Beyond light water SMRs, advanced reactor concepts promise even greater performance. High-temperature gas-cooled reactors use helium coolant and graphite moderation, achieving core outlet temperatures of 750°C or higher—far exceeding light water reactors' ~315°C. These high temperatures enable highly efficient electricity generation and direct process heat applications like hydrogen production, synthetic fuel production, or industrial processes requiring high-temperature heat.

China's HTR-PM (High Temperature Reactor-Pebble bed Module) achieved initial criticality in 2021, marking the world's first commercial high-temperature gas reactor. The HTR-PM consists of two 250 MWt reactor modules driving a single 210 MWe turbine. Each reactor uses TRISO fuel in spherical pebbles continuously circulated through the core, providing online refueling and excellent fuel utilization. The design's small core size and passive cooling enable inherent safety—even complete loss of cooling cannot cause fuel damage, as decay heat dissipates passively through the reactor vessel.

Molten salt reactors represent another advanced concept gaining attention. The Terrestrial Energy Integral Molten Salt Reactor (IMSR) uses liquid fuel salt containing dissolved uranium. This approach offers multiple advantages: no fuel fabrication required (fuel is added as liquid), online fission product removal possible, strong negative temperature coefficients providing inherent safety, and operation at atmospheric pressure despite high temperatures. The IMSR targets 195 MWe output with compact design suitable for factory fabrication and truck transport.

Fast neutron SMRs promise ultimate fuel sustainability by breeding new fuel from uranium-238 while consuming existing plutonium stocks. The TerraPower Natrium reactor combines 345 MWe sodium-cooled fast reactor with molten salt energy storage, enabling the reactor to operate at constant power while output to the grid varies from 100 MWe to 500 MWe as needed. This flexibility supports grid integration with variable renewable energy sources. The first Natrium plant is planned for Wyoming on the site of a retiring coal plant, demonstrating nuclear energy's potential to revitalize coal communities.

6.3 Manufacturing and Construction

SMR economics depend fundamentally on successful factory fabrication and modular construction. This represents a significant departure from traditional nuclear construction and requires new approaches to design, manufacturing, supply chain, and construction management.

Factory Fabrication Benefits

Factory construction offers numerous advantages over traditional stick-built approaches. Controlled environments eliminate weather delays, temperature extremes, and exposure that challenge outdoor construction. Factory workers operate from permanent facilities with better equipment, improving productivity and working conditions compared to temporary construction sites. Quality control is inherently better in factories through standardized processes, automated inspection, and experienced workforce trained on repeated tasks.

Learning curves through serial production drive continuous cost reduction. The first-of-a-kind module requires significant learning, tooling development, and process refinement. Subsequent modules benefit from this experience—workers become more efficient, processes are streamlined, problems identified in early units are corrected. Studies suggest each doubling of production quantity could reduce costs 10-20% through learning effects, potentially reducing costs by half or more over a production run of 100+ modules.

Factory fabrication enables advanced manufacturing techniques including automated welding with 100% inspection for nuclear-grade welds, 3D printing of complex components reducing manufacturing time and cost, prefabricated modular assemblies combining multiple components, and integrated testing of completed modules before shipment ensuring quality.

Transportation and Site Assembly

SMR deployment requires transporting large modules from factories to sites, then assembling into complete plants. Maximum practical shipping dimensions constrain SMR designs—about 5 meters diameter for rail or barge transport, larger for ocean transport or specialized heavy haul. Most SMR designs fit within these constraints, though transport requires specialized equipment and planning.

On-site work focuses on assembling delivered modules into complete plants—setting modules on foundations, making interconnections between modules and to balance-of-plant systems, installing instrumentation and control systems, and completing testing and startup. This approach dramatically reduces on-site construction time from 5-7 years typical for traditional plants to potentially 2-3 years for SMRs.

Supply Chain Development

Successful SMR deployment requires developing robust supply chains capable of fabricating nuclear-grade components at scale. This presents challenges—nuclear supply chain capacity declined as new construction stagnated in many countries, specialized capabilities like large forgings are concentrated in few suppliers globally, and regulatory qualification for nuclear components requires time and investment.

However, SMR deployment drives supply chain revival. Serial production provides business predictability encouraging supplier investment in capacity and capability. Modular designs use smaller components within capabilities of more numerous suppliers. Factory fabrication allows close integration between SMR designers and suppliers, improving quality and reducing costs. As SMR deployment scales, supply chains will expand and mature, further improving economics and construction timelines.

6.4 Economic Considerations

SMR economics are simultaneously promising and uncertain. Proven cost reductions from factory fabrication and learning effects offer potential for economics competitive with other electricity sources, yet first-of-a-kind SMR deployment will likely experience cost overruns typical of pioneering technology.

Capital Cost Projections

Capital costs for first SMR plants will likely exceed initial projections. NuScale's target capital cost for its first US plant is approximately $5,000-6,000 per kWe—higher than large nuclear plants but including first-of-a-kind costs. Subsequent plants should see significant cost reduction through learning. Studies suggest that by the 10th NuScale plant, costs could decrease 30-40%, with further decreases as deployment scales.

International SMR developers project competitive economics. China's HTR-PM capital cost is reported around $3,000 per kWe, benefiting from lower labor costs and China's efficient nuclear supply chain. Russian SMRs similarly project costs in the $3,000-4,000 per kWe range. These figures suggest that with sufficient deployment and learning, SMRs can achieve capital costs competitive with combined-cycle gas plants, coal plants with carbon capture, or large-scale renewables with storage.

Operating Costs and Economics

SMR operating costs per MWh will likely exceed large reactors due to economies of scale—fewer MWh over which to spread fixed costs. However, SMR designs emphasizing simplification, passive systems, and reduced staffing requirements target operating costs competitive with large plants. NuScale projects operation with approximately 50 staff per 12-module plant versus 400-700 staff typical for large plants, though whether this can be achieved in practice remains to be demonstrated.

SMR economic advantages emerge in specific applications. For replacing retiring coal plants, SMRs can reuse existing site infrastructure, transmission connection, and cooling systems, significantly reducing capital costs. For remote or off-grid applications where alternatives involve expensive diesel generation or long transmission lines, SMRs become economically competitive despite higher costs. For industrial applications requiring both electricity and process heat, SMR cogeneration efficiency exceeds electricity-only alternatives.

Financing and Risk

Perhaps SMRs' greatest economic advantage is reduced financial risk through modularity. Building a $10 billion large nuclear plant requires committing enormous capital upfront with no revenue for 5-7 years. Cost overruns threaten project viability—several recent large nuclear projects have experienced 50-100% cost increases, creating financial stress for utilities and investors.

SMR modularity reduces this risk. A utility can invest in one or a few SMR modules with capital requirements of perhaps $500 million to $1.5 billion, bringing modules online in 2-3 years to begin revenue generation, then adding capacity incrementally as needed. This approach better matches electricity demand growth, reduces exposure to construction cost uncertainty, and enables financing for smaller utilities unable to finance large plants. For developing countries, SMR economics and scale make nuclear power accessible for the first time.

6.5 Applications Beyond Electricity

SMRs enable nuclear energy applications beyond traditional baseload electricity generation. These diverse applications could ultimately represent larger markets than utility power generation.

Industrial Process Heat

Many industrial processes require high-temperature heat currently supplied by burning fossil fuels. Chemical plants, refineries, steel mills, cement factories, and other heavy industries consume enormous thermal energy, producing substantial CO₂ emissions. Nuclear heat could decarbonize these industries while providing cost-competitive reliable energy.

High-temperature SMRs like gas-cooled and molten salt reactors can supply process heat at temperatures suitable for most industrial processes. Chemical plants can use nuclear heat for steam cracking, hydrogen production, ammonia synthesis, and other processes. Refineries can replace fired heaters with nuclear steam. Steel plants can use nuclear heat for direct reduced iron production, eliminating coal use. The industrial heat market is vast—potentially larger than the electricity market—representing enormous opportunity for SMR deployment.

Hydrogen Production

Hydrogen is increasingly recognized as essential for decarbonization, particularly for applications difficult to electrify—heavy transport, aviation, industrial feedstocks, seasonal energy storage. Most hydrogen today is produced from natural gas in steam methane reforming, emitting substantial CO₂. Clean hydrogen production is crucial for hydrogen economy development.

Nuclear energy can produce hydrogen through multiple pathways. High-temperature electrolysis uses nuclear electricity and high-temperature heat to split water into hydrogen and oxygen more efficiently than conventional electrolysis. Thermochemical cycles use nuclear heat alone to drive chemical reactions that split water without requiring electricity. Both approaches can produce hydrogen at costs competitive with or lower than fossil-derived hydrogen, without CO₂ emissions. SMRs co-located with hydrogen production could supply clean hydrogen for transportation, industry, and energy storage, strengthening both nuclear deployment and hydrogen economy development.

Desalination

Water scarcity affects billions globally, driving increasing interest in desalination. Nuclear-powered desalination combines electricity generation with waste heat utilization for thermal desalination or direct electricity use for reverse osmosis. This cogeneration achieves high efficiency, producing both electricity and fresh water economically.

SMRs are particularly well-suited for desalination. Their compact size enables deployment near coastal cities requiring water. Multiple modules can provide scalability matching water demand growth. Passive safety features address concerns about nuclear facilities near population centers. Several countries with limited water resources but strong nuclear programs—including UAE, China, and Saudi Arabia—are actively developing nuclear desalination capabilities.

Remote Community Power

Remote communities often rely on expensive diesel generation, experiencing electricity costs many times higher than grid-connected regions while suffering poor reliability and environmental impacts from diesel combustion and fuel transport. SMRs could transform remote community energy access.

Micro-reactors (reactors below 20 MWe) target this application specifically. The US Department of Defense is developing micro-reactors for forward operating bases, proving concepts applicable to remote communities. Canada's Ontario Power Generation is developing SMRs for remote mining operations and northern communities. These applications value reliability and fuel security over absolute cost, making nuclear economics favorable despite higher capital costs.

6.6 Regulatory Framework and Licensing

SMR success requires efficient regulatory frameworks that maintain safety excellence while avoiding unnecessary conservatism that inflates costs and schedules. Regulatory approaches are evolving to address SMR characteristics.

Design Certification

Modern regulatory frameworks support design certification—comprehensive review and approval of standardized designs independent of specific site applications. Once certified, the design can be referenced in multiple plant license applications, avoiding repeated review of identical designs. This approach suits SMRs perfectly, as their value proposition depends on standardization and replication.

The US NRC has certified several large reactor designs and granted first SMR design certification to NuScale in 2020. Canada is developing comparable frameworks. The UK's generic design assessment provides similar standardized review. These frameworks dramatically reduce licensing uncertainty and cost for subsequent plants using certified designs.

Risk-Informed Performance-Based Regulation

Traditional nuclear regulation developed around large water-cooled reactors, sometimes imposing prescriptive requirements based on those designs rather than performance objectives. Advanced SMRs with fundamentally different designs and safety approaches may not fit prescriptive frameworks despite meeting or exceeding safety objectives.

Risk-informed, performance-based regulation judges designs against safety objectives rather than prescriptive requirements, using probabilistic risk assessment to quantify safety margins. This approach allows innovative designs to demonstrate safety through analysis and testing rather than conformance to requirements developed for different reactor types. Several countries including the US, Canada, and UK are advancing risk-informed frameworks specifically to enable advanced reactor licensing.

Conclusion

Small Modular Reactors represent nuclear energy's most promising near-term advance, addressing traditional nuclear power challenges through factory fabrication, enhanced safety, economic modularity, and deployment flexibility. While first-of-a-kind units will face typical pioneering challenges and costs, the potential for dramatic cost reduction through learning and scale production offers pathways to economics competitive with all alternatives. Beyond electricity, SMRs enable diverse applications—industrial heat, hydrogen production, desalination, remote power—potentially expanding nuclear energy's contribution far beyond its current ~10% of global electricity. Through implementation of standards like WIA-ENE-008, SMR deployment can proceed safely and efficiently, realizing nuclear energy's potential to benefit all humanity through clean, reliable, versatile energy.
