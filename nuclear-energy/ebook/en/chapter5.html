5.1 Categories of Nuclear Waste

Nuclear waste management is often cited as a major challenge for nuclear energy, yet nuclear power is unique among major energy sources in that it contains, monitors, and manages 100% of its waste products. A coal plant producing equivalent electricity releases millions of tons of carbon dioxide, toxic metals, and particulates into the environment, while nuclear plants contain all radioactive materials within controlled facilities. Understanding nuclear waste requires recognizing that not all radioactive waste is equal—it varies dramatically in radioactivity, volume, and required management.

Low-Level Waste (LLW)

Low-level waste constitutes about 90% of nuclear waste volume but contains less than 1% of the radioactivity. LLW includes contaminated protective clothing, tools, filters, rags, and other materials from nuclear facilities. Most LLW is only slightly radioactive—often barely above background levels—and decays to safe levels within decades or less. Some LLW can be decontaminated and released from regulatory control entirely.

LLW is typically managed through near-surface disposal facilities where waste is buried in trenches or vaults 5-30 meters deep. These facilities use engineered barriers like clay liners, covers, and monitoring systems to prevent migration of radioactivity. LLW disposal is routine and well-established, with numerous operating facilities worldwide. The United States, for example, has four commercial LLW disposal sites serving different regions of the country.

Intermediate-Level Waste (ILW)

Intermediate-level waste requires shielding but generally doesn't generate significant heat. ILW includes reactor components like control rods, filters and resins from water purification, and materials from decommissioning. Some ILW contains long-lived radionuclides requiring isolation for centuries or longer, necessitating more robust disposal systems than LLW but less demanding than high-level waste.

ILW management typically involves conditioning the waste (solidifying, encapsulation) for safe handling and disposal, interim storage in shielded facilities, and eventual disposal in geological repositories, often the same facilities intended for high-level waste. Some countries, like the United Kingdom, have developed specialized intermediate-level waste repositories separate from high-level waste facilities.

High-Level Waste (HLW)

High-level waste contains 95% of total radioactivity from nuclear operations despite being only a few percent of waste volume. HLW includes spent nuclear fuel (in countries with once-through fuel cycles) and the concentrated fission products from reprocessing (in countries with closed fuel cycles). HLW generates significant heat from radioactive decay and requires cooling, shielding, and long-term isolation from the biosphere.

A typical large nuclear reactor generates about 20 cubic meters of HLW per year—roughly the volume of a small car. Over its entire 60-80 year operational life, a reactor produces about 1,200-1,600 cubic meters of HLW. This is remarkably small compared to the waste from fossil fuels: a coal plant of equivalent output produces over 400,000 tons of toxic ash annually plus millions of tons of CO₂ released to the atmosphere.

5.2 Geological Disposal of High-Level Waste

The international scientific consensus supports deep geological disposal as the safest long-term solution for high-level waste. This approach isolates waste deep underground in stable geological formations, using multiple engineered and natural barriers to prevent radionuclide migration to the biosphere.

Multi-Barrier Concept

Geological disposal relies on defense-in-depth through multiple independent barriers. The waste form itself—ceramic fuel pellets or vitrified glass containing reprocessing waste—is highly resistant to dissolution and radionuclide release. These materials are immobilized in durable matrices designed to remain stable for hundreds of thousands of years. Waste containers, typically thick metal canisters made of copper, steel, or other corrosion-resistant materials, provide containment for thousands to tens of thousands of years even in contact with groundwater.

Engineered barriers surround waste containers. Bentonite clay buffer materials swell when wet, creating impermeable barriers that also slow any radionuclide movement through chemical sorption. Repository tunnels are backfilled with carefully selected materials that limit water flow and provide structural stability. Finally, the geological formation itself—granite, clay, salt, or volcanic tuff selected for stability, low permeability, and favorable chemistry—provides the ultimate barrier, isolating waste from the biosphere for geological timeframes.

Repository Development Programs

Several countries have made substantial progress toward geological repository implementation. Finland leads globally with its Onkalo repository at Olkiluoto, designed to dispose of 5,500 tons of spent fuel in crystalline bedrock 400-450 meters deep. Construction began in 2004, and the facility is scheduled to begin waste emplacement in the mid-2020s. Finland's success reflects early commitment to site selection, effective stakeholder engagement, and sustained political will.

Sweden follows closely with its planned repository at Forsmark, using a nearly identical design to Finland's. The Swedish repository received regulatory approval in 2022 after decades of development, demonstrating that democratic societies can make difficult decisions about nuclear waste through transparent, science-based processes that build public confidence.

France is developing a repository in clay formations at Bure in eastern France. The Cigéo project will dispose of both high and intermediate-level waste 500 meters underground in Callovo-Oxfordian clay that has remained stable for 150 million years. France's program benefits from advanced waste conditioning from decades of reprocessing experience and comprehensive research at underground laboratories.

The United States selected Yucca Mountain in Nevada for repository development in 1987, conducted extensive scientific investigations through a 25-year program, and submitted a license application in 2008. However, political opposition led to defunding in 2011, leaving the US without an active repository program despite spending over $15 billion on Yucca Mountain development. Current US policy emphasizes consent-based siting, seeking communities willing to host facilities. Meanwhile, spent fuel remains in safe dry cask storage at reactor sites.

Safety Case Development

Repository licensing requires demonstrating safety for up to one million years—far beyond human historical experience. This safety case integrates understanding of geology, hydrology, geochemistry, materials science, and radionuclide transport, combining site characterization data, laboratory experiments, natural analog studies, and sophisticated computer modeling.

Natural analog studies examine geological formations where uranium ore bodies have existed for millions or billions of years. The Oklo natural reactors in Gabon, Africa, where natural nuclear chain reactions occurred 2 billion years ago, demonstrate that fission products remained essentially immobile over geological time despite primitive containment—just the host rock itself. Such studies provide confidence that engineered repository systems with multiple enhanced barriers will perform as predicted.

Performance assessment models simulate repository behavior over hundreds of thousands of years, accounting for container corrosion, waste dissolution, radionuclide transport through barriers, and eventual release to the biosphere. Even in pessimistic scenarios assuming barrier failures, calculated doses to future populations remain far below natural background radiation levels, demonstrating robust protection.

5.3 Interim Storage Technologies

Before final disposal, high-level waste requires safe storage, often for many decades. This interim storage serves multiple purposes: allowing radioactivity and heat generation to decrease, providing flexibility in disposal timing, and in some cases, enabling retrievability if future generations develop better management approaches or wish to recycle waste materials.

Wet Storage in Pools

As discussed in Chapter 4, spent fuel is initially stored in water pools adjacent to reactors. These pools provide effective cooling, radiation shielding, and monitoring. Pool storage is proven technology with excellent safety records extending over 60 years. Water provides visual access for inspections and allows fuel handling using underwater tools. However, pools require active cooling systems, electrical power, and water makeup, creating dependencies that must be managed carefully.

Pool safety has received intensive scrutiny following the Fukushima accident, where loss of cooling to spent fuel pools raised concerns about potential fires or steam explosions if pools drained. Post-Fukushima analyses confirmed that pools are robust—fuel remains coolable even if pools partially drain, provided timely water addition. Nevertheless, the accident prompted enhancements: improved water makeup capabilities, instrumentation to measure pool levels remotely, and procedures for adding water under severe conditions including using fire engines or portable pumps.

Dry Cask Storage

Dry cask storage technology was introduced in the 1980s and has matured into standard practice for long-term interim storage. Fuel is loaded into sealed steel canisters filled with inert helium, placed in massive shielded casks, and stored on concrete pads. Once loaded, casks require no active systems—cooling occurs passively through natural convection and radiation. This passive nature provides exceptional robustness against extended power loss, natural disasters, or other events that might disable active systems.

Numerous cask designs have been licensed, including both metal and concrete casks. Metal casks use thick steel or ductile cast iron for both structure and shielding. Concrete casks use steel liners surrounded by heavily reinforced concrete providing both shielding and structural strength. Both types perform equivalently from safety perspectives, with selection depending on cost, manufacturing capability, and site-specific factors.

Experience with dry cask storage now exceeds 40 years and hundreds of thousands of cask-years of operation worldwide. Monitoring programs confirm cask integrity through radiation measurements, leak testing, and periodic inspections. In some cases, casks loaded decades ago have been opened to inspect fuel condition—examinations confirm fuel remains in excellent condition with no degradation from long-term storage. This extensive experience provides confidence that dry cask storage can safely continue for 100 years or more if necessary.

5.4 Advanced Waste Management Approaches

While geological disposal and interim storage are established technologies, research continues on advanced approaches that could further reduce waste volumes, toxicity, or disposal requirements.

Waste Partitioning and Transmutation

Partitioning and transmutation (P&T) aims to separate long-lived radionuclides from waste, then irradiate them in reactors or accelerators to convert them to shorter-lived or stable isotopes. Minor actinides (neptunium, americium, curium) and certain fission products (technetium-99, iodine-129) are primary targets due to their long half-lives and radiotoxicity.

If fully implemented, P&T could reduce waste repository requirements dramatically. Instead of repositories needing to isolate waste for hundreds of thousands of years, they might only need to contain fission products for several hundred years until radioactivity decays to negligible levels. This would significantly simplify repository design and safety cases.

However, P&T faces substantial challenges. Separating minor actinides from waste is chemically difficult and expensive. Fabricating targets or fuel containing these materials requires remote handling due to high radiation levels. Irradiation must occur in fast neutron spectra to achieve efficient transmutation, requiring fast reactors or accelerator-driven systems. Multiple recycle passes through reactors are needed to achieve high transmutation efficiency. Overall, P&T would add significant complexity and cost to the fuel cycle.

Several countries, particularly France and Japan, have conducted extensive P&T research. France's ASTRID fast reactor program (now suspended) aimed to demonstrate industrial-scale transmutation. Japan has built experimental facilities for minor actinide handling and fabrication. While P&T remains a long-term option, current consensus is that geological disposal should proceed regardless of P&T implementation, as repositories will still be needed for some waste streams.

Deep Borehole Disposal

An alternative to mined repositories is deep borehole disposal, where waste is emplaced in boreholes drilled 3-5 kilometers deep into crystalline basement rock. At these depths, rock is essentially impermeable, groundwater movement is negligible, and the enormous overburden pressure ensures long-term stability. Waste would be sealed in steel canisters stacked in the lower portion of boreholes, with the upper portions filled with bentonite and cement to prevent any upward migration.

Deep boreholes offer potential advantages: faster deployment since drilling technology is mature and commercially available, lower cost than mined repositories through avoided excavation, siting flexibility since suitable geology is widespread, and enhanced security through inaccessibility—recovering waste from 5 km depth would be extraordinarily difficult. However, boreholes also present challenges: limited capacity per hole requires numerous boreholes for large waste inventories, waste must be repackaged into smaller canisters suitable for borehole dimensions, and retrievability is limited compared to mined repositories.

Research programs in several countries are evaluating deep boreholes. The United States conducted a field demonstration drilling a 5.2 km borehole in crystalline rock to demonstrate technical feasibility. While initially promising, current focus remains on mined repositories as the primary disposal path, with deep boreholes potentially complementing repository capacity or serving specialized waste streams.

5.5 Decommissioning and Site Restoration

Nuclear facilities have finite operating lives, requiring eventual decommissioning. This process entails safely dismantling facilities, managing radioactive materials, and restoring sites for unrestricted use or other purposes. Far from being a problem, decommissioning represents an opportunity to demonstrate nuclear energy's complete lifecycle management.

Decommissioning Strategies

Three main decommissioning strategies exist. Immediate dismantlement (DECON) begins soon after reactor shutdown, removing equipment and structures and releasing the site within a few years. This approach minimizes long-term costs and uncertainties but requires working with more radioactive materials, increasing worker exposure and waste management challenges.

Safe enclosure (SAFSTOR) involves securing the facility for decades allowing radioactive decay before final dismantlement. This approach reduces worker exposure and may allow some materials to decay to unrestricted release levels, reducing waste volumes. However, it extends the overall decommissioning period and maintains surveillance requirements for decades.

Entombment permanently encloses radioactive materials in concrete or other long-lived structures, with the site remaining under institutional control indefinitely. This approach has rarely been used for power reactors, as it prevents complete site release and creates permanent land use restrictions.

Most power reactor decommissioning programs use DECON or combinations of DECON and SAFSTOR—removing equipment and systems quickly while delaying reactor vessel and biological shield removal for a cooling-down period. This optimizes schedule, cost, and worker protection.

Decommissioning Experience

Over 150 nuclear power reactors have been permanently shut down worldwide, with about 20 completely decommissioned to "greenfield" status where no restrictions remain on site use. These projects demonstrate that decommissioning is technically straightforward using conventional demolition techniques adapted for radiological controls.

The Maine Yankee reactor in the United States was fully decommissioned from shutdown to site release in just 8 years at a cost of about $700 million—higher than initial estimates but manageable compared to the plant's $3.6 billion in lifetime revenue. The site is now available for unrestricted use, with no radiological restrictions remaining. This and similar successes prove that decommissioning fulfills nuclear energy's commitment to complete lifecycle management.

Financial Assurance

Ensuring funds are available for eventual decommissioning is essential. Most countries require nuclear operators to establish decommissioning funds during operations, collecting small amounts from electricity sales over the plant's life. By shutdown, sufficient funds have accumulated to cover decommissioning costs. The United States requires approximately 0.1 cent per kWh be set aside—barely noticeable in electricity prices but sufficient to fully fund decommissioning.

These funds are maintained in protected trust accounts, invested conservatively to earn returns over time. Regulatory oversight ensures fund adequacy, periodically updating cost estimates and adjusting collection rates if necessary. This approach has proven effective—no operating reactor in developed countries lacks adequate decommissioning funding.

5.6 Environmental Monitoring and Long-Term Stewardship

Responsible waste management requires comprehensive environmental monitoring during operations and long after facility closure. This monitoring confirms that engineered barriers function as designed and provides early warning of any unexpected releases.

Operational Environmental Monitoring

Nuclear facilities conduct extensive environmental monitoring measuring radioactivity in air, water, soil, vegetation, and food products. Sampling stations are positioned around facilities at varying distances, with some stations located in prevailing downwind and downstream directions, while others serve as background references in unaffected areas.

Air monitoring includes both particulate (filters) and gaseous (charcoal filters, real-time monitors) sampling. Water samples from surface water, groundwater wells, and drinking water sources are analyzed for radioactivity. Soil and sediment samples track any long-term accumulation. Vegetation, milk, and food crops in areas surrounding facilities are sampled to detect any uptake into the food chain.

Decades of environmental monitoring data demonstrate that releases from nuclear facilities are minimal and result in negligible public doses, typically 0.01 mSv per year or less—less than 0.3% of natural background. This extensive monitoring, far exceeding requirements for other industries, provides transparency and public confidence.

Post-Closure Monitoring and Institutional Control

After facilities close, environmental monitoring continues to confirm site conditions remain protective. For waste disposal facilities, monitoring may continue for decades or centuries, gradually transitioning from active monitoring to passive institutional controls as confidence in facility performance grows.

Long-term stewardship recognizes that while engineered systems are designed to function without active maintenance, society has responsibility to maintain knowledge about waste locations and ensure future generations can make informed decisions. This includes maintaining records, preserving knowledge through archives and monuments, and potentially maintaining land use controls preventing inappropriate activities that might compromise disposal system integrity.

5.7 WIA-ENE-008 Waste Management Standards

The WIA-ENE-008 standard establishes comprehensive waste management requirements emphasizing complete lifecycle responsibility:

Waste Minimization: Design and operational practices minimize waste generation at the source. Equipment is maintained to prevent leaks and contamination. Processes are optimized to reduce secondary waste from water treatment and operations. Materials are segregated to avoid contaminating clean materials.

Waste Classification and Characterization: All waste is properly classified by radioactive content, physical and chemical properties, and hazard levels. Characterization ensures appropriate handling, treatment, and disposal matching waste characteristics to disposal facility requirements.

Treatment and Conditioning: Waste is processed to reduce volume, stabilize contents, and meet disposal facility waste acceptance criteria. Technologies include incineration, compaction, solidification, and vitrification as appropriate for waste type and disposal requirements.

Interim Storage: Before disposal, waste is stored safely using appropriate technologies—pools for spent fuel initially, dry casks for long-term storage, engineered buildings for other waste types. All storage facilities maintain multiple barriers preventing radionuclide release.

Disposal: Permanent disposal in facilities appropriate to waste classification and characteristics. Near-surface facilities for LLW, geological repositories for HLW and long-lived ILW. Disposal facilities must demonstrate safety for required timeframes through comprehensive safety assessments.

Financial Responsibility: Operators establish financial assurance for all waste management and decommissioning obligations, ensuring resources are available when needed regardless of company financial condition.

Transparency: Public access to information about waste inventories, management practices, environmental monitoring results, and long-term plans. Stakeholder engagement in decision-making, particularly for siting disposal facilities.

Conclusion

Nuclear waste management represents both technical achievement and ongoing commitment. Unlike other energy sources that disperse waste into the environment, nuclear power contains and manages all its waste products. While challenges remain—particularly political and social rather than technical—solutions exist and are being implemented. Geological disposal will safely isolate high-level waste for geological timeframes. Interim storage provides safe management until disposal facilities are available. Decommissioning returns sites to unrestricted use, closing the nuclear fuel cycle completely. Through implementation of comprehensive standards like WIA-ENE-008, the nuclear industry demonstrates that energy can be produced responsibly, benefiting current generations while protecting future generations and the environment.
