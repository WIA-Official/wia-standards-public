# Chapter 6: Intellectual Property and Open Source Biology

## Overview

The intersection of intellectual property rights and open sharing principles creates complex tensions in synthetic biology. This chapter explores the patent landscape, open source licensing models including the BioBricks Public Agreement, Material Transfer Agreements, and strategies for balancing innovation incentives with accessibility.

## 6.1 Intellectual Property Fundamentals

### Types of IP Protection

**Patents**:
- Protect inventions (processes, compositions, methods)
- Exclusive rights for limited time (20 years from filing)
- Requires: novelty, non-obviousness, utility
- Must disclose invention fully
- Territorial (must file in each country)

**Copyright**:
- Protects expression (text, code, images)
- Automatic upon creation
- Long duration (life + 70 years typically)
- Doesn't protect ideas, only expression

**Trade Secrets**:
- Confidential business information
- No registration required
- Protection lasts as long as secret maintained
- Examples: Coca-Cola formula, Google algorithm

**Trademarks**:
- Protect brand identifiers
- Names, logos, slogans
- Can last indefinitely with use
- Example: "CRISPR" as trademark

**Database Rights** (EU):
- Protect substantial investments in databases
- 15-year protection
- Relevant for sequence databases

### Patentability in Biotechnology

**Diamond v. Chakrabarty** (1980):
- US Supreme Court case
- Ruled genetically modified organism can be patented
- "Anything under the sun made by man" is patentable
- Opened door to biotech patenting

**What Can Be Patented**:
- Isolated genes and sequences (contentious, varies by jurisdiction)
- Genetically modified organisms
- Methods and processes
- Compositions of matter
- Kits and systems

**What Cannot Be Patented**:
- Laws of nature
- Natural phenomena
- Abstract ideas
- Human organisms (in most jurisdictions)

**Association for Molecular Pathology v. Myriad Genetics** (2013):
- US Supreme Court
- Naturally occurring DNA sequences not patentable
- Synthetic cDNA (complementary DNA) is patentable
- Significantly narrowed gene patenting

**Europe**:
- European Patent Convention
- Discoveries vs. inventions distinction
- Biotechnology Directive (98/44/EC)
- Generally more restrictive than US

## 6.2 Patent Landscape in Synthetic Biology

### Foundational Patents

**Key Patent Areas**:

**CRISPR-Cas9** (major patent dispute):
- **Broad Institute** (Feng Zhang): Eukaryotic cells
- **UC Berkeley/Vienna** (Doudna/Charpentier): General CRISPR
- **Other players**: Vilnius University, Sigma-Aldrich
- Ongoing licensing battles
- Billions of dollars at stake

**BioBricks Assembly**:
- Tom Knight et al.
- Some BioBricks methods patented initially
- Later donated to public or not enforced
- Intention to keep accessible

**Gibson Assembly**:
- Developed at JCVI (J. Craig Venter Institute)
- Patent held by Synthetic Genomics
- Licensed to New England Biolabs (NEB)
- NEB provides reagents under patent

**Golden Gate / Type IIS Assembly**:
- Multiple patents from various institutions
- Relatively permissive licensing
- Widely used despite patents

**Gene Synthesis**:
- Methods for efficient DNA synthesis
- Oligo assembly techniques
- Error correction methods
- Held by various companies and universities

**Metabolic Pathways**:
- Biofuel production pathways
- Pharmaceutical precursors
- Industrial chemical synthesis
- Extensive patenting by companies

### Patent Thickets

**Definition**: Overlapping patent rights requiring multiple licenses

**Problem in Synthetic Biology**:
- Build complex system → may infringe multiple patents
- Each part potentially patented
- Assembly methods patented
- Measurement tools patented
- Optimization methods patented

**Example Scenario**:
To create engineered bacteria for biofuel:
1. Need promoter (Patent A)
2. Need enzyme genes from pathway (Patents B, C, D)
3. Need assembly method (Patent E)
4. Need optimization algorithm (Patent F)
5. Need host organism strain (Patent G)
6. Need fermentation process (Patent H)

**Result**: Complex licensing negotiations, high costs, innovation barrier

**Solutions**:
- Patent pools
- Cross-licensing agreements
- Defensive publication (prior art)
- Open source approaches

### Freedom to Operate (FTO)

**Definition**: Ability to develop and commercialize without infringing others' patents

**FTO Analysis**:
1. Identify relevant patents
2. Assess claims scope
3. Determine infringement risk
4. Evaluate patent validity
5. Consider licensing or design-around

**Costs**:
- Comprehensive FTO search: $10,000-$50,000+
- Continuous monitoring as technology develops
- Legal fees if licensing needed

**Strategies**:
- Monitor patent landscape early
- Design around problematic patents
- Secure licenses proactively
- Use non-patented alternatives
- Challenge invalid patents

## 6.3 Open Source Biology Movement

### Philosophy and Motivation

**Core Principles**:
- Knowledge as public good
- Collaborative innovation faster than proprietary
- Reduce barriers to entry
- Democratize biotechnology
- Ethical obligation to share

**Inspirations**:
- Open source software (Linux, Apache, etc.)
- Open access publishing
- Wikipedia model
- Maker movement

**Benefits**:
- Accelerated innovation
- Reduced redundancy
- Enabling education
- Broader participation
- Rapid problem-solving (e.g., COVID-19 response)

**Challenges**:
- Funding sustainability
- Quality control
- Commercial exploitation concerns
- University tech transfer conflicts

### Open Source Licenses in Biology

**Creative Commons** (for documentation, not DNA):
- **CC0**: Public domain dedication
- **CC-BY**: Attribution required
- **CC-BY-SA**: Attribution + share-alike
- **CC-BY-NC**: Attribution + non-commercial only

**BioBricks Public Agreement** (BPA):
- Specific to biological parts
- Share-alike provision
- Allow commercial use
- Prevent restriction of parts

**OpenMTA** (Material Transfer Agreement):
- Alternative to traditional MTAs
- Reduce legal barriers
- Allow redistribution
- Developed by OpenPlant

**Open Source Seed Initiative**:
- Prevent patenting of shared seeds
- Maintain commons
- Model for biological materials

## 6.4 BioBricks Public Agreement (BPA)

### Structure and Intent

**Established**: 2009, BioBricks Foundation

**Goals**:
- Enable free use of standard biological parts
- Prevent patent restrictions on parts
- Allow commercial use and development
- Require improvements to be shared back

**Key Terms**:

**Grant of Rights**:
- Contributor grants rights to use part
- Build upon part
- Distribute part
- Commercial use permitted

**Share-Alike**:
- If you improve a part, must share under BPA
- Prevents "taking parts private"
- Similar to copyleft in software

**Patent Provisions**:
- Can't patent parts themselves
- Can patent applications using parts
- Example: Can't patent GFP gene, but can patent GFP-based diagnostic

**Attribution**:
- Acknowledge original contributors
- Maintain provenance records

### Adoption and Impact

**Adopters**:
- iGEM Registry (primary platform)
- Many academic labs
- Some startups (especially early-stage)

**Non-Adopters**:
- Most universities (tech transfer office concerns)
- Large corporations (IP strategy conflicts)
- Some commercial registries

**Challenges**:

**University Tech Transfer**:
- Universities required to patent federally funded inventions (Bayh-Dole Act)
- Tech transfer offices maximize patent revenue
- BPA conflicts with this model
- Some universities prohibit students from contributing to BPA materials

**Enforcement**:
- No clear enforcement mechanism
- Relies on good faith
- Community pressure
- Unclear what happens if violated

**Practical Issues**:
- Parts may be independently discovered/patented by others
- Prior art questions
- International variation

**Hybrid Models**:
- Some institutions use BPA for basic parts
- Reserve patents for complex applications
- Balance openness and commercialization

## 6.5 Material Transfer Agreements (MTAs)

### Purpose and Function

**MTA Definition**: Legal contract governing transfer of biological materials

**Why MTAs Exist**:
- Clarify ownership rights
- Limit liability
- Define permitted uses
- Control downstream distribution
- Protect IP
- Ensure proper attribution

**Typical Provisions**:
- Description of material
- Permitted uses
- Restrictions (e.g., no clinical use, no redistribution)
- Ownership of modifications
- Publication rights
- Liability limitations
- Dispute resolution

### Uniform Biological Material Transfer Agreement (UBMTA)

**Developed**: NIH, 1995

**Purpose**: Standardize academic material transfer

**Key Features**:
- Pre-negotiated terms
- Simplified process
- Academic non-profit to academic non-profit
- Research use only
- Cannot distribute to third parties without permission
- Ownership of modifications to recipient
- No warranties

**Limitations**:
- Not for commercial entities
- Not for clinical use
- Doesn't address patents well
- Some universities modify terms

**Impact**:
- Widely adopted (but not universal)
- Reduced negotiation time (weeks to days)
- Still bureaucratic delays
- Better than no standard

### Traditional MTAs (Non-Standard)

**Challenges**:
- Each institution has own template
- Extensive negotiations (months)
- Legal fees (thousands of dollars)
- Delayed research
- Can prevent sharing altogether

**Common Sticking Points**:
- Reach-through rights (provider claims rights to future discoveries)
- Publication delays
- Exclusive vs. non-exclusive access
- Geographic restrictions
- Commercialization rights
- Liability and indemnification

**Example Scenario**:
Researcher wants plasmid from another lab:
1. Request material
2. Institutional legal offices involved
3. Negotiation on terms (2-6 months)
4. Legal fees ($2,000-$10,000)
5. Material finally transferred
6. Or: Frustrated researcher synthesizes DNA themselves instead

### OpenMTA Initiative

**Launched**: 2018, OpenPlant Initiative

**Goals**:
- Reduce legal friction
- Enable rapid sharing
- Maintain flexibility
- Community-driven

**Key Differences from UBMTA**:
- Allow redistribution under same terms
- Clearer patent provisions
- Shorter, simpler document
- Open source development of terms

**Adoption**:
- Growing, but not yet widespread
- Some universities supportive
- Others resistant to change
- DIY bio community embracing

## 6.6 Addgene's Model

### Plasmid Sharing Platform

**Structure**:
- Scientists deposit plasmids with Addgene
- Addgene archives, verifies, and distributes
- Requesting scientists pay cost-recovery fees
- Depositing scientists retain ownership

**Legal Framework**:

**Deposit Agreement**:
- Depositor warrants they have rights to share
- Grants Addgene right to distribute
- Specifies terms for recipients

**MTA for Recipients**:
- Academic MTA: Simple, permissive (similar to UBMTA)
- Commercial MTA: More restrictions, higher fees

**Benefits**:
- Centralized, professional distribution
- Quality control (sequence verification)
- Removes burden from depositing labs
- Fast turnaround (days to 2 weeks)
- Cost-effective ($65 for academic requests)

**IP Handling**:
- Depositors can reserve commercial rights
- Academic use permitted regardless
- Patents don't prevent academic sharing
- Helps navigate complex IP landscape

### Scale and Impact

**Numbers** (2025):
- 100,000+ plasmids available
- From 6,000+ labs worldwide
- 100,000+ scientists use Addgene materials
- Distributed to 100+ countries

**Citation Impact**:
- Plasmids cited in 100,000+ publications
- Accelerates research significantly
- Reproducibility improved (verified sequences)

**Commercial Spin-Off**:
- Some deposited plasmids become commercially valuable
- CRISPR plasmids downloaded 500,000+ times
- Balance between open access and commercial value

## 6.7 Case Studies

### Case Study 1: CRISPR Patent Wars

**Background**:
- 2012: Doudna & Charpentier publish CRISPR-Cas9 in bacteria
- 2013: Zhang publishes CRISPR in eukaryotic cells
- UC Berkeley and Broad Institute file competing patents

**Patent Dispute**:
- Who invented CRISPR for genome editing?
- Broad patents granted first (expedited review)
- UC Berkeley challenges as interfering patent
- Patent Trial and Appeal Board (PTAB) sides with Broad
- Both parties have valuable patents
- Extensive licensing by both

**Implications**:
- Uncertainty for researchers and companies
- Expensive licenses for commercial use
- Startups founded around each patent holder
- Research use generally permitted
- Demonstrates high stakes of biotech IP

**Current Status**:
- Both parties licensing widely
- Multiple companies: Editas (Broad), Intellia (Berkeley), CRISPR Therapeutics (Charpentier)
- Ongoing litigation in Europe
- Practical impact: Research proceeds regardless

### Case Study 2: Myriad Genetics BRCA Patents

**Background**:
- Myriad held patents on BRCA1/BRCA2 genes
- Mutations increase breast/ovarian cancer risk
- Exclusive testing rights, high prices ($3,000-$4,000)

**Controversy**:
- Patents on human genes ethically questionable
- Limited access to testing
- Prevented second opinions
- Stifled research

**Supreme Court Decision** (2013):
- Naturally occurring DNA not patentable
- Synthetic cDNA is patentable
- Invalidated many Myriad patents

**Impact**:
- Test prices dropped dramatically
- Multiple labs now offer testing
- Improved patient access
- Shifted biotech patenting landscape
- Relevant to synthetic biology: synthesized genes may still be patentable

### Case Study 3: iGEM and University Tech Transfer

**Tension**:
- Students create parts for iGEM, want to share openly
- Universities claim ownership (students are inventors)
- Tech transfer offices concerned about losing patent rights
- BPA conflicts with university IP policies

**Example**: MIT

**Resolution**:
- MIT allows students to contribute parts
- University retains rights but doesn't enforce
- Recognizes educational and community value
- Not all universities follow suit

**Other Universities**:
- Some prohibit BPA contributions
- Some require case-by-case review
- Some ignore the issue
- Creates uneven landscape

**Impact on Students**:
- Frustration with bureaucracy
- Educational value vs. IP restrictions
- Some synthesize parts themselves to avoid issues
- Need for better harmonization

### Case Study 4: Open Insulin Project

**Mission**: Develop open source insulin production

**Motivation**:
- Insulin prices high in US ($300-$500/month)
- Decades-old technology still under patent protection (incremental improvements)
- Diabetes patients struggling to afford

**Approach**:
- Community biology project
- Reverse engineer production
- Share protocols and strains openly
- Enable generic production

**Challenges**:
- Technical complexity
- Regulatory approval (FDA)
- Safety and quality control
- Funding
- Sustainability

**Status**:
- Ongoing project
- Demonstrated feasibility
- Not yet commercial production
- Raised awareness of insulin pricing

**Lessons**:
- Open source can tackle social issues
- Regulatory hurdles significant
- Technical capability vs. commercial viability
- Community-driven innovation possible

## 6.8 Strategies for Balancing Openness and IP

### Hybrid Models

**Tiered Approach**:
- Basic parts: Open (BPA or similar)
- Complex devices: Selective sharing
- Applications: Patent protection

**Example**:
- Promoter library: Open
- Biosensor circuit: Shared with collaborators
- Diagnostic device: Patented and licensed

**Academic Exception**:
- Patent applications but allow academic use
- License aggressively for commercial use
- Balances openness and commercialization

### Defensive Publication

**Purpose**: Create prior art to prevent others from patenting

**How It Works**:
1. Disclose invention publicly
2. Published description prevents future patents
3. Others can't patent it
4. You can't patent it either
5. Remains in public domain

**When to Use**:
- Don't want to patent but don't want others to either
- Cost of patenting not justified
- Want to ensure freedom to operate
- Mission is openness

**Mechanisms**:
- Journal publication
- Technical disclosure bulletins
- Defensive publication services (IP.com)
- Arxiv preprints
- Registry submissions (iGEM, Addgene)

### Patent Pools

**Definition**: Multiple patent holders agree to license patents as bundle

**Benefits**:
- One-stop licensing
- Reduced transaction costs
- Lower fees
- Broader access

**Examples in Other Fields**:
- MPEG (video compression)
- HIV/AIDS treatments

**Potential for Synthetic Biology**:
- BioBricks assembly methods
- Basic genetic parts
- Measurement standards
- Still developing

### Licensing Strategies

**Non-Exclusive Licenses**:
- Multiple licensees
- Broader access
- Lower per-license fees
- Builds ecosystem

**Exclusive Licenses**:
- Single licensee
- High fees
- Incentivizes major investment
- Limits access

**Field-of-Use Licenses**:
- Different licenses for different applications
- Example: Academic vs. diagnostic vs. therapeutic use
- Maximizes access and revenue

**Geographic Licenses**:
- Different terms in different regions
- Can adjust for local conditions
- Tiered pricing for low-income countries

- Open source biology levels playing field

- Increasing patent filings
- Strengthening IP protection
- Still concerns about enforcement
- Strategic patent acquisitions

- Mix of open and proprietary approaches

**Unique Position**:

**Opportunities**:

- South-South collaboration

## Conclusion

Intellectual property in synthetic biology involves complex trade-offs:
- Patents incentivize investment but can hinder innovation
- Open source enables rapid progress but funding challenges
- Material sharing accelerates research but legal complexity slows it
- Various models emerging to balance competing interests

**Key Insights**:
- Patent landscape complex, especially for foundational technologies (CRISPR)
- Patent thickets create barriers; freedom to operate analysis essential
- Open source movement inspired by software, adapted for biology
- BioBricks Public Agreement pioneering but limited adoption
- MTAs necessary but often burdensome; standardization helps
- Addgene model demonstrates viable sharing with IP flexibility
- Case studies show high stakes and evolving norms
- Hybrid approaches balance openness and commercial viability
- International variation significant; global harmonization needed
