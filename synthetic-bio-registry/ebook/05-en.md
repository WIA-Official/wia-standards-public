# Chapter 5: Biosafety and Biosecurity Registration

## Overview

Biosafety and biosecurity are paramount concerns in synthetic biology. This chapter explores biosafety level classifications, sequence screening protocols, dual-use research of concern (DURC), international regulations, pathogen registries, and frameworks for responsible innovation in the context of biological part registration.

## 5.1 Biosafety Fundamentals

### Biosafety vs. Biosecurity

**Biosafety**: Protection of human health and environment from unintended harm
- Focus: Accidents, mistakes, negligence
- Measures: Containment, protective equipment, training
- Goal: Prevent exposure to hazardous biological agents

**Biosecurity**: Prevention of misuse of biological materials and knowledge
- Focus: Intentional harm, bioterrorism, weaponization
- Measures: Access control, screening, tracking
- Goal: Prevent malicious use of biology

**Overlap**: Many measures serve both purposes (e.g., access restrictions, inventory management)

### Risk Assessment Framework

**Three Key Factors**:

1. **Agent Hazard**
   - Pathogenicity (ability to cause disease)
   - Virulence (severity of disease)
   - Transmissibility (ease of spread)
   - Host range (what organisms affected)
   - Availability of preventions/treatments

2. **Exposure Potential**
   - Route of exposure (inhalation, ingestion, injection)
   - Quantity of agent
   - Frequency and duration
   - Protective barriers

3. **Consequences**
   - Individual health impacts
   - Public health impacts
   - Environmental impacts
   - Economic impacts

**Risk = Hazard × Exposure × Consequences**

**Risk Management**:
- Elimination (don't work with hazardous agent)
- Substitution (use safer alternative)
- Engineering controls (biosafety cabinets, containment)
- Administrative controls (protocols, training, access limits)
- Personal protective equipment (gloves, lab coats, respirators)

## 5.2 Biosafety Level Classifications

### BSL-1 (Biosafety Level 1)

**Criteria**:
- Agents not known to cause disease in healthy adults
- Minimal hazard to personnel and environment

**Examples**:
- E. coli K-12
- Saccharomyces cerevisiae (baker's yeast)
- Bacillus subtilis (non-pathogenic strains)
- Non-infectious plant viruses

**Practices**:
- Standard microbiological practices
- No special containment equipment
- Work on open bench
- Hand washing and basic hygiene
- Limited access during work

**Facilities**:
- Basic laboratory (teaching, research)
- Sink for hand washing
- Easily cleanable surfaces
- No special ventilation required

**Synthetic Biology**:
- Most iGEM projects
- Educational laboratories
- Proof-of-concept research
- Common chassis organisms

### BSL-2 (Biosafety Level 2)

**Criteria**:
- Agents associated with human disease
- Moderate hazard
- Rarely serious
- Preventions and treatments available

**Examples**:
- Staphylococcus aureus
- Salmonella species
- Hepatitis B virus
- Many clinical isolates
- Some plant pathogens

**Practices**:
- BSL-1 practices plus:
- Limited access (authorized personnel only)
- Biohazard warning signs
- Sharps precautions
- Biosafety manual
- Medical surveillance
- Decontamination of all waste

**Equipment**:
- Biosafety cabinet (BSC) for aerosol-generating procedures
- PPE: gloves, lab coats, eye protection
- Autoclave for decontamination

**Facilities**:
- Separate from public areas
- Self-closing doors
- Easily cleanable surfaces
- Eyewash station
- Autoclave accessible

**Synthetic Biology**:
- Clinical strain engineering
- Some pathway engineering (producing potentially toxic compounds)
- Work with human cell lines
- Advanced research projects

### BSL-3 (Biosafety Level 3)

**Criteria**:
- Agents causing serious or lethal disease
- Respiratory transmission possible
- Treatments may exist but not always effective

**Examples**:
- Mycobacterium tuberculosis
- SARS-CoV-2 (live virus)
- Brucella species
- Yellow fever virus
- Venezuelan equine encephalitis virus

**Practices**:
- BSL-2 practices plus:
- Controlled access (two-door entry, changing rooms)
- All procedures in biosafety cabinets
- Respiratory protection as needed
- Medical surveillance mandatory
- Decontamination before leaving

**Equipment**:
- Class II or III biosafety cabinets
- Full PPE: scrubs, gowns, gloves, respiratory protection
- Dedicated equipment (doesn't leave lab)
- Autoclave in facility

**Facilities**:
- Separated from public areas
- Sealed penetrations
- Directional airflow (negative pressure)
- HEPA filtration of exhaust air
- Hands-free sinks
- Self-closing, self-locking doors
- Shower facility

**Synthetic Biology**:
- Engineering attenuated vaccines
- Studying pathogen mechanisms
- Developing diagnostics for dangerous pathogens
- Rare in synthetic biology due to complexity and cost

### BSL-4 (Biosafety Level 4)

**Criteria**:
- Agents causing life-threatening disease
- High risk of aerosol transmission
- No treatments or vaccines available

**Examples**:
- Ebola virus
- Marburg virus
- Lassa fever virus
- Smallpox virus (Variola)

**Practices**:
- BSL-3 practices plus:
- Entrance through airlocks
- Shower-out required
- All materials decontaminated before exit
- Personnel work in positive-pressure suits
- Extensive training

**Equipment**:
- Class III biosafety cabinets or positive-pressure suits
- Dedicated air supply for suits
- Double-door autoclaves
- Chemical decontamination systems

**Facilities**:
- Separate building or isolated zone
- Multiple barriers
- Negative pressure cascades
- HEPA filtration (supply and exhaust)
- Redundant systems
- Continuous monitoring

**Synthetic Biology**:
- Extremely rare
- Development of vaccines or therapeutics
- Only in specialized government facilities
- Examples: CDC, USAMRIID, Vektor Institute

### Risk Groups (WHO Classification)

**Risk Group 1**:
- Unlikely to cause disease
- Equivalent to BSL-1

**Risk Group 2**:
- Can cause disease, rarely serious
- Preventions/treatments available
- Usually BSL-2, sometimes BSL-1 with modifications

**Risk Group 3**:
- Serious or lethal disease
- Preventions/treatments may exist
- BSL-3 required

**Risk Group 4**:
- Life-threatening disease
- No preventions/treatments
- BSL-4 required

## 5.3 Sequence Screening Protocols

### Rationale for Screening

**Purpose**: Prevent synthesis or distribution of hazardous sequences

**Scenarios to Prevent**:
- Inadvertent creation of pathogens
- Synthesis of toxin genes
- Reconstruction of eradicated viruses (e.g., smallpox)
- Gain-of-function modifications
- Bioweapon components

**Key Players**:
- Gene synthesis companies
- DNA registry distributors
- Sequence databases
- Research institutions

### International Gene Synthesis Consortium (IGSC)

**Establishment**: 2009

**Mission**: Promote biosecurity in gene synthesis industry

**Members**:
- Major gene synthesis companies
- Twist Bioscience
- Integrated DNA Technologies (IDT)
- GenScript
- Eurofins Genomics
- And many others

**Harmonized Screening Protocol**:

**Step 1: Sequence Analysis**
- BLAST against curated databases
- Identify matches to pathogens and toxins

**Step 2: Sequence of Concern (SOC) Database Check**
- Compare to list of regulated agents
- Check against Select Agents list (US)
- Check Australia Group lists
- Viral genomes (especially poxviruses, filoviruses)
- Toxin genes (botulinum, ricin, etc.)

**Step 3: Best Match Analysis**
- If match found, determine:
  - Identity percentage
  - Coverage
  - Biological function
  - Context of order

**Step 4: Customer Screening**
- Verify customer identity
- Check institutional affiliation
- Assess legitimacy of research

**Step 5: Decision**
- Approve: No concerns, synthesize and ship
- Reject: Clear threat, refuse order
- Escalate: Uncertain, consult biosecurity experts

**Thresholds** (example):
- >70% identity to pathogen over >200 bp: Flag for review
- >95% identity to Select Agent: Likely reject
- Complete viral genomes: Almost always reject unless authorized facility

**False Positives**:
- Many genes have homologs in pathogens
- Context matters (vaccine research vs. nefarious intent)
- Requires expert judgment

### Select Agents and Toxins

**US Federal Select Agent Program** (FSAP):

**Oversight**: CDC and USDA

**Categories**:
- HHS Select Agents (human pathogens)
- USDA Select Agents (animal/plant pathogens)
- Overlap Select Agents (both)

**Examples**:
- Bacillus anthracis (anthrax)
- Yersinia pestis (plague)
- Botulinum toxins
- Ebola virus
- Foot-and-mouth disease virus
- Avian influenza (certain strains)

**Regulations**:
- Registration required to possess
- Security risk assessments for personnel
- Inventory and transfer records
- Physical security measures
- Incident response plans
- Inspections

**Synthetic Biology Implications**:
- Cannot synthesize Select Agent sequences without authorization
- Even fragments may be regulated
- Attenuated strains have different rules
- Specific exemptions for diagnostic/vaccine work

### International Frameworks

**Australia Group**:
- Export control regime
- Lists biological agents and dual-use equipment
- 43 participating countries
- Informal consensus group

**Biological Weapons Convention (BWC)**:
- Treaty prohibiting development, production, stockpiling of bioweapons
- 183 state parties
- No verification protocol
- Compliance varies

**EU Dual-Use Regulation**:
- Controls export of dual-use items
- Includes biological agents and knowledge
- License requirements

**Cartagena Protocol**:
- Biosafety protocol under Convention on Biological Diversity
- Focuses on Living Modified Organisms (LMOs)
- Transboundary movement
- Risk assessment and management

## 5.4 Dual-Use Research of Concern (DURC)

### Definition

**DURC**: Research that could be directly misapplied to pose significant threat to public health, agriculture, plants, animals, environment, or national security

**"Dual-Use"**: Research with legitimate scientific purpose but also potential for harm if misused

### Historical Examples

**1918 Flu Reconstruction** (2005):
- Jeffery Taubenberger et al. reconstructed 1918 pandemic influenza virus
- Published complete genome sequence
- Controversy: Could enable bioterrorist to recreate deadly pathogen
- Justification: Understanding virulence factors for pandemic preparedness
- Outcome: Published with some sequence redactions

**H5N1 Transmissibility Studies** (2011):
- Ron Fouchier and Yoshihiro Kawaoka groups
- Engineered H5N1 avian flu for mammalian transmission
- Demonstrated pandemic potential
- Controversy: Publishing methods for creating transmissible deadly virus
- Outcome: Voluntary publication pause, then published with modifications

**Horsepox Synthesis** (2018):
- Canadian researchers synthesized related poxvirus
- Cost ~$100,000
- Demonstrated feasibility of synthesizing smallpox
- Controversy: Roadmap for bioterrorism
- Outcome: Ethical debate, no policy changes

### US DURC Policy

**NIH/US Government Policy** (2014):

**15 Agents of Concern**:
- Avian influenza (H5N1, H7N9)
- Bacillus anthracis
- Botulinum toxins
- Burkholderia mallei/pseudomallei
- Ebola/Marburg
- Foot-and-mouth disease
- SARS/MERS coronaviruses
- Variola (smallpox)
- Yersinia pestis

**7 Experimental Categories of Concern**:
1. Enhance transmissibility of agent
2. Enhance virulence of agent
3. Increase resistance to treatments
4. Increase stability or environmental persistence
5. Alter host range
6. Enhance evasion of detection/diagnosis
7. Enable weaponization

**Requirements**:
- Institutional review before starting
- Risk mitigation plans
- Oversight by Institutional Biosafety Committee
- Reporting to funding agency
- Pre-publication review

**Risk Mitigation Strategies**:
- Enhanced physical security
- Limited personnel access
- Detailed protocols and training
- Data access controls
- Pre-publication review

### Ethical Frameworks

**Benefit-Risk Analysis**:
- What are scientific benefits?
- What are potential misuse scenarios?
- Can benefits be achieved with less risky approaches?
- Are risk mitigation measures adequate?

**Considerations**:
- Scientific freedom vs. security
- Transparency vs. secrecy
- International cooperation vs. national security
- Precautionary principle

**Stakeholder Engagement**:
- Scientific community
- Biosecurity experts
- Public health officials
- Ethicists
- Policy makers
- Public

## 5.5 Registry-Specific Safety Measures

### iGEM Safety Program

**Safety Committee**:
- Reviews all team projects
- Provides guidance and resources
- Enforces safety standards

**Safety Forms** (required):
1. **About Our Lab**
   - Biosafety level
   - Safety training
   - Supervision
   - Emergency procedures

2. **About Our Project**
   - Organisms and parts used
   - Risks identified
   - Containment measures
   - Disposal methods

3. **Safety and Security by Design**
   - Could parts/project be misused?
   - Mitigation strategies
   - Ethical considerations

**Check-In System**:
- Teams must get approval before wet lab work
- Prevents high-risk experiments
- Educational intervention if concerns arise

**White List**:
- Pre-approved low-risk organisms
- E. coli K-12, DH5α, BL21
- S. cerevisiae
- B. subtilis (non-pathogenic)

**Red Flag Projects**:
- Pathogenicity islands
- Virulence factors
- Antibiotic resistance spreading
- Work with human pathogens

**Actions**:
- Education and guidance
- Require modifications
- Reject if unacceptably risky

### Addgene Safety Policies

**Screening Process**:
- Review all plasmid submissions
- Check for hazardous components
- Verify appropriate containment level

**Prohibited Materials**:
- Select Agents
- Toxin genes (full-length)
- Pathogenicity factors (case-by-case)
- Replication-competent viruses without authorization

**Labeling**:
- Biosafety level clearly indicated
- Special handling instructions
- Material Safety Data Sheets (MSDS)

**Distribution Controls**:
- Verify recipient institutions
- Require Material Transfer Agreements
- Track shipments
- Refuse suspicious orders

### SynBioHub Safety Features

**Metadata Requirements**:
- Biosafety level
- Risk group
- Safety notes

**Flagging System**:
- Community can flag concerning parts
- Administrator review
- Warnings or removal if necessary

**Access Control**:
- Sensitive parts can be restricted
- Require verification for access
- Audit trails

## 5.6 Biosecurity Technologies

### Genetic Safeguards

**Kill Switches**:
- Engineered organisms die outside lab
- Environmental triggers (temperature, nutrients)
- Chemical triggers (remove inducer → death)

**Example**:
```
Constitutive toxin expression + inducible antitoxin
- In lab: Inducer present, antitoxin blocks toxin
- Outside lab: No inducer, toxin kills cell
```

**Limitations**:
- Mutations can disable
- Selection pressure to escape
- Not 100% reliable

**Containment Circuits**:
- Require synthetic nutrients not in nature
- Auxotrophs (require specific amino acid)
- Orthogonal genetic systems

**Semantic Containment**:
- Use non-standard genetic codes
- Alternative codons
- Synthetic amino acids
- Incompatible with natural organisms

### Sequence Obfuscation

**Watermarking**:
- Insert identifying sequences
- Track origin of DNA
- Prove provenance

**Example**:
Silent mutations spelling "VENTER" in Craig Venter's synthetic Mycoplasma

**Encryption**:
- Encode information in DNA
- Decrypt with key
- Verify authenticity

**Limitations**:
- Can be removed
- Doesn't prevent misuse
- More useful for attribution than prevention

### Detection and Monitoring

**Environmental Surveillance**:
- Metagenomic sequencing of air, water, soil
- Detect engineered organisms
- Early warning for release

**Biodetection Systems**:
- Biosensors for specific agents
- Point-of-care diagnostics
- Rapid pathogen identification

**Synthetic Biology Applications**:
- Engineered biosensors
- Paper-based diagnostics
- CRISPR-based detection (SHERLOCK, DETECTR)

## 5.7 Regulatory Oversight

### United States

**Multiple Agencies**:

**FDA** (Food and Drug Administration):
- Therapeutic products
- Diagnostic tests
- GMO foods (consultation)

**EPA** (Environmental Protection Agency):
- Microorganisms in environment
- Biopesticides
- Industrial applications

**USDA** (Department of Agriculture):
- Plant pests and pathogens
- Veterinary biologics
- GMO crops

**CDC** (Centers for Disease Control):
- Select Agents
- Public health threats

**NIH** (National Institutes of Health):
- Research guidelines (NIH Guidelines)
- Recombinant DNA safety
- Funding oversight

**Coordinated Framework**:
- 1986 policy for biotech regulation
- Risk-based, not process-based
- Multiple agencies with overlapping jurisdiction
- Criticisms: Gaps, inconsistencies, outdated

### European Union

**Directives**:
- Directive 2009/41/EC (contained use of GMMs)
- Directive 2001/18/EC (deliberate release of GMOs)
- CLP Regulation (classification, labeling, packaging)

**EFSA** (European Food Safety Authority):
- Risk assessment of GMOs
- Food and feed safety

**EMA** (European Medicines Agency):
- Therapeutic products
- Advanced therapy medicinal products (ATMPs)

**National Implementation**:
- Each member state implements EU directives
- Some variation in stringency
- Generally more restrictive than US

### Other Countries

**Canada**:
- Health Canada
- Canadian Food Inspection Agency
- Environment Canada
- Product-based, risk-focused

**Australia**:
- Office of the Gene Technology Regulator (OGTR)
- Comprehensive GMO regulation
- License required for dealings with GMOs

**China**:
- Ministry of Agriculture
- Biosafety regulations
- Growing synthetic biology sector

**Japan**:
- Cartagena Act implementation
- METI, MAFF, MOE oversight

## 5.8 Responsible Innovation

### Anticipatory Governance

**Definition**: Prospectively consider implications of emerging technologies

**Principles**:
- **Anticipation**: Foresee potential impacts
- **Reflexivity**: Reflect on assumptions and values
- **Inclusion**: Engage diverse stakeholders
- **Responsiveness**: Adapt based on new knowledge

**Application to Synthetic Biology**:
- Scenario planning
- Technology assessment
- Public engagement
- Adaptive regulation

### Ethical Frameworks

**Principlism**:
- **Beneficence**: Do good
- **Non-maleficence**: Do no harm
- **Autonomy**: Respect individuals' rights
- **Justice**: Fair distribution of benefits and burdens

**Virtue Ethics**:
- What would a virtuous scientist do?
- Honesty, integrity, responsibility

**Consequentialism**:
- Maximize overall good outcomes
- Utilitarian calculations

**Deontology**:
- Duties and rules
- Some actions wrong regardless of consequences

### Community Initiatives

**Education**:

**International Cooperation**:

- Global norms development

## 5.9 Case Studies

### Case Study 1: COVID-19 Rapid Response

**Positive Example of Biosafety Infrastructure**:
- SARS-CoV-2 sequence shared rapidly (January 2020)
- Global research response
- Synthetic biology tools deployed:
  - CRISPR diagnostics
  - Synthetic viral RNA for tests
  - mRNA vaccines (synthetic sequences)

**Safety Considerations**:
- BSL-3 required for live virus
- Inactivated virus or subgenomic fragments for BSL-2
- Synthetic spike protein safer than whole virus

**Biosecurity Concerns**:
- Lab leak theories
- Gain-of-function debate reignited
- Need for improved transparency

**Lessons**:
- Biosafety infrastructure enabled rapid response
- Sequence databases critical
- Balance speed and safety
- Communication essential

### Case Study 2: Arsenic Bacteria Controversy

**Background**: 2010 paper claimed bacteria using arsenic instead of phosphorus

**Issues**:
- Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence
- Later largely refuted
- Not biosafety issue per se, but shows importance of verification

**Registry Implications**:
- Sequences submitted to databases
- Others attempted to replicate
- Community correction process worked

### Case Study 3: Gene Drive Organisms

**Technology**: Genetic elements that spread through populations
- CRISPR-based gene drives
- Can eliminate disease vectors (malaria mosquitoes)
- Or eradicate invasive species

**Biosafety Concerns**:
- Release is likely irreversible
- Could affect ecosystems unpredictably
- Cross-border impacts
- "Accidental" release possible

**Current Status**:
- Intense research with strict containment
- International discussions on governance
- Moratorium on environmental release
- Target Malaria project progressing cautiously

**Registry Issues**:
- Should gene drive components be in open registries?
- Require special safeguards?
- Educational vs. biosecurity balance

## Conclusion

Biosafety and biosecurity are integral to responsible synthetic biology:
- Risk assessment and biosafety levels guide laboratory practices
- Sequence screening prevents synthesis of dangerous agents
- Dual-use research requires careful oversight
- Registries implement safety measures in submission and distribution
- Regulatory frameworks vary internationally but share common goals
- Responsible innovation principles guide ethical development

**Key Insights**:
- Biosafety protects workers and environment; biosecurity prevents misuse
- Four biosafety levels match risk groups 1-4
- Gene synthesis screening prevents inadvertent creation of pathogens
- DURC policies balance scientific progress and security
- iGEM, Addgene, and other registries have comprehensive safety programs
- Genetic safeguards (kill switches) provide additional protection
- Multiple regulatory agencies oversee different aspects
- Anticipatory governance and ethics essential for emerging technology

As synthetic biology capabilities grow, safety and security measures must evolve. Next chapter explores intellectual property and open source biology.
