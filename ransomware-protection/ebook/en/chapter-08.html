Cyber Insurance, NIS 2, and CIRCIA Reporting in One Envelope

The continuity_report envelope is designed for board-level reporting and regulatory disclosure. The intention is that one signed envelope can feed many regulatory portals — SEC 8-K cyber filings, EU NIS 2 incident notifications, US CIRCIA 72-hour reports, K-ISA notifications, and a cyber-insurer’s first-loss intake form.

8.2 The bundle pattern

A continuity report does not duplicate evidence; it references it. The evidence_refs field contains identifiers of the underlying detection, response, backup, drill, and identity envelopes. Receivers fetch the referenced envelopes separately, verifying signatures along the way. The bundle pattern keeps individual reports small and lets the same evidence be cited by multiple reports without re-signing.

8.3 Mapping to NIS 2

The EU NIS 2 directive (Article 23) requires an early warning within 24 hours, an incident notification within 72 hours, and a final report within one month. Each of these maps to a continuity_report envelope at the appropriate issued_at timestamp; the scope field distinguishes the early warning from the final report.

8.4 Mapping to CIRCIA

The US CIRCIA 72-hour cyber incident reporting rule and the 24-hour ransom payment reporting rule both consume the same continuity-report shape, with different scope values. Tenants subject to both jurisdictions can satisfy them from one signed bundle rather than re-keying the same facts twice.

8.5 Cyber insurance binding

Cyber insurers are increasingly tying premium and coverage to attested controls rather than questionnaires. The insurance_attestation envelope bundles the tenant’s most recent posture_report, a representative recovery_drill, and a sampled set of identity_hardening_attestation records, signed by the tenant’s risk officer.

8.6 The board-level lens

For audit committees and boards, the same continuity report renders as a one-page narrative summary: incident scope, customer impact, time to contain, time to recover, lessons learned. The narrative summary is generated from the structured envelope; it does not replace it.

8.7 What this standard does not do

The standard does not file regulatory reports on the tenant’s behalf, does not negotiate with adversaries, and does not promise that signing an envelope will prevent the next attack. It promises one specific thing: when the next attack happens, the chain of evidence from the first canary trip to the final board update will be cryptographically continuous, machine-comparable across tenants and vendors, and small enough that even a 50-person organisation can produce it.

Worked example

Consider a mid-size hospital network running 18 EHR servers, 240 imaging workstations, 42 IoT medical devices, and a separate biomedical engineering subnet. The SOC has one full-time analyst, two contracted analysts, and an MSSP relationship. The cyber-insurer requires annual posture evidence; the national health regulator requires 24-hour breach notification.

In a pre-standard world, the SOC re-keys every observation into the EDR portal, the SIEM, the MSSP ticketing system, the insurer questionnaire, and the regulator filing form. The total work per real incident exceeds the analyst headcount by a factor of three to five during the first 72 hours, and that is the work done after the technical containment is finished.

In a post-standard world, the same observation is signed once, propagated automatically to every consumer that has subscribed to the tenant under appropriate TLP and federation rules, and rendered into each consumer’s preferred form by a downstream adaptor. The SOC analyst spends the first 72 hours doing analysis and decision-making, not data entry. That is the entire return on signing the envelope.
