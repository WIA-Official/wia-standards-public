Recovery Drills as First-Class Evidence

Recovery posture is impossible to assess from policy documents. The only honest measure of recovery readiness is the result of a drill conducted under realistic conditions. The standard treats drills as first-class signed evidence rather than ad-hoc internal exercises.

5.2 Three drill kinds

The standard distinguishes tabletop (analyst walkthrough, no live systems), functional (restore to an isolated environment using real backups), and full-scale-cutover (cut production traffic to the recovered environment). Each kind has different evidentiary weight: a tabletop demonstrates plan literacy; a functional drill demonstrates recovery feasibility; a cutover demonstrates business continuity.

5.3 Observed RTO and RPO are not negotiable

The drill envelope records observed RTO and RPO, derived from started_at, ended_at, and the snapshot's captured_at. The values are not the targets the tenant aspires to; they are the values the tenant achieved. A drill where the observed RTO exceeds the target is still valid evidence — it is the most useful kind of evidence, because it identifies the gap.

5.4 Forensic isolation during drills

Restore drills SHOULD execute against a forensically isolated network. Recovering a compromised credential into production during a drill is a textbook way to extend an incident. The standard requires the drill envelope to record the isolation boundary identifier and an attestation that the boundary was active for the full drill duration.

5.5 Issue tracking

Every drill emits zero or more issues, each with a severity and a remediation_due. Issues that pass their due date without resolution feed the next posture_report as overdue items. The accountability loop is structural: there is no honest way to "lose" a drill issue between the drill and the next regulatory cycle.

5.6 Drill cadence

The standard does not mandate a specific drill cadence — that is a tenant and regulator decision — but does require that the posture_report envelope record the date of the most recent drill and the next scheduled drill. Cadence drift is the most common precursor to a failed live recovery.

5.7 Insurance and regulatory consumption

Drill envelopes are referenced from the insurance_attestation and continuity_report envelopes. One signed drill envelope can satisfy a cyber-insurer renewal, a NIS 2 supervisory inspection, and a board-level audit committee request — the same evidence, presented through different lenses.

Worked example

Consider a mid-size hospital network running 18 EHR servers, 240 imaging workstations, 42 IoT medical devices, and a separate biomedical engineering subnet. The SOC has one full-time analyst, two contracted analysts, and an MSSP relationship. The cyber-insurer requires annual posture evidence; the national health regulator requires 24-hour breach notification.

In a pre-standard world, the SOC re-keys every observation into the EDR portal, the SIEM, the MSSP ticketing system, the insurer questionnaire, and the regulator filing form. The total work per real incident exceeds the analyst headcount by a factor of three to five during the first 72 hours, and that is the work done after the technical containment is finished.

In a post-standard world, the same observation is signed once, propagated automatically to every consumer that has subscribed to the tenant under appropriate TLP and federation rules, and rendered into each consumer’s preferred form by a downstream adaptor. The SOC analyst spends the first 72 hours doing analysis and decision-making, not data entry. That is the entire return on signing the envelope.
