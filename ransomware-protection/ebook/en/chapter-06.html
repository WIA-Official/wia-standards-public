Cross-Organisation Federation and TLP Discipline

Cross-organisation federation is the highest-leverage and highest-risk part of this standard. High-leverage because a single confirmed indicator from one tenant can warn ten downstream tenants in time to matter; high-risk because the same channel can leak sensitive enterprise telemetry to unrelated parties.

6.1 The handshake

Two organisations federate by exchanging signed federation_handshake envelopes that name the share scope, the maximum TLP level the receiver is authorised to handle, and the cryptographic identity of each peer. The handshake is durable; once established, individual events are propagated automatically subject to the gating rules.

6.2 The Traffic Light Protocol (TLP) field

Every federated envelope carries a tlp field with one of five values: RED, AMBER+STRICT, AMBER, GREEN, or CLEAR. The field is not advisory; downstream consumers are required to honour it. A RED indicator may not be re-shared even within the receiving organisation beyond the named recipients.

6.3 Severity and confidence gating

Detection envelopes are eligible for federation only when severity ≥ HIGH and confidence ≥ 0.85. The thresholds are deliberately conservative; the cost of an incorrect federated indicator multiplies by the size of the federation.

6.4 Replay defence at the federation boundary

The 96-bit nonce and the ±300-second skew tolerance described in Chapter 2 apply at the federation boundary as well. A federated indicator that exceeds the skew window is rejected even if its signature verifies.

6.5 ISAC bridging

Many tenants federate not directly but through an ISAC (Information Sharing and Analysis Centre). The standard treats an ISAC as a special tenant with one-to-many fan-out semantics. A signed indicator delivered to an ISAC is automatically eligible for fan-out subject to the indicator’s own TLP and the ISAC’s membership rules.

6.6 Audit trail

Every federation event — handshake, propagation, rejection, revocation — is captured as a federation_audit envelope. The audit trail is queryable by either peer; mutual auditability is what keeps the federation honest.

6.7 Revocation

Either peer may revoke a federation handshake at any time by emitting a signed federation_revocation. Subsequent propagations are dropped at the originator, not at the receiver, so that revoked partners cannot be subjected to involuntary continued data flow.

6.8 Independence from underlying transport

Federation is defined at the envelope level, not the transport level. Implementations may carry envelopes over MISP, STIX/TAXII, OpenDXL, mutual-TLS HTTP, or any future transport. The signing discipline is the integrity guarantee; the transport is interchangeable.

Worked example

Consider a mid-size hospital network running 18 EHR servers, 240 imaging workstations, 42 IoT medical devices, and a separate biomedical engineering subnet. The SOC has one full-time analyst, two contracted analysts, and an MSSP relationship. The cyber-insurer requires annual posture evidence; the national health regulator requires 24-hour breach notification.

In a pre-standard world, the SOC re-keys every observation into the EDR portal, the SIEM, the MSSP ticketing system, the insurer questionnaire, and the regulator filing form. The total work per real incident exceeds the analyst headcount by a factor of three to five during the first 72 hours, and that is the work done after the technical containment is finished.

In a post-standard world, the same observation is signed once, propagated automatically to every consumer that has subscribed to the tenant under appropriate TLP and federation rules, and rendered into each consumer’s preferred form by a downstream adaptor. The SOC analyst spends the first 72 hours doing analysis and decision-making, not data entry. That is the entire return on signing the envelope.
