Why Ransomware Defence Needs an Open Wire Format

Ransomware is no longer a single-vendor problem. A modern incident touches an EDR, an email-security gateway, a SIEM, a SOAR, a backup vendor, a cyber-insurer's intake portal, two or three upstream ISACs, and at least one government regulator (NIS 2 in the EU, CIRCIA in the United States, K-ISA in Korea). Every one of those parties speaks a slightly different schema for the same underlying observation.

1.1 The schema-drift tax

When a single observation has to be re-keyed into seven schemas during the first hour of an incident, three things happen. First, timestamps drift, because each schema rounds time differently. Second, severity classifications drift: the EDR's "critical" becomes the SIEM's "high" becomes the insurer's "tier 2." Third, evidence binding is lost, because each system mints its own identifier and there is no canonical way to say "this row in your table and that row in mine are the same event."

The schema-drift tax is invisible during steady state. It becomes lethal during an active incident, when the organisation needs every analyst on the same page in the same minute.

1.2 What an open wire format actually buys you

This standard defines a small set of signed envelopes — detection_event, response_action, backup_snapshot, recovery_drill, identity_hardening_attestation, continuity_report — and a discipline for signing them with Ed25519 over the canonical JSON form (RFC 8785 JCS).

The envelopes are not a replacement for STIX 2.1, OpenC2, OpenEDR, or any vendor's native event model. They are a cross-vendor binding: every system continues to emit its native form, plus one signed envelope per observation that downstream consumers correlate without ambiguity.

1.3 Accountability is cryptographic, not procedural

Once a detection_event is signed by a tenant's SOC and a response_action is signed by their SOAR, the chain of custody is cryptographically continuous. A regulator examining an incident six months later does not have to take the tenant's word for when the event was first observed; the signature and the JCS canonicalisation make that timestamp tamper-evident.

1.4 The MITRE ATT&CK alignment

Every detection_event carries at least one MITRE ATT&CK v15 Enterprise tactic / technique / sub-technique triple. This is not a recommendation; it is a hard requirement. The cost of not requiring it is exactly the schema-drift tax described above.

1.5 What this book covers

The remaining seven chapters walk through each envelope in detail with worked examples and the operational discipline that makes the envelope worth signing. Chapter 2 covers detection envelopes. Chapter 3 covers response actions and the rollback-token discipline. Chapter 4 covers immutable backup attestation. Chapter 5 covers recovery drills as first-class evidence. Chapter 6 covers cross-organisation federation. Chapter 7 covers identity hardening. Chapter 8 closes with a worked example: a single bundle that satisfies a US CIRCIA 72-hour report, an EU NIS 2 incident notification, and a cyber-insurer's first-loss intake form.

The standard is open, the envelopes are public, and the philosophy that drives both is simple: 弘益人間 — benefit all humanity, including the small organisations that cannot afford a six-vendor SOC stack but can still afford to sign a JSON envelope.

Worked example

Consider a mid-size hospital network running 18 EHR servers, 240 imaging workstations, 42 IoT medical devices, and a separate biomedical engineering subnet. The SOC has one full-time analyst, two contracted analysts, and an MSSP relationship. The cyber-insurer requires annual posture evidence; the national health regulator requires 24-hour breach notification.

In a pre-standard world, the SOC re-keys every observation into the EDR portal, the SIEM, the MSSP ticketing system, the insurer questionnaire, and the regulator filing form. The total work per real incident exceeds the analyst headcount by a factor of three to five during the first 72 hours, and that is the work done after the technical containment is finished.

In a post-standard world, the same observation is signed once, propagated automatically to every consumer that has subscribed to the tenant under appropriate TLP and federation rules, and rendered into each consumer’s preferred form by a downstream adaptor. The SOC analyst spends the first 72 hours doing analysis and decision-making, not data entry. That is the entire return on signing the envelope.
