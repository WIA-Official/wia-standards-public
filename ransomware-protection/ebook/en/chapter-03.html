Response Actions and the Rollback Token Discipline

A response_action envelope describes a SOAR action taken in response to one or more detection_event records. The standard treats response as a first-class signed observation because the act of containing a host is, itself, evidence: it changes the state of the environment and that change must be auditable.

3.1 The envelope schema

{
  "action_id": "01HXR9C2KQ3V7N4M8B6P0Z9F1A",
  "incident_id": "01HXR9C2KQ3V7N4M8B6P0Z9F1B",
  "kind": "isolate_host",
  "executed_at": "2026-04-27T14:23:42.991Z",
  "executed_by": "did:wia:soar:acme-eu",
  "rollback_token": "opaque",
  "signature": { "alg": "Ed25519", "value": "..." }
}

3.2 Action classes

The standard recognises six baseline action classes: isolate_host, revoke_session, snapshot_volume, block_egress_cidr, rotate_credential, and quarantine_file. Vendors may add proprietary classes, but the six above are interoperable across all conforming SOAR implementations.

3.3 The rollback-token discipline

Every response_action MUST carry a rollback_token. The token is opaque — its internal structure is the SOAR's business — but its existence is mandatory. The reason is operational: automated containment will, eventually, contain the wrong host. When that happens, an IR lead must be able to reverse the action without re-reading the SOAR's internal playbook state. A signed envelope plus a rollback token is the smallest unit of safe reversal.

This is a deliberate departure from "fire and forget" SOAR primitives. Fire-and-forget is fine until the containment quarantines the CFO's laptop during a board meeting. The rollback discipline costs the SOAR nothing during normal operation and saves the IR lead a 2 a.m. escalation during a false positive.

3.4 Linking actions to detections

Each response_action references the incident_id that aggregated the triggering detections. The aggregation is intentional: a single response usually answers multiple correlated detections, and the standard preserves that fan-in rather than forcing a one-to-one binding.

3.5 Privileged-action attestation

Actions in the rotate_credential and block_egress_cidr classes carry blast radius beyond the immediate incident scope. The standard requires an additional signature from a privileged-action attestor (usually the SOC manager or an automated risk-rules engine) before the action is accepted by downstream auditors.

3.6 Time discipline

The executed_at timestamp is the wall-clock time at the SOAR, not the detection time. The lag between detection and action — colloquially "mean time to contain" — is a key SOC metric and is computed by downstream systems from the difference between observed_at on the originating detection and executed_at on the action.

3.7 Failure modes

If a SOAR attempts an action and the underlying enforcement plane refuses (network timeout, credential expired, target host unknown), a response_action envelope is still emitted with kind: "failed" and a structured failure_reason. Silent failures are the worst possible outcome; the standard makes them impossible by construction.

Worked example

Consider a mid-size hospital network running 18 EHR servers, 240 imaging workstations, 42 IoT medical devices, and a separate biomedical engineering subnet. The SOC has one full-time analyst, two contracted analysts, and an MSSP relationship. The cyber-insurer requires annual posture evidence; the national health regulator requires 24-hour breach notification.

In a pre-standard world, the SOC re-keys every observation into the EDR portal, the SIEM, the MSSP ticketing system, the insurer questionnaire, and the regulator filing form. The total work per real incident exceeds the analyst headcount by a factor of three to five during the first 72 hours, and that is the work done after the technical containment is finished.

In a post-standard world, the same observation is signed once, propagated automatically to every consumer that has subscribed to the tenant under appropriate TLP and federation rules, and rendered into each consumer’s preferred form by a downstream adaptor. The SOC analyst spends the first 72 hours doing analysis and decision-making, not data entry. That is the entire return on signing the envelope.
