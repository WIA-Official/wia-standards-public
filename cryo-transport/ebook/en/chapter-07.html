7.1 REGULATORY FRAMEWORKS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

International cryogenic transport must comply with multiple regulatory
frameworks that vary by region, transport mode, and cargo classification.

[CRYO] KEY REGULATORY AUTHORITIES:

United States:
  - DOT (Department of Transportation): Hazardous materials
  - FDA (Food and Drug Administration): Human tissues
  - TSA (Transportation Security Administration): Air cargo security
  - State regulators: Cryonics-specific licensing

European Union:
  - ADR: Dangerous goods transport by road
  - RID: Dangerous goods transport by rail
  - IATA DGR: Air transport dangerous goods
  - EU Tissues Directive: Human tissues/cells

International:
  - IATA: International air transport authority
  - ICAO: International civil aviation organization
  - WHO: Biological substances guidelines
  - UN: Hazardous materials classification


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
7.2 HAZARDOUS MATERIALS COMPLIANCE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Liquid nitrogen is classified as a hazardous material due to its cryogenic
properties and asphyxiation risk.

[!] UN CLASSIFICATION:

UN Number: UN1977
Proper Shipping Name: Nitrogen, refrigerated liquid
Class: 2.2 (Non-flammable, non-toxic gas)
Packing Group: Not applicable
Labels: Non-flammable gas label
ERG Number: 120 (Emergency Response Guidebook)

DOT Placarding Requirements:
  - Quantity >1000 lbs (454 kg): Placards required
  - Placard type: Non-flammable gas (green)
  - Four-sided diamond display

Shipping Papers:
  - UN1977, Nitrogen refrigerated liquid, 2.2
  - Quantity in kg or liters
  - Emergency contact phone
  - Shipper and consignee information

7.3 CUSTOMS AND BORDER PROCEDURES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Cross-border transport requires advance planning and documentation.

[CRYO] CUSTOMS REQUIREMENTS:

Pre-Transport Planning:
  1. Identify all border crossings
  2. Research country-specific requirements
  3. Obtain necessary permits (2-4 weeks advance)
  4. Verify customs broker if needed
  5. Prepare all documentation in required languages

Required Documents:
  ☐ Commercial invoice or pro forma invoice
  ☐ Packing list
  ☐ Certificate of origin
  ☐ Import/export permits
  ☐ Biological material certificates
  ☐ Health certificates (for human tissue)
  ☐ Customs declaration forms

Border Crossing Procedure:
  1. Arrive at designated crossing point
  2. Present all documentation
  3. Customs inspection (may include container opening)
  4. Pay duties/fees if applicable
  5. Receive customs clearance
  6. Proceed with transport

Inspection Considerations:
  - Opening container causes heat leak
  - Have LN2 refill capability ready
  - Temperature monitoring continues during inspection
  - Document any temperature excursions


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
7.4 REGIONAL CONSIDERATIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Specific regions have unique requirements that must be addressed.

[i] REGION-SPECIFIC REQUIREMENTS:

European Union:
  - Schengen Area: Simplified internal borders
  - Non-Schengen: Full customs procedures
  - Intra-EU tissues directive compliance
  - CE marking for medical devices if applicable

United Kingdom (Post-Brexit):
  - Separate from EU regulations
  - Import permits required from EU
  - Biological materials require health certificate
  - Customs declarations mandatory

Asia-Pacific:
  - China: Special permits for biological materials
  - Japan: Strict quarantine procedures
  - Australia: Biosecurity clearance required
  - Variable LN2 availability - plan refills carefully

Middle East:
  - Cultural considerations for human remains
  - Some countries prohibit cryonics
  - Verify legal status before planning transport
  - Temperature extremes (summer heat)

Africa:
  - Limited backup facility network
  - Infrastructure challenges
  - LN2 availability varies by country
  - Extended transit times common

CHAPTER SUMMARY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Key Takeaways:

  [1] International transport requires compliance with multiple regulatory
      frameworks including DOT, IATA, WHO, and country-specific authorities.

  [2] Liquid nitrogen classification as UN1977 Class 2.2 hazardous material
      requires proper labeling, placarding, and shipping documentation.

  [3] Customs procedures demand advance planning (2-4 weeks), complete
      documentation in required languages, and preparation for inspection.

  [4] Regional variations in requirements necessitate detailed research for
      each country on the transport route, with special considerations for
      EU, UK, Asia-Pacific, Middle East, and Africa.

  [5] International transport complexity increases costs and risks; air
      transport is generally preferred for distances >2000 km despite
      higher expense.
