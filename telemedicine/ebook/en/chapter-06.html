Cross-Border Federation and the Patient Consent Flow

Cross-border federation is the highest-leverage and highest-risk part of the standard. High-leverage because a patient can reach a sub-specialist no matter where the patient lives; high-risk because the same channel can leak sensitive personal-health information across jurisdictions if mis-operated.

6.1 The federation handshake

Two provider organisations federate by exchanging signed federation_handshake envelopes that name the patient consent reference, the share scope, and the maximum TLP level. Healthcare federation operates at TLP AMBER+STRICT by default — the receiver may share within the receiving organisation only.

6.2 Patient identity binding

Cross-jurisdiction federation relies on the patient identity having a verifiable mapping across both jurisdictions. The standard supports four mechanisms: eIDAS (EU), KFTC (Korea), national health identifier (NHS, Medicare), and patient-asserted DID (last resort). The federation handshake records which mechanism applies.

6.3 60-second revocation

The patient revokes federation consent by signing a revocation envelope. The originating provider stops emitting to the counterparty within 60 seconds; the counterparty stops querying within 60 seconds. The 60-second SLA is the most time-sensitive control in the protocol.

6.4 Cross-border clinical bundle

The cross_border_bundle envelope packages the relevant encounter, prescription, and PRO envelopes plus FHIR Bundle resources into a single signed artefact. Translations are referenced by language; the bundle records translation provenance so downstream consumers know which language pairs are derived versus original.

6.5 Pharmacy federation

A prescription emitted in one jurisdiction may be filled in another only when the prescriber is licensed in both jurisdictions, or when a receiving prescriber re-issues under their own licence after review. The prescription_re_issuance envelope captures the latter case, preserving the original prescriber's intent while making the re-issuing prescriber's licence the legal basis.

6.6 Emergency exception

In emergencies (loss of consciousness, life-threatening event) where the patient cannot give consent, federation MAY proceed under a treating-clinician exception envelope signed under their professional licence. The exception is itself signed and audit-trailed; the patient (or next of kin) reviews the exception after the fact and may challenge it through normal regulatory channels.

Worked example: telestroke

A rural emergency department without 24/7 neurology coverage federates with a tele-stroke service in another jurisdiction. A patient presents with stroke symptoms; the local clinician initiates federation under emergency exception. The remote neurologist reviews imaging in real time and recommends thrombolysis. The local clinician administers under their own licence. The patient counter-signs the consent retroactively during recovery; the recommendation is recorded as a referral envelope from the remote neurologist.
