Chapter 1 · WIA Telemedicine v1.0.0

Why Telemedicine Needs an Open Wire Format

Telemedicine has gone from a niche service to the dominant care channel for whole categories of patients — psychiatry, dermatology, chronic disease management, second opinions. Every clinic and every payer has spun up a stack to deliver it. Almost none of those stacks talk to each other.

1.1 The schema-drift tax in healthcare

A patient who had a teleconsultation in one clinic six months ago and is now seeing another provider arrives without a portable record. The patient's symptoms, prescriptions, and lab orders live in proprietary EHR fields, fragmentary FHIR exports, screenshotted prescription PDFs. Each handoff loses information; each information loss is a clinical risk.

1.2 What an open wire format buys

The standard defines a small set of signed envelopes — consultation_session, clinical_encounter, e_prescription, appointment_slot, patient_consent, patient_reported_outcome, referral — and a discipline for signing them with Ed25519 over the canonical JSON form (RFC 8785 JCS). The envelopes are not a replacement for FHIR R5 or IHE; they are a cross-vendor binding that wraps existing FHIR clinical content with patient-controlled consent and signature semantics.

1.3 Patient ownership

The most important property of the standard is that the patient's signature on consent is what makes downstream clinical envelopes legitimate. This inverts the traditional model — provider creates record, patient receives copy — into a model where the patient is the durable owner of the record and providers contribute signed observations to it.

1.4 What this book covers

Chapter 2 covers consultation session envelopes and the WebRTC signalling path. Chapter 3 covers clinical encounter envelopes and FHIR R5 binding. Chapter 4 covers e-prescriptions and the drug-interaction discipline. Chapter 5 covers appointment scheduling across jurisdictions. Chapter 6 covers cross-border federation and the patient consent flow. Chapter 7 covers patient-reported outcomes and chronic-care continuity. Chapter 8 closes with worked examples spanning psychiatry, telestroke, and chronic-care diabetes management.

Worked example

A 67-year-old patient living in a rural county with no resident endocrinologist needs ongoing diabetes management. Pre-standard: the patient travels four hours each quarter for a 20-minute appointment. Post-standard: monthly 30-minute video consultations with their preferred endocrinologist anywhere in the jurisdiction, continuous CGM data flowing under WIA Vital-Sign Streaming, prescriptions filled at the local pharmacy via the e-prescription envelope, the patient owns the entire encounter record. The patient's outcome improves; the system's cost falls.
