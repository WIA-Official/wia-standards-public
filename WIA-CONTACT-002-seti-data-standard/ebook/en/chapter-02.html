Chapter 2: Signal Classification and Taxonomy

2.1 The SETI Signal Hierarchy

Not all signals are created equal. A radio telescope pointed at the sky receives countless signals every second—from terrestrial interference to natural cosmic phenomena to (potentially) artificial extraterrestrial transmissions. The WIA-CONTACT-002 taxonomy provides a systematic framework for categorizing these signals based on their characteristics, origin probability, and verification status.

Primary Classification Levels

Level Category Origin Action Required
0 RFI (Radio Frequency Interference) Terrestrial/Human-made Discard
1 Natural Cosmic Pulsars, quasars, masers Catalog, study separately
2 Ambiguous Unknown/Uncertain Further analysis
3 Candidate (Unconfirmed) Possibly artificial Immediate verification
4 Candidate (Multi-site) Likely artificial Protocol escalation
5 Confirmed Contact Extraterrestrial intelligence Activate WIA-CONTACT-001

2.2 Signal Characteristics

Frequency Domain Features

Signals are first characterized by their frequency properties:

  • Narrowband: Bandwidth < 5 Hz (high technosignature probability)
  • Wideband: Bandwidth > 1 MHz (typically natural or terrestrial)
  • Drifting: Frequency changes over time due to relative motion (Doppler shift)
  • Pulsed: Regular or irregular temporal patterns
  • Continuous: Steady-state transmission

Temporal Characteristics

Time-domain analysis reveals critical information:

  • Duration: Transient (milliseconds) vs. persistent (hours/days)
  • Periodicity: Repeating patterns or single events
  • Modulation: Amplitude, frequency, or phase modulation indicating information content
  • Sideral correlation: Whether signal follows celestial coordinates (not Earth-based)

2.3 The Rio Scale

The Rio Scale (analogous to the Richter scale for earthquakes) quantifies the significance of SETI detections. WIA-CONTACT-002 mandates Rio Scale calculations for all Level 3+ signals.

Rio Scale Components

Q × δ = R

Where:

  • Q: Class of phenomenon (1-10, based on observation type and class)
  • δ: Distance factor (0-1, based on source distance)
  • R: Rio Scale value (0-10)

Rio 0-3: Low significance, likely natural or terrestrial
Rio 4-6: Moderate interest, requires verification
Rio 7-8: High significance, probable contact
Rio 9-10: Extraordinary, confirmed extraterrestrial intelligence

2.4 Machine-Readable Classification

All signals must be tagged with standardized metadata in JSON format. This enables automated verification workflows and machine learning analysis across facilities worldwide.

2.5 False Positive Categories

Experience teaches us that 99.9% of interesting signals are false positives. Understanding common false positive types accelerates elimination:

Terrestrial Interference

  • Satellite downlinks: Regular frequency patterns, orbital periodicity
  • Aircraft radar: Pulsed signals with Doppler shift
  • Cell towers: Persistent wideband noise
  • Electronic equipment: Harmonics from local electronics

Natural Astrophysical Sources

  • Pulsars: Highly periodic, broadband pulses
  • Masers: Narrowband but predictable celestial sources
  • Fast Radio Bursts (FRBs): Transient, usually extragalactic
  • Lightning/atmospheric: Broadband, impulsive

2.6 Candidate Signal Requirements

For a signal to achieve Level 3 (Candidate Unconfirmed) status, it must satisfy specific criteria that distinguish it from routine cosmic phenomena and terrestrial interference.

2.7 Escalation Pathways

When a signal is promoted from one level to the next, specific actions are automatically triggered through the WIA-CONTACT-002 API, ensuring rapid global coordination.

2.8 Living Taxonomy

The signal classification system evolves as we discover new phenomena. The WIA-CONTACT-002 standard includes a governance process for proposing new categories, updating thresholds, and refining criteria based on observational experience.

Every six months, the SETI Science Committee reviews classification accuracy, false positive rates, and candidate signal statistics to recommend standard updates. This ensures the taxonomy remains scientifically rigorous while adapting to advancing technology and knowledge.
