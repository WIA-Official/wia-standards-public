Chapter 7: Candidate Verification Workflows

When a detection pipeline identifies a potential extraterrestrial signal, the critical work of verification begins. False positives vastly outnumber genuine detections, and rigorous verification workflows are essential to avoid both false alarms that erode public trust and missed detections that could represent humanity's first contact with alien intelligence. This chapter defines the step-by-step procedures that transform raw candidates into verified discoveries.

7.1 Verification Philosophy

"Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence." — Carl Sagan

The verification philosophy balances urgency against rigor. A transient signal may disappear within hours, demanding rapid response. Yet a premature announcement of extraterrestrial contact would cause massive social disruption and potentially discredit legitimate future discoveries. WIA-CONTACT-002 threads this needle through structured escalation.

Core Principles

  • Independent confirmation: Multiple facilities must detect the signal independently
  • Systematic elimination: Rule out all plausible terrestrial and natural explanations
  • Documented chain: Every step recorded for scientific and historical record
  • Appropriate speed: Urgency scaled to signal characteristics and stakes

7.2 Verification Tiers

Tier 0: Automated Screening

Before human review, automated systems filter out obvious false positives:

Check Criterion Action if Failed
Known RFI match No match in RFI database Auto-reject, log for database update
Satellite exclusion Not coincident with known satellite Auto-reject
Directional consistency Signal tracks astronomical source Downgrade, manual review
Beam pattern Not present in all beams Pass to Tier 1

Tier 1: Same-Site Verification

The originating facility attempts to reconfirm within 2 hours using different methods:

  1. Re-observation: Point telescope back at source, verify signal persists
  2. Nodding observation: Alternate on-source/off-source to confirm directionality
  3. Different backend: Process with alternate receiver or digitizer if available
  4. Independent analyst: Second team reviews data blind to initial detection

7.3 Elimination Checklist

Before a signal can be classified as potential ETI, all known explanations must be systematically ruled out:

Terrestrial Sources

Source Type Verification Method Exclusion Criterion
Satellites TLE database cross-reference No satellite within 1° at signal time
Aircraft ADS-B flight data No aircraft in beam
Ground transmitters Direction finding, RFI database Signal tracks celestial coordinates
Internal interference Multi-beam anti-coincidence Only one beam affected

Natural Astrophysical Sources

Phenomenon Distinguishing Feature SETI Differentiation
Pulsars Broadband, periodic pulses Narrowband signal (< 5 Hz)
Masers Known spectral lines, locations Not at maser frequency, unknown location
FRBs Millisecond duration, dispersion Longer duration, no dispersion
Interstellar scintillation Affects known sources Signal at empty sky coordinates

7.4 Signal Persistence Analysis

Temporal Behavior Classification

Signal persistence provides important clues to origin:

  • Transient (< 1 hour): Could be satellite, aircraft, or intermittent transmitter
  • Intermittent (hours-days): May indicate rotating source or scheduled transmission
  • Persistent (weeks+): Strong indicator of stable, intentional beacon
  • Repeating (regular period): Could indicate artificial scheduling or natural rotation

Drift Rate Analysis

The signal's Doppler drift provides crucial verification evidence:

Expected Drift Rates

A signal from a planet orbiting a distant star would show drift rates consistent with:

  • Earth's rotation: up to ±0.5 Hz/s at 1 GHz
  • Earth's orbit: ±0.02 Hz/s at 1 GHz (seasonal variation)
  • Source planet's rotation: Variable, potentially larger

A terrestrial source would show different patterns—stationary objects show only Earth drift, while satellites show predictable orbital signatures.

7.5 Human Expert Review

After automated verification, human experts provide critical judgment:

Review Panel Composition

  • Radio astronomers: 2-3 experts in radio interferometry and signal processing
  • SETI specialists: 2-3 researchers with technosignature expertise
  • RFI engineers: 1-2 experts in radio frequency interference
  • Statisticians: 1 expert in detection theory and significance

Review Criteria

Criterion Question
Data quality Are the observations properly calibrated and free of artifacts?
Analysis validity Was the detection pipeline correctly configured and functioning?
Exclusion completeness Have all plausible alternatives been adequately ruled out?
Cross-facility agreement Do independent observations yield consistent results?
Statistical significance Is the detection statistically robust against false alarm?
